Disruption of a neuronal-specific protein degradation system, the plasma membrane-bound neuroproteasome, triggers the conversion of endogenous tau into aggregates that mirror those seen in patients with Alzheimer's disease (AD). Aging and APOE4 — the largest risk factors for AD — induce neuroproteasome loss, which provides a potential mechanistic basis for sporadic AD. You have full access to this article via your institution. Many neurodegenerative diseases are defined by the conversion of soluble proteins into aggregates. In AD, the protein tau misfolds into paired helical filaments (PHFs) in the absence of mutations in tau or overexpression of tau protein. For decades, a fundamental question, of how endogenous wild-type tau converts into this specific aggregated state, has plagued the field. Although aging and the APOE4 allele are the strongest risk factors for developing sporadic AD, how they dysregulate specific cell biological mechanisms to trigger AD-relevant protein aggregates has been a mystery. We previously described a neuronal-specific system of protein degradation, termed the neuroproteasome1,2, which is differentiated from canonical intracellular systems by the fact that these complexes span the plasma membrane. This localization implies unique functions3 and regulation, but a lack of tools has precluded further understanding. We developed and leveraged selective neuroproteasome inhibitors to understand their function. Simultaneously, we generated endogenous conditional epitope knock-in mice to enable the purification and visualization of neuroproteasomes. We intentionally avoided using mouse models of AD, as we felt it was crucial to understand the cell biology in wild-type physiological conditions. We demonstrated that disrupting neuroproteasome function alone is sufficient to rapidly trigger the conversion of endogenous tau into an insoluble state4 (Fig. These tau aggregates resembled the hallmark PHFs seen in AD and were phosphorylated at AD-relevant sites. Crucially, we established a direct molecular link between neuroproteasomes and APOE. We found that the presence and function of neuroproteasomes decline considerably with age and are impaired by the high-risk Apoe4 isoform in mice. These patterns are also observed in human brain tissue and correlate with brain regions that exhibit tau pathology (Fig. Neuroproteasome dysfunction shifts the threshold for tau aggregation in an APOE-isoform dependent manner, rendering APOE4-expressing neurons more susceptible and APOE2-expressing neurons relatively protected (Fig. Together, our findings suggest that aging and APOE converge on neuroproteasome function to determine neuronal susceptibility for protein aggregation. Understanding how tau aggregation begins is crucial to shift the therapeutic focus from reactive strategies that target existing protein deposits to proactive measures that maintain the neuron's proteostatic capacity before irreversible neurodegeneration begins. As we learn more about neuroproteasomes, we expect that future studies will reveal such therapeutic targets. APOE, aging and probably other unidentified factors influence neuroproteasome function, which raises the possibility that tau aggregation occurs sporadically only after multiple factors sufficiently compromise the system over time. Our studies, even in vivo, are over very acute timeframes, so — given the chronic nature of AD — the long-term consequences of neuroproteasome loss will be important to determine. It will also be important to determine whether other protein degradation systems eventually react to or compensate for neuroproteasome loss. Finally, whether and how neuroproteasomes influence other major AD drivers, such as amyloid-β aggregation or gliosis, are unknown and important to determine. We are focused on deeply understanding the neuroproteasome itself — how it is positioned in the membrane, how it is regulated, where within the neuron and in the brain it is localized, and what functions it has in different cells, circuits and brain regions. It is a remarkably complex system performing some unconventional and important biology. “This is an elegant cell biology paper that provides a mechanism, specifically neuroproteasome dysfunction, for how endogenous tau protein can form insoluble aggregates in neurons. Notably, this mechanism is modulated by APOE genotype and aging, suggesting clues into how AD pathology develops.” Shari Wiseman, Chief Editor, Nature Neuroscience. began an undergraduate cloning project to identify neuroproteasome interactors. It still amazes us that as outsiders to the AD field, we uncovered an important mechanism by not studying tau itself, but through a basic science investigation of a poorly understood (at least for now) mechanism of neuronal protein degradation. We remember when we saw the first evidence of insoluble tau in mice after neuroproteasome inhibition — everyone huddled around the developer in disbelief. We tested this in many different ways over years, leading to the stunning observation though electron microscopy that the insoluble tau we had been seeing by immunoblot was indeed PHFs. From starting a laboratory to moving institutions, and enduring the collective pressures our community has faced, this work stands as a testament to what can be achieved through curiosity, teamwork, a bit of serendipity and sheer grit. Rapid and high-fidelity nanoscale 3D printing is highly desirable, but it is difficult due to the tradeoff between speed and accuracy. This challenge is typically addressed by using multiple projections, but it slows down printing. Here, we present grayscale projection two-photon lithography to overcome this tradeoff. Despite using a binary mask, it enables projecting more than 15,000 focal spots, each with independently tunable intensity. It advantageously leverages constraints imposed by optical diffraction to achieve grayscale tuning over the entire field at once.
