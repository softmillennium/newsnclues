Too tricky to cancel: Amazon faces US trial over alleged Prime subscription deceptions
Source: {'href': 'https://www.theguardian.com', 'title': 'The Guardian'}    Published: 2025-09-22 20:12:00

The Federal Trade Commission's complaint, filed in June 2023, alleges that Amazon knowingly used designs known as “dark patterns” to deceive consumers into signing up for the $139-per-year Prime service during checkouts. The case centers on two main allegations: that Amazon enrolled customers without clear consent through confusing checkout processes, and that it created a deliberately complex cancellation system internally nicknamed “Iliad” – after Homer's epic about the long, arduous Trojan War. The case was heard in a federal court in Seattle by judge John Chun, who is also presiding over a separate FTC case that accuses Amazon of running an illegal monopoly, due to go to trial in 2027. Amazon was allegedly aware of widespread nonconsensual enrollment in Prime but resisted changes that would reduce these unwanted sign-ups because they negatively affected the company's revenue. The FTC alleges that Amazon's checkout process forced customers to navigate confusing interfaces where declining Prime membership required finding small, inconspicuous links while signing up for the service was done through prominent buttons. Crucial information about Prime's price and automatic renewal was often hidden or disclosed in fine print, the FTC also alleges. The lawsuit also targets Amazon's cancellation process, which required customers to navigate what the FTC describes as a “labyrinthine” four-page, six-click, 15-option process to cancel their membership.
