The second season of “Jury Duty” dramatically expands the scope of its “everyone is muppets except for one human actor” conceit. In “Company Retreat,” the reality-scripted hybrid recruits talented improvisers to feign working for a small hot sauce business, Rockin' Grandmas, for the benefit of one real person who is recruited under the belief that they are here to work a real temp job — albeit one under the scrutiny of a documentary crew. While “Jury Duty” had set courtroom hours and one core location, “Company Retreat” required the actors to be “on” almost constantly, ready to guide its real-life hero, Anthony Norman, down the scripted rails of a weeklong retreat designed to go awry in ways both quietly and cartoonishly cringe. That put a lot of pressure on the shoulders of casting director Susie Farris to find performers who could pivot on a dime and constantly embody the much narrower character demands of the Rockin' Grandmas employees.
