Amazon & Walmart Are Creating Stablecoins: Why Is This a Big Deal?

Imagine logging into your Amazon account or walking into a Walmart, and instead of paying with a credit card or cash, you use digital dollars issued by Amazon or Walmart themselves. This isn't sci-fi - it's the very real possibility of Amazon and Walmart launching their own stablecoins (digital currencies pegged to real money like the US dollar). Such a move could shake up the way we shop, bank, and pay, in ways that both disrupt the financial system and improve everyday customer experiences. In this blog, we'll break down what's going on in simple terms and why it matters, even if you're not into crypto or tech. What on Earth Is a Stablecoin, Anyway? Think of it like a digital dollar bill stored in an app on your phone. Unlike Bitcoin, a stablecoin's price doesn't swing wildly; it's stable (hence the name) because it's backed by real assets or currency reserves. In other words, it's the digital lovechild of crypto and cash: it lives on a blockchain (a fancy public ledger on the internet) but behaves a lot like the money in your bank account. Why would you care about a digital dollar? Because it can make money super easy to move around. You could send a stablecoin to anyone in the world in seconds, 24/7, like sending an email - no bank scheduling needed. For example, a farmer in India could sell goods to a buyer in Brazil and get paid within minutes using stablecoins, without either of them worrying about exchange rates or a payment getting held up. Stablecoins take away the “Will $100 today be $90 tomorrow?” anxiety - $100 in a stablecoin is designed to stay $100 tomorrow. This reliability has made stablecoins hugely popular in the crypto world: by 2024, people were transacting trillions of dollars in stablecoins each year, even surpassing some major credit card networks in volume! In short, stablecoins are digital cash that's fast, global, and doesn't fluctuate in value - perfect for everyday money matters. Retail Giants Jumping In: What's the Big Deal? Amazon and Walmart, the two retail behemoths, have reportedly been exploring launching their own stablecoins (imagine something like “Amazon Coin” or “Wal-Coin”). When companies of this size and reach delve into crypto, it sends a strong signal across industries. If Amazon or Walmart creates a stablecoin, it could suddenly bring this digital money into the mainstream in a massive way. Why would they bother creating their own currency? The short answer: to save money and improve your shopping experience. Today, when you buy something on Amazon or swipe your card at Walmart, banks and credit card networks (like Visa or Mastercard) take a cut (via processing fees, interchange fees, etc.). Those fees might be invisible to you, but they cost retailers billions. Reports suggest Walmart and Amazon want to use stablecoins to cut out the middlemen - no bank or card network needed - which could save them billions in credit card fees over time. In fact, the news alone of these retailers considering stablecoins sent credit card company stocks tumbling, because it's a direct threat to their fee-based business. But it's not just about fattening Walmart's or Amazon's profit margins. Crucially, some of those savings could trickle down to shoppers. With their own stablecoin, Amazon could reduce prices or offer discounts when you pay with “Amazon Coins” - essentially rewarding you for using their currency. (They already do something similar with their store credit cards; a stablecoin could amplify that.) Likewise, Walmart might give cash-back in Wal-Coins or special deals to loyal customers who adopt its coin. The appeal for these companies lies in locking in customer loyalty and keeping you within their shopping ecosystem. A Walmart Coin, for instance, might work like a super-charged store credit: you'd be more inclined to spend it at Walmart, and in return you might get better prices or perks - a win-win for loyalty. Let's break down the potential benefits these retail stablecoins could offer, both to the companies and to everyday people: If they pull it off, paying for your groceries or gadgets might never be the same. Shaking Up Banks and Credit Cards (Financial System Disruption) It's not just about tech giants playing banker - this trend could rattle the traditional financial system. If millions of customers start holding and spending Amazon Coins or Walmart Coins, that money isn't sitting in bank accounts anymore. So if Amazon and Walmart convince everyone to load up their apps with stablecoins, banks might have to scramble. One finance writer warned that this stablecoin shift could force banks to reevaluate their whole model, as they risk losing a key source of funding. Then there are the credit card networks. For decades, Visa, Mastercard, and AmEx have sat in the middle of our transactions, quietly taking a cut every time you swipe or tap. Retailer stablecoins could cut these middlemen out of many transactions. If you pay Amazon directly with Amazon Coin, Visa doesn't earn a cent from that sale. Multiply that by millions of sales and you see why this is a huge threat to the card companies. In fact, accepting stablecoins could help retailers sidestep those hefty interchange fees (the fees merchants pay to card issuers) entirely. Less reliance on credit cards also means consumers might not need to run up balances on high-interest cards for everyday purchases - you could use a stablecoin that represents actual money you have, not borrow on credit. It's telling that when the news broke about Amazon and Walmart's stablecoin explorations, Visa's stock price dropped ~5% and Mastercard's ~4% - investors know this could shake up the status quo. From a customer's perspective, these changes could be positive, but they come with new considerations. On the plus side, as we noted, shoppers could enjoy simpler and cheaper payments. Who wouldn't want a payment method that's as quick as a text message and doesn't ding you with hidden fees? Consumers are always chasing anything that saves time or money, and stablecoins promise both. You might get the same $50 value to spend, but because it's in an Amazon/Walmart coin, you skip extra charges and maybe even earn perks. If Amazon or Walmart holds some of your funds in their coin, they'll need to provide customer service and security on par with banks. People will expect that their Amazon Dollars are safe, insured, and easily refundable if something goes wrong - otherwise they won't trust it for their paycheck or savings. This is new territory for retailers: dealing with fraud claims, lost passwords, or technical glitches that affect money. As one Nasdaq analysis put it, traditional players and new stablecoin systems will be in a “collision course,” and customer trust and service will be tested in new ways as we all adjust to this hybrid of retail and finance. In other words, Amazon might need to staff up its customer support not just for late packages, but for issues like “I sent $100 in Amazon Coin to the wrong person, help!” It's a big responsibility. Banks won't go down without a fight, either. We might see them respond by offering their own digital currencies or better deals to keep customers onboard. Some are already lobbying for clearer regulations to ensure a level playing field. Ultimately, the entry of retail giants into currency creation is blurring the lines between tech companies and financial institutions. The challenge is ensuring stability and fairness - regulators will be watching closely to make sure these new coins are safe and don't destabilize the wider economy. But make no mistake, the financial system is poised for a shake-up. As one expert noted, money might shift from bank accounts to digital wallets managed by retailers, and that changes decades-old financial norms. It's akin to a power shift: some power moving away from Wall Street and the banking sector, into the hands of Silicon Valley and Main Street retail companies. A New Era for Everyday Shoppers (Customer Experience 2.0) Beyond the big finance implications, what would your day-to-day experience look like in an Amazon/Walmart stablecoin world? Both Amazon and Walmart built their empires by obsessing over customers. Amazon, in particular, has set gold standards for convenience - remember how they made two-day shipping the norm? So, imagine the same kind of focus being applied to how you pay and manage your money as a customer. For one, payments could become almost invisible. If you're using an Amazon stablecoin, it might be integrated right into your Amazon app or browser. This removes a lot of the minor friction that we've just accepted up until now. Think about Uber: you get out of the car and the payment happens without you doing anything. Stablecoin payments could bring that kind of effortlessness to many more transactions. You might simply scan a code in the Walmart app at a store, and your Walmart Coin balance instantly transfers to the store for your purchase. No counting cash or waiting for chip readers; it's as fast as handing over a dollar, but digitally. Customer service around finances might improve in some ways. For instance, refunds and returns could be processed quicker. If you return an item, rather than waiting 5-7 business days for your bank to reflect the refund, the retailer could just release your stablecoins back to you on the spot. Also, consider loyalty programs: instead of juggling gift cards, coupons, and rewards points, a stablecoin could unify these. Your rewards might be in the coin itself. Starbucks had a huge success with its Starbucks card/digital app - effectively customers pre-load money and are rewarded for it. Amazon and Walmart stablecoins could work similarly but on a larger scale. It's simpler and more tangible than points that you later convert. Data from stablecoin transactions can give retailers insights into customer habits. (Amazon already excels at using data to recommend products and streamline your experience.) For example, if Walmart's data sees you often transfer money to family abroad using Walmart Coin, maybe they offer a new low-cost remittance service just for that. Or Amazon notices you spend a lot on electronics, so paying with Amazon Coin gives you an extra warranty or support plan automatically. The possibilities for innovative customer perks are wide open. However, we should note: using a retailer's currency means you're somewhat tied to their ecosystem. Amazon might make it easy to pay other merchants with Amazon Coin via its payment gateway, but if it doesn't, you're incentivized to keep shopping on Amazon to use your balance. Some critics might say this gives companies even more control over our spending. On the other hand, if it comes with enough benefits (savings, speed, inclusion), people may be fine with that trade-off. Security and trust will be paramount for a good experience. These stablecoins would need to be ultra-secure (Amazon and Walmart will likely implement strong security to prevent hacks or fraud). They know that one high-profile hacking incident could sour consumers on the whole idea. And because not everyone is crypto-savvy, expect Amazon and Walmart to hide the blockchain complexity under a very user-friendly interface. You might never have to think about private keys or crypto wallets - it'll just be your Amazon account balance. This “hiding of the tech” is key for mass adoption; people will use what's convenient and trustworthy, not what's technically cool. So, a big part of the customer experience change is that using digital currency could become as easy as using a gift card or a Starbucks app, without needing to know how the underlying system works. Lastly, think about those who have limited access to banking today. A Walmart stablecoin could be loaded at the store with cash and then used online or in-store, bridging a gap for people who don't have credit cards. It effectively can turn cash into a versatile digital payment. That's a significant service improvement for many customers - no bank account needed, no prepaid card fees; just convert cash to Walmart Coin and you're set to shop or even send money to others at low cost. In a way, Amazon and Walmart might compete with banks by offering a quasi-bank account experience (minus lending, for now) that lives on your phone. To sum up, the change in customer experience could be dramatic: faster and cheaper payments, more rewards, simpler global transactions, and greater inclusion for those left out by the current banking system. If executed well, you as a shopper might say: “Wow, paying for stuff is easier and more rewarding than it used to be!” The key will be whether Amazon and Walmart can build enough trust and demonstrate clear benefits to get everyday people on board.
