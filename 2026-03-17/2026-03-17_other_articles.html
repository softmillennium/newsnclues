Archaeologists find the largest extraterrestrial iron object from the Bronze Age in China, impossible to manufacture with the technology of the time
Source: {'href': 'https://www.labrujulaverde.com', 'title': 'La Brújula Verde'}    Published: 2026-03-17 17:01:58

Chinese scientists have confirmed that a peculiar iron object unearthed in the famous sacrificial area of Sanxingdui has an extraterrestrial origin. The discovery, published in the journal Archaeological Research in Asia, presents the results of analyses carried out on a metal piece shaped like an axe or elongated tool, about 20 centimeters long, which was recovered from pit number 7 of the sacrificial area of Sanxingdui. This site, one of the most important in China, dates from between 2800 and 600 BCE, and its period of greatest splendor coincides with the Shang dynasty (1600–1046 BCE). The eight pits discovered in the area have yielded thousands of ritual objects, such as bronze masks, figurines, and bronze trees, which have completely transformed the understanding of Bronze Age culture in China. Initial examinations using portable X-ray fluorescence already yielded a surprising result: the piece was composed of more than 90% iron, but also contained 7.41% nickel, an element very uncommon in terrestrial iron obtained through smelting. This chemical composition, together with the antiquity of the archaeological context—well before the development of iron smelting technology in China—led researchers to suspect that they might be dealing with meteoritic iron. To confirm this, the team took a sample from fragment 2 and subjected it to metallographic analysis and scanning electron microscopy with X-ray spectroscopy (SEM-EDS). When observing the piece under an electron microscope, scientists saw a microstructure composed of equiaxed ferrite grains, that is, crystals of pure iron of uniform size and without deformation. The chemical maps obtained by SEM-EDS showed that nickel—with an average concentration of around 20%—was distributed completely homogeneously throughout the sample. Nickel and iron diffuse at very different rates, and obtaining such a uniform, nickel-rich alloy would have required technologies that did not exist until millennia later. In the words of the study, this chemical homogeneity in terms of high-nickel iron would have been extremely difficult to achieve using any smelting technology known during the late Shang period, further supporting a meteoritic origin rather than cast iron for this artifact. Moreover, the dark areas visible in the microphotographs after chemical etching turned out to be cracks produced by corrosion, not different metallic phases. Spot analysis of these areas revealed high levels of oxygen and a decrease in iron and nickel, confirming that they are oxidation products. The Sanxingdui specimen stands out for several reasons. It is, so far, the largest in all of China. As for its shape and decoration, the authors note that, although intense corrosion prevents determining whether it had ornaments, it shows no evidence of inlays, unlike other northern objects that do feature turquoise inlays.In this respect, it more closely resembles finds from Xinjiang, also outside the cultural sphere of the Central Plains. However, differences in shape and deposition context indicate distinct regional metallurgical traditions, with no direct connections. Another striking feature is its very high nickel content, around 20%, well above the average of other known objects, which is about 11% and can range between 0.03% and 29.3%. One of the questions archaeologists are asking is what this object was used for. In the Central Plains, meteoritic iron was usually employed as cutting edges inserted into bronze weapons or tools, forming bimetallic objects associated with high-status burials. In those cases, researchers believe that artisans cast bronze around the already existing iron blade, embedding it in molds. However, the authors point to an intriguing detail: many of the bronzes from Sanxingdui, including masks and vessels, show cut marks made after casting, suggesting that very hard tools were used to modify them. The meteoritic iron axe, being harder than bronze, could have been one of those tools. As the oldest meteoritic iron artifact from the Bronze Age found in southwestern China, it fills a critical gap in the region's metallurgical records and provides new insights into the early use of iron at both regional and global levels. For example, it has not been possible to determine which specific group of meteorites the iron belonged to. This would require more detailed analyses, but the fragile condition of the piece makes further cleaning or extraction difficult. For now, what is already certain is that, more than three thousand years ago, in a ceremonial center in southwestern China, someone placed in a pit an object made from a material that came from the sky, and that gesture has reached us as a fascinating testimony to the technical ability and symbolic thinking of Bronze Age societies.
