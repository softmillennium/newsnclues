Robot Swarms Could Solve Blockchain's Oracle Problem, Researchers Say
Source: {'href': 'https://finance.yahoo.com', 'title': 'Yahoo Finance'}    Published: 2025-09-23 23:43:21

The idea, detailed in a new preprint study titled Swarm Oracle: Trustless Blockchain Agreements through Robot Swarms, builds on earlier peer-reviewed research where researchers demonstrated that mobile robots could reach reliable consensus, even in times of disruption, cyberattack, or in hostile environments. The new study applies that approach to a persistent problem in blockchain design: how to get verified real-world data into smart contracts without introducing new points of trust. Blockchains like Ethereum are built to be trustless—each node independently verifies transactions. But that same design prevents smart contracts from accessing external information, such as weather reports, price feeds, or sensor readings, without third-party input. Today's blockchain oracles, like Chainlink, aggregate data from multiple sources to reduce reliance on any one feed. But they can still reintroduce centralized risks, either through opaque aggregation methods or single points of failure. The system uses a collective of simple, low-cost mobile robots—each equipped with basic sensors and communication hardware—to gather environmental data and reach consensus through a Byzantine fault-tolerant protocol. Once a consensus is reached, the swarm can publish its findings to a blockchain, where the data becomes available to smart contracts. In a 2023 Nature study, researchers showed how swarms could maintain consensus accuracy even when up to one-third of robots were compromised, misreporting data, abstaining from voting, or physically interfering with other robots. In the new system, the robots host a permissioned blockchain locally, allowing them to store and verify data without needing continuous internet access. When appropriate, they can upload finalized agreements to public blockchains like Ethereum. This provides a mechanism for “self-healing,” with faulty or malicious robots excluded from future consensus rounds. Use cases for Swarm Oracle include verifying disaster damage for insurance claims, monitoring air or water quality, or supporting decentralized physical infrastructure networks (DePINs). By operating independently and across varied terrain, the robots can reach areas that are inaccessible or too costly to monitor. While robots can recover from temporary disconnections, long distances may strain communication. The idea of robots as blockchain participants isn't new—projects like Helium have explored decentralized hardware oracles for specific tasks such as network connectivity. The concept is a part of a growing interest in using autonomous agents to make economic decisions, such as routing deliveries or managing grid loads. Robotics developers are also embedding cryptocurrency wallets into autonomous systems to carry out transactions for their users. Whether Swarm Oracle can move from simulation to real-world deployment remains to be seen, with cost, availability of the robots, and a general mistrust of AI slowing adoption.

Book Review: Digital Currency or Digital Control?: Decoding CBDC and the Future of Money, Nicholas Anthony
Source: {'href': 'https://www.independent.org', 'title': 'Independent Institute'}    Published: 2025-09-23 18:06:26

Policymakers and commentators increasingly propose central bank digital currencies (CBDCs) to address shortcomings in the payments system. Advocates argue that CBDCs would address several issues with the traditional monetary system, especially financial exclusion and delayed settlement. The shortfalls of modern payments closely resemble issues often noted with social media. Social media platforms exclude users and throttle content—delaying “settlement” with the public. Despite these parallels between payments and posting, calls for state-run payment infrastructure far exceed those for state-run social media. The asymmetry, I suspect, springs from an uneven sensitivity to risk and potential abuse. But such a platform for spending—what could go wrong? Digital Currency or Digital Control, Nicholas Anthony's new volume, offers a needed corrective to idealistic CBDC proposals that overpromise and would likely under-deliver. Anthony is perhaps uniquely situated to evaluate the rise of CBDC technology, in all its forms and guises. He maintains the Human Rights Foundation's CBDC Tracker, which “documents CBDC development and civil liberties concerns around the world.” In Digital Currency or Digital Control, Anthony subjects CBDCs to sustained scrutiny. The result is fair, rigorously argued, exhaustively researched, and highly readable. The book offers an overall introduction to and evaluation of CBDCs. He then argues that CBDCs would fail to make good on their promises of improved financial inclusion, faster payment settlement, and more effective monetary policy (Chapter 3). He concludes cynically, but plausibly, that “the only ‘benefit' of CBDCs accrues to governments seeking to solidify their control over money and finance” (87). The final chapter ends with a call to raise awareness and pass legislation that prohibits CBDCs. Sample legislative language appears in one of the three informative appendices. Against the pro-CBDC argument from financial inclusion, Anthony correctly notes that CBDCs would not “bank the unbanked” since CBDCs exist to facilitate payments, not banking services (17). Anthony also cites FDIC survey data to argue that few of the unbanked would find a CBDC attractive. For example, many cite a lack of trust in private banks as a reason for self-exclusion from banking services. Overall people tend to trust the government even less than private banks (18 ff. On whether CBDCs would improve transaction settlement, existing infrastructures already enable near-instant payments for retail users, leaving little for CBDCs to improve. Stablecoins, in particular, provide near-instant settlement over global, permissionless payment networks that operate 24/7/365 with low fees. It's not clear that the government could build an equally performant system without substantial cost or repealing current rules that require customer documentation. In 2023, the UK's HM Treasury advertised a listing for the Head of Central Bank Digital Currency with a salary range between £61,260-£66,500. Proponents of CBDCs often claim that central banks could use them to spur spending through negative interest rates and fine-tune the economy at the level of individuals. But we now have access to alternative savings vehicles like foreign currencies, stablecoins, and Bitcoin. Moreover, using CBDCs for targeted interventions would require collecting user-level financial data. This would threaten financial privacy and alienate many of the unbanked, who cite “financial privacy” as their second-most common reason for avoiding private banks (FDIC). To appreciate the threat CBDCs pose to financial privacy, consider how uneasy we already feel about the separate buckets of personal data harvested by social media and AI companies. Now imagine consolidating all that data into a single honeypot on government servers—exploitable by malicious hackers, rogue bureaucrats, and authoritarian politicians. CBDCs would expose citizens in similarly dangerous ways. As Anthony argues, our payments data tell detailed stories about the whats, whos, wheres, whens, and whys of our economic lives. Cypherpunk Nick Szabo famously says that trusted parties are security holes. But they are nonetheless also security buffers, as Anthony argues in Chapter 7. By consolidating several trusted parties into one—the central bank—CBDCs consolidate potential points of failure. Many CBDC proposals also include built-in programmability for restricting locations, times, and types of transactions. This centralization of power also exposes citizens to government abuse. Even if today's officials act responsibly, the historical record offers no assurance that tomorrow's will. A single-tier CBDC would also compete with private banks and payments providers. Any CBDC that survives Congress would likely emerge limping, scorched, and missing a few parts. Perhaps, then, governments should integrate the existing banking system into CBDCs. Some scholars, such as Easwar Prasad, have proposed such a two-tiered model where the currency remains a direct liability of the central bank while private entities serve customers and collect their data (“Central Bank Digital Currencies and the Future of Central Banking,” Cato Journal 41, no. Such a system might sound like the best of all worlds, with banks serving as safeguards for financial privacy and a buffer against government attempts to control and censor. Like Anthony, however, I'm skeptical whether governments would invest in a system that does not ultimately expand their surveillance and control over retail transactions. I'm also not convinced that a proposal like Prasad's would improve enough on leading stablecoins to justify the costs of developing a new payments system. Even if it did in the abstract, governments recently have a poor track record when they try to bring plans into reality. Or consider FedNow, the Federal Reserve's real-time payments network. Anthony suggests that Bitcoin users could fork the Bitcoin software to create a new network, leaving the old, vulnerable network behind. I disagree with little else in the book. Anthony's analysis is especially timely, as governments around the world continue to advance their own designs.
