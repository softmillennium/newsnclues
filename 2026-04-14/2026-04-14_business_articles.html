I quit my six-figure job in New York City and now make less than $1,000 a month. I regret nothing.
Source: {'href': 'https://www.businessinsider.com', 'title': 'Business Insider'}    Published: 2026-04-14 14:54:00

I had been thinking about quitting my job as a vice president of a New York City-based boutique public relations agency for years. Reaching this milestone made me immensely proud. I didn't realize how much it would intensify the pressure to perform. Agency work in public relations is a breeding ground for endless work: clients pay monthly retainers in exchange for being in the press as much as possible. While I tried to stick to regular hours, I was often overwhelmed by the workload and voluntarily put in overtime. When I started traveling in South America last March, I thought the physical distance from New York would help alleviate some of the pressure. I was in an enviable position, making US money while living in countries with a lower cost of living. But there was a side effect: the NYC grind mindset, which I had previously deemed motivating, unmasked itself to be toxic. I would pull myself away from my computer for 10-minute breathers to watch people making a fraction of my salary walk their dogs and cheerfully dance in the park. I barely had the energy to lift my coffee cup. That's when I realized: money wasn't worth sacrificing my health and sanity. When I resigned last October, seven years of pressure lifted in an instant. For the first time, I wasn't working toward a goal. But the burnout revealed itself soon after my last day of work. I slept over 10 hours a night and struggled to get out of bed. My mind had tried for years to keep up with my ambitions, and it was finally done. I've been staying with my parents since then, saving money and enjoying some family time after 12 years abroad. I've reconnected with old friends, tried new hobbies, and traveled Europe on a budget. Most days, I don't set an alarm. After a hearty breakfast, I sit down at my computer to read the news, pitch stories, and plan projects. I can also use my time to help friends move or babysit their kids. I currently make less than $1,000 a month through different revenue streams: freelancing in journalism, working as an indoor cycling instructor, and taking on odd jobs at reception desks and the like. The fitness instructor gig makes me especially happy; I get to work out while motivating others to do the same. A few weeks ago, I started to feel the burnout leave my body. Slowly, I am rediscovering my old self, the one who's not afraid of change and worries about the future another day. I often have to stop myself from doom-scrolling the LinkedIn posts of laid-off journalists and freelancers struggling to make ends meet. The clock is ticking, and I can't live like this forever. I haven't made this little money since college, and I have adjusted my spending habits accordingly. Quitting and walking away from financial security, colleagues, and clients-turned-friends, was one of the hardest things I've ever done. Entering uncertainty terrified me, and it still does. For now, I'm putting together a new life piece-by-piece. The other day, a client at the gym mused over what she would do if she didn't have kids and a full-time job.

Rwanda's visa-free model fuels Africa's shift toward open borders
Source: {'href': 'https://africa.businessinsider.com', 'title': 'Business Insider Africa'}    Published: 2026-04-14 06:44:58

Rwanda's decision to grant visa-free entry to all African nationals is accelerating a broader continental shift towards freer movement, as governments increasingly align with trade ambitions and competition for tourism, investment, and business travel intensifies. These countries consistently rank highly on the Africa Visa Openness Index, widely used by policymakers and investors to assess travel accessibility. Analysts say Rwanda's approach demonstrates how national policies can translate continental ambitions into tangible outcomes for travellers, entrepreneurs and investors. Ghana recently eliminated visa requirements for all African passport holders, a move widely interpreted as a strategic effort to reinforce its position as a regional business hub and host of the AfCFTA secretariat in Accra. Meanwhile, Kenya has simplified entry rules for African travellers by scaling back earlier administrative requirements, particularly for citizens of the East African Community. Industry observers note that these reforms are not occurring in isolation. “Visa openness is increasingly viewed as a competitive advantage,” one regional policy analyst said, pointing to growing rivalry among African cities to attract conferences, investment, and talent. Airlines are responding by launching more direct routes between African cities, reducing reliance on traditional hubs outside the continent. Analysts say the combination of open borders, national carrier expansion and investment in infrastructure has enabled the city to compete with established centres such as Nairobi and Addis Ababa. Destinations such as Seychelles and coastal regions in Tanzania are increasingly marketing simplified entry requirements to attract African tourists. At a policy level, the push for openness is underpinned by the African Union's long-standing goal of free movement, including plans for a common African passport. Despite notable progress, a significant proportion of intra-African travel still requires advance visas, and implementation varies widely. Administrative hurdles, digital processing systems, and inconsistencies at border points continue to affect traveler experiences. Countries such as Nigeria and South Africa are gradually easing restrictions, though both face pressure to accelerate reforms amid competition from more open destinations. Improved rankings on the visa openness index suggest progress, particularly in expanding visa-on-arrival schemes and streamlining procedures for business travellers. Even so, the trajectory is clear, with Rwanda joining a growing bloc of visa-free pioneers and more countries adopting partial reforms, Africa's travel landscape is undergoing a structural shift. As mobility improves, analysts argue that the real test will be whether policy changes translate into seamless travel experiences. If they do, the vision of a more integrated and economically connected Africa may move closer to reality.
