At the World Cheese Awards, taste trumps smell as thousands of offerings compete for the crown
Source: {'href': 'https://www.theglobeandmail.com', 'title': 'The Globe and Mail'}    Published: 2025-11-14 17:54:42

As the 37th edition – part competition, part a celebration of cheese – kicked off in Switzerland on Thursday, some visitors might wish the offerings had more holes. Connoisseurs, culinary experts and curious consumers flocked to the three-day event in a country where cheese is both food and folklore. All those offerings in the same Bern exhibition hall made for an original mélange of odours. John Farrand, managing director of the Guild of Fine Food in Britain, the event organizer, says some people who tell him they “don't like cheese, it's that awful smelly thing” just need to take time and consider the myriad choices. “I get so many people say to me just impulsively ‘I don't like blue cheese' and that's impossible really. There's so much of a range of blue cheese from over here to over here,” – Farrand gesticulated around the hall – “there's always a blue cheese for somebody.” He rhapsodized about a 120-kg wheel of cheese that teams rolled in and “broke” – or cut open – unleashing a powerful cloud of odour. “The aroma of this Emmentaler just hit me,” Farrand said. Some 46 countries took part in the World Cheese Awards, a record count of competitors from Australia to Austria, Bulgaria to Brazil.Anthony Anex/The Associated Press Some may turn up their noses at bacteria-blued “bleu” cheeses or reject the strong odours of varieties like Limberger, Taleggio, “Stinking Bishop” and Époisses de Bourgogne – a Burgundy specialty reputed to be Napoleon's favourite, and one so stinky that urban legend claims it's banned from public transport in France. More adventurous tasters will try the most gooey or mouldy cheeses, looking for the most rich, creamy or meaty varieties on hand. For the judges, no such compunction: It's more about scrutiny, savouring, criticism and curiosity. They sliced wedges out of hard cheeses and pressed them to their noses, or used spreaders to scoop up soft cheeses, inspecting the consistency and licking or dribbling them onto their tongues. Journalists were allowed into the area only under escort, and were only allowed to view and smell the cheeses – not taste or even touch them. It was a blind taste test for the 265-odd judges on hand: All identifying packaging or marking was removed from the cheeses. Their job was to poke, peruse, sniff, touch and taste the offerings – a tall order with so many to choose from – before making their selections for gold, silver and bronze awards based on attributes like aroma, body, texture, flavour and “mouthfeel.” While the final products from the “caseiculture” – the curdling, coagulating, cheddaring and other processes involved in making cheeses – can be judged on aspects like craftsmanship and quality, taste is an individual thing. This year's winner was Swiss: A “spezial” Gruyere from the Vorderfultigen Mountain Dairy about 20 kilometres south of Bern, which scored 85 points from the jury. The raw cow's milk cheese was drained overnight and dry salted before being matured for more than 18 months. A creamy, flower-sprinkled “Crémeux des Aldudes aux fleurs” from the village of Etxaldia in French Basque country was runner-up, trailed by a 9-month-aged Swiss Appenzeller Edel-Würzig. Many cheeses come away with other accolades: More than 20 were selected as national or regional “bests” – such as best American, Basque-country, Japanese or Ukrainian cheese. The competition started in Britain, but Italy, Spain and Norway have also hosted.
