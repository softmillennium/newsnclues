A statement from a national organization of architects regarding the alleged misuse of the word “architect” in negative news contexts has sparked discussion among Filipinos. The United Architects of the Philippines (UAP) released an open letter, saying it “strongly denounces the persistent misuse of the word ‘architect'” when “describing masterminds of plunder, corruption, crime, or other negative acts.” It was signed by its national president, architect Jonathan Manalad. “Recent headlines and articles — such as referring to a government official as an ‘architect of plunder' — reflect a long-standing trend of equating the word ‘architect' with the mastermind of wrongdoings,” the organization said on Tuesday, September 23. Recent headlines in the Philippines have focused on alleged corruption in flood control projects, with claims that government officials and lawmakers received kickbacks from these initiatives. “This is not only misleading but gravely unjust to a profession that has, for centuries, been dedicated to the highest ideals of public service, safety, progress, and nation building,” the UAP added. “Words matter,” the group said, adding that using “architect” in news reports as a synonym for ‘mastermind of crime' supposedly “corrodes public perception of the profession, trivializes years of education, licensure, and ethical responsibility, and undermines the trust that architects have worked hard to earn from the Filipino people.” The statement drew mixed reactions from Filipinos, with some arguing that there are more pressing issues, such as the reported government corruption involving flood control projects. New book explores the murderous and paranormal history of North Central Ohio
Source: {'href': 'https://www.mansfieldnewsjournal.com', 'title': 'Mansfield News Journal'}    Published: 2025-09-24 09:05:42

Decades before Mark Sebastian Jordan wrote about the murderous and paranormal history of North Central Ohio, his convictions wrangled with discoveries he made every day. “I very much kind of came at things from a scientific perspective.” As a youngster, he thought he might grow up to be a paleontologist, volcanologist or some other scientist. “I saw no provable scientific basis for ghosts, so I didn't really give it much thought,” Jordan said. Now, 40 years of research later, he has so much evidence of hauntings throughout the region that he published them in a book called "In These Haunted Hills: Ghosts of the Ohio Highlands." The transition from doubter to devotee happened one day after college when Jordan clocked out for his lunch break. “I was still living at home with my folks at this time, about halfway between Mansfield and Shelby,” he said. Exhausted, he sat down and turned on the television. “I can hear the sound of Mom fiddling around with some pots and pans in the kitchen,” Jordan said. Like a good uncle, he reached back to acknowledge the baby's playful advances. The experience forced him to reevaluate his worldview. “That was the first point when I was confronted with the realization, ‘Oh, maybe there are things going on that I can't easily explain or dismiss,' and that was all it took,” Jordan said. Searching for and recognizing new encounters became one of his favorite hobbies. “It's almost like a radio thing,” Jordan said. “I've been all over the state and beyond doing ghost hunting,” Jordan said. He is known throughout the Buckeye State as an author, playwright, columnist, newspaperman, music critic and folk artist. “I've been for the last few years trying to have out a new book every fall.” He took a sip of coffee with one hand, then lovingly patted his kitten, Lady Jane, with the other. He decided in August that nothing would stop him from having a new book, so he called a second publisher and asked the absolute fastest they could edit and print his first run. The new book is his ninth completed, but eighth published. The book, "In These Haunted Hills: Ghosts of the Ohio Highlands," is a mixture of his own personal experiences, folklore from the region, eyewitness accounts he has collected and history he has researched. “In a couple cases, I've been able to go and find the roots of stories that everyone just dismissed as folklore,” Jordan said. The stories are from Richland, Ashland, Holmes, Coshocton, Knox and Crawford counties. “I have one chapter that is just specifically about hauntings in private residences throughout the region that I've experienced,” Jordan said. There's also a look at The Renaissance Theatre in downtown Mansfield. And some of the stories will be news to many readers, like the tale of Sarah's grave in a haunted cemetery near Greer that eventually became popular among high school students. “Guys would take their dates and, hopefully, the dates would get scared and hug them a little closer,” Jordan said. “I dug through the detritus of time and found that there is a true story that whole situation arose from and there actually was a Sarah.”
