By Samuel López | USA Herald - A concept once confined to classrooms and late-night science programming is now being pulled into the center of a rapidly evolving national conversation on UFO disclosure. The framework developed and popularized by Carl Sagan—built on the logic of Flatland—is reemerging as policymakers and public officials begin exploring explanations for unidentified aerial phenomena that extend beyond conventional aerospace theory. Luna and others have suggested that the traditional extraterrestrial narrative—physical craft traveling from distant star systems—may not fully account for what is being observed. That idea, once relegated to speculative science fiction, aligns in a surprising way with Sagan's teaching model. Sagan expanded that analogy to suggest that if higher dimensions exist beyond human perception, interactions from those dimensions would not appear as complete, stable objects. They would appear fragmented, shifting, and at times physically impossible. These characteristics have resisted traditional analysis, leaving investigators with data points that do not comfortably fit within known models of physics or engineering. From a legal and investigative standpoint, the implications are significant. Evidence must always be evaluated within the limits of observation. If those limits are inherently constrained—whether by human perception or by the dimensional structure of reality itself—then the conclusions drawn from that evidence must remain open to reinterpretation. What lawmakers like Luna appear to be doing is testing the boundaries of that framework in a public policy setting. For years, the debate over UFOs was framed as a binary question: either these objects were misidentified human technology, or they were extraterrestrial visitors traveling through space. The interdimensional concept introduces a third possibility—one that does not rely on long-distance travel, but instead on proximity within a layered reality that we do not fully perceive. If these phenomena are not bound to our three-dimensional constraints in the same way we are, then their behavior would naturally appear anomalous. There is no confirmed evidence that UAPs are interdimensional entities. What exists is a growing acknowledgment that current explanations are insufficient to account for all observed data. As disclosure efforts continue to unfold—through hearings, reports, and ongoing investigations—the public is being asked to engage with ideas that were once considered purely academic. The challenge is to do so without abandoning critical thinking or evidentiary standards. From where I stand, analyzing this as both a journalist and a legal professional, the most compelling aspect of this development is not the theory itself, but the willingness of public officials to explore it openly. That alone marks a departure from decades of dismissal and silence. But the fact that it is being discussed at all signals a broader shift—one that aligns, perhaps unexpectedly, with the intellectual groundwork laid by Sagan decades ago. And in a moment where the questions are becoming more complex by the day, that method may prove to be more relevant than ever.
