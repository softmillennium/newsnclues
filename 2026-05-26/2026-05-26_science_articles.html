Abdoun et al. argue that there is no evidence for anticipatory preactivation of sound frequency-specific information in predictable tone sequences. Their primary argument is that the manner of sound transition—typically to an adjacent tone frequency—is confounded with a bias in decoding, where confusions between neighboring tone frequencies are more probable. According to their interpretation, this confound could independently contribute to above-chance decoding in the ordered sequence, even without invoking predictive processing. To support their claims, the authors present multiple lines of evidence, most notably a computational (a.k.a. The latter involves reshuffling epochs from random sequences to mimic ordered ones. This reordering procedure is the principal empirical support for Abdoun et al.'s claim: by systematically rearranging random sequences to resemble increasingly ordered ones and applying a decoding approach similar to that used in Demarchi et al., they obtained parametric changes in pre‑target decoding accuracy that resemble the empirical data. Because these random sequences lack predictive information about the upcoming sound, the authors conclude that our observed pattern cannot reflect anticipatory predictive activity. Despite these challenges, their critique raises valid considerations that merit discussion within the methodological constraints of the original study. However, the authors further claim that “there is no evidence of a feature-specific prediction-related neural activity in the human auditory system based on the open access dataset of Demarchi et al.” Yet, research also cited by the authors, such as work by ref. 2, introduced an alternative analytical framework using variations of the Demarchi paradigm that directly address the core issue raised by Abdoun et al. (see detailed discussion below). When applying both approaches, we have consistently reached similar conclusions2,3 Adapting the analysis approach of Schubert et al., we will first demonstrate that the original dataset also contains strong evidence of feature-specific neural preactivation. This challenges the blanket assertion made by Abdoun et al. Also, to move the discussion beyond theoretical debates, we present novel data that control for the confounding issue at the group level, providing compelling evidence for feature-specific neural predictive preactivation patterns in the auditory system. We conclude with a non-exhaustive list of unresolved issues, and lessons-learned by engaging in this valuable discussion with Abdoun et al. Aware of the issue described by Abdoun et al., Schubert et al.2,3 introduced an analytical approach specifically designed to address whether brain activity patterns indicate predictions of the most probable transition direction, assuming an ordered sequence. This was accomplished by training a classifier on ordered sound presentations using all off-diagonal (i.e., transition) trials and testing it on the on-diagonal (i.e., repetition) trials of both the ordered and random sequences. Because the test set comprises only identical self‑repetition trials, the concern raised by Abdoun et al. that decoding performance and the stimulus sequence of the test set may both be biased, does not apply. Here, we present an adaptation of the elegant Schubert et al. approach and apply it to both the real data and reordered sequences in the manner of Abdoun et al. This approach has recently been further developed and successfully applied4. Here, we outline the key elements of the method to aid clarity, while referring the reader to4 for a full account, including detailed implementation and additional considerations. In the first step, we train an LDA classifier to decode carrier frequency from both the ordered and random sequences, using only off-diagonal trials and excluding self-repetitions. This results in two models, each containing information about the carrier frequency presented at 0 ms training time. We interpret this model as reflecting top-down predictive recruitment of carrier frequency-specific patterns. The classifier derived from the random sequence, by contrast, is trained only on sequences that lack predictable transitions and captures bottom‑up, stimulus‑driven preactivation. Self‑repetition trials are relabeled as the expected next tone (e.g., A/A is relabeled A/B), ensuring that predictive recruitment can be quantified without transitional bias. Consequently, when the trained classifiers are applied to the test set, the top-down model (trained on ordered sequences) draws its informative pattern from negative training times, whereas the bottom‑up model (trained on random sequences) derives its relevant information from positive training times when tested in a time‑generalized manner (see Fig. Self‑repetition trials are relabeled as the most probable next tone and used for testing, ensuring that transitional bias is minimized. To substantially advance the discussion, novel experiments are required that empirically disentangle predictive processes from the bias introduced by transitions between neighboring tones. As a first step along these lines, we conducted a pilot study with twenty‑seven healthy volunteers (18 female, sex/gender info was self-reported; mean ± s.d. : 23.26 ± 2.72 years; subjects recruited within a course and compensated with credits). The task was closely modelled on1 but incorporated key modifications to eliminate the impact of decoding bias. Participants completed four continuous blocks of passive sound listening, each containing 4 000 tones. Two blocks served as baselines for the decoding approach: one comprised a fully randomized sequence of four pure tones logarithmically spaced between 200 Hz and 2000 Hz, and the other comprised eight such tones. The remaining two blocks were “ordered” sequences: within each block, the order of tones was fixed, but the specific order was randomized for each participant. As a result, across participants the ordered frequencies were randomly distributed across positions, preventing any consistent mapping between sequence position and neighboring frequency. To reduce decoding bias, future experiments would benefit from incorporating more carrier frequencies than those used in Demarchi et al. (see, e.g., ref. 7) and from varying the transition matrices across participants or even within participants (as in ref. For within-subject manipulations, faster stimulation rates (> 3 Hz) may be worth exploring. When eight tones are used, transition slopes lie almost entirely within a narrow range near zero; with four tones the average slope is similarly low, but the distribution includes some large values. In such cases, increasing the sample size beyond what is typically used would help, though we note that the issue raised by Abdoun et al. is already mitigated at the group level in our sample of 27. A drawback of randomizing transition matrices is that it produces jumpy stimulus sequences. In natural environments, stimuli typically exhibit temporal autocorrelation, characterized by structured dependencies across time. For sound frequency, this is reflected in smooth pitch transitions or spectral content changes in speech, as well as melodic and harmonic progressions in music. Consequently, many scientific questions regarding neural preactivation patterns may require retaining the confound described by Abdoun et al. In such cases, we recommend employing the analysis approach outlined by Schubert et al.2 or the extension described above. If the original Demarchi et al. analysis is applied—for instance, to compare conditions within participants (e.g., attended versus non-attended ordered sequences) or across groups—it is crucial to also assess the decoding bias of the trained classifier. Such contributions are invaluable for advancing the field and deepening our collective understanding of neural prediction processes. While some issues remain—e.g., selective interpretation of findings for example, questions about how certain findings are interpreted—we agree that the concern they raised is important for the specific combination of design and analysis used in the original manuscript. Their critique prompted us to refine our analytical approaches and to conduct a new study, both of which provide evidence for frequency‑specific predictive preactivation in the auditory system. More broadly, our work underscores that while theoretical arguments and simulations can highlight potential issues, the broader questions about predictive processing in the auditory system must ultimately be resolved empirically through carefully designed experiments. The gut microbiota can profoundly influence host behavior, yet underlying mechanisms for microbiota-driven behavioral adaptation remain elusive. Here, we demonstrate that this oviposition preference is gut microbiota-dependent, as it is abolished by microbiota depletion and restored by microbiota transplantation. This colonization facilitates bacterial dissemination while reducing F1 fitness during larval feeding. Comparative transcriptomics implicates the takeout gene (TO1) in regulating oviposition preference, and TO1 knockdown abolishes this behavior. Moreover, P. putida supplementation restores TO1 expression and mature-leaf preference in germ-free females. These findings reveal an insect–microbe–plant feedback loop driving microbiota-associated behavioral adaptation. Petroleum chemicals reach freshwater habitats and can elicit acute toxic effects on ecosystem members.1Until recently standardized acute toxicology experiments and water quality standards (also referred to as Maximum Allowable Concentrations in some jurisdictions) focused on mortality of aquatic organisms after 96 h of toxicant exposure2,3, but in nature sublethal effects can manifest into mortality and in lotic environments (streams and rivers) exposure times can be far shorter (< 24 h). Here we show that Plains Minnow (Hybognathus placitus), Fathead Minnow (Pimephales promelas), and Flathead Chub (Platygobio gracilis) (Family Leuciscidae) exposed to sublethal concentrations of diesel fuel for 24 h experienced sublethal injuries which render them susceptible to death by predation. Diesel-exposed prey represented 77% of predated fish across all predation events. Diesel-exposed Fathead Minnow had significantly decreased lateral line neuromast counts and severe ophthalmic pathology. Standardized toxicity experiments would have missed these key effects. Sublethal petroleum exposures have global implications for the conservation of fish species and derivations of water quality standards intended to protect fish from petrochemical pollution need to include sublethal endpoints with the potential to manifest into population loss. Sex differences in ageing and lifespan are widespread across taxa, yet their evolutionary causes remain debated. A leading hypothesis proposes that such differences arise adaptively from sex-specific life-history trade-offs, but formal theoretical support is lacking. We therefore develop a mathematical model to examine how these trade-offs shape lifespan evolution across ecological and mating system contexts. Varying trade-off strengths shows that male-biased longevity emerges across a wider parameter range, consistent with patterns in monogamous species. This asymmetry arises because increased female longevity reduces offspring production, lowering population size and mating rates and thereby constraining selection on female lifespan, whereas longer male lifespan evolves more readily unless mating costs are high. Incorporating density dependence shows that ecological feedbacks disproportionately reduce survival in the intrinsically longer-lived sex, potentially masking evolved differences. Beyond monogamy, we show that mating system variation shapes longevity through competition intensity, determined by the limiting sex, and demographic scaling, where female number sets reproductive output and the selective context for longevity. Overall, we highlight that life-history trade-offs and demographic feedbacks jointly determine the direction and magnitude of sex differences in lifespan.
