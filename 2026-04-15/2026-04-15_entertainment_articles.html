Instead, the producers kept returning to another idea, about a small-town bureaucrat working hard to make the world a better place. Even as NBC entertainment co-chair Ben Silverman begged them to stay focused on a spinoff, they made a series of counterintuitive decisions, insisting on the small-town non-spinoff, and giving up the Super Bowl for Poehler. More than a decade later, Silverman would remain irritated by this. It continued to stay on for seven seasons despite low ratings and coming near cancellation almost every year. It didn't even sell in syndication that well — traditionally the hallmark of success for sitcoms until fairly recently — but it took off once it hit streaming, with a presumed assist from the mood of the country. Now many original admirers share it with their kids when they hit 12 or so, and it continues to attract young fans. Unlike its predecessor, it dropped (most of) the cringe early to focus on a portrait of a hyper-competent woman and her friends working their hardest to serve their constituents, no matter how wacky, unreasonable, or distasteful they may be. It developed a clear philosophy of life, asserting that work, friends, and love can all coexist — and, in fact, function best when they are enmeshed cozily. Its sunny optimism, emphasis on community, and deeply nerdy sense of civic engagement made it stand out like a dorky teacher's pet among the cooler kids of its time, which tended toward the cringe and the cynical. It was also overtly feminist at a time when we preferred to say “female-empowered.” I tell the story of its unlikely survival and afterlife ascendance in my new book “Parks and Rec: The Underdog TV Show That Lit'rally Inspired a Vision for a Better America.” It's a behind-the-scenes story that mirrors the show that it's about, with a good-hearted group of eccentrics coming together to make something great against all odds. “PARKS AND RECREATION's overwhelming resemblance to THE OFFICE caused many viewers to simply see it as a ‘carbon copy' of a successful show,” Deadline reported. “The pilot was seen as ‘predictable' and lacking in character development, even for a pilot.” Further, “SNL fans felt [Poehler's] character Leslie was a bit ‘too serious' and ‘too low-key' and many expected her to have more energy and enthusiasm, especially when she is getting drunk at the end of the show.” Who doesn't love getting drunk with gusto? At the same time, NBC launched a marketing campaign that revealed their continued lust for an “Office” spinoff. The six-episode first season was, indeed, hit-and-miss, as it followed Leslie and her team's efforts to fill in a dangerous pit, brought to her attention by local nurse Ann (Rashida Jones) after her boyfriend Andy (Chris Pratt) fell into it and broke his leg. The pacing moved slowly, it was excessively pit-focused, and Leslie was uncomfortably clueless, a la Michael Scott (Steve Carell) of “The Office.” This quality didn't wear as well on Leslie Knope, or perhaps the supporting characters didn't do enough to balance her out, or perhaps, alas, a bumbling woman just read differently. The character set up as her love interest, city planner Mark Brendanawicz (Paul Schneider), was a bummer, too. The backstory was that they'd slept together once and Leslie still held a flame for him, but he did not reciprocate. Andy's band turns out to be not necessarily good, but entertaining, particularly when delivering their anthem “The Pit,” which plays as a self-aware joke about the show's pit focus. The episode also closes many loops from the pilot: Andy and Ann break up, and Leslie gets a chance to decline Mark's drunken advances. “Parks and Rec” eked out a next-season order on the promise of that episode. Its next season improved noticeably, particularly with the very first episode back, “Pawnee Zoo,” in which Leslie unknowingly “marries” two male penguins at the zoo during a publicity stunt, becoming a flashpoint in the gay marriage debate. From then on, “Parks” would become a small-town satire of big-time politics, which gave it a distinct identity from “The Office,” and Leslie would grow into a hyper-competent public servant up against the foibles of everyone around her. Near the end of the second season, Schur wanted to reward Leslie with a truly great love interest, and he thought back to a guy who'd auditioned for the role that became Mark, Adam Scott. He would slot into a very different part from the ill-fated planner, who was being written off. Scott would play Ben Wyatt, a former teen mayor who'd ruined his town financially and was now paying penance by becoming a state budget auditor. NBC asked Schur to meet with Rob Lowe, who was leaving the drama “Brothers and Sisters” and had a knack for attracting masses of viewers to shows. Lowe had asked the network about being on “30 Rock,” a series he loved, maybe as a nemesis for Alec Baldwin's network president character, Jack Donaghy. The network gently suggested he consider saving this other fledgling show. Though Schur didn't think Lowe was right for Leslie's love interest, he took the meeting and immediately envisioned a character: hyper-vigilant about his health and relentlessly optimistic, even while slashing small towns' budgets. Schur took a chance and asked the network to hire both men; he got his wish. So the producers came up with a scheme to use it to their advantage: They proposed continuing production immediately after Season 2 wrapped, banking seven episodes that could begin airing in the fall. NBC agreed, and the writers threw everything they had into those episodes, with Leslie planning a Harvest Festival that had to fund the parks department to save it from being cut, and Ben and Leslie falling in love along the way. Here, we got some of the show's best episodes, like “Flu Season,” “Time Capsule,” “Indianapolis,” and “Harvest Festival.” Even still, the network announced plans to replace “Parks and Rec” on the fall schedule with a new comedy, “Outsourced.” The cast and crew were crushed; they couldn't believe that their relentless work — which had turned out astonishing episodes — still wasn't enough. But they were vindicated when “Outsourced” failed and they returned to the schedule in January of 2011 with a barrage of greatness. This resulted in a very funny internet promo spot for the return, with Rob Lowe incensed to learn how long he hasn't been on TV: “Parks and Recreation” signed off in 2015 with an emotional crowd-pleaser of a finale that showed the characters — the major ones, and quite a few minor ones — enjoying successful futures by fast-forwarding through decades to come. Even though the show had made it to its seventh season, it had done so just barely, having faced probable cancellation nearly every year. Its shortened final season — 13 episodes aired at a clip of two-per-week in January and February — was something of a gift to the producers from NBC, which had come to respect the series' skill, its chutzpah, and its dedicated fanbase … even though that fanbase was never very large, particularly in comparison to contemporaries such as “The Office” and “30 Rock.” It became a time capsule of an era when faith in government seemed possible, and community connection made life worth living. During the 2020 pandemic in particular, this became an appealing message, giving viewers a place to hang out with optimistic, hopeful TV friends who were delivering comfort from the recent — but now seemingly all-too-distant — past. Leslie and her gruff-but-caring, staunch Libertarian boss Ron Swanson (the incomparable Nick Offerman) became symbols of bygone bipartisanship, not to mention endless memes. Leslie's invented holiday, Galentine's Day, became, essentially, real, with merch and celebrations going mainstream. This all made “Parks and Recreation” the perfect choice to produce the pandemic's first major original network programming, a reunion episode — via video conferencing — that aired on April 30 and raised $2.8 million for Feeding America. Its run through its go-to bits, like Ron enjoying his isolation and Leslie insisting on a phone tree to foster togetherness, came with extra emotional impact this time. Whether sparring with Sylvester Stallone over screen credit, trading threats with CAA power agent Mike Ovitz, or battling with directors and studio executives for creative control, Eszterhas — a Hungarian-born former journalist who made his name at Rolling Stone during its heyday — could always be counted on for attention-grabbing copy. He was still capable of commanding the spotlight when it suited him — as when he went public about his tumultuous collaboration with Mel Gibson on an unproduced project about the Maccabees — but for the most part, Eszterhas seemed content to live a mild-mannered life in Ohio, going to his kids' ball games and carrying the cross at his Catholic Church. Eszterhas made headlines before the movie even came out when his spec script earned him $3 million for what was supposedly about 13 days of work — though Eszterhas said that timeline is misleading. “Really, I was thinking about that script for years and years,” Eszterhas said, noting that the inspiration for Sharon Stone's Catherine Trammell began with an affair he had in college with a faculty member's wife. Over 10 years later, Eszterhas was a reporter at the Cleveland Plain Dealer when he met the second person who would exert a powerful influence on “Basic Instinct.” “I was a police reporter, and I got to be drinking buddies with a policeman who had shot three or four people,” Eszterhas said. Invincible highlights its best and worst natures almost simultaneously
Source: {'href': 'https://www.avclub.com', 'title': 'AV Club'}    Published: 2026-04-15 07:02:20

At points—especially as it accelerates toward a spectacular, apocalyptic climax—”Rash” manages to transcend Invincible's usual cruising-altitude tendencies, making for a fitfully fascinating hour of TV. But then my mind flicks back to that absolutely nothing, two-line exchange, tossed between Nolan Grayson and Viltrumite third-stringer Anissa as they briefly face off amid a wider Goku-on-Goku space fight, and I'm reminded of why this show so frequently exhausts me. The back-and-forth—which doesn't boil down to much more than “You're weak!” “Nuh-uh!”—illustrates nothing about Nolan's strained relationship with his people, or what makes Anissa different from any of her near-identical cohorts, or even the actual moment-to-moment content of their face-off. We open with (god help me) another Viltrum Empire flashback, showing how a brief moment of leniency on Imperial Regent Thragg's part allowed Thedas to assassinate Viltrum's beloved abusive dad/formative emperor Argall. But as sales pitches for that concept go, it's pretty weak. There's at least a bit of queasy fun to be had when Thragg uses Argall's funeral as an attempt at a little light bonding-through-atrocity, inviting his people to violently cull each other with various Mortal Kombat finishers as a celebration of “strength.” (The death count for this little communal murder orgy isn't clear, but the sheer scale of the slaughter is one more point against the show's repeated attempts to try to make me feel bad by showing the big ring of corpses surrounding the planet. Any suggestion that Viltrumite lives were precious to these psychopaths before the Scourge virus hit them is pretty clearly bullshit.) But that spark of excitement, winning out over mustachioed stoicism, is short-lived, as we finally return to the exact moment in the present that we left off on last week. Only Battle Beast gets to have any fun, with Michael Dorn taking nice, juicy bites out of the scenery.) Which means it's time to talk about The Problem With Viltrumites again. I've said my piece on the topic of Space Beef before, but this episode is lousy with it: largely anonymous Viltrumites floating weightlessly in space, shouting generic taunt barks at our heroes before slamming into them, with Thragg watching over the carnage like some sort of demonic Mario. It feels telling that, despite appearing in a number of episodes at this point, I can only keep some of these guys clear in my memory by their voice actors. My notes are full of lines like “The Clancy Brown one” or “The guy voiced by Phil LaMarr.” (They're joined tonight by “Grandma Hair Knife,” who at least has a slightly more diverse set of fighting moves.) Invincible tries to add some diversity to this latest slab battle, giving Tech Jacket darts that make Viltrumites easier to rip apart and introducing Space Racer and a pack of the ice dinosaurs from back in episode two after the initial wave of energy has flagged. But a decent chunk of this episode is still given over to that “Don't count on it!” stuff: action figures smooshing into each other because someone told this show it needed to be an hour long, despite frequently only seeming to have 30 minutes' worth of material. But, hey: That was all pretty negative, so let's take a second to give out some kudos. Outside the fights themselves, which are mostly pretty dull, this is the first Invincible in a minute that's seemed to have any interest in genuine visual storytelling, using the expansive canvas of animation to not just tell a story yanked from the comic books but to do something cool as animation. There are grace notes throughout “Don't Do Anything Rash,” from the way the hostile Viltrumites are seen lurking like murderous ghosts amid their dead billions to the demonic flash that fills the screen when Mark Grayson shoots his shot and tries to punch Thragg, only to find out how thoroughly he's been outclassed. There are moments here where director Stephanie Gonzaga and her team are clearly thinking seriously about how pacing and visual flourishes contribute to an episode's sense of mood without having to lean on music or dialogue. And while that sounds like a pretty low bar for an animated project to clear, it's one Invincible has rarely felt all that interested in hurdling over in the past. At the same time, the show briefly remembers that it can be a pretty great character drama when it wants to be, as Thragg takes a flash-step smacking of Nolan's ass down through Viltrum's atmosphere as an opportunity to privately pitch him on a very different flavor of redemption arc. This is literally the first time I've heard Lee Pace, a fine actor elsewhere, seem to actually give a shit about anything happening around him in this show, and it's compelling: Thragg lays out everything seductive about supremacist ideology for a lightly receptive Omni-Man, reminding him of how comforting it can be to lay down your morality and just embrace a home that looks and feels like a philosophy of strength. (Which, conveniently, always seems to match up with Viltrumite strength; anything else is simply trickery or “cowardice.”) J.K. Simmons has fun playing verbal tennis here; Nolan doesn't entirely dismiss what Thragg is saying, so much as he points out that it's a losing position; Viltrum's already dead. Which is when we finally see a crack of emotion in Thragg, one that recontextualizes all that skull-fetishizing he did last week: Despite ruling over a planet of 50 people surrounded by a planetary ring of corpses, he snaps when Nolan suggests Viltrum is a “tomb.” It only took all season, but we've finally got something compelling to latch onto with our main villain. He spends the rest of the episode taking it out on everybody else, as we learn that Thragg really is in a whole other class of strength compared to every other character on this show. Invincible has had a lot of fights where new villains shrug off our heroes' best hits, but rarely so convincingly; at no point does Thragg even sweat as he trounces everybody around him. In the end, Nolan and Thedas conclude that they can't score any points by hitting the man himself, so they strike instead at his heart: Space Racer fires his “goes through anything” gun at Viltrum's core, and Mark, Nolan, and Thedas fly down through the hole, eventually cracking the planet in half. Thragg is slightly less appreciative, of course, dispatching Thedas with a decapi-tantrum before pulling the ol' “impale a guy on your own arm” trick on Nolan. It's only when he's literally crushing Mark's eyes—after the young hero previously caught his attention by revealing he killed Conquest—that we get any sense that the Imperial Regent can feel restraint at this point. This is a frustrating episode of television, because it highlights Invincible's better and worse natures nearly simultaneously. There is a lot of filler here, as too many characters with not enough characterization slam into each other for long stretches of running time. That said, those moments when the show clearly gives a shit, instead of just killing time, are genuinely impressive. The more I've thought about season four of Invincible, the more convinced I am that its hour-long runtimes do it no favors. Cut the rote, fighting game moments out, and this could have legitimately been a great 30 minutes of TV.
