How soccer won over America and became part of U.S. sports culture
Source: {'href': 'https://www.usatoday.com', 'title': 'USA Today'}    Published: 2026-06-14 10:01:00

On a sultry evening, we had gathered at a U.S. military base in South Korea, not far from the Demilitarized Zone. Our makeshift viewing room was soulless, with plastic chairs loosely arranged around a TV on a stand wheeled in especially for the occasion. Surprisingly, the United States looked far more dangerous — a team that, just four years earlier, had left the World Cup finals in France with the worst record of all 32 nations. And while the Americans continued to fight valiantly, Germany ultimately hung on for a 1-0 win. The United States had gone out on its shield and deserved huge congratulations. Instead, all I got was a chorus of “woulda, coulda, shoulda.” Not just disappointment but disdain. Clearly, Americans didn't do gallant failure, instead demanding domination over mere participation every time. But in soccer, the U.S. was an upstart, not an icon, and that's when I realized why the sport was such a hard sell in America. Twenty-four years on, and times have changed. Soccer has now replaced baseball as the third most popular sport in America, behind football and basketball, according to data analytics firm Ampere Analysis. Eleven U.S. cities are hosting games with the final set for MetLife Stadium in New Jersey on July 19. Including this year's tournament, the U.S. has hosted or co-hosted four World Cups, two apiece for the men and women. And the 2031 Women's World Cup will make it five. And the immersion shows no sign of stopping. As an expat Brit, that's a development I never thought I'd see because America's rejection of soccer once seemed absolute. Conversely, in the U.K., the game is part of our DNA, played and watched by toddlers, geriatrics and everyone in between. From playground kickabouts with coats as goalposts to Sunday morning scrimmages on muddy park pitches to the velvety green turf of futuristic stadiums, soccer is close to a religion. A teenage trialist at Chelsea and Crystal Palace, my bubble burst almost immediately, and I ended up playing on various no-name teams for little more than beer money… I didn't even drink. Still, my one goal in an FA Cup preliminary round, and another in a pro-celebrity game (past the then-England goalkeeper, no less), remain absurd sources of personal pride. I coached soccer, too, helping shape a new generation of dreamers as a physical education teacher in London before the journalism bug bit. Switching professions ultimately allowed me to live the dream from the sidelines, rubbing shoulders with soccer's elite at World Cups, European Championships, the Olympics and major club competitions far and wide. That dazzling spectacular – headlined by aging foreign superstars like Pelé, Franz Beckenbauer, Johan Cruyff and George Best – didn't last. The game survived – played indoors professionally, played at youth level, played and watched in expat communities – but it had neither the popularity nor the credibility to be anything but background noise to American sports fans. Soccer remained in the doldrums for more than a decade. So, in somewhat of a quid pro quo, FIFA awarded the United States hosting rights to the 1994 World Cup Finals on the back of America's promise to create a new top-tier domestic league. Two years after that World Cup, Major League Soccer was launched. Filling the void from scratch was tough, as current MLS commissioner Don Garber recalled. "When MLS was originally born out of the 1994 World Cup," he said, "people didn't have a professional league to support… They had no stadiums that were soccer-specific. Kids were playing, but not with the organization that it has today." Underfunded, lightly regarded and with insufficient star power to attract mainstream attention, the league struggled to find its footing. MLS lost an estimated $250 million in its first six seasons. And with modest attendances and limited TV coverage, it teetered on the brink of following the NASL into oblivion. Among them was Landon Donovan, the U.S. Men's National Team's joint all-time record goal scorer and one of the players I got to know during the 2002 World Cup run. "Prior iterations of the league were unstable and they went away. "And so the first, second, third, fourth, and fifth goal were to create a league that is stable, that is here for good… The reality is they needed to create a league in our country that was sustainable, that would be around for a long time." And bearing in mind America's love of winners, that domination was a timely boost as soccer's evolution continued. That early flourish on the international stage wasn't matched at home, where successive women's leagues struggled for lasting success. However, today, the National Women's Soccer League is thriving, with record attendance, rising viewership, a landmark media rights deal and greater visibility than ever before. For Morgan, the difference from her playing days is night and day. She remembered her family could not watch her competing for Lyon, France, in the Women's Champions League final. "Now I have the opportunity to watch Women's Champions League every single week... I just think the appetite is there." Players like Morgan and her illustrious predecessors on the U.S. Women's National Team — such as Mia Hamm, Brandi Chastain, Michelle Akers, and Kristine Lilly — helped give American women's soccer an identity. They made the casual audience look twice. Basketball had Kobe Bryant, Shaquille O'Neal and Tim Duncan. The face of American soccer was Donovan. However, while highly talented, the LA Galaxy's captain was not quite the marquee name MLS needed to turn heads. By 2007, the former England captain was a global icon and serial winner. He'd claimed multiple trophies at the highest level with England's Manchester United and the ultimate glamour club, Real Madrid of Spain. U.S. Men's National Team defender Tim Ream was a college kid at the time, playing at Saint Louis University in Missouri, and he remembers the sense of excitement that Beckham's arrival created. "When he came over, it was crazy," Ream said. Now all of a sudden we have a legitimate superstar playing in MLS and people want to watch." The so-called "Beckham effect" also had a global impact. Donovan was his teammate at the Galaxy. Beckham stayed for six seasons and brought Hollywood glamour with him when A-list friends like Tom Cruise and Will Smith showed up for games. He also added some glitter to the Galaxy's trophy cabinet, helping them win two MLS Cups. Financially, the man nicknamed "Golden Balls" was a marketing juggernaut who also changed the way MLS does business. Part of Beckham's playing deal included a future opportunity to buy an MLS expansion team, which he exercised when he became co-owner of Inter Miami CF. And, as Donovan confirmed, the impact of the Argentine World Cup winner has been similar to Beckham's. "David was the perfect owner to bring a guy like Lionel Messi because he understood what it was like and what it would take to get someone like Messi and what that would look like," Donovan said. Acquiring Messi was a big statement for MLS, and Garber hopes to make many more as the league continues to enhance its status. "David brought us through the growth years. Each one has had a unique impact," Garber said. Not quite sure, but I can assure you that if there's a player who wants to come here who will be the next Leo Messi, we'll figure out the way to bring him into Major League Soccer." From 10 teams in 1996, the MLS has since expanded to 30 franchises. Most play in soccer-specific stadiums and average about 23,000 spectators per game. Of course, challenges remain, including an overreliance on foreign players and a youth development system in need of work. As for winning a men's World Cup any time soon? Well, only eight national teams have ever done so, and FIFA has 211 member nations – so do the math. Still, even without that ultimate prize, the United States is now a soccer-friendly nation, with the stats to prove it. I wonder what my soldier friends from South Korea are thinking now.

A lot has changed in Mexican women's football. The Mexican Football Federation (FMF) has poured heavily into its national team since missing World Cup qualification in 2022, bringing in Spaniard Pedro López as the team's head coach and restructuring its program with 2027 in mind. Momentum for the national team until then relies heavily on qualifying for next summer's World Cup in Brazil, with Concacaf qualifiers beginning in late November. Mexico enjoyed a historic win over Australia in Newcastle, New South Wales, topping the 2023 World Cup quarterfinalist 1-0. Though Mexico's success was short-lived — Australia responded with a 3-1 win three days later — it learned valuable lessons. “We could take a lot of positives from that,” Sánchez says. “Facing teams that have been successful in tournaments like the World Cup or the Olympics is a big challenge for us, as a team that's trying to grow and trying to make the next World Cup.” Guillermo Zamarripa, co-founder of the global sports agency TMJ, has had a front-row seat to the women's soccer explosion in Mexico. “The Mexican national team has gone through a very aggressive transformation,” Zamarripa told The Athletic on a video call last week. He attributes that growth to the success of Liga MX, noting that most of the senior national team players are signed with clubs in Mexico; a small fraction play in the NWSL. “There's no doubt that it's contributing, and it's giving Pedro López what he needs on the day-to-day,” Zamarripa says. Last summer, Zamarripa helped orchestrate forward Jacquie Ovalle's transfer from Tigres to the NWSL's Orlando Pride for a world-record fee of $1.5 million. This, he says, illustrates the potential that Liga MX can offer to its players. (Just this week, the San Diego Wave signed Spanish goalkeeper Sandra Paños from Club América.) Ovalle, nicknamed “La Maga,” or magician, for her stunning footwork and goal-scoring pedigree, is also a beloved member of the Mexican national team. She was not called up to the latest window in Australia, however, and has been recovering from a thigh injury. She was listed as questionable on Orlando's player availability report ahead of its last game against Bay FC, featuring for limited minutes at the end of the 3-1 win for the first time since April. For former players, like 36-year-old Janelly Farías, seeing the sport's evolution in Mexico feels a little dumbfounding. “The recurring theme throughout my life was, ‘Well, you can't play because you're a girl,'” Farías tells The Athletic. That persisted even when she was 13 and the national team came calling. “My dad didn't let me go because I was a girl. By then, Farías remembers telling her father, “I'm going to do this.” She wasn't asking for permission, and her professional career began. She joined Liga MX in its early years, when it was filled with growing pains. Even while she represented Mexico at the national team level, she couldn't sign with a Mexican club until 2019. … When I started playing for the Mexican national team, it was still kind of surreal,” Farías says. That's about nine, 10 years ago, and that's crazy to think because I had been playing at the highest level already for so many years.” Farías juggled multiple jobs until the federation said she needed to play full-time. The pay was still low, so she lived off her savings. After tearing her Achilles tendon while in Europe, she took a break from the sport, joining a reality TV show, “Exatlón Estados Unidos,” for the pay. Those early paychecks while playing in Liga MX were still not enough, she says. She remembers making 30,000 pesos a month, roughly $1,500 monthly. “I was very insistent on that, and of course, as soccer began growing and more people started watching, eventually teams had no choice but to pay top players what they wanted.” By her last contract, Farías estimates she was earning about 200,000 pesos a month ($10,000 monthly). “I never imagined I would be making that as a professional female soccer player, but that's still only, I'd say, the top 5 percent of female soccer players in Mexico,” Farías says. Farías is now an analyst and advocate in the women's soccer space. That's especially important for Mexican women's soccer as it finds itself at an inflection point. Though Liga MX has made significant strides, parity between its teams remains wide, Zamarripa says. “I wish I were a few years younger and getting to experience everything and all the evolution that women's soccer has had within these last few years,” Sánchez says, “but I'm happy to be a part of what it is right now. Speaking from Mexico City, Sánchez says there's a noticeable buzz in the air from the men's World Cup starting this week. Sánchez hopes the women's team can carry this World Cup momentum into its own qualifiers. A successful qualification is paramount to sustain the sports' momentum, Farías says. If the Mexican national team were to not qualify, it would be an absolute failure,” Farías says. “But I really do think we have the right people in place to qualify. “The next step would be, hopefully, hosting a World Cup (in 2031). I don't even have the words to explain what that would mean for Mexico as a country.” Farías takes a moment, tries to explain, but can't. “As a former Mexican national team player, as a fan now, and now on the other side of things as an analyst,” she says, “being able to call a game at the most iconic stadium, with the women's Mexican national team playing there — wow. That would be more than a dream come true, and it would be a reflection of just how far women, in general, have come in Mexico.”

Far from being a simple accessory, the gear is part of the CLIMACOOL system, a technology developed to help players lower their body temperature and speed up physical recovery. The equipment includes a cooling vest, an insulated jacket and, in some cases, specialized boot covers that help keep the body cooler before, during and after training sessions. According to Spain's fitness coach, Carlos Cruz, the goal is to delay the onset of fatigue and improve player recovery in extreme heat conditions. The system can lower core body temperature and significantly reduce skin temperature, an important advantage in a tournament where weather conditions have become a major factor.
