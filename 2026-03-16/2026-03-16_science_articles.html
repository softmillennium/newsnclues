Most investigations of the distributions of anthropogenic organic chemicals (xenobiotics) have been regionally focused, which limits our understanding of the extent of chemical pollution in the world's largest biome. To address this gap, we mapped the presence of xenobiotics across marine ecosystems. Here we present a meta-analysis of 21 public non-targeted tandem mass spectrometry datasets, which are not restricted to a predefined set of compounds but rather capture thousands of chemicals. These datasets comprise 2,315 seawater samples, spanning coastal to open ocean environments across three ocean basins. Our analysis revealed that common pollutants such as pesticides and pharmaceuticals were predominantly detected in estuaries and coastal areas but declined with distance from shore, whereas industrial chemicals and additives, including polyalkylene glycols, phthalates and organophosphates, were widely distributed across marine ecosystems. A total of 248 annotated xenobiotic features contributed a median of 2% to the total detected peak area per sample. We observed highest median levels of xenobiotic contribution in coastal datasets (up to 20%) and lowest levels (0.5%) in open ocean datasets, which indicates that anthropogenic organic substances contribute substantially to the dissolved organic matter pool in the surface ocean. Experts had no idea that a large Roman-era water channel was hiding beneath the historic streets of Trabzon, Türkiye. But when construction crews working on a restoration project in the city center came upon a nearly 1,300-foot-long tunnel made of interlocking stones, archeologists were quick to take notice. The tunnel was uncovered about six feet below ground by construction crews working on a modernization project in Trabzon's Comlekci neighborhood, a short distance uphill from the Roman-era port. The structure measures roughly 4 by 4 feet and features small walkways on both sides. The vaulted tunnel features an arched roof, which was typical of Roman-era design, but highly unusual for any area of Roman-era Anatolia. The subterranean channel is principally made from finely-cut stone, reinforced by rubble stone and a layer of plaster meant to serve as waterproofing, according to a report from the Hurriyet Daily News. “We did not previously consider that area to have been an intensely inhabited zone during the Roman or Hellenistic periods.” It may well have been commissioned during the rule of the emperors Trajan or Hadrian, and it's possible that it was part of a larger aqueduct system connecting Trabzon to inland cities where Roman soldiers were garrisoned. The team of researchers from Trabzon Museum Directorate and Karadeniz Technical University hopes to continue exploring, according to Türkiye Today, as they believe that there is a possibility of locating additional connections to other tunnels or key above-ground sites. Though some sections have already been destroyed by construction activity above, as they search the length of the tunnel, experts could uncover more clues about its history and use. Just 24 minutes of specially designed music could significantly reduce anxiety

A new randomized clinical trial suggests that listening to specially designed music for just 24 minutes may significantly lower anxiety. The music was paired with auditory beat stimulation (ABS), a technique that uses rhythmic sound patterns to influence brain activity. The clinical trial was conducted by psychology researchers Danielle K. Mullen and Frank A. Russo at Toronto Metropolitan University (TMU) in partnership with LUCID, a digital therapeutics company that emerged from TMU's Zone Learning ecosystem. Common treatments include medications and cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), but these options can come with challenges such as side effects, long wait times, cost, and the time required for ongoing treatment. Because of these barriers, researchers have been exploring music based digital therapeutics as a low cost and widely accessible way to help people manage anxiety symptoms. These tools aim to deliver quick relief through guided listening experiences that can be used almost anywhere. The study included 144 adults who had moderate trait anxiety and were already taking medication to help manage their symptoms. Participants were randomly assigned to one of four listening conditions: "What we're seeing is a dose-response pattern where about 24 minutes of music with ABS seems to be the sweet spot," said Russo, Professor of Psychology at TMU and Chief Science Officer, LUCID. "Investigating the dose-response relationship between music and anxiety reduction: A randomized clinical trial" was published in PLOS Mental Health on January 21, 2026. Scientists discover what really happens during sourdough fermentation

Many people value it for its natural ingredients, nutritional benefits, and distinctive flavor. What exactly occurs during this fermentation, and how do wheat fibers influence the final quality of bread? These questions guided the doctoral research of Víctor González Alonso at the Vrije Universiteit Brussel (VUB), who investigated how specific wheat dietary fibers called arabinoxylans behave during sourdough fermentation. Water-extractable (WE-AX) typically have either beneficial or neutral effects on dough, while water-unextractable (WU-AX) can negatively influence bread quality. Until recently, scientists had limited understanding of how the microorganisms in sourdough interact with these fibers. To explore this question, González Alonso examined fermentation in several flour types, including some enriched with additional AX. He tracked changes in the microbial populations using advanced DNA analysis and metabolite profiling to better understand how fermentation unfolds. "We observed that sourdoughs develop into stable microbial ecosystems, with lactic acid bacteria and yeasts in a complex balance," he says. "A higher fiber content barely altered this process, although we clearly demonstrated that sourdough fermentation converts part of the WE-AX into WU-AX." These enzymes become active as the dough grows more acidic during fermentation. Lactococcus lactis was associated with buttery aromas, while Limosilactobacillus fermentum produced sugar alcohols that can add a mild sweetness. In a pilot scale baking trial, bread was made using wheat flour enriched with high levels of AX. The resulting sourdough loaves were not only higher in nutritional value but also showed a broader range of flavors. "Sourdough remains a fascinating interplay of biology and craftsmanship," González Alonso concludes. "And our research shows that sourdough fermentation influences wheat fibers to a greater extent than previously thought." Here we present EBClite, a battery-free smart mask for multiday EBC biomarker monitoring, using lactate as a model analyte. The platform integrates a regenerable antidrying porous hydrogel that achieves sustained and reactivatable breath condensation over several days, extending operational lifetime while reducing material cost. EBClite further incorporates a long-term stable electrochemical lactate sensor and an ultrathin quasi-two-dimensional perovskite solar cell with a power-to-weight ratio of 10 W g−1 and a power conversion efficiency exceeding 30% under ambient indoor illumination, enabling autonomous operation across diverse lighting conditions. (2026)Cite this article Agricultural intensification increases crop production but often causes declines in soil biodiversity and functioning, thus threatening long-term environmental sustainability. To buffer these impacts, environmental policies aim to increase the amount of organic farming; however, this potentially compromises yield and nitrogen and phosphorus availability. Given such complexities, we evaluated the overall impacts of shifting agricultural management by comparing the effects of conventional versus organic farming on soil biodiversity (richness of 6 faunal and microbial groups), functioning (21 indicators) and crop yield on 179 global croplands across a wide range of ecological contexts, including contrasting levels of soil degradation. High crop yields did not necessarily trade off against soil biodiversity, ecosystem multifunctionality or nitrogen availability, with often positive correlations found between them. Yield, biodiversity and functioning variables showed a broad range of values within and between conventional and organic sites. Our findings provide guidelines for sustainable agriculture, showing how prioritizing the transition to organic farming in moderately to highly degraded soils would maximize its benefits while minimizing yield loss. Here, we characterize 274,682 enhancers and 25,975 promoters across 24 tissues from an adult female sheep using ChIP-seq, ATAC-seq, CAGE-seq, RRBS, WGBS, and RNA-seq. We identify seven neural development-related genes with over 10 enhancers in brain tissues, highlighting the role of tissue-specific regulation. Cis-regulatory enhancer-promoter combinations provide insights into tissue-specific enhancers, such as the cerebellum-specific enhancer (chr15: 57390520-57390685) regulating BDNF, which is expressed in both the cerebellum and cerebral cortex. Comparative analysis of enhancer-promoter combinations in human, mouse, pig, cattle, and sheep reveals ruminant-specific pathways, including pentose catabolism and long-chain fatty acid import regulation. A milk fat yield quantitative trait locus (QTL) identified within an enhancer interacts with the fat metabolism-related gene COMMD1, and a birth weight-associated QTL detected within a cerebellum-specific enhancer regulates XKR4. This study provides a robust framework for exploring cis-regulatory mechanisms and tissue-specific regulation, advancing the functional annotation of the sheep reference genome. The annotation of regulatory elements in the genome and assessment of their function in various tissues are essential for understanding gene regulation and interpreting the potential impact of genomic variants on phenotype. The encyclopedia of DNA elements (ENCODE) project annotated functional sequences on a genome-wide scale in human cells and tissue types1, and subsequently in model organisms2,3. These studies identified similarities and differences in regulatory elements across species. Functional annotation involves identifying and annotating the functional sequences in a genome, which include both expressed sequences (coding and non-coding RNAs) and regulatory sequences. Early research primarily focused on identifying expressed sequences using technologies like ESTs and cDNA libraries4, laying the groundwork for understanding gene expression profiles. The advent of RNA-seq then provided a more comprehensive view of the transcriptome, quantifying gene expression across tissues and developmental stages5,6, thereby enhancing the functional annotation of genomes. In contrast, identifying regulatory sequences on a genome-wide scale has been more limited, largely concentrated on humans and a few model organisms through projects like ENCODE1. These initiatives have created extensive datasets that illuminate the roles of regulatory elements, such as enhancers, promoters, and insulators, in shaping gene expression and driving complex phenotypes7. Both expressed and regulatory sequence annotation are paving the way for a more complete understanding of genome function. Recently, significant progress has been made in the study of regulatory sequences in animal genomes, particularly in livestock species such as cattle8, pig9, and chicken10 through the project Functional Annotation of Animal Genomes (FAANG). In cattle, research identified associations between weak enhancers, active transcription start sites (TSS), and economic traits during rumen development8. In pigs, multi-omics data were used to annotate chromatin states across 14 tissues, revealing the importance of active promoters and enhancers in complex traits and domestication, with distinct regulatory patterns between Asian and European pigs9. Similarly, researchers annotated 1.57 million regulatory elements in chicken, predicting some enhancer-gene combinations and super-enhancers, providing valuable resources for poultry genetics and genomics research10. These projects not only deepen our understanding of genetic regulation but also fuel comparative and evolutionary studies, as well as enabling the investigation of the genetic control of phenotypes. As an important livestock species, the study of regulatory elements in sheep is equally valuable. The ovine FAANG project offers a unique opportunity to characterize transcriptional regulatory elements in sheep, advancing our understanding of the biological mechanisms underlying complex traits in this species. Recently, we initiated the characterization of regulatory elements in three tissues (liver, spleen, and cerebellum) using chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing (ChIP-seq) data to define chromatin states, including active and poised enhancers and promoters7. Other studies have identified regulatory elements in sheep macrophages, cartilage, cecum, pituitary gland, hypothalamus, liver, longissimus dorsi, rumen, spleen, and tail fat tissues11,12. However, current annotations remain limited to a few tissues, especially in the sheep genome. A comprehensive, systematic study identifying regulatory elements across multiple tissues is urgently needed, particularly focusing on enhancers, promoters, and their interactions. Emerging research increasingly highlights the regulatory impact of enhancer-promoter interactions on gene expression, revealing their dynamic nature and marked tissue- and cell-type specificity13. A common approach for identifying enhancer regulations involves correlating chromatin signals at enhancers with gene transcriptional activity. This correlation-based approach has proven an effective strategy for mapping regulatory interactions in research. For instance, Lin et al. identified putative enhancer-gene interactions within the same topologically associated domain based on significant positive correlations between H3K27ac signals at enhancers and gene expression14. Furthermore, transcription signatures, such as bidirectional and unidirectional transcription captured by cap analysis gene expression and deep sequencing (CAGE-seq), have become defining marks of active enhancers and promoters15. The FANTOM5 project utilized CAGE expression profiling to catalog enhancers across diverse human tissues and cell types, providing expression-based support for enhancer-promoter pairing that collectively modulate target gene expression16. Tissue-specific enhancer activity provides compelling examples of how these interactions underpin genomic regulation. Enhancer regions in the human prefrontal cortex selectively activate FOXP217, a gene crucial for neural development and vocal communication. Similarly, in cattle, muscle-specific enhancers marked by H3K27ac target Myod, regulating muscle growth in skeletal muscle cells19. These reports highlight the pivotal role of enhancer-promoter or enhancer-gene interactions in shaping tissue-specific gene expression, offering valuable insights into the regulatory mechanisms underlying the complexity of genomic regulation. Recently, genomic research has advanced dramatically through the construction of hundreds, or even thousands, of pan-genomes and achieving telomere-to-telomere (T2T) assemblies34,35. Despite these significant strides in genome assembly, the functional annotation of assembled genomes has not kept pace, leaving a critical gap in our applied understanding of genome functionality. Regulatory elements, such as enhancers and promoters, play pivotal roles in orchestrating complex biological processes, and their systematic identification and characterization are fundamental to uncovering the intricate mechanisms underlying gene regulation and phenotypic diversity. Bridging this gap is essential for maximizing the utility of high-quality genome assemblies and advancing insights into functional genomics. The ENCODE and FAANG projects are two global initiatives aimed at addressing the critical gap in functional genome annotation for humans and animals1,36, respectively. FAANG, in particular, seeks to expand the functional annotation of farmed animal genomes, covering a wide range of species, including cattle8, sheep7, pigs9, and chickens10. As contributors to the NCBI submission of three successive updates to the sheep reference genome and as leading participants in the Ovine FAANG project7,20, we present herein the comprehensive endeavor to systematically identify and characterize regulatory sequences within the sheep genome using an integration strategy using multi-omics data assays from 24 tissues in this study. This work is anticipated to provide substantial advancements for the sheep research community, enhancing our understanding of functional genomics and facilitating discoveries in sheep biology. Recent advancements in histone modification, chromatin accessibility, and chromatin structure analysis have enabled comprehensive, genome-wide identification of enhancers and promoters10,26,37. Traditional strategies for identifying enhancers and promoters often rely on a cell/tissue-peak approach, where ChIP-seq signals (e.g., H3K4me3, H3K4me1, H3K27ac, and H3K27me3) are used to infer chromatin states in individual cell lines or tissues, and the active, poised, and repressed states are aggregated to predict potential enhancer or promoter regions within specific cells or tissues. While effective, this approach faces challenges and does not entirely consider potential tissue differences. Regulatory elements inferred from ChIP-seq peaks alone can both overestimate enhancer or promoter regions (false positives) and/or fail to identify peaks due to signal complexity resulting definitively correspond to functional elements39,40. Moreover, variations in peak boundaries across tissues lead to positional inconsistencies, complicating the alignment of enhancers and promoters on the reference genome39. Additionally, data from cap analysis gene expression and deep sequencing (CAGE-seq), and CCCTC-binding factor (CTCF) binding allow for delineation of these regulatory elements as well10,41. In this study, we propose an integration strategy to accurately identify genomic promoters and enhancers by integrating multi-omics assays, including ChIP-seq, CAGE-seq, ATAC-seq, and RNA-seq. This approach transcends the traditional tissue-peak signal-based identification of potential enhancers and promoters from active, poised, and repressed chromatin states, advancing to the precise identification of enhancers and promoters along reference genome coordinates using the independent multi-omics assays. Our strategy addresses these challenges through a three-step integrative process. (1) Definition of candidate enhancer or promoter regions: Candidate regions are identified based on ChIP-seq peaks for H3K4me1, H3K4me3, and H3K27ac. (2) Identification of confident potential enhancer or promoter: Candidate peaks are intersected with additional CAGE-seq and ATAC-seq, retaining the peaks which overlap with at least one dataset and are present in at least two tissues. To avoid excluding strong individual signals, peaks with H3K4me1 or H3K4me3 signals with p values <0.0001 are also included. Both intersected and individually strong peaks are merged as confident potential enhancers or promoters. Promoter regions are further refined by integrating RNA-seq and CAGE-seq data to identify all potential transcription start sites (TSS), resulting in high-confidence promoters for each tissue. (3) Consistent genomic coordinates for confident potential enhancers or promoters: Potential enhancer and promoter regions are unified by consolidating overlapping regions across multiple tissues. The median start and end positions of overlapping peaks are calculated to define consistent genomic coordinates on the reference genome. This strategy effectively integrates independent datasets, ensuring precise and consistent annotation of enhancers and promoters across all tissues. To further demonstrate the robustness and reliability of our enhancer and promoter annotations, we generated and analyzed replicate ChIP-seq datasets for 16 representative tissues from the same individual sheep. These replicates, sequenced at higher depth, provided an independent evaluation of the reproducibility of our original data and the stability of our integrative analytical framework. Consistent with the original findings, the replicate datasets exhibited high sequencing quality and strong concordance across all evaluated metrics. Specifically, the mapping rates exceeded 98% for most tissues, and the proportion of duplicate reads was generally below 20%, indicating high library complexity and consistent experimental performance across tissues (Supplementary Data 21). Even in tissues where duplicate rates were higher (e.g., up to ~50%), the number of uniquely mapped deduplicated reads remained above 20 million, which is widely accepted as sufficient for reliable peak calling and downstream regulatory analyses. Then, we assessed the reproducibility of regulatory element identification between the original and replicate datasets. Across the 16 tissues, enhancer regions derived from H3K27ac and H3K4me1 ChIP-seq showed a mean overlap rate of 62% with their corresponding replicates, while promoter regions defined by H3K27ac and H3K4me3 exhibited a higher concordance of approximately 82%, with some tissues exceeding 90% (Supplementary Table 5). These results clearly indicate that the regulatory elements identified in the original dataset are highly reproducible and biologically meaningful. Notably, the cerebellum and spinal cord tissues, which displayed particularly high reproducibility (enhancer overlap >80% and promoter overlap >90%), also demonstrated strong tissue-specific regulatory signatures consistent with neuronal gene expression patterns. To confirm that this reproducibility reflects biological consistency rather than technical redundancy, we performed comparative analyses using both Nextflow-based ChIP-seq pipelines and independent evaluations with Picard's MarkDuplicates module. Both analyses produced nearly identical mapping and duplication profiles, demonstrating that the reproducibility of our results is not pipeline-dependent. Moreover, the overlap between enhancers and promoters identified from independent omics datasets (CAGE-seq, ATAC-seq, and RNA-seq) further corroborated the reliability of our integrated enhancer-promoter framework. Candidate enhancers were retained only when supported by at least two independent datasets and detected in two or more tissues, minimizing the influence of variability from any single experiment. Together, these replicate-based validations provide compelling evidence that our enhancer and promoter annotations are both technically sound and biologically reliable. The consistent overlap between original and replicate datasets across multiple histone marks and tissues demonstrates that our multi-omics integration strategy effectively mitigates experimental variability and ensures reproducible detection of regulatory elements. This robustness strengthens the overall confidence in our dataset and supports its broad utility as a foundational resource for future studies on ruminant functional genomics, gene regulation, and evolutionary biology. We combined experimental data on histone modifications (ChIP-seq), chromatin accessibility (ATAC-seq), and transcript expression and abundance (CAGE-seq and RNA-seq) to enable the de novo definition of transcriptional regulatory elements in the sheep genome. These regulatory elements, including promoters and enhancers, were annotated across 24 tissues from a single Rambouillet ewe, the source of DNA for the current reference genome assembly20. The use of multiple tissues from a single animal, rather than a few tissues from several individuals, was guided by four key considerations: (1) obtaining a broader understanding of tissue-specific differences was prioritized over achieving higher confidence in annotations within a limited number of tissues; (2) comparisons between tissues with similar expression profiles increased confidence in promoter, enhancer, and enhancer-promoter combination annotations; (3) using tissues from the reference animal ensures broad adaptability for researchers and facilitates timely updates, making the findings more universally applicable and valuable as a reference for other studies; and (4) biological replication within this breed is likely to be breed-specific, and replication efforts are more appropriately addressed by ongoing projects focused on specific breeds rather than as part of this annotation initiative. Building upon the accurate identification of enhancers and promoters, cis-regulatory enhancer-promoter combinations were further defined by correlating enhancers located within a ±1 Mbp range of promoters with gene expression (TPM), retaining combinations with a correlation coefficient (r) ≥0.8. Through this approach, genes regulated by enhancers were identified, uncovering hidden regulatory mechanisms that influence gene expression. Functional enrichment analysis of tissue-specific genes associated with enhancers demonstrated superior results compared to using promoter-associated gene sets alone, providing a more accurate reflection of tissue-specific characteristics. This not only validated the reliability of our enhancer-promoter combination identification but also highlighted the regulatory role of enhancers in gene expression, which is stronger and more tissue-specific compared to promoters. Consequently, the number and tissue-specificity of enhancers, as well as their associated genes, exhibited greater variation across tissues compared to promoters. This discovery aligns with existing biological insights and previous findings, which demonstrate that enhancers exhibit higher tissue specificity than promoters13,42. While promoters often function across multiple tissues to initiate gene transcription, enhancers are more likely to be active in specific tissues. However, the enhancer-promoter combination approach revealed that the enhancer chr15: 57390520-57390685 is a cerebellum-specific enhancer, providing critical insights into its regulation. Additionally, a total of 554 enhancer-promoter combinations involving 68 genes, including BDNF, were identified as having tissue-specific enhancers. This study represents the genome-wide identification of tissue-specific enhancer-promoter combinations across multiple tissues in the sheep genome. It significantly enhances the annotation of the sheep reference genome and provides crucial regulatory elements for understanding gene regulation in sheep. These findings offer valuable contributions to the broader understanding of tissue-specific gene regulation and the functional genomics of this economically significant species. While gene expression signatures in specific tissue types tend to be conserved across mammals, regulatory elements exhibit greater species specificity. This aligns with previous reports that enhancer sequences change rapidly across mammalian evolution, despite maintaining similar gene expression profiles in comparable tissues26. Including sheep regulatory elements in this comparative analysis enhances our understanding of regulatory dynamics across mammalian species, contributing to a broader perspective on gene regulation. Additionally, a direct comparison between sheep and cattle regulatory annotations allowed us to identify potential ruminant-specific features shared by these two ruminant species. We identified 69 gene-associated enhancer-promoter combinations that were present in both sheep and cattle but absent in the non-ruminants (pig, mouse, and human), suggesting that these combinations may have a role in ruminant-specific biology. These pathways involved genes such as ELMO1, CTNNA3, AKT2, SGPL1, THTPA, RAPGEF6, and HSPA4. Notably, these pathways are primarily associated with the microbial digestive environment of the rumen, a unique fermentation chamber found only in ruminants. The dynamic balance of rumen microorganisms, such as bacteria, is crucial for proper rumen function, underscoring the importance of these pathways in ruminant-specific digestive physiology. This functional annotation provides a critical framework for evaluating genomic variations and interpreting their potential impact on biologically important complex traits in sheep. Variations in enhancer and promoter sequences have been demonstrated to influence phenotypes and regulate conserved processes, including mammalian development26. Although the number of QTLs reported in sheep is substantially lower compared to cattle, this study identified 38 QTLs colocalized in promoter regions of 17 genes and 215 QTLs overlapping with 214 enhancers. Notably, one QTL with a strong effect on milk fat yield, originally identified through a genome-wide association study (GWAS) of milk production traits27, was pinpointed at chromosome 3 (chr3: 46457793). This genomic position lies within a specific enhancer region (chr3: 46457598-46458034) that interacts with COMMD1. COMMD1 is expressed in at least 21 tissues and is a compelling candidate gene given its established role in copper metabolism, which has been linked to lipid metabolism and milk fat composition in ruminants27,28. These results suggest a plausible regulatory link between this enhancer, COMMD1 expression, and milk fat yield, although additional functional validation (eQTL analyses or experimental perturbation) will be required to establish causality. More broadly, our findings demonstrate how the regulatory element annotations of the sheep reference genome provide a valuable context for understanding enhancer and promoter variations across individual sheep and breeds selected for diverse production traits. As more QTLs are discovered with the advancement of trait-related research, this regulatory element annotation will serve as a powerful tool for linking SNPs to critical traits, enabling a more precise interpretation of the biological significance of QTLs. This comprehensive dataset will facilitate deeper insights into the genetic mechanisms underlying economically important traits in sheep. This study represents a significant step forward in the systematic identification and annotation of enhancers and promoters in the sheep genome, providing a unified framework for understanding the transcriptional regulatory landscape across multiple tissues. By transitioning from traditional cell/tissue-peak approaches to the incorporation of a multi-omics faceted strategy, we integrated multi-omics data to achieve precise mapping of enhancers and promoters to unified genomic coordinates. This strategy not only enhances the functional annotation of the sheep reference genome but also provides a foundation for exploring the regulatory mechanisms underlying complex traits and tissue-specific gene expression. While rigorous, this approach may have been overly stringent, potentially excluding some true enhancers or promoters. Future improvements might include integrating more specific and high-resolution experimental data, such as Hi-C across multiple tissues, for enhancer and promoter identification, as well as leveraging artificial intelligence models trained on diverse and comprehensive datasets. These advancements hold the potential to enhance the accuracy of genome-wide predictions of regulatory elements, ultimately providing a more complete and nuanced understanding of gene regulation in sheep and other species.
