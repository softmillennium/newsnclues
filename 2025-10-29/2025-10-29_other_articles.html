The Winchesters' Hunt Begins Again in an Engaging New Supernatural Series
Source: {'href': 'https://www.cbr.com', 'title': 'Comic Book Resources'}    Published: 2025-10-29 17:20:22

The following contains spoilers for Supernatural #1, on sale now from Dynamite Entertainment For instance, Marvel has had great success in the past decade of its Star Wars licensed comic books by picking a specific time period, and then having all of its ongoing comics built around that period. Currently, the Star Wars line is based in adventures following Return of the Jedi, but that lesson is important even for a series that ISN'T part of a whole line of books, and since Greg Pak is VERY familiar with this concept from his excellent work on the aforementioned Star Wars line of books at Marvel, it is unsurprising that he has learned that lesson well for this new Supernatural series, which isn't part of a line of books, of course, but still gets that it is important to pick one era and explore it as best you can. Supernatural #1 is from writer Greg Pak, artist Eder Messias, colorist Thyago Brandao, and letterer Jeff Eckleberry, and it is smartly set between the first and second seasons of the extraordinarily long-running TV series about two brothers who are part of a family of "hunters," people who hunt down demons and other supernatural forces. This was a famous aspect of The X-Files (that's where the term "Monster of the Week" came from), and it has been a regular fixture in these sorts of shows ever since. This issue kicks off with a compelling idea, as a man flat out BURSTS INTO FLAMES while drinking outside of his home. This, naturally, is very important for Dean and Sam Winchester, as Sam Winchester was brought back into the world of "hunting" by his girlfriend (likely soon to be fiancée) Jess being killed by a demon through a horrific process (similar to the one where Dean and Sam's mother was killed), burning alive after being badly wounded. It was unlike, say, Spider-Man, where his very existence means that all sorts of crazy stuff is happening in his life at any given moment, so that you could easily throw in all sorts of different adventures into his past without it affecting anything (a hallmark of the classic series, Untold Tales of Spider-Man, and the current fun miniseries, Amazing Spider-Man: Torn). Here, when the Winchesters' adventures were still "new," there was much more area for Pak to move in with new untold stories, while still giving them some heft, and not feel disposable (you know, sort of like, "Well, if this story was important at all, we'd have heard about it already, right? At the same time, it also allows Pak to dig deeper into the feelings of Sam and Dean, which are obviously still raw in the early days of their partnership. A really helpful tool for licensed comics like this is something that Pak obviously is smart enough to get, which is that you should introduce new villains, since you can't really mess too much with the show's established villains, so a new villain gives you a lot of freedom to do whatever you want with them, and Pak introduces an intriguing new villain in this issue, a billionaire who is interested in going beyond the world of science to also exploit the world of magic for legit, as well. That's a very natural enemy for the Winchesters, and the fact that she is an attractive young woman has room for some interesting entanglements between her and Dean. Messias and Brandao have the inglorious task of having to play against their instincts in this series, as Messias is a big, bold artist whose work crackles with energy, and yet here, since it is a TV show adaptation, a lot of the plot is delivered through "just" talking scenes, and Messias has to make those compelling, as well, and he uses a lot of little tricks to pull it off, with a lot of "business" in the background. Dave Gibbons is iconic for how much extra "business" he had going on in every Watchmen panel, and Messias follows that path in this issue (I don't want to mislead you, he doesn't do nearly as much as Gibbons, just saying that he takes that same basic approach to liven up the talking panels). This was a strong first issue, and it feels nice to have such a strong guiding hand like Pak leading this book. You know he will make it worth your while. Israeli-American Harvard University physicist Avi Loeb recently discussed employing a multi-front approach to finding extraterrestrial life in space, as the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS continued to gain attention. Loeb continued to stress the need for proper interceptors, specially-designed cameras, as well as a significant allocation of funds in the “planetary defense” fund to create a system that will protect Earth. Apart from that, Loeb suggested the tracking of artificial lights and other traditional ways of searching for intelligent life. Conversations surrounding extraterrestrial beings or an intelligent presence came to the forefront after the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS, discovered on July 1, 2025, began showing multiple anomalies. The “anti-tail,” in particular, which Loeb speculated may be thrust exhaust from an intelligent life, trying to slow down the object, sparked alien theories among experts. “We need interceptors, a spacecraft that when we detect with those two observatories, we detect an object that comes from outside the solar system, then we can maneuver a spacecraft so that it will meet it along its path,” he explained. He recalled writing a paper discussing the change in the trajectory of Juno, which U.S. Representative Anna Pauline Luna read. Thus, Loeb concluded that “a potential fleet of interceptors” had the capacity to “come really close and take a close-up photograph.” According to Loeb, photographic proof of the 3I/ATLAS or any other object would automatically debunk all theories besides the truth. Consequently, he believed they needed cameras that could not only take photographs but also collect samples and bring them back to Earth. Loeb argued that once alien technology was verified, confirming that it put Earth at risk, a significant fraction of the military budget should be allocated to create a system that would protect the planet. “If we encounter a clearly technological alien object, then the budget will rise by a factor of 1,000 from the military budget portion going into it.” Loeb also suggested the search for artificial lights, as it would allow experts to get more of an understanding. Dynamite Entertainment's new Supernatural comic series returns to Sam and Dean's formative days as monster-hunters. The first issue dials the franchise back to its essence, delivering the classic Winchester brothers dynamic from before it was burdened by later seasons' metaphysics and mythology. The issue is at its best, though, in its back-and-forth between Sam and Dean, setting a strong foundation for the stories to come. As such, it deliberately strives for a “lost episode” quality. Scientists have found a connection between UFOs and nuclear testing
Source: {'href': 'https://baku.ws', 'title': 'Baku.ws'}    Published: 2025-10-29 09:38:38

Scientists have identified a statistical connection between nuclear tests and the appearance of unidentified flying objects. Researchers analyzed astronomical images from the mid-20th century and compared them with dates of atmospheric nuclear weapon explosions. As reported by BAKU.WS with reference to Phys.org publication, within 24 hours after the tests, the probability of transient objects appearing increased by 45 percent, and each UFO report was accompanied by an 8.5 percent increase in the activity of such objects. As part of the work, researchers studied images of the starry sky taken by the Palomar Observatory from 1949 to 1957. Scientists were looking for so-called transient objects - brief flashes visible in only one image and absent in subsequent ones. Researchers emphasize that the origin of these phenomena remains a mystery. Meanwhile, theories about damaged photographic plates or the presence of atmospheric debris after explosions have been ruled out. Since observations were made before the launch of the first artificial Earth satellite in 1957, scientists consider it unlikely that the objects were of terrestrial origin. The research results were published in the journal Scientific Reports.
