Planetary scientists have found a wide range of molecules that are crucial for life among a lot of other organic substances present in asteroids, meteors, and even interstellar space. Now, scientists report new insights into asteroid Bennu, which was sampled by the NASA mission OSIRIS-REx. The latest research suggests tentative evidence for a substance we have not encountered before in extraterrestrial material. When it comes to molecules fundamental to life, we usually focus on the nucleotide bases: adenine, cytosine, guanine, thymine, and uracil, which are the fundamental units of DNA and RNA. Of those 22, only 14 have been found in stuff from outer space – until now, possibly. They also report tentative detections of tryptophan, a pretty important amino acid. A form of vitamin B3 has previously been detected in the Bennu sample, which gives credence to this new detection. Tryptophan is pretty fragile, so it is not a big surprise that it has not been found before in meteorites. The delicate collection of 121.6 grams (4.3 ounces) of material by OSIRIS-REx might have kept this and other dainty molecules safe. A former soldier who participated in leaflet-dropping operations against North Korea has testified that the Defense Psychological Operation Group, a unit directly under the Ministry of National Defense, secretly sent leaflets to North Korea from October 2023 until just before the emergency martial law declaration on Dec. 3, 2024. While some media reports previously indicated that propaganda leaflets were sent by military authorities as well as civilian groups during the Yoon Suk-yeol administration, this is the first time a soldier involved in the operation has provided testimony. The Defense Psychological Operation Group, or DPOG, is a unit tasked with conducting psychological warfare operations, such as distributing leaflets and broadcasting via loudspeakers, in times of emergency. The whistleblower's unit reportedly conducted operations about once or twice every two months from then until November 2024. From then on, according to the former soldier, the unit released about 100 balloons per operation, each carrying approximately 10 kilograms (22 pounds) of leaflets. “Before the Constitutional Court's decision, we conducted leaflet distribution training in rear bases. He added that he and others conducted realistic training for about a month and then, the following month in October, actually sent balloons carrying leaflets to North Korea. They then calculated wind direction and speed in real-time to determine the most suitable targets for leaflet balloons. “Around 9 to 11 pm, unit members would change out of their military uniforms, which had their names and unit affiliations, into black clothing worn by special operations personnel before being deployed for the operation,” the former soldier said. Rep. Boo Seung-chan of the Democratic Party, who has been tracking the DPOG's leaflet activities, said that the operations were part of Yoon's plan to concoct an excuse for invoking martial law. “The Yoon administration sought to provoke North Korea not only with drones infiltrating Pyongyang but also with propaganda leaflets in order to create justification for declaring martial law,” Boo said.
