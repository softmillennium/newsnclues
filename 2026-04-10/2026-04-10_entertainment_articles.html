It's the simplest description I can muster for “The Audacity,” Jonathan Glatzer's promising but flawed hourlong satire of America's wealth swindlers. Whereas HBO's Shakespearean comedy highlights a cadre of siblings, spouses, and hangers-on trapped in arrested development by a patriarch who refuses to trust any one of them with genuine responsibility, AMC‘s pitch-black comedy features a slew of entrepreneurs, inventors, and hangers-on trapped in Palo Alto's techno-futurism (or -doomerism) bubble that refuses to allow any one of them access to their inherent humanity. Related Stories ‘The Christophers' Review: Ian McKellen's Best Screen Performance Since Gandalf Brings Some Much-Needed Life Back to Soderbergh's Late Style What Should ‘Jury Duty' Present After ‘Company Retreat'? Given Glatzer (who shouldn't be confused with “Zone of Interest” director Jonathan Glazer) spent two seasons as a writer/producer on the Emmy-winning drama, any perceived overlap hardly feels like happenstance. But “The Audacity” isn't on the level of “Succession” (or even “Succession's” successor) in part because it's missing a simple, personal hook. For all its pointed real-world parallels and incisive takedowns of the uber-rich, “Succession” revolves around one critical question: Who among the Roy children will win a kiss from daddy? The lengths they go to get there prove fascinating, hysterical, and sickening (often all at once), but had those kids not been fighting tooth and nail for parental approval, their nasty machinations would've lacked the emotional oomph felt by raising relatable family drama to operatic heights. While that may be a more fair assessment of narcissistic tech bros and their morally (and financially) bankrupt dependents, it makes for a colder, less compelling TV show, while creating the impression that viewers are just watching a bunch of man-children fight over who gets to be the first to burn down the world. First and lowest of the lot is Duncan Park (Billy Magnussen), the curly-haired co-founder of Fah-Fa (a “juggernaut” that made him his first fortune) who's now running HyperGnosis, a data collection business that's about to be acquired by “Cupertino” (which is the show's code word for Apple, presumably out of legal precaution). He only has himself to blame, but that's obviously not an option, so he seeks “affirmation” from his therapist, Joanne (Sarah Goldberg). Known around town as a tech whisperer, Joanne doesn't empathize with her brash clientele so much as she bends over backward to accommodate them. Sure, they might benefit from boundaries for once in their coddled lives, but Joanne needs them to depend on her more than she needs to do no harm — a moral compromise that she'll make again and again over the course of Season 1. Other clients (notably not patients) include Carl Bardolph (Zach Galifianakis), a pioneer from the internet age who's become a modern-day curmudgeon. (His billions come from a platform built to support email spam.) So he slums around Silicon Valley investing in companies he can control and take credit for, growing his wealth by wielding it like a bludgeon. Then there's Anushka Bhattachera-Phister (Meaghan Rath), a HyperGnosis board member who works full-time for Cupertino and whose divided loyalties don't stop there. To be fair, everything she does is behind her husband's back, because Martin (Simon Helberg) gives all of his attention to his pet AI project, Xander — which Anushka derisively refers to as an advanced Tamagotchi. Xander is a promising piece of tech (and a delightful source of dark humor), but Martin should probably be keeping at least one eye on his actual child, Tess (Thailey Roberge). That goes double for Duncan and Lili (Lucy Punch), who've managed to raise a semi-normal daughter in Jamison (Ava Marie Telek), as well as Joanne and Gary (Paul Adelstein), who are thrust into parenthood when her teenage son from a past marriage, Orson (Everett Blunck), leaves his dad's place in Baltimore to live with his mom in Palo Alto. Rather than rely on either of these outsiders for non-tech-pilled perspectives, “The Audacity” prefers to envelop everyone in the industry's toxic cloud. Joanne is threatened by the vast wealth disparity between therapist and client, especially when it's clear how much smarter she is than these so-called titans. Even Tom Ruffage (Rob Corddry), a rep for Veterans Affairs, can't escape after dipping a baby toe into the Silicon Valley cesspool on behalf of servicemen and -women in need. Such uniformity of vision aptly conveys how it feels to be surrounded by people who don't act like living, breathing, feeling human beings, but it also keeps “The Audacity” at arm's length. Each eccentric weirdo is given a grounding element, something that hints at a soul surviving under all that soulless behavior, be it Joanne's kid, Carl's respect for the military, or Duncan's residual guilt over his deceased college roommate and Fah-Fa co-founder. But the first season savors slamming the door on those scant light sources, rather than let their warmth sink in. As hard to crack as “The Audacity” can be, there's relief to be found in its clarity of purpose. Helberg gets some of the biggest laughs with Xander, perhaps because their weird, misguided relationship is also the show's most compassionate. By collecting people's data and then selling it back to interested parties, he's merely profiting from a system others put into place. “Your outrage is fake,” he says to a booing crowd of his fellow techies. “You just don't like seeing your reflection in my business model.” What more Glatzer has to say with “The Audacity” may ultimately settle its fate. Even “Succession” took a minute to fully find its feet, so maybe “The Audacity” just needs to walk before it can run. Those are the words of Lawrence Montague on a jail phone call, words that now sit at the center of a broader legal reckoning unfolding in Maryland over the use of rap lyrics as evidence in criminal proceedings. Maryland prosecutors introduced Montague's rap verse, recorded using a jailhouse telephone and later posted to Instagram as evidence of his guilt for the killing of George Forrester. On appeal, the state's highest court affirmed Montague's conviction, finding that Montague's lyrics made it more probable that he shot and killed Forrester. That ruling set a dangerous precedent, particularly for rap and hip-hop artists in America, and prompted Variety to publish our January 2021 opinion piece. What we didn't realize at the time was that the article would help spark a national movement — now a united front of influential academics, defense and civil rights attorneys, and prominent music industry advocacy organizations including Songwriters of North America, the Black Music Action Coalition, The Recording Academy, and more. Together, we've partnered under a coalition known as Free Our Art, led by high-profile music executive Kevin Liles and co-chaired by me and Prophet. This week, in a full circle moment, Maryland became only the third state to pass a bill reconsidering how creative works are used in criminal trials. Critically, the legislation establishes clear standards for when creative works may be admitted as evidence in criminal proceedings. This law addresses a growing concern among the music industry, legal scholars, and civil rights advocates, as rap lyrics have almost exclusively been used against Black and Brown artists in more than 820 cases since the 1980s. Any creative expressions the government is looking to present as evidence must be presented to the judge before a jury trial even begins. Race has long shaped how rap lyrics are interpreted in the legal system. Courts have often misunderstood the history, purpose, and cultural significance of rap music in America, which emerged in the 1970s in the South Bronx as a response to poverty, unemployment, gang violence, isolation from mainstream America, and unfair treatment by government institutions. The hidden camera show was built around a brilliant premise that seemed impossible to replicate: call an unsuspecting citizen in for jury duty and then build a fake trial in which everyone except him is an actor. After it aired, anyone else who sees some bizarre behavior and a documentary crew at their local courthouse would immediately suspect something was up. “Jury Duty Presents: Company Retreat” managed to capture everything we loved about the original show — an ensemble of improv performers subjecting one naive civilian to an endless stream of absurd scenarios — without setting foot in a courtroom. The only real parameters are that it needs to take place in an isolated location with a closed circle of people, and the one non-actor needs to either have voluntarily sought out the opportunity or feel legally prohibited from leaving. With those loose guidelines in mind, IndieWire took the liberty of dreaming up a few ideas for scenarios the show could drop people into. If something as beautifully absurd as “Love Is Blind” didn't exist, it's not hard to see a world where the “Jury Duty” team invents it. And even as Netflix seems determined to develop every possible riff on the reality dating show format, there are bound to be a few crazy ideas that nobody has stumbled onto yet. A fake dating show could be a perfect set-up for an unsuspecting applicant, as they require auditioning, on-camera interviews, and everyone enters knowing they'll be forced to interact with strangers with large personalities. The show could also go the “Windy City Heat” route and create a fake movie as an excuse to cast one unsuspecting actor. It would also create opportunities for any real actors who are interested in giving a James Marsden-esque performance as themselves on the show. They say nothing films in L.A. anymore, but the town would be more than capable of creating a first-class fake movie set. All you have to do is rent out venues in multiple cities, put someone on a tour bus with a crew that all stays in the same hotels, and let the comedy ensue. You could either create a fictional band or enlist a real one to play themselves — either option is rife with possibilities for creative comedy writers. Youth sports are more competitive than ever these days, particularly for anyone who thinks (or has been grifted into thinking) their kid has a shot at the pros. A fake one could enlist an entire family for a weekend retreat of drills, team-building, and mentorship, with actors playing coaches, student athletes, and parents. Bear with me on this one… if the show was ready to take the leap into truly meta territory, it could stage a fake casting call for an actor who thinks they're auditioning to be one of the performers. It would be a brilliant way to lower someone's defense mechanisms, and watching someone who thinks they're in on the joke become the butt of it would make for excellent television.
