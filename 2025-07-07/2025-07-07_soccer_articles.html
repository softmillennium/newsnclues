What Pochettino and USMNT players learned about each other at the Gold Cup
Source: {'href': 'https://www.nytimes.com', 'title': 'The New York Times'}    Published: 2025-07-07 17:43:20

HOUSTON — For all of his experiences managing at some of the world's biggest clubs, Mauricio Pochettino spent this summer learning on the fly. The U.S. logged about 7,500 miles traveling to nine cities: Chicago, East Hartford, Nashville, San Jose, Austin, Dallas, Minneapolis, St. Louis and Houston. Pochettino's side played eight games, with opponents ranging from 17th-ranked Mexico and 20th-ranked Switzerland to 106th-ranked Guatemala. “It's a good experience to be able to feel what it means to be constantly traveling … I think we're learning a lot,” Pochettino reflected last week ahead of the semifinal. It's an experience that will be very useful to us as we prepare for the World Cup.” Pochettino got plenty out of a group of players that was not the team many envisioned he would be coaching this summer (or envision he will be coaching next summer, for that matter). Since arriving last October, Pochettino and his staff had very little time with the player pool. Players would arrive and have a recovery session and one training session, then play a game; then have another recovery session and one or two training sessions before the next game. This summer gave the group extended time together. “We talked about from from day one, this being an opportunity for a lot of guys, myself included, it was an opportunity to to grow as as players, but (also) grow as people, to create a culture and a togetherness that that we've maybe lacked in a lot of moments in the past six months to a year,” U.S. center back Tim Ream said. “I love being able to sit back and watch interactions. And their interactions with everybody is just, it's so impressive. His entire staff, the interactions that they have with players, they know when it's time to be serious and they know when it's time to put an arm around a guy. They know when it's time to poke a guy to get the best out of them, and they know when it's time to have a little laugh and a joke. And I think that's a sign of a great manager, when they can see when those moments are necessary and what those moments call for. And it's been probably the most impressive thing to see with all his staff.” Those interactions ultimately are what build chemistry and understanding within a group. Many of these players have been together since 2019 helping to rebuild a program that missed the 2018 World Cup. That familiarity, though, started to feel too much like comfort in the last year. The Copa América exit set off alarm bells and prompted U.S. Soccer leadership to fire then-coach Gregg Berhalter. In a way, this goal around this summer was to find players that could challenge the “core” of this U.S. team and make them feel that it's no guarantee they'll be called into every U.S. camp. It is not easy to inject that mentality into the group while simultaneously building trust. “They kind of come off as intimidating when you first come into camp, because they have such a good CV. But then once you finally get to meet them, you understand that they're all just big teddy bears, and they all really care about family. … One of the biggest things that we kind of learned this summer is that the coaching staff and players are starting to really understand each other.” While Pochettino undoubtedly got significant value in team building and camaraderie this summer, it may have been more important how he came to understand roster building and depth for a tournament. In some ways, the team he had this summer was out of his hands. Beyond the many players who were unavailable, several players originally summoned for the tournament left due to injury, including Sergiño Dest, Haji Wright, Folarin Balogun and Zack Steffen. Injuries in camp limited minutes for Damion Downs and Johnny Cardoso. But Pochettino's decision to leave off some players who had been involved in the Nations League camp and other competitions hurt the quality and depth of the squad in this tournament. Players like Tanner Tessmann, Joe Scally, Alejandro Zendejas and Josh Sargent undoubtedly would have improved this team's matchday flexibility. There was a balance between sending a message to the player pool and picking the best possible squad to maximize performance. When it comes time to the World Cup, Pochettino will have to hope that the messages he sent were received, because he'll need every ounce of competition and depth, without sacrificing quality. At bare minimum, he should now have a much better idea of the level of the player pool top to bottom. On Sunday after the game, Pochettino wasn't quite ready to talk about the players who weren't there. He seemed a bit thrown by a question about how to integrate some of the regulars who weren't there this summer back into the team. “You already made the list?” Pochettino responded when asked about reintegrating players like Christian Pulisic, Weston McKennie, Antonee Robinson and Dest. It's up to us now to analyze, but all the names you told me, all are under scrutiny, and we are following them. “It's still one year from the World Cup. But now we need to build a roster for September. The answer hinted that there is no guarantee that every “regular” will be back with the group in September. Pochettino's overarching message from this Gold Cup was about valuing every call-up and taking the job seriously. Richards said there were “a few non-negotiables now.” Anything short of those foundational standards will leave you on the outside looking in. Asked Sunday how to carry the togetherness and mentality gained this summer into the full group this fall, U.S. midfielder Tyler Adams was blunt. “And I think that's gonna lead to positive results.” If that's the lasting legacy of this Gold Cup, it will have been a successful first international tournament for Pochettino – even if it ended without a trophy. Five Observations from Bayern Munich's devastating 2-0 loss to PSG in the Club World Cup
Source: {'href': 'https://www.bavarianfootballworks.com', 'title': 'Bavarian Football Works'}    Published: 2025-07-07 15:36:14

Bayern Munich's much-anticipated clash against European champions PSG ended in a 2-0 loss for the Rekordmeister, following goals from Désiré Doué and Ousmane Dembélé. This means Bayern's journey in the inaugural edition of the new Club World Cup has come to an end. Here are some observations from the game. Jamal Musiala suffered what can only be described as an absolutely terrible injury to his ankle at the end of the first half, following an unnecessary, overplayed and slightly malicious tackle from Gianluigi Donnarumma at the edge of the field. In what marked the German starlet's first start following his injury in April, this is without a doubt extremely gutting. Even PSG players were horrified by the state of Musiala's leg/ankle area. Musiala deserved far better than to meet such a fate. Having skipped the Nations League to focus on getting completely ready for this tournament, getting injured in such a manner must be heartbreaking. Fans are finding it hard to imagine what Musiala must be going through, let alone what this might mean for his career. His tears and pain resonate with every Bayern fan in the world. Losing time at such a crucial stage in his career, a career so promising and bright, is an excruciating thought. Musiala's talent will be sorely missed — and here's to hoping he is alright. It is difficult now to talk about a football game after Bayern's most important player suffered such a horrific injury. Vincent Kompany set this team up to win from the very get-go. Bayern's quick wing play, speedy buildup by bypassing the midfield entirely, and added focus on going forward were exactly what they needed in a game such as this. Bayern countering PSG relentlessly pressing them with equal pressure of their own was not only a joy to watch but also a brilliant way to face a technically superior team. It quickly became clear that Bayern do not have practice playing with such intensity or against teams of PSG's level. Watching Bayern's defence caught on the counter and scrambling behind lightning-fast PSG forwards became a running trend (pun intended), and that was down to the easily exploitable defensive set-up. In that aspect, PSG's tactics with a more fluid structure that was able to cover ground more effectively saw them maintain a solid defensive structure without compromising their chance-creation. In the end, this is something for Kompany to build upon. There is a lot that can be done here, but it was also clear that this is a game the players aren't accustomed to playing. It is almost every matchday that this complaint pops up: Bayern Munich simply weren't clinical enough. Bayern caught PSG off guard with their setup and were able to penetrate their box far more often, but they failed to capitalize. This came off the back of a fairly weak Michael Olise performance before he settled into the game, and a performance that reminded us why Kingsley Coman was a candidate for sale. Konrad Laimer made his fair share of blunders, and Harry Kane's missed header early on sent what was a surefire goal above the crossbar, a moment that captured his game in itself. Bayern had their moments to capitalize, and didn't take it. PSG weren't far better in the first half. Bayern's pivot completely nullified the PSG midfield in the first half, courtesy of a fantastic game from Aleksandar Pavlović, forcing them to play through their wingers. PSG's wingers rose to the challenge and created clear chances for themselves in admirable fashion (admittedly due to lapses in judgement from Jonathan Tah & Co.), but fell short in front of the goal. João Neves had an early break following a dispossesion of Kane but slowed the pace of the attack, completed a quick one-two, allowing PSG players to occupy the box in their typical 2-3 formation while also allowing Bayern to regain structure. Doué's incredible strike took Bayern's now-structured defense by surprise and sent Neuer the wrong way. By taking chances on the break in a more sustained manner, PSG were able to overcome their limitations and even allowed their midfield to ease back into the game. The same approach showed when they scored the second with just nine men, as Achraf Hakimi toyed with the Bayern defence and laid Dembélé up for a perfect finish. Sure, it does help that PSG has technically better forwards than Bayern. But maybe that is something for Bayern to work on over the summer. Manuel Neuer has been written off time and time again, and he continues to show why he is Bayern's #1. Enough cannot be said about his game today. Almost nothing got past him for the most part. Thomas Müller, unfortunately, played his final game for Bayern Munich today. This is far from the send-off he deserves, especially after everything that went down with Musiala. Yet he almost won a penalty in his cameo and saw the end to one of the most dramatic games in recent history. He couldn't leave without a bit of fun and drama. Good luck to the Raumdeuter in his future endeavors. It is difficult to imagine FC Bayern without him. Sorry, but it had to be said. And this is not because Bayern lost today. The new Club World Cup is nothing more than FIFA's attempt to generate more money at the players' expense. This is a loss that resonates no more than any ordinary game. The tournament crams even more games into the footballing year, whose schedule becomes increasingly more packed every season. And the players are at serious risk. Playing all these games is one thing, but the conditions the players play in at this new tournament are nothing short of diabolical. Musiala's injury came as a result of poor pitch management, causing his leg to get lodged into the grass, which is why Gianluigi Donnarumma's tackle had the devastating impact that it did. And to no one's surprise, the same stadium today is booked for a Beyoncé concert the same week. Bayern have lost their star player likely for an extended amount of time in a tournament that, as it stands today, has little footballing relevance. What did you think about the game? HOUSTON — For 23 fleeting minutes here at NRG Stadium on Sunday night, the U.S. men's national team was back atop CONCACAF. It was beating Mexico, 1-0, in the Gold Cup final, and rising past expectations, toward a regional summit. But in the end, it succumbed to a better team — El Tri, which came back to win 2-1 and claim Mexico's 10th Gold Cup title. Edson Álvarez's 77th-minute goal, awarded after a dramatic VAR review, was the difference. An hour and a half earlier, before some of 70,925 fans here could even settle into their seats, the U.S. was ahead. Patrick Agyemang won a free kick on the right. Chris Richards hunted it, and nodded a world-class header over Mexican goalkeeper Luis Malagón, onto the crossbar, and narrowly over the line. The head referee, Mario Escobar, noticed it and whistled for a goal. Richards wheeled away in celebration, with a finger to his ear, as U.S. teammates erupted. Throughout a fascinating first half, though, Mexico's quality began to show. Álvarez, 16-year-old Gilberto Mora and Marcel Ruíz began to run the show. The U.S. struggled to cope with their movement in midfield, and struggled to get out of its defensive shell when possession changed hands. From Richards' goal until first-half stoppage time, the Americans didn't take a single shot. Tyler Adams got caught between him and Alexis Vega. U.S. right back Alex Freeman got caught in “no-man's land,” and didn't recover quick enough to Ruíz. Ruíz slid a clever reverse pass to Raúl Jiménez, who finished like the most accomplished player in the field — which he was. In fact, the Americans never truly lost their footing. They limited Mexico to very few legitimate looks at goal. But they failed to create much of anything going forward. Several players showed their inexperience under the “pressure” and “stress” that head coach Mauricio Pochettino promised this game would bring. The USMNT, with no true wingers, offered essentially zero vertical threat. But Álvarez got the last laugh, with his diving header off a flicked free kick. It had come a long way, from the brink of crisis to this final, but it couldn't quite clear the last — and tallest — hurdle. Less than four weeks ago, the Americans were reeling. After a 4-0 capitulation to Switzerland, their fourth straight loss, “everyone basically counted us out,” defender Tim Ream later said. In the sanctity of locker rooms and meal rooms, though, on flights and training fields, throughout their last true tournament before the 2026 World Cup, they jelled. Richards and others also used that word, family. Ream felt like he'd added “20 more kids” to his. Over 3 million people tuned into their quarterfinal, and didn't see Christian Pulisic or Weston McKennie; but did see Luna and a group of likable underdogs who, as Pochettino said, were “desperate” to “represent our country, and … fight for our flag, for our shirt.” They came to Houston with some expectations already met. “I think there's been a lot of success this summer, with how the team's grown,” Richards said Saturday. “But me personally,” he continued, “I like to win trophies — especially against Mexico.” When Pochettino gathered players and staff before training this weekend, he recounted a trophy-lifting dream he'd had the night before. They came with a six-game unbeaten streak against Mexico in official matches, but without Pulisic and McKennie, without Sergiño Dest and Antonee Robinson, without several architects of the streak, players who could equal Mexico's Álvarez and Jiménez. And besides: “Streaks are meant to be broken,” Jiménez said Saturday in Spanish. “And we think tomorrow is a great day for us to break [this one].” They became the first team to successfully defend a Gold Cup title since their Mexican predecessors went back-to-back in 2009 and 2011. They will head into North America's home World Cup next summer as the outright kings of the continent once again.
