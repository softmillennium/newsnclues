The trio recently won a 2026 Breakthrough Prize for this work. MAGUIRE: Leber's congenital amaurosis is a retinal disease that affects photoreceptors [light-sensitive cells]. It is a progressive degeneration because of genetic biochemical defects which result in blindness. There are numerous genetic subtypes of Leber's congenital amaurosis, and early on, they look different in terms of severity. For instance, type LCA5 is an early onset severe visual disability characterized by oculodigital behavior, in which people press on their eyes to stimulate mechanical light. From birth, they really do not have very useful, functional vision. That's a very severe form, which is still being worked on. People with LCA2 haveabnormal, jiggling eye movements soon after birth because they can't see things sharply enough to fix their eye on them. They usually have very poor sensorial acuity [inability to see details or objects from backgrounds]. They have nystagmus, which is when their eyes are moving around, sort of sweeping the area to pick up on things. They tend not to look at faces, and that's a kind of peculiar visual behavior from birth, which is usually what triggers parents to get an evaluation. If you could imagine covering all the lights in your room and just putting a few pinpricks through cardboard to let a little light through, that's about what they're seeing. They can get around reasonably well in high ambient lighting, but they go from legally blind to just blind over two decades or so. When 11-cis-retinal absorbs light, it converts into 11-trans-retinal, which starts the process that converts light into electrical signals and is important in sight. We know most people have been infected with it because we can detect antibodies in the blood showing exposure. It's usually coinfected with the common cold in childhood, and it very rarely has been shown to cause disease in humans or animals. We basically pack it with a type of synthetic DNA that encodes for the missing gene, RPE65, and a promotor, which acts as an “on” switch for gene activity. We did this in mice with extremely small cannulas or pipettes. In larger animals, I adapted available tools that had been developed for human surgery for the dog eye, which is pretty similar in size and anatomy to human eyes. The development of mechanically tunable hydrogels that replicate the dynamic mechanoelastic properties of native extracellular matrices (ECMs) is essential for advancing 3D tissue engineering. DNA, with its precise, programmable architecture and exceptional control at the nanometre scale, offers a valuable platform for designing ECM-mimicking scaffolds. This study presents stiffness-tuneable DNA supramolecular hydrogels with different branching architectures for programming cellular and organellar states. Utilizing precise DNA motifs—including DX (Double Crossovers), PX (Paranemic Crossovers), and Tensegrity architectures—we engineer hydrogels with widely adjustable mechanical properties (50–185 kPa) without chemical additives or enzymatic crosslinking. These hydrogels exhibit excellent strain-bearing and load-bearing capacity, making them suitable for biomedical applications. Additionally, these DNA hydrogels influence cellular behaviour in retinal pigment epithelial (RPE1) cells by enhancing cellular adhesion, encouraging elongation (a 3–8-fold increase in area compared to control), and improving viability (dependent on concentration, 1–8-fold increase vs. control), while also maintaining organellar homeostasis, including mitochondrial fragmentation and ER stress reduction. This work presents a framework for automating the production of stiffness-tunable DNA hydrogel scaffolds, aligning with the mechanical needs of various cells and tissues, thereby advancing personalized, high-throughput tissue engineering platforms. Hydrogels have already proven their indispensable role in tissue engineering by providing a three-dimensional, porous, and hydrated ECM-mimicking microenvironment for the comprehensive growth and development of cells and tissues1. Moreover, conventional hydrogels like collagen and alginate lack the mechanical tunability required to match the mechanoelastic needs of diverse human tissues (e.g., brain and adipose tissues: ~0.1–1 kPa; cartilage: ~10–25 kPa; skin: ~10–100 kPa; bone: >1 MPa)2,3. To modulate the mechanical stiffness of these conventional hydrogels, the addition/removal of polymers, linkers, bridging agents, or other materials is required. For example, in an attempt to create a void-forming hydrogel, Huebsch et al. proposed removing solid-phase porogens through hydrolysis after post-polymerization of the gel4. In this specific case, these solid-phase porogens were removed to modulate the mechanoelastic properties of the void-forming gels. Another example includes an attempt to fabricate superstrong, superstiff, and conductive alginate hydrogels by Ji et al. in 2022. They proposed to achieve this by densely connecting an interconnected network via an anisotropic densification of the pre-gel, followed by ionic crosslinking mediated by rehydration5. There are many other case scenarios where the mechanical or elastic properties of hydrogels have been tweaked, but at the cost of either the addition or removal of some macro- or micro-molecules. Molecules like DNA, protein, and carbohydrates can store information as well as to form diverse complex structures based on their specific shape, size, and information; however, amongst these, DNA offers a unique combination of ease of synthesis, high stability, and, most notably, precise structural programmability via base-pairing rules. These limitations often hinder the use of conventional hydrogels in regulating cell fates, influencing their adhesion, differentiation, and organellar functions in response to the mechanoelasticity of the scaffolds6,7. All conventional polymeric hydrogels can regulate their mechanical stiffness through various modifications, including, but not limited to, increasing polymeric concentrations8, increasing molecular weights9, the addition of chemical/enzymatic crosslinkers10,11,12, or physical assistance by adding nanomaterials13. However, many of these techniques that are used to modify the mechanical stiffness of the hydrogel matrix also alter the physicochemical properties, accompanied by topological changes of the polymer in focus, which are extremely important for cellular functions14,15,16,17,18. These alterations include changes in matrix density, porosity, or reliance on amorphous synthetic materials, which can cause unavoidable cytotoxicity. These complications can be avoided by incorporating DNA polymers to form a Hydrogel network. The DNA strands are well-characterized to form predictable Watson-Crick-based Hydrogen Bonding with complementary nucleobases. The biological significance of DNA makes it very stable and inherently cytocompatible in nature compared to other polymers. Because of this reliable base-pairing, it is now possible to design a 3D structure from DNA19,20,21,22,23,24, as was first elaborated by Dr. Ned Seeman25,26,27. Following this development, many researchers followed suit and explored various capabilities of DNA nanostructures in various areas of science, particularly in biomedical applications28,29,30,31,32, neuroscience33,34, cellular programming27,35,36,37,38, therapeutics39,40, and tissue engineering41. Dan Luo et al. explored the potential of DNA to form extrinsically interlinked 3D scaffolds, forming DNA hydrogels. DNA hydrogels offer unique advantages, both in form and function, by their inherent biocompatibility, biodegradability, along with biological functionality coupled with atomic-level precision through sequence design25,26,42,43,44,45,46,47. Recent advancements have demonstrated their efficiency and potential in providing ECM-mimicking environments for tissue engineering48,49,50,51,52,53. The fabricated hydrogel with ECM-mimicking properties should also provide ways for cells to modulate ECM regulation. A hydrogel system that does not degrade in response to the cellular needs also affects the cells during normal homeostasis and developmental phases18. For example, a study conducted by Zhou et al. found that the hydrogel stiffness alone may not be sufficient to drive the differentiation of NPCs. Many research groups have demonstrated diverse ways of forming DNA-based hydrogels with mechanical stiffness in the range of 100 Pa–100 kPa52,54,55,56,57,58. The growing interest in DNA hydrogels stems from their inherent biological properties and ease of customization59,60,61,62,63,64, offering a platform for modulation of cellular responses in ways that conventional hydrogels cannot. The architecture of DNA hydrogels is fundamentally determined by the interconnectivity of constituent DNA junctions, which regulate the branching architecture and stiffness of the hydrogels. At the molecular level, these junctions serve as a structural node that defines the network topology. The properties of hydrogel can be programmed by regulating these junctions. In our previous work, we tried to explore the role of soft DNA hydrogels made from branched simple DNA duplex junctions in programming the cellular behaviour of RPE1 cells, including enhanced cellular spreading, elongation, endocytic uptake, perturbation of ECM-regulating surface markers, mitochondrial fragmentation, and focal adhesion complex regulation52,53. These hydrogels exhibited a mechanical range of 0.1 Pa to 10 Pa, without the addition of crosslinkers or chemical modifications. Extending the scope of our previous work, reinforcing simple duplex junctions with mechanically robust and topologically stable junctions may serve as a key to unlock more features for DNA-based hydrogels in designing scaffolds for automated 3D bioprinting while systematically probing mechanobiology. While prior studies have explored DNA hydrogels for drug delivery or biosensing, their potential as a mechanically tunable scaffold for tissue engineering applications remains underexplored. The integration of more complex DNA motif structures, such as i-motifs, G-quadruplexes, and DNA crossovers, into junctions may lead to diversification of geometry and mechanical properties. DNA motifs such as double-crossovers (DX) contain two crossover sites between helical domains, while a paranemic-crossover (PX) offers an even more complex design, featuring a four-stranded coaxial DNA complex with crossovers happening at every possible point65. These structures must be designed while keeping the topology of DNA strands in mind, where crossovers are separated by specific half-turns to avoid torsional stress. The increased structural rigidity of DX, and even so for PX, compared to simple duplexes, makes them valuable building blocks for constructing more stable DNA nanostructures and hydrogels65,66. Increasing the degree of sustained crossovers is one way to increase the rigidity of the hydrogels. Another significant advancement is the development of hydrogels with tensegrity architectures inspired by the mechanochemical principles underlying cell-ECM interactions67,68,69. These motifs are made from ssDNA strands forming an isolated rigid compression unit, held in place by a continuous network of tensile elements, enabling tensile moduli up to 30 MPa69. These compression units act as a junction point, where, depending on the branching numbers, it is possible to vary the mechanical stiffness of the hydrogel network so formed. The incorporation of Tensegrity units into DNA hydrogel design represents a biomimetic approach to create a robust, adaptive platform for advanced mechanoregulated tissue engineering67,68,69. We hypothesize that the degree of branching complexity in a DNA hydrogel network, via regulating the degree of crossovers and changing the number of branching arms (DX to PX to Tensegrity (trimers-hexamers)), directly governs hydrogel stiffness, which in turn can modulate cellular mechanotransduction pathways. To address the above hypothesis, we envisioned a set of DNA hydrogel networks, each varying in its precise junction architecture and branching arms. The measurements carried out by AFM-based force spectroscopy are direct and precisely measure the stiffness of the polymers at the nanometre scale. However, the cantilever fails to precisely apply any shearing deformation to the material. The most common interaction is through indented force curves, which follow Newtonian mechanics and hence only simplified Hertz-Sneddon models, which assume the material to be perfectly elastic, isotropic, and homogeneous. The hydrogels, however, do not fit this description. As a result, bulk behaviour estimation is required, which can be aptly achieved through rheological studies. AFM-based force spectroscopy can provide an accurate estimation of the surface stiffness of polymeric hydrogels at the nanometre scale, due to the scale of the cantilevers used. The macroscopic mechano-elastic characterization can be achieved by rheometers. These rheological tests enable us to understand the holistic behaviour of DNA hydrogels under strain deformations and decouple their linear viscoelastic region and mechanical stiffnesses. To probe the bulk mechano-elastic properties of DNA hydrogels, oscillatory rheology is preferred, which provides key insights into the material characteristics of the hydrogel at various amplitudes, frequencies, and shear strains. To assess the structural integrity and deformation limits of the DNA hydrogels, amplitude sweep tests were performed at a constant frequency of 6.28 rad/s or 1 Hz. This LVR plateau indicates a stable, solid-like network where the microstructure recovers elastically from the small deformations. Below 1% shear strain, the storage modulus of hydrogels remained significantly higher than the loss modulus, suggesting that at no or lower shearing strain levels, the materials represented a solid-network forming hydrogel response. This point represents the critical strain or yield strain of the control samples, marking the transition from an elastic to a viscous domain-flow state. The ssDNA strands are designed through a rational approach while relying on the Watson-Crick base-pairing rule. The strands were enriched in high GC content, where binding was expected/ programmed to be higher (at the ends) compared to the availability of AT-rich regions (cross-over sites), where flexibility of the strands was to be expected. Last week researchers published a small, preliminary study in Nature Medicine outlining a potential treatment for patients stuck in this limbo. The pilot trial, which featured 16 women with preterm preeclampsia, filtered a troublesome protein out of patients' blood. The treatment targets a protein called sFlt-1, which previous research has identified as a key driver of the blood vessel constriction that can make preeclampsia so dangerous. The procedure seemed to stabilize their blood pressures and extended their pregnancies by an average of 10 days—that's twice what the researchers would have expected for an untreated group. Five days might not sound like an impressive achievement if you've never had to play this dangerous waiting game yourself, but it can make a huge difference. When it comes to preterm birth—birth before 37 weeks of gestation—research shows that even an extra day or two in the womb can drastically improve a baby's outcomes. The next step is a much larger, randomized control trial to help us understand how effective the treatment really is and how best to implement it. In global geopolitics sometimes rumors emerge that this country can crack any encrypted code or that country has developed a new kind of high-tech weapon. And that's exactly the purpose: to determine how feasible any wild technology claim might be. And DARPA built its own AI to be one of the central tools in this program. In a really bare-bones example, let's say some unnamed rival country claims that it made military armor out of a material that can repair itself. The SciFy AI might do the following fact-check: In order for a self-healing material to exist, it would need to be solid in a frigid tundra and solid in a steamy jungle with much higher temperatures. Starting a bit over 300 million years ago and for around 100 million or so years after that, if you wanted to be on land, Pangaea was the only game in town. Nature Structural & Molecular Biology (2026)Cite this article Chromatin dynamics control the timescales of essential biological processes and reflect how chromatin is organized in the nucleus. Previous studies have reported widely varying degrees of chromatin subdiffusion without consensus. For robust subdiffusion measurements, chromatin should be tracked across a sufficiently large dynamic range. Here we track chromatin movement across seven orders of magnitude in time by integrating MINFLUX microscopy with single-molecule and locus tracking in five mouse and human cell types. We discover two fundamentally different dynamics classes that are cell type specific and not described by common chromatin polymer models. In one class, chromatin displays strong subdiffusion across all timescales (α ~0.3), indicating that the local environment is more quickly searched than the distal. In the other class, chromatin shifts from strongly subdiffusive at short timescales to less subdiffusive, making search less local over long timescales. Both classes are only moderately sensitive to perturbations. Search times under these observed dynamics are extremely short for nearby loci (<100 nm) but almost impossibly long over larger distances (>1 µm); this has important implications for processes involving two-locus contacts, such as enhancer–promoter search and DNA double-strand break repair.
