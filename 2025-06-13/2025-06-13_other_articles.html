This Is the Actual “Jump the Shark” Moment When ‘Supernatural' Changed and Truly Went Off the Rails
Source: {'href': 'https://collider.com', 'title': 'Collider'}    Published: 2025-06-13 19:28:00

Okay, yes, it's true that there is a literal Supernatural episode titled "Jump the Shark," but, ironically, that was nowhere close to the actual shark-jumping moment that would later shift the series' tone into what would essentially become parody over time. There are a lot of opinions about when exactly Supernatural began to turn for the worst. Some say that anything after Season 5 could be discarded as the original mytharc was complete and series creator Eric Kripke stepped down as showrunner. Others say it was the Leviathan arc or the fact that Sam (Jared Padalecki) didn't look for Dean (Jensen Ackles) when he was trapped in Purgatory. After being killed in mortal combat against the angel Metatron (Curtis Armstrong) in the Season 9 finale, "Do You Believe In Miracles?," Dean opened his eyes to reveal himself to have become a demon. Having taken on the Mark of Cain in order to battle Metatron and Abaddon (Alaina Huffman), Dean learned that the mark was (apparently) irreversible and would ultimately turn him into a demon upon his death, cursing him to live forever. This led to the very short-lived Demon Dean (or "Deanmon") arc in Season 10's opening episodes, which, frankly, betrayed what so many people loved about the show. For years, Supernatural had pushed Dean into becoming more cozy with certain monsters and demons. It was these small compromises that led to Dean becoming the very thing he swore to hunt, all coming to a head the moment he opens his newly blackened eyes at the end of Season 9. For starters, the series never actually intended to turn Dean into a demon. Rather, that scene from the third season was a way for Dean to wrestle with his own death after making a demon deal in "All Hell Breaks Loose" to bring Sam back from the dead. Furthermore, Supernatural didn't even commit to Demon Dean. Rather than turning him into a cold-blooded monster (as so many demons are), he's portrayed really as just another jerk with black eyes. To make it worse, Dean is human again after only a few episodes, but the show would never be the same. It was an interesting medium to explore those tendencies that already existed in the character, and it was a promising new direction for the series. However, when it came to Demon Dean, the whole thing was terribly inconsistent. Why was Demon Dean all bark with no real bite? In the end, Demon Dean was nothing but shock and awe. It never contributed anything substantial to Dean's long-term arc, and it only opened the door further for the show to assassinate his character. In many ways, Demon Dean is a perfect example of how the second half of Supernatural's 15-season run would often introduce a new plotline, character, or story, only to forget about it entirely. Of course, what it did manage to do was give the series precedent to change Dean into someone his younger self would hardly recognize. Wanting to kill Jack (Alexander Calvert) for no reason after years of befriending "friendly" monsters, saying "yes" to becoming possessed by Michael at the end of Season 13, and even killing Death (Julian Richings) at the end of Season 10 (as if the Winchesters never learned anything from their consequences) all occurred after Dean was transformed into a demon. Demon Dean represents the gradual shift in Supernatural as the show became more of a parody of what made it great originally. The early seasons were great because it was a road horror show that followed two brothers as they hunted ghosts, monsters, and demons, only to eventually take on the Devil (Mark Pellegrino) himself. Even when the show fumbled to find a new voice in Seasons 6 and 7, it maintained its grip on who Sam and Dean were (for the most part). Season 8 continued to offer more promise for the show, but by the end of Season 9, it felt that the show — and Sam and Dean as characters — had become a spoof of what it once was. As the show went on, Dean felt more like a stock character (compared to his younger self), and while we still got some meaty material in select episodes, the show grew beyond what made it work so well at first. So much of this can be traced back to that brief Demon Dean arc. It was a moment where the show, just threw an idea at the wall to see if it would stick. This same technique would occur over and over again as the show ran for more seasons, and it's no wonder that Dean's arc had to ultimately be resolved on the spin-off series, The Winchesters. Make no mistake, Supernatural had great moments throughout its entire run, but there's a reason that the first half of the show is far more beloved than the second — and with plotlines like Demon Dean, we can see why. While groundwater is being depleted, it's unlikely the water will ever run out completely. Some 40 million people, including those in Phoenix and Las Vegas, depend on it for their water needs. But as supplies of this surface water have dried up over the past two decades — reaching record lows — more and more people have been pumping groundwater from far below the surface, mainly for agricultural use. To get a better idea of how much groundwater is being extracted, Jay Famiglietti, director of science for the Arizona Water Innovation Initiative at Arizona State University, and his colleagues turned to data from NASA's Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment (GRACE) and GRACE Follow-On satellite missions. These satellites track changes in Earth's gravity field to measure shifts in the amount of water above and below the ground, and when combined with data on snowpack, surface water and soil moisture, this information can allow scientists to estimate how much groundwater has been depleted. The researchers estimate that since 2003, pumping from wells has drained about 28 million acre feet (34 cubic kilometers) of groundwater from the Colorado River Basin. In these largely rural areas, farms aren't usually hooked up to municipal water systems, and instead rely on water pumped from wells on the property. Because the wells are private, there is often no municipal, county- or state-level measurement of how much water they are drawing up.
