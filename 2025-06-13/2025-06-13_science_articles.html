To travel beyond our solar system to destinations like Alpha Centauri, our nearest neighboring star system, it would take many human lifetimes with current technology. It began in 2016 and aims to design small, lightweight spacecraft powered by light that can reach Alpha Centauri in just over 20 years. If this type of machine comes to fruition, it will vastly outpace today's fastest spacecraft, like the Helios probes that NASA and West Germany collaborated on in the 1970s. Those older probes would take nearly 20,000 years to travel 4.4 light years (roughly 25 trillion miles) to Alpha Centauri, scientists estimate. But a new featherweight probe would give scientists unprecedented access to the farthest reaches of space, allowing them to capture close-up images of stellar objects like exoplanets for the first time in history, says Harry Atwater, Ph.D., a professor of applied physics and materials science at the California Institute of Technology. Formerly, Atwater was the lightsail research director for Breakthrough Starshot; he is continuing this work independently at Caltech. “There's a lot of really transformative work going on in astronomy right now using large telescopes [. to do remote observations of distant stars and exoplanets,” Atwater says. “But there are certainly new things that we [can] learn as a result of actually going to them.” One way to lighten your spacecraft is to do away with rocket fuel altogether and rely on a type of weightless fuel instead: light. For this project, scientists supporting Breakthrough Starshot are working to develop what they call a “photon engine.” If successful, the device will create a powerful light beam that can propel a swarm of future Starshot craft into deep space. This may seem counterintuitive at first, because the light from an everyday laser pointer or flashlight could never propel a feather let alone a spacecraft. But when scaled to high enough intensities, a laser beam can act similarly to wind propelling a sailboat across water. Instead of absorbing solar power, the panels would be composed of many densely packed lasers that can emit 45 watts each. He stresses that this photon engine would not be hazardous to unsuspecting passersby or create a threat to national security in the form of a weapon. “If you were flying in an airplane above [the laser field] within the Earth's atmosphere, you would experience something that would be roughly a few times higher than theintensity of sunlight, but not enough to burn yourself,” Atwater says. To achieve the kind of acceleration that these craft will need to reach an interstellar target like Alpha Centauri in just about 20 years, they not only need a strong power source like a souped-up laser beam, but also must weigh no more than 1 gram per component—that includes the lightsail itself and a microchip payload. In this case, the sail must be about 200 nanometers thick, which is a thousand times thinner than a human hair. Building these types of sails is a daunting task that can be expensive and time intensive. After nearly a decade of work, a full-fledged photon engine is still far from reality, but Atwater says scientists have worked out most of the technical kinks. Still, actually launching these craft into interstellar space remains a distant goal. While we have a pretty decent grasp of how individual stars are born, there are still gaps in our knowledge of their statistics en masse. Say you have a giant gas cloud that is busily manufacturing stars. As a percentage, how many of them will be like the sun? The mass of a star—how much matter is in it—determines most of its properties, including its temperature, color, brightness, evolution and even its destiny, so this is something astronomers are very keen to understand. And in general, these objects are so dim that even nearby ones can elude detection. There could also be smaller binary companions to some stars that have gone undiscovered. Extrapolating it to the Milky Way and other galaxies will help us understand how galaxies behave—and how they change their behavior over time, churning out different mixes of stars as they age. New research from MIT shows that one key factor in determining where microparticles are likely to build up has to do with the presence of biofilms. These thin, sticky biopolymer layers are shed by microorganisms and can accumulate on surfaces, including along sandy riverbeds or seashores. The study found that, all other conditions being equal, microparticles are less likely to accumulate in sediment infused with biofilms, because if they land there, they are more likely to be resuspended by flowing water and carried away. The open-access findings appear in the journal Geophysical Research Letters, in a paper by MIT postdoc Hyoungchul Park and professor of civil and environmental engineering Heidi Nepf. "Microplastics are definitely in the news a lot," Nepf says, "and we don't fully understand where the hotspots of accumulation are likely to be. Most experiments looking at the ways microparticles are transported and deposited have been conducted over bare sand, Park says. "But in nature, there are a lot of microorganisms, such as bacteria, fungi, and algae, and when they adhere to the stream bed they generate some sticky things." These substances are known as extracellular polymeric substances, or EPS, and they "can significantly affect the channel bed characteristics," he says. In some experiments the bed consisted of pure sand, and in others the sand was mixed with a biological material to simulate the natural biofilms found in many riverbed and seashore environments. Water mixed with tiny plastic particles was pumped through the tank for three hours, and then the bed surface was photographed under ultraviolet light that caused the plastic particles to fluoresce, allowing a quantitative measurement of their concentration. Guest molecules ride perfect waves in dna droplets—A breakthrough for synthetic biology
Source: {'href': 'http://www.sciencedaily.com', 'title': 'ScienceDaily'}    Published: 2025-06-13 08:08:31

Researchers at Johannes Gutenberg University Mainz (JGU), the Max Planck Institute for Polymer Research and the University of Texas at Austin have identified a form of molecular motion that has not previously been observed. When what are known as 'guest molecules' - molecules that are accommodated within a host molecule - penetrate droplets of DNA polymers, they do not simply diffuse in them in a haphazard fashion, but propagate through them in the form of a clearly-defined frontal wave. "This is an effect we did not expect at all," points out Weixiang Chen of the Department of Chemistry at JGU, who played a major role in the discovery. It is usually the case that molecules are distributed throughout liquids by means of simple diffusion. For instance, if you add a blue dye to a glass of water, the dye gradually disperses in the liquid, forming soft, blurry color gradients. However, the observed behavior of guest molecules in DNA droplets is quite different. Moreover, these droplets have their counterparts in biological cells, which are able to employ similar condensates to arrange complex biochemical processes without the need for membranes. "Our synthetic droplets thus represent an excellent model system with which we can simulate natural processes and come to better understand them," emphasizes Chen. Chen adds: "The well-defined, highly concentrated front continues to move forward in a linear fashion over time, driven by chemical binding, material conversion and programmable DNA interactions. Something that is completely new when it comes to soft matter." This would also be of interest when it comes to the treatment of neurodegenerative disorders in which proteins migrate from cell nuclei into the cytoplasm, forming condensates there. "It is quite conceivable that we may be able to find a way of influencing these aging processes with the aid of our new insights, so that, over the long term, an entirely new approach to the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases could emerge," concludes Walther. Researchers at the University of California, Davis, have developed an investigational brain-computer interface that holds promise for restoring the voices of people who have lost the ability to speak due to neurological conditions. In a new study published in the scientific journal Nature, the researchers demonstrate how this new technology can instantaneously translate brain activity into voice as a person tries to speak -- effectively creating a digital vocal tract. The system allowed the study participant, who has amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), to "speak" through a computer with his family in real time, change his intonation and "sing" simple melodies. "Translating neural activity into text, which is how our previous speech brain-computer interface works, is akin to text messaging. By comparison, this new real-time voice synthesis is more like a voice call," said Sergey Stavisky, senior author of the paper and an assistant professor in the UC Davis Department of Neurological Surgery. Decoding brain signals at heart of new technology His ability to communicate through a computer has been made possible with an investigational brain-computer interface (BCI). "The main barrier to synthesizing voice in real-time was not knowing exactly when and how the person with speech loss is trying to speak," said Maitreyee Wairagkar, first author of the study and project scientist in the Neuroprosthetics Lab at UC Davis. "Our algorithms map neural activity to intended sounds at each moment of time. This makes it possible to synthesize nuances in speech and give the participant control over the cadence of his BCI-voice." The brain-computer interface was able to translate the study participant's neural signals into audible speech played through a speaker very quickly -- one-fortieth of a second. This short delay is similar to the delay a person experiences when they speak and hear the sound of their own voice. He was able to modulate the intonation of his generated computer voice to ask a question or emphasize specific words in a sentence. The participant also took steps toward varying pitch by singing simple, short melodies. His BCI-synthesized voice was often intelligible: Listeners could understand almost 60% of the synthesized words correctly (as opposed to 4% when he was not using the BCI).
