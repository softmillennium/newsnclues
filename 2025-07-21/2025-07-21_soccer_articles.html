In his years with the United States Soccer Federation, Steve Fell has seen them all. Following years alongside the United States men's national team at world soccer's highest level, he's adding a new role in 2025 as head of performance for Sporting Jax and its young roster. "There are differences in maybe the experiences of a higher-level player, because of what they deal with day-to-day, but I would say from a physical preparation side, you treat them all the same," Fell said. Even after some recent experienced additions, most recently former University of Florida defender Julia Lester on loan from the Seattle Reign of the National Women's Soccer League, Sporting Jax is racing toward August with youth. Head coach Stacey Balaam has the assignment of molding those newcomers into a cohesive unit for the club's Super League kickoff on Aug. 23, and she expects that Fell's background in the sport can accelerate the process of bringing them up to speed. "It's been valuable for our players to learn from him and for him to give them the necessary feedback. Right now, we're just soaking up all the information." A graduate of Towson University in Maryland, Fell is also at home on the First Coast, and he became Flagler College's director of sports performance in July 2015. Since early 2022, Fell has worked as a performance coach alongside the full U.S. men's national team, including the FIFA World Cup in Qatar, and he intends to continue in that role while also assisting Sporting Jax's efforts. For Fell, the summer has raced along at near-nonstop pace. After the United States lost that tournament final 2-1 to Mexico on July 6 in Houston, almost immediately, he was on his way back to Florida for Sporting Jax camp. "It was good to get right back into it and almost erase the thought of [the loss] quickly," he said. "It's not just technical and tactical, we're talking the physical attributes," Fell said. "That is the universal thing when you go from college to a professional level. You see it all over the place, where individuals who were doing very well in college have this little adjustment period." The goal for Fell and the Sporting Jax staff is making that adjustment period as speedy as possible for rookies like defender Paige Kenton, who owns substantial credentials in her own right. She graduated magna cum laude from Penn with a degree in neuroscience and completed her fifth NCAA year last fall while pursuing her master's degree in the University of Delaware's Department of Linguistics and Cognitive Science. "It's not just about making a good pass, or making a pass, but it's about maximizing your decisions to help the team." It's a similar feeling for players like defender Grace Philpotts, in her first professional season out of the University of Kentucky. "We're having vets, we're having girls with pro experience of many years that are coming now and training, so I'd say the tempo is a lot faster [than in college]." Balaam, who came to Sporting Jax in April from SEC soccer at Vanderbilt, recognizes the step up awaiting the players. "It's something that they've probably not had before," Balaam said. "So it's an example of this as the next level for them." However, time is on the side of Sporting Jax. In addition to closed-door training matches later this month, the club also tunes up at UNF on Aug. 2 against Hibernian of Scotland and on Aug. 10 against Wrexham of Wales, games that will help Fell assess how players are advancing. "We're building these athletes who are just coming out of college progressively, in a way that's safe," Fell said, "to help them attain that level where they're able to handle the demands of the professional level."

Chelsea have won two trophies this season, credit to Enzo Maresca, and in large part, Cole Palmer. Yes, everyone expected Maresca's men to win the Conference League, but then again, everyone also expected Paris Saint-Germain to win the Club World Cup after wins over Bayern Munich and Real Madrid with ease. Chelsea's performance in the Club World Cup deserves recognition for several reasons. Maresca had the sense not to sit back against Paris Saint-Germain, but also not to press them too far forward, even though he deployed a high line. The Blues boss relied on his team's pacy backline to deal with the over-the-top through balls that Luis Enrique's side tried to play in behind. This should be noted, as the Italian manager has demonstrated this kind of pragmatism at several points throughout the season. If Chelsea can consistently deploy this style next season, it could lead to invaluable victories over direct rivals. That kind of efficiency should be acknowledged, though for a different reason. In fact, Chelsea missed 2.2 of every 3.2 big chances they created per game in the Premier League last season, across 38 games. That wastefulness extended to the Club World Cup final too. Joao Pedro is likely to help Maresca's men become more clinical next season. If the Blues want to compete for more than just a top-four finish, improving their efficiency in front of goal is essential. Chelsea's players showed a level of confidence that, admittedly, made me uneasy at first. Their performance reflected a team that believed in its game plan. Next season, Maresca's men will need that same belief, not only for big matches but also to meet the season's expectations as a whole. Levi Colwill's comments about “not being Inter or Madrid” may have come across as arrogant, but confidence and arrogance often wear the same face. That belief will also help the coach, as confident players take necessary risks without being overly cautious. These three factors are why Chelsea should be considered serious contenders for a top-two finish in the Premier League in the 2025/26 season. That is, as long as Maresca doesn't revert to a rigid “his way” approach, the kind that saw them lose 4-0 to Celtic in July 2024. While most of those opponents weren't expected to pose much of a challenge, the win over the Champions League holders was significant because it finally earned the Blues some long-overdue respect from rival fans. Premier League teams will be at least twice as difficult as most of the sides Chelsea faced in the Club World Cup.
