A city report says that the cost of some services the province said it would pay for out of its $97-million contribution are “substantially higher” than budgeted, which may leave Toronto on the hook for more money. A city report says that the cost of some services the province said it would pay for out of its $97-million contribution are “substantially higher” than budgeted, which may leave Toronto on the hook for more money. Toronto expects it will cost $380 million to stage six games for the 2026 FIFA World Cup. Toronto may be forced to take on a greater share of hosting costs for the 2026 FIFA World Cup, as the city and Premier Doug Ford's government remain at odds over the province's contribution with just over a year before the tournament is set to kick off. Queen's Park agreed in late 2023 to provide up to $97 million of that, while the federal government has pledged about $104 million, and the city is responsible for about $179 million. But while Queen's Park had previously said its contribution would include the value of provincial services required for the tournament — such as increased policing, transportation and health care — the report states that Ontario's estimated value of those services are “substantially higher than budgeted, and in some cases completely unbudgeted.” According to the document, which will be debated at council this week, there are more than $39 million in costs for provincial services still under negotiation between the two governments, which would come out of the province's $97-million share, “increasing the city's funding gap.” The report from executive director of Toronto's World Cup secretariat Sharon Bollenbach says that the city will continue negotiations with the province “to reduce uncertainties around scope and lowering ministry service costs.” The city was already facing a $18-million shortfall in its hosting budget, according to a separate report released this month. It planned to make up that gap through a combination of private donations, merchandise sales, hospitality packages and other measures. The tournament is scheduled to kickoff June 11, 2026 in Mexico City, with Toronto's BMO Field staging the Canadian national team's opening game on June 12. In a letter to city manager Paul Johnson, Deputy Minister of Tourism, Culture and Sport Sarah Harrison said Queen's Park's contribution would be “exclusively limited” to investments that would deliver public infrastructure and community benefits that would outlast the games. That would include “provincial priorities such as community infrastructure projects with long-term public benefit, sport legacy and tourism support impacts.” It would exclude any costs where the “primary beneficiary” would be a nonpublic entity, such as a private or for-profit company.
