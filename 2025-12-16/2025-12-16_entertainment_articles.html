At Rob Reiner's Brentwood Home, Shock and Sorrow Crowd Out the Frame
Source: {'href': 'https://www.hollywoodreporter.com', 'title': 'The Hollywood Reporter'}    Published: 2025-12-16 16:41:48

If you were to stick a pin into a map of Los Angeles at the precise location where one might least expect a heinous act of murderous violence, it would likely land right here, on South Chadbourne Avenue. This narrow lane in Brentwood, tucked discreetly a few blocks south of Sunset, lined with palm trees and gated multimillion-dollar mansions, is usually as calm and idyllic as a David Hockney landscape. On this day, Dec. 14, it looks like something out of a James Ellroy fever dream. Critic's Appreciation: Rob Reiner Was So Much More Than a Capable Company Man Nick Reiner Revealed Violent Outburst, Family Issues and "Cocaine Heart Attack" on 2018 Podcast Taping At one point, a resident wanders out of her home, glances at the mob milling in front of her driveway, breaks into sobs, then rushes back inside. Police hadn't yet officially confirmed it, but everybody here knows what's just happened. Rob Reiner and his wife, Michele, have been brutally murdered. There have been some hard Hollywood deaths this past year — Gene Hackman, Robert Redford, Diane Keaton — but it's fair to say this one hit the town particularly hard, and not just because of the shocking circumstances. As a teenager, Reiner occasionally popped up elsewhere on the dial — there was a non-speaking part in Wagon Train — and when he was 20 his dad gave him a role in 1967's Enter Laughing, his semiautobiographical movie about growing up in Depression-era New York. But, of course, it was the part Reiner landed just a few years later, at the age of 23, that would set the course for a career that would one day rival and even surpass his father's. For a generation of TV viewers, Reiner will always be “Meathead,” Archie Bunker's long-suffering liberal son-in-law on Norman Lear's seminal 1970s sitcom All in the Family. The show, which gleefully pushed political and social hot button issues — racism, sexism, homophobia, they all were treated as punchlines — was such a cultural phenomenon it could easily have defined Reiner's legacy on its own. For starters, there was This Is Spinal Tap, his 1984 directorial debut that pretty much invented the mockumentary (poignantly, last September, it was bookended by its long-in-the-works sequel, Spinal Tap II: The End Continues, Reiner's final film). Over the next incredibly productive eight years, he'd make Stand by Me (River Phoenix's breakout film); The Princess Bride (still a cable-TV holiday staple); When Harry Met Sally (the Nora Ephron-scripted rom-com that made Meg Ryan a star — and also the film on which Reiner met his wife, who was a photographer on the set); Misery (which won Kathy Bates an Oscar); and A Few Good Men (noms for best picture and best supporting actor for Jack Nicholson). He was a founding board member of AFER, the organization that, in 2013, helped overturn California's ban on same-sex marriage. His opinions sometimes drew fire from the right — especially recently, with President Trump not even letting Reiner's death get in the way of attacking him for being “deranged” and “very bad for our country” — but most reasonable people, even when they disagreed with him, could find something to respect in his humanity and dignity. His 1994 high-concept family comedy North — with future-Frodo Elijah Wood playing a hyper-articulate 11-year-old who sues his parents for not being appreciative enough — was the flop that finally cracked his winning streak, not only tanking at the box office but also famously earning zero stars from Roger Ebert. Still, despite such later-career setbacks, Reiner's track record at his peak, in the 1980s and early 1990s, puts him right up there with Ron Howard, Robert Zemeckis and maybe even Steven Spielberg, planting him squarely in the A-list echelon of late 20th century auteurs, part of Hollywood's most elite cadre. Except for one other — the one Reiner and his 67-year-old wife of 35 years joined this past weekend, after the horrific events at their Brentwood home; it's a much sadder pantheon, but equally famous and even more enduring — the icons of Hollywood's most infamous crimes and catastrophes. It's not too far from another famous Brentwood address: 875 Bundy Drive, where Nicole Brown Simpson was murdered. And that house, it turns out, is only a short distance from Fifth Helena Drive, where Marilyn Monroe overdosed. There are scores of other equally grim landmarks strewn all over the city. Box office is synonymous with struggle, streamers retreated from discovery, and the festival-to-distribution pipeline feels like a straw. Everyone knows the drill: fewer buyers, slower deals, shrinking risk tolerance, and a talent pool stuck waiting for a system that isn't correcting itself. At the same time, a growing cohort of filmmakers is opting out of the long march toward institutional validation. Related Stories ‘The Housemaid' Review: Amanda Seyfried Turns in Her Second Great Performance of 2025 in Thriller Too Timid to Go Full Camp ‘Disclosure Day' Teaser: Spielberg's UFO Thriller Reveals Title and Something Very Wrong with Emily Blunt It's nepotism as apprenticeship, and a case study in what happens when filmmakers stop waiting for permission and start building. In another era, a straight-to-streaming film made for $65K would have been dismissed. Today, it looks like part of a larger power shift — one in which distribution, marketing, and even greenlights are no longer handed down, but designed from the ground up. Evan Shapiro and Jamie Shapiro's candid, sometimes combative exchange is both blueprint and provocation for filmmakers still clinging to the old model. What follows is their unvarnished conversation about making Skit, navigating a creator-driven marketplace, and why the future of indie film may belong to those who greenlight themselves first. EVAN SHAPIRO: I am really worried about indie movies — basically panic stricken over their future. I've worked in independent film for over 20 years: I was general manager of IFC and then president of both IFC and Sundance TV. In 2006, I executive produced the Kirby Dick documentary “This Film Is Not Yet Rated,” which demonstrated the bias Hollywood and movie theater owners have against indie film, using the MPA as bag man. Last year, I collaborated with Keri Putnam on her study of independent and documentary film which showed that, despite streamers, commissioners, and theaters all but abandoning independent cinema, there is still a huge demand among Americans for indies and documentaries — one that goes underserved as the general economics of Hollywood slip into systemic failure. At that very moment, you, my daughter, pitched me on producing an indie movie called “Skit.” JAMIE SHAPIRO: I moved to LA a year and a half earlier in the hopes of living out my dream as an actress, only to be met with the industry-wide strikes. Ever dramatic, I was mourning the death of my nonexistent career. Post-strikes, Hollywood resembled one of those dinosaur dioramas right before the asteroids hit: actors scrambling for work, directors teaching workshops they don't believe in, writers pretending to “staff” on imaginary shows. You had been coaching me (aka screaming at me) to take control of my career now, or I'd be at risk of waiting tables at a hipster Silverlake restaurant for the rest of my life (or worse, living at home with you and Mom). And then you texted me budget spreadsheets, demanding we drive toward production far faster than any of us had originally planned. EVAN: Simultaneously, I had been writing and speaking about the rise of the creator economy — how it was becoming the gravitational center of global culture, andhow, if they so choose, producers and publishers could and should become creators themselves. We ran the entire production like a DIY startup: casting brilliant, up-and-coming actors and comedians who hadn't yet gotten the roles they deserve; utilizing a SAG micro-budget contract (which feels like the only part of Hollywood tethered to the current media reality); and embarking on a whirlwind eight-day shoot that took years off our lives. EVAN: As we finished the film, we started collaborating with BBC Studios on content about their fandom-first business model and creator mindset. I also fell down the rabbit hole of #filmtok. All of this helped me solidify a distribution strategy for Skit built entirely on the foundation of the creator economy. JAMIE: The wild part is how quickly that creator theme boomeranged back at us in real time. I met Anjali Sud (on whom I have an insane career crush) at a Fordham Business School event where you were interviewing her, and I all but ambushed her with: “Hi, I love your whole thing, please continue reinventing streaming so people like me don't starve.” Then at StreamTV in Denver I met Sam Harowitz, head of content acquisitions at Tubi, who spoke about the future of content like he was reading prophecy off a stone tablet. EVAN: So we built a fully fleshed-out meta marketing and community strategy. I took it to our friends at Filmhub over a sushi dinner. We partnered with the amazing PR team at FerenComm. Then we went to Tubi, where Sam introduced us to Rich Bloom — who Anjali had just brought on board to build out their creator platform — and where Filmhub already had a strong content partnership. It allowed Tubi to come on board without crafting a one-off deal for a single tiny indie film from smaller creators. Instead, we were able to use existing infrastructure to move quickly and make a deal for an exclusive premiere on the platform. Then go make your stuff, with a budget and timeline that lets you take advantage of the market — before the market changes again. Unfortunately, making films or TV series isn't only about the art. It's not just camera angles and narrative structure. Filmmakers need to be strategists, marketers, press agents, and dealmakers. I know that sounds daunting, but when artists think beyond the art and focus on the business part of show business, they can make things happen. JAMIE: That's been the most surprising part of this process. Just: yes, let's do it, and here's how. EVAN: But that's because we came in with everything already figured out. We made it easy for them to say yes. As Rich Bloom said on our podcast: Yes, it was a really good film; we had the right expectations; the project perfectly matched their strategy; we came to the table with an existing partner; and we arrived with a fully fleshed-out marketing plan. That's how we got a worldwide, day-and-date premiere for a $65,000 film shot in eight days, one year after we wrapped. But the reality is that by understanding our audience — and the needs of potential buyers — we found genuinely good partners who believe indie doesn't mean insignificant. EVAN: So what do you think this experiment has taught us? JAMIE: “Skit” taught me that the old-school indie philosophy of “just start making stuff” isn't quaint nostalgia. I've learned that a young artist who wants to work needs to greenlight themself — make the thing in a way they can actually afford. Then we're releasing it on YouTube — an idea I would have rejected out of hand before this experience. Young filmmakers still cling to traditional distribution — festivals, theaters, gatekeepers — because of a romantic Hollywood fantasy we were all raised on. Film schools still preach it, partly because the people teaching haven't made a movie since dial-up internet, and partly because it's easier to teach submission than subversion. I loved making “Skit” with you because it's timely and fresh — but also because it's a real case study for the next generation of indie filmmakers. Making “Skit” didn't just give me a film. There's more plot than that, too, some of which more closely rehashes material from the first two films—Water in particular, to the point where it's easy to wonder whether some sequel material was dragged and dropped from the second movie into the third during their lengthy gestation period. As Fire And Ash goes on, it feels increasingly unsure of just how much of a wrap-up this will be—or even what's on the table for story threads that can be wrapped up, versus what will be left as the kind of dangling (or are they frayed?) threads that, until the Avatar sequels, rarely had a place in Cameron's fantastical worlds.
