The dynamic vision sensor captures visual information as discrete events, enabling high-speed imaging with reduced data redundancy, but is limited by lack of color sensitivity, a speed-noise trade-off, and inefficient data transfer. Here we show an amphibian-inspired dynamic vision system (ADVS) based on ferroelectric field-effect transistors that emulates the hierarchical functions of amphibian retinas, including spectral perception, spatial preprocessing, and event-driven neural encoding. Heavy metal contamination in aquatic systems poses a significant and growing environmental challenge globally, threatening both ecosystem integrity and public health. Unlike organic pollutants, heavy metals are non-biodegradable and can persist in the environment. Consequently, these persistent contaminants accumulate in various environmental compartments, including sediments, the water column, and within living organisms through processes of bioaccumulation and biomagnification across trophic levels. The enduring presence and toxicity of heavy metals in water sources are serious concerns for environmental monitoring initiatives1,2. Anthropogenic sources are the main contributors to heavy metals contamination, primarily responsible for the contemporary influx of heavy metals into aquatic environments. Industrial discharge is the leading contributor, releasing toxic metals like Lead (Pb), Copper (Cu), Arsenic (As), Cadmium (Cd), Chromium (Cr), Nickel (Ni), and Mercury (Hg), from processes such as mining, Coal combustion, smelting, metal plating, and chemical manufacturing3,4. Urban and municipal runoff also contributes heavy metals from sources such as vehicle emissions, sewage, landfill leachates, and aging infrastructure. Furthermore, agricultural practices introduce metals such as Cd and As through phosphate fertilizers and pesticides, which can subsequently contaminate groundwater5,6. Natural processes establish a background level of contamination, primarily through the weathering of metal-containing rocks and volcanic eruptions7. Even at trace concentrations, heavy metals are harmful because of their reactivity, mainly disrupting cellular function by binding to essential biological molecules such as enzymes and proteins. This interference frequently triggers the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), which induce oxidative stress and subsequent cellular damage8. A critical concern is the strong potential for these metals to bioaccumulate and biomagnify within the aquatic food chain, ultimately leading to human exposure. This exposure, particularly through contaminated drinking water and seafood consumption, is significantly correlated with severe health consequences9. Adverse health impacts span a wide spectrum, including kidney dysfunction, neurological disorders, cardiovascular issues, and multiple types of cancer8,9. The World Health Organization (WHO) highlights that environmental determinants, particularly water contamination, substantially contribute to the global disease burden10. In response to the serious public health risks posed by heavy metal contamination, various international and national regulatory agencies have established enforceable drinking water quality standards11. These guidelines are fundamental for the protection of public water supplies and for informing remediation Actions. Among these, the Guidelines for drinking-water quality established by WHO serve as a scientific foundation for countries worldwide to formulate their national standards12. The Environmental Protection Agency in the United States (US EPA) is tasked with defining Maximum Contaminant Levels (MCLs) under the Safe Drinking Water Act. These MCLs are legally enforced standards that pertain to public water systems, set based on assessments of health risks, technological feasibility, and cost-benefit evaluations. Similarly, the European Union has enacted stringent standards under the Drinking Water Directive (EU DWD), which specifies parametric values for a range of contaminants, including heavy metals, with which all member countries must comply12 The MCLs for various heavy metals, as specified by the WHO, EU DWD, and US EPA, are presented in Table 1. Currently, gold standard analytical techniques for detecting trace heavy metals in water are advanced spectroscopic techniques, particularly atomic absorption spectrometry (AAS), inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (ICP-OES), and inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS)9. These techniques provide superior analytical performance, especially regarding sensitivity and simultaneous multi-element analysis. ICP-MS stands out by providing ultra-trace detection limits, often at parts-per-trillion levels, rendering it the benchmark for research and regulatory compliance13. While AAS remains a cost-effective alternative for analyzing single elements, ICP-OES is recognized for its high throughput and multi-element analysis capabilities14,15. Despite their effectiveness, these traditional techniques possess considerable limitations that restrict their usage, particularly in resource-limited situations and for on-site assessments16. Both ICP-OES and ICP-MS instruments require considerable initial investment, high operational costs (such as argon gas), costly consumables, and specialized laboratory setups17,18. Furthermore, the operation and maintenance of these systems require well-trained technical personnel, and the complex sample preparation steps are both time-consuming and vulnerable to contamination19,20. The large and delicate nature of these instruments renders them non-portable, necessitating that samples be collected and transported to a centralized laboratory, which can lead to delays and potential degradation16. Additionally, ICP techniques can be affected by spectral and matrix interferences, whereas AAS demands individual element lamps and specialized accessories for volatile metals such as mercury and arsenic. Moreover, these routine techniques usually report only total elemental concentrations, failing to differentiate oxidation states (e.g., Cr(VI) and Cr(III)), which is a key factor in toxicity evaluation9,21. These constraints highlight the demand for a portable, low-cost analytical system that combines selectivity, sensitivity, and the ability to deliver immediate results. Modern voltammetric approaches are tailored to close this gap, with methods already utilized by the American Public Health Association (APHA method 3130B) for the detection of Zn, Cd, and Pb. Voltammetry is a dynamic electroanalytical technique that applies a time-dependent potential to a working electrode and measures the resulting current, which is proportional to the analyte concentration. This fundamental principle lies in examining the current–potential relationship to extract information about the analyte's electrochemical behavior at the electrode–electrolyte interface22. The distinct interplay between the applied potential and the resulting current generates an electrochemical profile that is characteristic of each analyte, providing valuable insights into its redox behavior, where the peak potential identifies the analyte (qualitative information), and the peak current quantifies its concentration (quantitative information)23. A voltammetric measurement typically involves a three-electrode configuration, consisting of a working electrode (W.E), a counter electrode (C.E), and a reference electrode (R.E)23 as illustrated in Fig. The W.E facilitates the redox process and is typically fabricated from materials such as platinum, glassy carbon, carbon paste electrode, gold, or mercury, selected according to the analyte and potential range considerations24. These innovative materials leverage their unique electronic and structural properties to achieve improved selectivity and ultra-low detection limits, significantly outperforming traditional bare or basic film-coated electrodes. Voltammetry is an electroanalytical method that quantifies the current generated at a working electrode as a function of the applied potential. The principle fundamentally relies on the controlled application of a potential waveform to facilitate a redox reaction at the electrode-solution interface. The resulting current provides valuable quantitative and qualitative information regarding the analyte22. In the process of heavy metal detection, metal ions (Mn+) are typically reduced from their solution phase to an elemental state (M0) on the electrode surface during a deposition phase, followed by the reverse oxidation (stripping) phase28. Mass transport of analytes to the electrode surface is governed by three primary mechanisms: migration (movement driven by the electric field), convection (movement resulting from stirring), and diffusion (movement resulting from concentration differences)29. For Successful electrochemical detection, a rapid and efficient electron transfer process is critical, which is greatly influenced by the working electrode material, its surface area, and its interaction with the analyte. In voltammetry, the measured current responds proportionally to the concentration of electroactive analyte, making it highly sensitive for trace-level measurements. The Selection of an appropriate voltammetric methodology is critical for enhancing both analytical selectivity and sensitivity during the experimental measurements. Technique selection is primarily determined by the targeted sensitivity threshold, analyte chemistry, and sample matrix complexity. LSV is a fundamental voltammetric technique in which a linear potential ramp is applied from an initial to a final potential while the resulting current is continuously recorded. It is particularly valuable for elucidating electrode mechanisms (e.g., probing electron-transfer kinetics) and serves as the reference waveform for stripping analyses, where a linear potential sweep is applied during the stripping step, typically without pulsed features. However, LSV is rarely selected for trace heavy metal quantification because the large background charging current results in comparatively high detection limits, typically above 100 µg/L. Consequently, for routine analytical applications that require sensitivity below 10 µg/L, DPV or SWV are generally preferred30,31. Even though CV is typically hindered for ultra-trace measurement of heavy metals owing to considerable non-Faradaic capacitive charging currents, it remains essential for elucidating mechanistic details of the electrode/electrolyte interface32,33. This method involves linear ramping of the potential of the working electrode in a triangular waveform between defined switching limits, including the analyte to undergo forward reduction and reverse oxidation processes, while the resultant current generates a diagnostic hysteresis loop. Within the realm of heavy metal sensor development, CV is utilized as a primary diagnostic method to investigate the redox behavior of target ions (for example, the reversibility of the \({M}^{n+}/{M}^{0}\)) and to precisely define the electrochemical potential range necessary for subsequent stripping steps. However, CV is not suitable for trace metal quantification in environmental samples due to poor signal-to-noise ratio at low concentrations (typically > 100 µg/L). Use CV only for diagnostic and optimization purposes; switch to stripping voltammetry or pulsed techniques for actual sample analysis. Additionally, CV is fundamental in characterizing the kinetic behavior of modified electrodes, as assessing and analyzing the relationship between peak current and scan rate facilitates the differentiation between diffusion-controlled processes and surface-confined sorption processes34,35. Such characterization is crucial for validating the enhancement of both electron transfer kinetics and active surface area attributed to advanced nanocomposite modifiers. ASV is the method of choice when the analytical goal is ultra-trace quantification (sub-µg/L to low µg/L) of metals that can be reductively deposited as amalgams or metallic films—notably Pb (II), Cd (II), Cu (II), Zn (II), and Hg (II). It is ideal for drinking water compliance monitoring, natural water surveys, and any application where detection limits below 10 µg/L are required. However, ASV is not suitable for metals that do not form amalgams (e.g., Fe, Ni, Co, As) unless they are converted to a detectable form. Intermetallic interferences (e.g., Cu–Zn) can occur in complex mixtures; if such interferences are suspected, consider using pulsed stripping variants (DPASV or SWASV) with shorter deposition times or chemical masking31,36. The procedure consists of two key steps: (i) deposition (preconcentration): Mn+ are reduced and deposited onto the W.E at a constant negative potential for a specified duration, thereby concentrating the analyte. (ii) Stripping: a linear or pulsed scan of the potential toward the positive (anodic) direction, prompting the re-oxidization of the deposited metal, which is then stripped back into the solution31. Hg, Bi, Au film electrodes are widely employed in ASV for the determination of trace metal species in natural waters, with frequent application to seawater samples37. For instance, voltammetric in situ profiling systems for online trace metal measurement employ a mercury-deposited iridium microelectrode that is further shielded by a gel layer38. An Au-film-modified glassy carbon working electrode was used for copper speciation in seawater via ASV. This approach quantified electroactive, acid-dissolved, inert, and totally dissolved copper, yielding results comparable to those from ICP-MS39. For a metal to be measurable by ASV, it must be reducible, capable of accumulating at the electrode surface, and subsequently re-oxidizable under defined potential conditions. Therefore, the technique is not universally suitable for all trace metals, and elements such as Fe, Ni, and Co typically require other voltammetric techniques for accurate quantification30. CSV is employed for the determination of analytes that, during the preconcentration step, form insoluble salt or strongly adsorbed complexes with the electrode surface or species in solution. In contrast to ASV, preconcentration occurs at positive potential through anodic film formation or adsorption of a surface layer, whereas the stripping phase necessitates a negative scan in which the deposited species is cathodically reduced and stripped from the electrode30. This approach is particularly suited for the analysis of species including sulfides, halides, and selected Mn+, including As (III) and, under appropriate conditions, Hg (II), often in the form of surface complexes or sparingly soluble salts40. For example, gold wire was used as a W.E for As(III) detection by CSV on various samples of groundwater, achieving a LOD (3 × standard deviation of multiple successive determinations (σ) method) of 0.037 μg/L examined in simulated deionized water with real samples validated against ICP-MS; however, the analytical response was adversely affected by the presence of chloride and humic acid41. In addition, CSV is the preferred technique when the analyte does not form a reversible amalgam but can be oxidatively deposited as an insoluble salt or a strong complex on the electrode surface. Typical targets include As (III), Se (IV), halides, and certain sulfur-containing species. CSV is also uniquely powerful for speciation, distinguishing between oxidation states such as As(III)/As(V) or Cr(III)/Cr(VI) by carefully selecting the deposition potential or using selective complexing ligands. Use CSV when speciation is critical for toxicity assessment, but be aware that organic matter and chloride can strongly suppress signals; sample pretreatment or standard addition is often necessary41,42. SWV and DPV, which are based on pulsed potential modes, achieve sensitivity levels superior to those of linear scan techniques, through the effective suppression of the non-faradaic charging current43. In both approaches, the applied potential takes the form of a staircase ramp onto which a series of small potential pulses is superimposed43. The current is determined immediately prior to the application of each pulse and again at the end of the pulse, after which the difference ΔI is graphed relative to the base potential. In practice, SWV is generally chosen due to its higher sensitivity and faster scan rates, as it records both forward and reverse currents during each pulse, thereby enhancing the faradaic component44. These approaches are frequently combined with a subsequent stripping step (e.g., SWASV) to achieve ultra-trace detection thresholds. For instance, a nanocomposite of SnO₂ combined with reduced graphene oxide (GO) has been used for the quantification of Pb(II), Cd(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II), offering very low detection limits. In deionized spiked water, the detection limit evaluated by the 3σ method does not exceed 5.60 × 10−2 μg/L for these metal ions45. Both techniques are excellent for simultaneous multi-metal detection and for samples with overlapping stripping peaks. Choose DPV when peak resolution is the highest priority (e.g., Pb2⁺ and Sn2⁺ at low concentrations). Choose SWV when speed and sensitivity are more important—SWV scans up to 100 Hz, completing an analysis in seconds rather than minutes, and it is less affected by dissolved oxygen, making it ideal for field-deployable sensors. For ultra-trace detection, combine either technique with a stripping step (SWASV or DPASV). In complex matrices rich in organic foulants, use short deposition times (30–60 s) with SWV to minimize electrode fouling31,46,47. The overall analytical response of a voltammetric sensor is critically influenced by a combination of operational conditions and material-related characteristics of the sensing interface. Effective tuning of these parameters is fundamental to maximizing signal-to-noise ratio, controlling matrix-related interferences, and attaining the lowest practicable detection limits for the heavy metal analytes. Each of the key parameters governing voltammetric performance can be described in detail, and their respective roles can be outlined for applications in trace heavy metal analysis. The deposition potential corresponds to the fixed potential used during the preconcentration stage; it must be adequately negative in ASV to drive efficient electroreduction and immobilization of the metal ions on the electrode surface48. For example, nano-cellulose modified electrodes were used for the detection of Cd(II), Cu(II), Pb(II), and Hg(II), where an optimum deposition potential of −0.90 V was selected to ensure efficient preconcentration of all metal ions48. The deposition time, which specifies how long preconcentration is maintained, is directly proportional to the analyte loading on the electrode and therefore improves sensitivity with reduced detection limits; although excessive deposition periods lengthen the analysis and can induce electrode fouling49. The scan rate represents the rate of potential change in the stripping phase and influences both peak height and resolution, as faster scans usually yield larger peak currents yet tend to broaden peaks and diminish the separation between closely spaced features50. For example, in this study a mercury working electrode was employed for the simultaneous determination of Tl(I), Pb(II) and Cu(II), and it was shown that increasing the scan rate in DPV enhanced the peak current but led to peak broadening and poorer separation of the metal signals; thus an intermediate scan rate of 10 mV/s was chosen to balance sensitivity and resolution50. The pulse amplitude, which denotes the height of the potential steps in DPV or SWV, boosts faradaic current and analytical sensitivity as it is raised, but at the cost of a higher background signal and potential peak shape distortion51. Finally, the electrode material, including both its surface morphology and chemical composition, plays a central role in governing selectivity, controlling overpotential, and facilitating efficient preconcentration through adsorption or complexation, thereby strongly affecting the sensor's sensitivity and robustness52,53. Traditional electrode materials have historically underpinned electrochemical strategies for the detection and removal of heavy metals in water treatment applications. Within analytical chemistry, their use has been especially prominent in stripping voltammetry, where their characteristic physicochemical behavior facilitates highly sensitive and selective measurement of toxic metal ions. Although recent research increasingly prioritizes alternative electrode materials in response to toxicity and cost issues, a clear understanding of the performance and constraints of these conventional systems remains essential for interpreting contemporary advances. Mercury-based electrodes have been widely valued for their elevated hydrogen overpotential and their renewable, smooth surface, attributes that render them especially well-suited to ASV54. Mercury-based electrodes, including the hanging mercury drop electrode (HMDE) and the dropping mercury electrode (DME), have long been recognized for their outstanding analytical performance. Their capacity to form amalgams with a wide range of metal ions enables preconcentration of the analyte before the stripping stage, thereby achieving very low detection limits. Historically, the bubbling mercury electrode illustrated this advantage by enabling the determination of zinc, cadmium, lead, and copper in natural and wastewater samples, where a clear proportionality between analyte concentration and peak current was established54. Additionally, mercury electrodes face several limitations, including contamination in complex sample matrices, time-consuming cleaning procedures, and poor long-term stability. Consequently, research into sustainable alternatives has accelerated. Mercury-free materials aim to match or exceed the analytical performance of mercury-based electrodes while reducing both environmental and health hazards55. Despite the advantageous physicochemical features of mercury and its broad use in trace-level heavy metal analysis, both the dropping mercury and HMDEs exhibit insufficient mechanical stability for automated applications, since their droplets can detach easily. The substantial consumption of elemental mercury in these systems also contradicts current efforts toward sustainable analytical chemistry, where carbon-based and surface-modified materials are favored to improve analytical sensitivity56,57. A study focusing on a rice agroecosystem employed HMDE for the simultaneous determination of trace metals and reported highly favorable analytical figures of merit. The simultaneous determination of Zn(II), Cd(II), and Pb(II) was performed with a HMDE in 0.1 M sodium acetate buffer (pH 4.5). For each metal, the anodic stripping peak heights varied linearly over the concentration range 20-100 µg/L. The limits of detection were 4 µg/L for Cd, 10 µg/L for Pb, and 10 µg/L for Zn. Collectively, these data demonstrate the electrode's robustness for multi-element analysis at trace levels pertinent to water quality assessment58. One reported approach utilized a thin-film mercury electrode (TFME) formed on carbon tips to simultaneously measure Cd(II), Cu(II), and Pb(II). This electrode architecture not only enabled multi-metal analysis but also improved sensitivity and achieved lower limits of detection compared to unmodified electrodes, while the experimental results were accurately captured by an artificial neural network model \(\left({R}^{2},|,0.9933\right)\)59. One critical investigation of mercury-film-modified commercial screen-printed electrodes (SPEs) for Pb(II) sensing reported a satisfactory LOD of 8.9 µg/L in ultrapure spiked water using ASV, but also highlighted problems associated with non-uniform mercury deposition on the rough electrode substrate60. Furthermore, the use of TFMEs, produced by electrodeposition on solid supports such as glassy carbon, represents one method for trace metal analysis. A study utilized low-current stripping chronopotentiometry (SCP) to quantify Fe(III) in filtered coastal and estuarine waters61. Despite its high sensitivity, the approach required multiple pre-treatment procedures, notably filtration and complexation with solochrom violet. The same electrochemical setup was also effective for detecting Sb(III) at ultra-trace levels in ultrapure simulated water, achieving a detection limit of 0.008 µg/L (3σ method), and was successfully applied to seawater samples using the standard addition method. Furthermore, the implementation of a double electrolysis potential during the preconcentration step effectively reduced interference from copper62. According to a study63, the mercury film electrode exhibited inadequate sensitivity and reproducibility when applied to Se(IV) analysis. The inclusion of Cu(II) ions in the plating process on the GC electrode effectively resolved this limitation. The dissolved metallic copper in the Hg film was identified as the key factor responsible for current peak intensification. The optimized method yielded a LOD of 0.06 µg/L in deionized water/0.1 M HNO3 (signal-to-noise (S/N) ratio of 2) and a relative standard deviation of 5.2% (n = 5) for 1 µM Se(IV) determined by CSV63. Another study developed a potentiometric stripping analysis technique enabling the concurrent quantification of Cu(II), Pb(II), and Cd(II) in seawater samples64. In this method, a mercury film was electrodeposited directly onto a rotating glassy carbon electrode before each measurement by applying a potential of −1.1 V vs SCE for 10 min. Following the deposition, an electrolysis-stripping sequence was executed. The resulting metal concentrations were benchmarked against a mixed standard solution containing the same analytes. The method achieved detection limits (2σ method) of 0.044 µg/L for Cu(II), 0.003 µg/L for Pb(II), and 0.001 µg/L for Cd(II), all evaluated from a natural seawater sample, although the total analysis time was relatively long at about 75 min. Despite their exceptional and renewable atomically smooth surface that provides strong selectivity, the performance profile of mercury electrodes collapses under the criteria of operational durability and translational feasibility. Their mechanical instability prevents automation, while time-consuming cleaning and matrix contamination further limit practical use; moreover, long-term stability and reproducibility are compromised by non-uniform film depositions on solid substrates55,56,62,65,66. Critically, their intrinsic toxicity necessitates stringent handling, storage, and disposal protocols, increasing operational cost and precluding field-deployable, single-use sensing formats. Collectively, these limitations have driven a decisive shift away from this class, driving a transition to solid-state electrodes. Carbon electrodes represent a fundamental class of materials in electrochemical sensor technology for heavy metal detection, providing an advantageous combination of electrochemical efficiency, adaptability, and environmental sustainability. These electrodes are the most versatile and widely adopted solid-state platforms for heavy metal voltammetry, yet their analytical performance cannot be generalized; it is fundamentally dictated by the choice of carbon substrate, the strategy used to modify its interface, and the electrochemical protocol employed. Their remarkable characteristics-high electrical conductivity, substantial specific surface area, and modifiable surface chemistry-make them particularly effective for the sensitive and selective quantification of toxic metal ions such as Pb(II), Cd(II), and Hg(II) in environmental samples67. Typically, the detection process entails an initial enrichment of metal ions on the electrode surface driven by adsorption or electrostatic forces, succeeded by ASV measurement that generates distinct current signals correlated with analyte concentration68. This strategy overcomes key drawbacks of traditional spectroscopic methods, which, although highly accurate, depend on costly instruments, laborious sample pretreatment, and are generally unsuitable for in situ applications69. The raw analytical performance is attributable to the modifier, and advances in nanomaterial science have markedly improved the analytical capabilities of carbon-based electrodes, with graphene and its derivatives, carbon nanotubes (CNTs), and emerging nanocomposites being widely investigated as electrode modifiers70. For example, a biosensor based on GO composite onto screen-printed carbon electrodes (SPCEs) was reported for heavy metal detection. The LOD (Inhibitory Concentration (IC15)) for Hg2⁺, Pb2⁺, and Cd2⁺ were 0.0075 µg L−1, 0.015 µg L−1, and 0.0312 µg L−1, respectively, determined in buffered measurement solution71. Similarly, functionalized multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs) have enabled Pb(II) detection with a LOD of 2.1 µg/L in simulated double distilled water68. Furthermore, advanced sensor platforms that integrate artificial intelligence with carbon nanomaterial-based electrodes have demonstrated outstanding performance, enabling the concurrent determination of several metal ions with limits of detection of 1.6 µg/L for Pb(II) and 1.9 µg/L for Hg(II) using the 3σ method69. SPCEs and carbon paste electrodes (CPEs) have attracted considerable interest due to their suitability for portable, field-deployable sensing platforms. Somerset and co-workers fabricated conducting polymer-coated SPCEs and modified CPEs for the electrochemical determination of Hg(II), Pb(II), Ni(II), and Cd(II). The SPCE modified with a polyaniline-poly(2,2'-dithiodianiline) (PANI-PDTDA) film exhibited an exceptionally low LOD of 4.41 × 10−5 µg/L in laboratory model solutions (calculated using the 3σ method), with linearity observed across the concentration range of 0.20–200 µg/L72. Sensitivity and LOD are thus governed not by the substrate itself, but by interfacial design and the reproducibility of the modification step. Selectivity in carbon-based sensors is not intrinsic to the substrate but is conferred by the modifier's chemical functionality, typically through coordination-based analyte chelation or electrostatic exclusion of matrix interferents. For instance, a sulfur-doped graphitic carbon nitride with sulfur-doped carbon quantum dots (QDs) nanocomposite modified SPCE demonstrated excellent sensitivity for Hg(II), Cu(II), and Pb(II). The sensor exhibited wide linear ranges of approximately 1.50–300 µg/L for Hg(II), 16–600 µg/L for Cu(II), and 26–1100 µg/L for Pb(II), with corresponding limits of detection of 0.56 µg/L, 6.0 µg/L, and 10.0 µg/L (S/N = 3), respectively, measured in spiked ultrapure water73. This performance is fundamentally governed by the modifier's chemistry: the sulfur-doped nanocomposite provides high-affinity coordination sites that promote preconcentration and selective stripping analysis, while the carbon QD expands the active surface area and accelerates charge transfer. Practical applicability was demonstrated through good recoveries (95.3–106.4%) in tap water and juice samples. It should be noted that the robustness of this selectivity in unspiked environmental matrices containing humic substances, colloidal particles, and high levels of competing ions was not investigated in the study, leaving the sensor's performance under such field-relevant conditions to be established in future work. From another perspective, the use of a porous carbon-fiber microelectrode coated with gold nanoparticles (Au NPs) enabled the parallel quantification of Cd(II), Pb(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II). The LOD were determined in spiked deionized water to be 127 µg/L, 294 µg/L, 61.4 µg/L, and 148 µg/L for Cd(II), Pb(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II), respectively, each within its own linear concentration range (1124–12364 µg/L for Cd(II), 2072–22792 µg/L for Pb(II), 636–6991 µg/L for Cu(II), and 2006–22066 µg/L for Hg(II)). No interference between the metals was observed, and the method was further validated for simultaneous determination in lake water samples using the standard addition approach74. This study shows that modest \({\rm{LOD}}\) values can be offset by real-sample validation using standard addition, which substantially strengthens confidence in sensor performance and should be benchmarked across material classes. GCEs are extensively employed as a basic working electrode for electrochemical analysis of heavy metal ions in wastewater, attributed to their excellent conductivity, wide operational potential range, minimal background current, and robust chemical inertness75. In trace-level determinations, the electrode's performance is generally optimized by surface modification with carefully selected materials to boost sensitivity and selectivity, frequently employing nanomaterials like graphene, metallic nanoparticles, and polymeric phases, or constructing composite films that expand the effective surface area and furnish specific binding domains for the analytes of interest76. The versatility of the GCE enables a wide range of functionalization approaches, establishing it as a fundamental platform in designing sensors for the determination of pollutants such as lead, cadmium, mercury, and arsenic in complex environmental matrices77,78. They offer outstanding analytical performance across a broad spectrum of metal ions and can be readily modified; notably, very low LOD values are attainable when a GCE is employed. For instance, one study employed a simple Bi-film coating on the electrode surface, enabling detection limits in double-distilled synthetic water (S/N ratio of 3 method) of approximately 1.07, 0.93, 0.65, and 0.94 µg/L for Zn, Cd, Pb, and Cu, respectively. The analysis of real river samples showed good agreement with AAS results, without compromising accuracy or repeatability79. In one reported Pb(II) determination, a GCE functionalized with a graphene/Nafion composite layer afforded a linear dynamic range from 4.1 to 5000 µg/L, with a detection limit of 1.1 µg/L (3σ method) determined in spiked deionized water using DPV80. The Nafion composites on a GCE use the cation-exchange properties of Nafion to impart anti-interference capabilities and sensitivity in spiked river water. Even higher sensitivity was obtained when GO was covalently immobilized on the GCE, providing linear calibration ranges for Pb(II) and Cd(II) from 2.1 µg/L down to 2.1 × 10⁻⁴ µg/L and from 1.1 µg/L down to 1.1 × 10⁻⁴ µg/L, respectively. The corresponding limits of detection were remarkably low, at 5.18 × 10⁻⁵ µg/L for Pb(II) and 3.15 × 10⁻⁵ µg/L for Cd(II), as determined in spiked ultrapure water81. Among the latest advances is a pyrolytic glassy carbon-graphene composite electrode integrated within a microfluidic system, which attains detection limits of 0.020 µg/L for Pb(II) and 0.100 µg/L for Cd(II) in simulated deionized water (S/N ratio method). This configuration highlights how sensor architecture can be finely optimized to achieve sub-parts-per-trillion sensitivity78. In the cited studies, stability is typically assessed over only a few consecutive cycles or days, with no quantitative data on signal decay over weeks of continuous immersion or repeated use in contaminated matrices. Similarly, the resistance to common fouling agents, such as humic and fulvic acids, is seldom quantified; where real-water validation exists, it often relies on standard addition methods that do not isolate fouling effects from other matrix interferences. This lack of data represents a significant research gap, as it prevents a meaningful comparison with the inherent anti-fouling advantage offered by disposable SPCEs. Furthermore, the scalability of GCE modification processes, often involving manual drop-casting or in-situ electrodeposition, raises concerns about batch-to-batch reproducibility at a manufacturing level, an aspect that must be standardized before GCE-based sensors can transition from laboratory prototypes to commercial products. Here, the carbon sub-classes sharply diverge, defining a clear trade-off in field deployability. SPCEs are inherently mass-producible using thick-film and printing technologies, offering low-cost, disposable platforms ideal for on-site sensing, which eliminates cumulative electrode fouling and cross-contamination concerns. Conversely, GCEs, while offering superior baseline performance due to their robust chemical inertness, are typically non-disposable polishing-dependent electrodes. Their performance for trace metal detection is thus markedly dependent on antifouling resistance and long-term stability of the modifying film, a critical property that is rarely subjected to rigorous, quantitative evaluation in the cited literature, representing a significant research gap. The cost-effectiveness of GCE-based sensors is high per-electrode but low per-measurement if reusability is proven; SPCEs offer the opposite trade-off. In electrochemical analysis of heavy metal ions in water, gold electrodes now represent a key enabling technology, distinguished by their superior conductivity, favorable biocompatibility, and reproducible electrochemical characteristics. These electrodes can be employed in forms varying from simple bare gold to elaborately nanostructured systems, with each design tailored to enhance trace-level detection. Due to the intrinsic interaction of gold with specific metals, particularly mercury and arsenic, highly sensitive direct measurements are possible, and the chemical stability of gold as a support material allows the construction of advanced, functionally diverse sensing interfaces82,83. Modifying gold electrodes with nanostructured materials, such as AuNPs or nanoclusters, leads to a substantial improvement in analytical capability. The increased surface-to-volume ratio and elevated electrocatalytic activity of these nanomaterials generate a higher abundance of active sites that facilitate metal ion accumulation and electron transfer83,84. For example, a gold electrode functionalized with gold nanoclusters (GNPs-Au) has shown remarkable sensitivity for the concurrent determination of lead and cadmium. The sensor provides a broad linear calibration range of 1–250 µg/L and an ultra-low detection limit of 0.001 µg/L for both analytes, measured in spiked ultrapure water, underscoring the effectiveness of nanoscale surface modification. The results show only minor deviations from those obtained by AAS, indicating high accuracy and reliability84. Similarly, nanocomposite strategies employing electrodes modified with graphene QD and gold nanoparticles have demonstrated excellent performance toward other toxic metals, such as mercury, achieving detection limits (S/N ratio method) down to 0.004 µg/L83. In addition, researchers facile sensing platform by electrochemically co-depositing a gold nanofilm onto a screen-printed carbon electrode for the detection of trace mercury (II). The sensor, tuned through response surface methodology, demonstrated a linear response from 1 to 40 µg/L and, after optimization of chloride conditions, achieved a highly sensitive detection limit of 0.16 µg/L (3σ method) in simulated deionized water. It also showed satisfactory performance in real water samples, with results in good agreement with those obtained by ICP-MS85. A recent study described a nanoporous gold (NPG)-modified screen-printed carbon electrode for the concurrent determination of Pb(II) and Cu(II) using ASV. The device achieved linear response intervals of 1–100 µg/L for Pb(II) and 10–100 µg/L for Cu(II), with corresponding detection limits of 0.4 µg/L and 5.4 µg/L (S/N ratio of 3 method) in simulated deionized water, indicating its reliability for monitoring these metals in drinking water. Measurements in real water samples were in good agreement with those obtained by ICP-MS86. Enhancing the performance of gold-based electrochemical platforms, one study described an IoT-integrated sensor that employs gold nanoparticle-functionalized carbon thread electrodes for multiplexed detection of Cd(II), Pb(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II). Using DPV, the sensor achieved LOD (3σ method) of 111 µg/L, 128 µg/L, 87.6 µg/L, and 144 µg/L for Cd(II), Pb(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II), respectively, in simulated deionized water. The approach was validated by examining real lake water samples using the standard addition approach47. A sophisticated study introduced a microfluidic multichannel electrochemical cell engineered for the simultaneous monitoring of multiple metal ions. In this configuration, a bare gold electrode was used to directly sense Hg(II), achieving a detection limit of 0.9 µg/L (3σ method) in spiked ultrapure water, while a gold electrode modified with a thin mercury film was employed for Pb(II), attaining an LOD as low as 0.1 µg/L. Application of this device to industrial effluents and river water demonstrated excellent concordance with ICP-OES measurements87. The primary weakness of gold electrodes is their cost and poor resistance to fouling in complex matrices. This high material cost drives two key design strategies to improve translational feasibility: (1) using gold as a thin-film surface modifier on a low-cost, disposable substrate like an SPCE to reduce the total precious metal usage; and (2) integration into highly controlled microfluidic systems to manage sample matrix complexity and extend sensor life. The scalability of nanostructured gold electrodes via controlled electrodeposition is feasible, but maintaining high batch-to-batch reproducibility of complex nanocomposite interfaces remains a significant challenge. Bismuth-film and antimony-film electrodes represent a direct interface engineering strategy to replace mercury: a non-toxic metal film is plated in-situ or ex-situ onto a carbon substrate. Their performance is a direct consequence of mimicking mercury's ability to form alloys, but with distinct property trade-offs that dictate their optimal protocol selection. Bismuth film electrodes represent a promising advancement in electrochemical methodologies, offering attributes such as simple fabrication, heightened sensitivity, distinct peak resolution, tolerance to dissolved oxygen in aqueous phases, limited toxicity, and operational capability under strong alkaline conditions88,89. The use of bismuth is considered a viable and environmentally friendly alternative to mercury, which has traditionally been utilized in ASV for detecting heavy metals such as Cu(II), Pb(II), Cd(II), and Zn(II). For example, research reported the fabrication of a Nafion/bismuth nanoparticle-modified screen-printed graphene electrode, which they applied to the anodic stripping voltammetric detection of Pb(II) and Cd(II)90. The sensor achieved LOD of 0.28 µg/L for Pb(II) and 40.34 µg/L for Cd(II), confirming its effectiveness for on-site monitoring of environmental samples such as treated wastewater and bay water. The results were also benchmarked against those obtained by AAS, demonstrating good agreement. In a distinct methodological direction, a study reported the fabrication of a bismuth-modified porous graphene heterostructure for the ultrasensitive determination of Cd(II)91. By coupling the bicontinuous porous network of bismuth with the superior electrical conductivity of graphene, the resulting sensor delivered a linear range of 0.11–1.12 µg/L and an exceptionally low detection limit of 0.011 µg/L(S/N ratio of 3 method) for Cd(II) in synthetic ultrapure water, highlighting the efficacy of nanostructured designs91. A further notable trend is the use of biopolymers for constructing eco-friendly sensing platforms. In this context, research developed a screen-printed carbon electrode modified with a bismuth-chitosan nanocomposite for the concurrent quantification of several metal ions92. The presence of chitosan, a biocompatible polymer, significantly enhanced the analytical response. Under optimized conditions, the device achieved LODs (3σ method) of 0.1 µg/L for Zn(II), 0.1 µg/L for Cd(II), and 0.2 µg/L for Pb(II) in synthetic deionized water. Applied to real wastewater collected from a mining operation and benchmarked against ICP-MS, the method yielded LOD of 0.4 µg/L for Cd(II) and 0.3 µg/L for Pb(II). The sensor showed high precision, with relative standard deviations ranging from 1.3% to 5.6%92. Innovation in electrochemical monitoring now extends beyond the composition of the sensing material to include sophisticated electrode design and operational methodology. An anonymous researcher reported the use of a bismuth film-modified glassy carbon rotating disk electrode for concurrent detection of Pb(II) and Cu(II) in Danube River samples using the standard addition method. Optimization of the experimental conditions, particularly the incorporation of EDTA as a complexing ligand and fine-tuning of the rotation speed, enabled the attainment of competitive detection limits (σ method) in synthetic ultrapure water of approximately 4.29 µg/L for Pb(II) and 0.67 µg/L for Cu(II)93. The introduction of antimony film-modified electrodes has marked an important step forward in electrochemical heavy metal monitoring in wastewater, serving as a greener alternative to traditional mercury-based systems. Owing to their capacity to operate under more acidic conditions and their reduced toxicological impact, antimony film electrodes (SbFEs) conform well to the ethos of green analytical chemistry94,95. Typically, these electrodes function by electroplating an antimony film, in situ or ex situ, onto a carbon-based substrate such as a glassy carbon electrode or a screen-printed carbon electrode. After modification, the electrode preconcentrates heavy metal ions via reduction, which are subsequently reoxidized during an ASV scan, giving rise to current intensities proportional to their concentration. The quality of the electroanalytical response is critically influenced by both the type of carbon substrate and the morphology of the antimony film, prompting sustained efforts to design new nanocomposite materials that provide superior sensitivity and long-term stability95,96. A sequential injection-ASV approach employing an in-situ deposited antimony film on a GO-modified screen-printed carbon electrode (SbF-GO-SPCE) exhibited outstanding analytical performance for the concurrent quantification of several heavy metals. Linear calibration ranges of 0.1–1.5 µmol L−1 were established for Cd(II), Pb(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II), with LOD (3σ method) of approximately 6.1 µg/L, 5.4 µg/L, 3.8 µg/L, and 13.2 µg/L in synthetic ultrapure water, respectively, thereby demonstrating the system's enhanced capability for multi-element detection97,98. The electrode was successfully applied to determine these metals in real wastewater samples collected from sewage, fertilizer effluents, and seawater, and the results were compared with those obtained by AAS. A particularly noteworthy approach involved fabricating a reduced GO-antimony nanoparticles (rGO-Sb NPs) nanocomposite on a glassy carbon substrate. This electrochemical sensor capitalized on the excellent charge-transport performance of rGO in combination with the high specific surface area and catalytic efficiency of Sb NPs. The method provided very low detection limits (3σ method) in synthetic purified water: 5.1 µg/L for Cd(II), 3.4 µg/L for Pb(II), 2.2 µg/L for Cu(II), and 0.96 µg/L for Hg(II), indicating a substantial gain in sensitivity, particularly for Pb(II) and Hg(II) determination96. An additional innovative strategy was described that involved the application of two-dimensional (2D) antimonene. A Sb-SPCE was effectively employed for the simultaneous voltammetric determination of Pb(II) and Cd(II) using DPV. The sensor exhibited a linear response for Pb(II) in the range 1.1–128.3 µg/L with LOD (3σ method) of 0.3 µg/L, and for Cd(II) in the range 9.1–132.7 µg/L with a detection limit of 2.7 µg/L in spiked purified water. These results demonstrate the feasibility of using antimony allotropes themselves as the electrode modifier for trace analysis in real matrices such as tap water, providing performance comparable to that of ICP-MS99. This class presents a significant trade-off between analytical merit and operational stability. Their low toxicity, compatibility, and low cost with SPCEs grant them excellent field scalability, deployability, and cost-effectiveness90,92. However, their major analytical limitation is their long-term stability and batch reproducibility. The morphology of the electroplated film is critically dependent on deposition parameters (time, potential, solution composition) and the underlying substrate roughness. This leads to film leaching during stripping cycles and poor sensor-to-sensor reproducibility, a problem well-documented for mercury films that is inherited by these analogous systems. Electroanalytical chemistry has experienced a notable shift regarding the detection of heavy metal ions (HMIs) in water, greatly accelerated by the introduction of advanced nanomaterials. These materials overcome the inherent drawbacks of traditional electrodes, including slow electron transfer rates, small active surface area, and a higher chance of surface fouling. Among the diverse classes of emerging materials, carbon-based nanomaterials, MOF, metal NPs, and conducting polymers have gained remarkable attention due to their outstanding physicochemical properties, including high electrical conductivity, high surface area, chemical stability, and facile surface modification. Graphene, composed of a single atomic layer of \(s{p}^{2}\) bonded carbon, and its derivatives rGO and GO, have emerged as a benchmark material in high-performance electrochemical sensing platforms. The exceptional carrier mobility coupled with extensive theoretical surface area (\(2600\,{{\rm{m}}}^{2}\,{{\rm{g}}}^{-1}\)) of graphene serves as an efficient substrate for the accumulation of metal ions throughout the preconcentration stage of stripping voltammetry101. Recent research underscores the utility of rGO, that restored \(s{p}^{2}\) carbon lattice and promotes conductivity compared to GO. As a case in point, an rGO/Bi nanocomposite electrode enabled trace detection of Pb(II) and Cd(II) in tap water using SWV, affording LOD of 0.46 and 0.66 µg/L in spiked ultrapure water, respectively102. Similarly, the Bi nanoparticle/laser-induced graphene sensor exhibited nearly identical limits of detection (3σ method, 0.5 and 0.8 µg/L) in synthetic deionized water, underscoring that the combined effect of the high-surface-area rGO framework and the mercury-like properties of Bi promotes alloy formation and enhances peak current responses103,104. These two studies exemplify a crucial design principle: the rGO scaffold provides the conductive and high-area platform, but the selectivity and preconcentration efficiency are imparted by the Bi component, which mimics mercury via amalgam formation. The trade-off is that the long-term stability of such composite films under repeated stripping cycles is rarely reported; gradual Bi leaching and rGO restacking can degrade performance over time, a gap that must be addressed for continuous monitoring applications. Furthermore, Ag-functionalized rGO enabled sensitive As(III) detection via DPV, achieving a 0.24 µg/L LOD in spiked purified water using the 3σ method, with performance that showed consistent reproducibility and reliability in real water samples analyzed by the standard addition method105. This real‑sample validation using standard addition strengthens confidence in matrix robustness, though long‑term fouling data in organic‑rich natural waters remain absent. The introduction of ruthenium into the GO framework enabled electrochemical sensing of As(III), Cd(II), Hg(II), and Pb(II) by DPV, with LODs in simulated purified water of 0.17, 0.31, 0.32, and 0.29 µg/L, respectively. This system achieved simultaneous determination of these heavy metals in river and tap water samples, with the results validated by AAS106. The Ru/GO sensor is notable for its multi-element capability and successful cross-validation with AAS, yet the operational stability over 60days showed a marked decline for mercury (retained 73.8%), highlighting that even high-performance graphene-based sensors suffer from gradual signal degradation for certain analytes, a critical consideration for field deployment where recalibration is impractical. Another graphene/dimethylglyoxime/Nafion-modified GCE was employed for the selective determination of Ni(II) ions in water samples via adsorptive stripping voltammetry in the presence of common interfering ions such as Co(II) and Zn(II). It demonstrated good selectivity, achieving a LOD (3σ method) of 1.5 µg/L in spiked ultrapure distilled water, which complies with drinking-water guideline levels107. Selectivity here is achieved via a dual mechanism: dimethylglyoxime provides specific Ni(II) chelation while the Nafion film electrostatically excludes anionic interferents-an elegant example of interfacial engineering that requires precise control over film thickness to maintain reproducibility across electrodes. A QDs/GO composite integrated on a GCE functions as an ASV platform capable of detecting Cd(II) and Pb(II) simultaneously, exploiting abundant oxygen groups and a large surface area to facilitate electrostatic accumulation of the metal species. The homogeneous dispersion of QDs enhances the metal re-oxidation kinetics during the stripping step, resulting in improved analytical performance compared with QDs/GCE and GO/GCE electrodes. This is evidenced by wide detection ranges and limits of detection (3σ method) for Cd(II) of 11.24–11241 μg/L with an LOD of 7.45 μg/L, and for Pb(II) of 20.72–10360 μg/L with an LOD of 1.17 μg/L, along with consistent recoveries in real water samples determined using the standard addition method108. Combining \(0\)D QD with \(2\)D GO can enhance surface area, conductivity, and binding-site density. However, the scalability of such composites remains constrained by the need for precise control over QD size, interfacial coupling, and loading uniformity. Overall, graphene-based electrodes can achieve among the lowest reported LODs, and their selectivity is readily tuned through functionalization. Yet, literature is still dominated by short-term measurements in spiked samples. Quantitative assessments of fouling resistance in humic-acid-rich matrices, long-term (beyond several months) signal stability, and batch-to-batch reproducibility of drop-cast graphene films remain scarce. MWCNTs, and single-walled (SWCNTs) are known for their high aspect ratio, electrical conductivity, surface area, excellent electron transfer, high transduction capacity, and remarkable mechanical strength. In the context of voltammetry, CNTs serve as “molecular wires”, facilitating electron transfer between redox species and the electrode surface109. An illustrative example is the modification of a GCE with an amino clay-based polymer, featuring MWCNTs to boost conductivity for the detection of Cu(II), Hg(II), and Pb(II) via the DPV technique. With the MWCNT network, rapid signal transduction is achieved, enabling excellent sensitivity in complex matrices. Under these conditions, LOD (S/N ratio of 3 method) of 1.0 × 10⁻⁴, 6.0 × 10⁻⁵, and 1.2 × 10⁻⁴ µg/L were obtained, with corresponding linear ranges of 3.18 × 10⁻⁴–6.36 × 101 µg/L, 2.01 × 10⁻⁴–2.01 × 102 µg/L, and 4.14 × 10⁻⁴–2.07 × 102 µg/L for Cu(II), Hg(II), and Pb(II), respectively110. CNTs, acting as conductive conduits within a chelating polymer matrix, enable very low LODs by markedly amplifying the faradaic response; however, such ultra-trace detection is often coupled to relatively narrow linear ranges, a trade-off that must be weighed against the concentration window of the intended application. ZnO NPs decorated with MWCNT electrodes enabled simultaneous and sensitive electrochemical quantification of Cu(II), Pb(II), and Hg(II) in water samples by SWV using the spike recovery method. Linear ranges of 1–800 µg/L, 5–1000 µg/L, and 1–900 µg/L were achieved, with LOD (3σ method) of 0.8, 1.2, and 0.7 ppb for Cu(II), Pb(II), and Hg(II), respectively, determined in simulated deionized water. The electrode demonstrated approximately three to five times the sensitivity compared with the unmodified counterpart, accompanied by sharp, well-resolved peaks and negligible overlap from Co(II), Mn(II), and Fe(III), yielding recoveries ranging from 95% to 102%111. However, the lack of data on signal decay under repeated use or in the presence of organic foulants means that their field-readiness remains only partially established. Furthermore, Zn(II), Cd(II), Pb(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II) were simultaneously detected by SWV using a GCE functionalized with a Fe3O4/fluorinated MWCNT nanocomposite, delivering clear peak profiles with enhanced sensitivity. Optimized electrochemical parameters yielded LOD (3σ method) of 1.57 µg/L for Cd(II), 1.74 µg/L for Pb(II), 0.78 µg/L for Hg(II), 0.78 µg/L for Zn(II), and 0.34 µg/L for Cu(II) in spiked purified water. The sensor remained stable for at least 30 days, a performance attributed to the negatively polarized C–F groups, the conductive architecture, and the synergistic interaction between Fe3O4 and CNTs112. The 30-day stability data represent a valuable reference point for long-term performance reporting and provide a useful template that future studies across different nanomaterial platforms should aim to match or exceed. Compared with graphene, CNT-based sensors can deliver similar levels of signal amplification, yet uniform dispersion and reproducible film morphology are typically more difficult to achieve. As zero-dimensional nanomaterials typically below 10 nm, carbon and graphene QDs are increasingly employed as functional modifiers, owing to their high surface functionality and compatibility with biological environments. QDs have been effectively applied in environmental fields, drug delivery, light-emitting technologies, biosensing, anti-counterfeiting, water monitoring, and food analysis113. Compared with their bulk equivalents, QDs exhibit tunable electronic and optical characteristics controlled through heteroatom doping (N, S, B, P, or Se)114. For example, Heteroatoms N, O, and S-doped carbon QDs were used to identify and quantify copper in water samples113. Furthermore, N-doped graphene QDs showed superior selectivity toward Hg(II) over a broad range of competing metal ions, including Cd(II), Zn(II), Cu(II), Ni(II), Co(II), Ba(II), Mn(II), Pb(II), Cr(III), Al(III), and Fe(III). Under these conditions, a LOD of 10 µg/L for Hg(II) was achieved in synthetic ultrapure water using the S/N ratio of 3 method115. The strong Hg(II) selectivity of the sensor across such a broad ion panel illustrates how heteroatom doping can create favorable coordination sites; however, the LOD of 10 µg/L remains modest compared with gold- or bismuth-based sensors, underscoring that selectivity and sensitivity do not necessarily track and must be balanced according to the intended application. Notably, the literature documents that Graphene QDs doped Au NPs were successfully detected Cu and Hg by ASV, with exceptionally low LODs (S/N ratio method) of 3.2 × 10−3, 4.0 × 10−3 µg/L, respectively116. As a new generation of functional materials, MOFs and COFs have recently been recognized as advanced platforms for voltammetric heavy-metal ion detection, owing to their remarkable architectural diversity and finely adjustable functional sites. MOFs possess crystalline porous frameworks constructed from metal ions or clusters bridged by organic linkers, providing both abundant active sites and a significant internal surface area, facilitating the accumulation of analytes153,154. Likewise, COFs are crystalline porous frameworks formed from light elements (C, H, N, O, B) connected via strong covalent interactions, delivering exceptional chemical durability and well-defined pore size and structural geometry155. A broad range of COF materials, including triazine- and imine-linked architectures, feature an ordered porous network that facilitates selective diffusion of metal ions, while their inherently modular design permits the installation of functional groups (amino, thiol, carboxyl, etc.) to enhance coordination efficiency and selectivity156,157. Consequently, these frameworks serve simultaneously as pre-concentrating adsorbents and as active sensing phases, thereby collectively enhancing voltammetric sensitivity and selectivity153,154. The trend towards “green” analytical chemistry has stimulated the search for eco-friendly materials in electrochemical sensing, focusing on reducing the ecological footprint of traditional electrode manufacturing. The driving force behind this transition is the demand for affordable, renewable, and non-toxic substitutes for noble metals and synthetic carbon nanomaterials. Sustainable electrode materials, particularly those sourced from biomass and agricultural waste, feature a unique combination of structural diversity, natural heteroatom doping, and large surface area, which are essential for selectively and accurately monitoring heavy metal ions. The process of converting lignocellulosic waste into functional carbon frameworks via hydrothermal carbonization, pyrolysis, or chemical activation techniques preserves the intrinsic porous structures and the inclusion of naturally occurring heteroatoms (such as O, N, S, and P). These attributes facilitate the pre-concentration of heavy metal ions via a combination of physical adsorption, surface complexation, and ion exchange processes. In addition, the graphitization, along with oxygen-containing functional groups present in biomass-derived carbon materials, further enhances electron transfer rates and provides a reliable interface for stripping analysis182. The conceptualization of biomass-derived electrodes has transitioned from simple waste valorization to the strategic design of hierarchical pore structures and surface chemistries targeting specific analytes. By leveraging the synergistic advantages of biomass-derived carbons alongside other green modifiers, including non-toxic metal films and natural polymers, researchers are innovating high-performance sensors that compete with traditional platforms in terms of sensitivity and selectivity, supporting both environmental sustainability and circular economy practices183,184. A novel technique has been described for the simultaneous synthesis of nitrogen-doped biochar loaded with RuO2 sourced from cotton husks that is drop-cast on GCE. The analytical performance of methods designed for heavy metal removal is rigorously evaluated through key figures of merit, including LOD, linearity, precision, and recovery. These parameters are essential for validating that a treatment process or monitoring technique is fit for its intended purpose, particularly when dealing with trace concentrations in complex matrices like wastewater. The evaluation of analytical methods for heavy metal determination in wastewater primarily relies on three principal figures of merit: sensitivity, selectivity, and LOD. These indicators are essential for establishing the suitability of a given technique, especially considering stringent water quality regulations that frequently require detection at the \(\mu \text{g}/\text{L}\) concentration range192. Sensitivity, defined as the change in the recorded signal corresponding to a unit change in analyte concentration, shows considerable variation between different detection approaches. As an illustration, electrochemical biosensors may achieve markedly high sensitivity, such as 0.432 mA/mg/L, for a PANI/ZnO/Urease-modified sensor applied to Hg(II) detection193. In contrast, optical techniques typically express sensitivity as a spectral shift. A recently developed microfiber sensor array incorporating an ion-imprinted polymer demonstrated a high sensitivity of 0.01086 nm/nM toward Cr(III), exploiting the evanescent field surrounding the tapered fiber to intensify the analyte-light interaction194. Selectivity, or the ability to detect a target ion in the presence of interfering species, remains one of the greatest challenges in heavy metal analysis due to the complex matrices of real wastewater samples. Researchers have employed various strategies to achieve this. Ion-imprinted polymers offer a powerful solution by creating specific recognition sites for a target ion; a sensor array utilizing this technology was able to distinguish Cr(III) and Ni(II) even when interfering ions were present at a concentration of 1 μM194. LOD is arguably the most important metric for confirming that a method meets regulatory standards, which for many toxic metals are set at or below the μg/L level. The pursuit of lower LODs has led to remarkable innovations. For example, the GDMP-LIBS method achieved LODs of 0.2 μg/L for Cu and 0.6 μg/L for Cr, which are orders of magnitude lower than standard methods and well below the minimum allowable concentrations in water quality standards192. Table 1 reports WHO guideline levels for toxic metals such as Pb(II) (10 µg/L), Cd(II) (3 µg/L), and Hg(II) (6 µg/L). Many of the sensors summarized in Table 2 exhibit LODs below these levels, demonstrating their suitability for regulatory-compliant monitoring. Furthermore, voltammetric methods facilitate intrinsic access to oxidation-state speciation by resolving the distinct redox signals of individual chemical forms under controlled electrochemical conditions. In particular, differential formal potentials, coupled with variations in deposition kinetics at the electrode surface, can be translated into distinct, species-diagnostic responses, thereby allowing concurrent speciation of Cr(III)/Cr(VI), As(III)/As(V), and Hg⁰/Hg(II) within a single analytical protocol. As a representative case, adsorptive stripping voltammetry yields resolved responses for Cr(III) and Cr(VI) in water samples through modulation of the deposition potential or incorporation of oxidation state selective ligands42, and likewise tailoring electrode/electrolyte conditions isolates As(III) from As(V) signals in freshwater samples195. In like manner, mercury speciation can be achieved electrochemically, whereby dissolved Hg(II) is cathodically reduced to Hg⁰ at the electrode and subsequently oxidatively stripped during the voltammetric scan, whereas elemental Hg⁰ (such as gaseous Hg) can be collected on Au-based substrates and then reoxidized to Hg(II) for measurement196,197. The capacity for direct speciation is a defining strength of voltammetric approaches, by comparison, AAS and ICP-MS, which are limited to total metal determinations without valence information and thus require prior fractionation or coupling to chromatographic systems to distinguish different oxidation states30,42. Overall, the intrinsic electrochemical speciation capability (distinguishing Cr(III)/Cr(VI), As(III)/As(V), Hg⁰/Hg(II)) is a major advantage of voltammetry over ICP-MS/AAS, enabling direct multi-species analysis with minimal sample preparation. The validation of voltammetric sensors using real environmental water samples represents a pivotal intermediate stage in advancing these devices from controlled laboratory studies to practical, in situ monitoring tools. Increasingly, researchers seek to evaluate sensor performance in a range of matrices, such as tap water, river water, and industrial effluents, to demonstrate robustness under complex interferences and fluctuating sample conditions. This validation not only verifies key analytical figures of merit, including sensitivity and limits of detection, but also examines operational reliability in the presence of natural organic matter, pH variability, and competing metal ions that typify genuine environmental systems198,199. Extensive studies have employed tap water matrices for validation, primarily addressing issues of drinking water quality and regulatory conformity. One notable case involves a hand-held voltammetric device, the so-called E-Tongue, which is based on SPCEs modified with gold nano stars and was deployed for community-level assessment of Pb(II) contamination in tap water across several towns in Massachusetts. The instrument showed strong practical performance by successfully analyzing 634 household samples, attaining Pb(II) recoveries between 84% and 105% even in copper-rich conditions, with results in good agreement with established AAS and ICP-MS methods198. Similarly, additional studies have verified sensor applicability in tap water, for instance, through an Al₂NiCoO₅ nanoflake-modified electrode capable of detecting several heavy metals concurrently and a conductive polymer sensor modified with bismuth nanoparticles for trace metal assessment; in both cases, the reported recovery percentages were satisfactory, confirming the accuracy of these methods in this widely encountered matrix200,201. The analysis of river water is particularly challenging, as this matrix contains suspended particles, natural organic constituents, and a wide variety of potential interfering species. To address these complexities, voltammetric sensors have undergone comprehensive testing in such environments to demonstrate their feasibility for field use. One notable example is a sophisticated electrochemical platform integrating an oxygen-eliminating electrocatalyst that was validated with samples from Tunisia's Mejerda River for the detection of Hg(II), Pb(II), and As(III), enabling precise on-site analysis without the necessity of purging the samples202. Industrial wastewater represents the most demanding test for any analytical sensor due to its highly complex and often polluted nature, containing high levels of organic and inorganic interferences. Successful validation in this matrix is a testament to a sensor's selectivity and resilience. For example, a sensor based on AuNPs/CPE was applied for the detection of Congo red dye in wastewater samples, with calculated recoveries falling within an acceptable range of 88% to 106%203. The conclusive proof of a voltammetric sensor's analytical competence is demonstrated through its real-sample applications204. This ensures the demonstration of accurate and stable operation within heterogeneous aqueous matrices such as household water, rivers, and industrial effluents, contexts markedly more complex than laboratory solutions205. In such validations, the standard addition method remains the benchmark for addressing unpredictable matrix interferences204. Numerous recent studies corroborate the efficiency of portable voltammetric technologies for rapid, field-level monitoring, yielding data consistent with ICP-MS reference outcomes206. Conducting measurements directly on-site affords key benefits, including minimized contamination and maintaining the native chemical state of analytes207. Recent progress in nanomaterial-based interfacial engineering, in parallel with the refinement and diversification of voltametric protocols, has markedly improved the sensitivity and selectivity of heavy-metal monitoring. Despite these advances, the upscaling of these electrochemical sensors from proof-of-concept in reliable, field-operable analytical instruments, remains obstructed by a series of systemic bottlenecks. Beyond technical considerations, these issues are rooted in the fundamental separation between idealized experimental conditions, and the chemically heterogeneous nature of real-world samples, which weakens analytical precision and long-term robustness of the sensing platforms208. The lack of harmonized procedures for electrode modification and systematic performance assessment across research groups represents a key constraint, making cross-study benchmarking difficult and obstructing scalable deployment of these systems. Although numerous studies report very low LODs and broad linear ranges, the batch-to-batch reproducibility of modified electrodes, especially for manually drop-cast or complex functionalization schemes, remains problematic for mass production209. In addition, long-term sensor performance under dynamic conditions is frequently not rigorously evaluated, with many reported platforms exhibiting rapid deterioration of the analytical signal caused by mechanical degradation of the modifier film or continuous leaching of active constituents during repeated stripping cycles46. The intellectual emphasis in the field is moving from a “performance-centric” approach to a “reliability-centric” evaluation, highlighting the need for in-situ and operando probes of the evolving electrode/electrolyte interfacial processes. Such reorientation is indispensable for probing the pathways of surface fouling and the interfacial dynamics that shape electron-transfer kinetics in challenging analytical matrices. Moreover, the ecological footprint and toxicological implications associated with large-scale production and disposal of nanomaterial-functionalized electrodes highlight the use of circular material frameworks and green chemistry-based synthesis to secure sustainable operation of the technology210. Mitigating these multi-faceted challenges is a prerequisite for advancing autonomously, real-time monitoring platforms that function reliably in heterogeneous environmental and industrial scenarios. Operational stability of the modified electrode interfaces remains a central obstacle for implementing continuous and autonomous monitoring. The time-dependent degradation of sensing performance is associated with both the inherent instability of the modifier layer and the progressive contamination of the electrode surface by matrix constituents. A systematic understanding of these intertwined degradation pathways is essential for translating laboratory-demonstrated sensitivity into robust field-deployable platforms. Regarding the physical integrity of the modifier itself, mechanical stability under hydrodynamic regimes presents a primary challenge, notably during the high-speed agitation commonly employed in stripping voltammetric preconcentration. Furthermore, repeated oxidation–reduction cycles can promote leaching of the active layer, a phenomenon most evident in bismuth and other mercury-free film electrodes, manifesting as a gradual reduction in sensitivity and a systematic shift of peak potentials46. The performance of modified electrodes is also markedly sensitive to environmental fluctuations such as pH and ionic strength, which can compromise both the integrity of the restored sp2 network in carbon-based nanocomposites and the coordination architectures of MOFs, motivating the development of more durable anchoring and coating strategies210. Electrode fouling, the nonspecific adsorption of matrix species, persists as the principal limitation in electrochemical measurements performed on complex environmental and biological systems. The mechanisms by which fouling agents passivate the electrode surface can be systematically categorized. Organic fouling arises from the accumulation of dissolved organic matter, including humic and fulvic acids, proteins, and anthropogenic surfactants211,212. These macromolecules adsorb onto the electrode, physically masking active sites and impeding the diffusion of target metal ions toward the interface. The resulting passivation reduces the faradaic stripping current and simultaneously generates pronounced overpotentials, complicating peak separation in multi-analyte voltammetry. Inorganic fouling involves the precipitation of metal hydroxides or salts, particularly in hardwater samples, forming insulating surface layers that block electron transfer211. Biofouling, the formation of microbial biofilms, represents a severe challenge in long-term in-situ deployments, as the biofilm creates a diffusive barrier with its own local chemical microenvironment, altering both mass transport and the effective redox conditions at the electrode surface213. A systematic study of fouling in seawater demonstrated that surfactants such as sodium dodecyl sulfate, Triton X-100, and dodecylamine cause a concentration-dependent decline in peak height during DPV measurements of Pb(II), Cd(II), and Cu(II), with the magnitude of signal suppression directly correlated with the surfactant's critical micelle concentration212. Similarly, the accumulation of reaction products generated during high-overpotential processes, such as the oxidation of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide, represents an additional pathway for electrode passivation214. The development of effective antifoul interfaces has consequently become a research priority, with strategies broadly classified by their mechanism of protection. Physical barrier approaches utilize size and charge-selective coatings that block macromolecular foulants while permitting the permeation of small metal ions. Silica isoporous membranes, formed by the self-assembly of surfactant-templated mesoporous silica, have been integrated with SPEs to exclude proteins and humic substances based on size exclusion, while maintaining sensitivity for Pb(II) and Cd(II) detection215. Similarly, hydrogel coatings based on cross-linked hydrophilic polymers create a hydrated interfacial layer that resists protein adsorption through entropic and osmotic effects216. Chemical antifouling strategies involve the design of surfaces that actively repel nonspecific adsorption. Zwitterionic polymer films, containing equimolar positive and negative charges, form a tightly bound hydration layer that renders the surface thermodynamically unfavorable for protein and organic matter adhesion. These zwitterionic interfaces have shown promise for maintaining voltammetric performance in biological fluids and wastewater matrices217. A third category, sacrificial or self-cleaning architecture, employs materials that degrade fouling agents or regenerate the active surface under electrochemical control, though this approach adds complexity to the measurement protocol218. A notable illustration of the efficacy of antifouling design is the comparative evaluation of Bi₂WO₆ coupled with rGO vs a g-C3N4 composite. The former experienced a severe reduction in average current density in both human serum and human plasma, highlighting the impact of biofouling on unprotected electrode surfaces. By contrast, the incorporation of g-C3N4 composite yielded a sensor with robust antifouling performance, enabling the determination of Zn, Hg, Pb, and Cd in wastewater at LODs of 0.045, 0.030, 0.019, and 0.09 μg/L, respectively, calculated using the 3σ method in simulated ultrapure water and cross-validated by ICP-MS46. Despite these advances, the long-term operational performance of antifouling coatings under harsh and dynamically fluctuating environment including extremes of temperature, ionic strength, and biological activity, remains insufficiently substantiated, and systematic comparative studies across multiple real-world matrices are notably absent from the current literature. Compounding the challenge of fouling, the complexity of calibration procedures and the intrinsic drift of the voltammetric signal further limit the practical applicability of voltammetric sensing platforms. The sensor output exhibits pronounced sensitivity to environmental variables such as temperature, pH, and ionic strength, which can cause the response to diverge considerably from the original calibration model210. For complex real-world samples, matrix-induced biases typically necessitate the use of standard addition methods, a workflow that is time-demanding and challenging to integrate into automated field measurements212. Consequently, chemometric approaches combined with ML algorithms are being increasingly adopted to manage baseline drift, deconvolute complex multi-analyte signatures, and strengthen predictive modeling in dynamically fluctuating matrices219. While the limitations of the standard addition method are acknowledged, the field has witnessed rapid advancements in data-driven calibration that extend well beyond traditional chemometric approaches. Recent years have seen a paradigm shift toward ML-assisted calibration strategies that address the fundamental constraints of conventional methods, particularly their inability to correct for non-matrix interferences such as intermetallic compound formation, which standard addition cannot resolve53. Advanced machine-learning frameworks, including support vector regression, random forest, XGBoost, and artificial neural networks, are now being integrated with ASV to surmount its limitations. For example, coupling SVR with square-wave ASV enabled precise concurrent detection of Cd (II), Pb (II), Cu (II), and Hg (II); SVR attained area-under-ROC values exceeding 0.95, and XGBoost concentration-prediction models achieved R2 values above 0.95 in complex multi-ion samples220. Similarly, radial basis function artificial neural networks (RBF-ANN) have been successfully applied to resolve strongly overlapping voltammetric peaks of lead and tin at bismuth film electrodes, outperforming conventional chemometric methods, including principal component regression (PCR), partial least squares (PLS), and least-squares support vector machines (LS-SVM)36. Beyond peak deconvolution, ML algorithms are increasingly deployed to compensate for signal drift, correct for electrode fouling, and enable adaptive recalibration under field conditions-capabilities that are essential for autonomous, long-term environmental monitoring221. ML and chemometric approaches have been comprehensively reviewed as powerful tools for signal deconvolution, drift compensation, and adaptive calibration, with particular emphasis on their incorporation into continuous monitoring workflows221. These data-driven calibration strategies not only enhance analytical accuracy in complex matrices but also reduce the frequency of resource-intensive recalibration, supporting the feasibility of decentralized, real-time heavy metal surveillance53. Nevertheless, compromises persist: the superior predictive performance of black-box ML models must be weighed against the mechanistic interpretability needed for regulatory approval, and ensuring transferability across varied environmental matrices and sensor platforms remains a significant unresolved issue. The exceptional selectivity and sensitivity of voltammetric sensors are often evaluated in compositionally well-defined buffer solutions. However, extending this performance to practical samples is frequently hindered by the sample matrix. Untreated environmental samples can degrade electrode performance via electrode fouling, and lead to severe signal suppression. In practice, sample pretreatment can be more than a simple preparatory task, functioning as a critical operation that determines the trustworthiness of quantification. Field-deployable implementations can depend critically on their successful integration. Raw environmental waters pose multiple challenges, notably the strong binding of heavy metal ions to humic and fulvic acids, which produce electro-inactive species that are not captured by the sensor response222. Additionally, the sensitivity of voltammetric detection is strongly pH dependent. Since field samples span a wide pH range, achieving optimal detection demands stringent pH control, a requirement that is difficult under on-site conditions where liquid reagents must be manipulated223. Moreover, suspended solids can physically damage the electrode surface, whereas microbial activity can promote biofouling. In response, the field is moving away from elaborate, confined methods toward small-footprint, automated, reagent-free approaches designed for in situ use. A key approach involves traditional solid-phase extraction that selectively captures heavy metals, effectively separating and pre-concentrating the analyte from the sample matrix224,225. Moreover, instead of relying on added buffers, pH can be modulated electrochemically. An anodically polarized acidifier electrode catalyzes water electrolysis, generating protons at the sensor interface and precisely adjusting the local pH223,226. Adsorptive matrix removal with Amberlite is a fast sample clean-up step where the sample is mixed with the resin to scavenge organic surfactants and humic substances before analysis227. One of the ideal solutions is integrated lab-on-a-chip (LOC) platforms, which allow sample pretreatment to be performed on the same platform as detection. A foldable, paper-based microfluidic design brings together sample collection, pretreatment, and standard addition, automating traditional laboratory workflows in a compact format suitable for on-site heavy metal analysis228,229. Every pretreatment approach designed for field use involves an inherent trade-off. Simple filtration and acidification are appealing from a practical standpoint, yet may not achieve complete liberation of metals or adequate suppression of interferences. The next frontier lies in integrating these pretreatment capabilities directly with electrochemical sensor platforms, enabling a shift from basic measurements in relatively clear waters to resilient, automated monitoring in turbid and organic-rich environments. Advancing voltammetric sensors from proof-of-concept laboratory prototypes to regulatory-ready analytical instruments is critically dependent on achieving high inter-electrode reproducibility and durable long-term operational stability. Although nanomaterial-enhanced sensing platforms typically offer remarkable sensitivity, the intrinsic physicochemical complexity of these materials, including batch-to-batch variations in particle size, defect density, and surface chemistry, poses significant challenges to process standardization230. Manual drop-casting often yields coatings with uneven thickness and non-uniform distribution, resulting in significant sensor-to-sensor differences in electroactive surface area (EASA) and electron-transfer kinetics, highlighting the necessity for precise, scalable fabrication technologies such as inkjet printing, screen-printing, or controlled electrodeposition to ensure adequate analytical reliability for large-scale environmental monitoring231. For instance, the reproducibility assessment of ZIF-8/CNT using five independently prepared electrodes for the detection of Pb(II) yielded relative standard deviations of 8.51%232. A major remaining challenge is operational stability, notably when sensors are exposed to hydrodynamic stress and repeated potential scans typical of stripping voltammetry. A 30-day monitoring of the ZIF-8/CNT electrode stability revealed that the oxidation peak currents of Cd(II) and Pb(II) followed a decreasing trend, pointing to a gradual deterioration in stability232. Moreover, the progressive decline in current sensitivity under repeated measurements limits the practical applicability of the voltammetric sensor. As a representative case, detection of Cu(II) at 2.54 × 10−11 µg/L gave five current responses of 0.059, 0.122, 0.059, 0.121, and 0.097 mA, corresponding to an RSD of 34%233. The broad commercialization and on-site implementation of voltammetric sensors for heavy-metal monitoring remain constrained by the intersection of economic feasibility, scalable manufacturing, and the lack of standardized regulatory provisions. Although laboratory-scale prototypes often exhibit outstanding analytical responses, their translation to high-volume manufacturing frequently stalls in the “Valley of Death”, primarily because the use of expensive nanostructured materials MOFs, COFs, and noble metal nanoparticles such as Au, Pt, and Ag, significantly inflates the cost per analysis234. In response to these cost-related barriers, emerging studies prioritize the use of sustainable, low-cost systems such as biomass-derived carbons and SPCEs, which are well established to be low-cost, facile, and highly reproducible in fabrication, making them ideal for large-scale production; mass production has already made SPCEs inexpensive and maintenance free235. Further, flexible and fully printed low-cost graphene sensors fabricated via additive manufacturing are being investigated as sustainable and scalable alternatives for heavy metal detection236; inkjet-printed sensors in particular offer a mass-automated printing process, uniform deposition, minimal material waste, and scalable manufacturing236. One of the most promising techniques for truly industrial-scale fabrication is roll-to-roll gravure printing, which can produce uniform redox kinetics on flexible substrate rolls of up to 150 m length, enabling large-scale, low-cost production of disposable wearable sensors for heavy metal monitoring237. A formidable challenge, especially during the transition from manual laboratory procedures to automated, industrial-scale processes, where uniformity in nanomaterial attributes, film thickness, and EASA across large batches is required to maintain electrode-to-electrode consistency and analytical reliability208. To this end, advanced manufacturing approaches such as roll-to-roll printing, inkjet printing, and controlled electrodeposition are under active exploration to realize large-scale manufacturing of uniform sensing interfaces. Notably, inkjet printing yields thin, laterally uniform coatings and significantly reduced electrode-to-electrode variability compared to conventional drop-casting, which tends to produce edge-enriched films with pronounced interfacial heterogeneity. Electrochemical performance is governed by the spatial accessibility and distribution of the functional layer rather than by the nominal deposited mass, which positions the deposition technique among the primary design parameters in sensor development238. Nevertheless, integrating these electrochemical platforms within established IoT architectures and microfluidic networks for widespread environmental monitoring calls for additional optimization of energy consumption, data-transfer reliability, and long-term robustness239. Building on recent demonstrations of low-cost transducer platforms, disposable electrochemical paper-based devices can be fabricated for less than 0.20 USD per unit using commodity carbons such as graphite powder combined with simple binders on cellulose substrates, without compromising voltammetric performance240. Paper-based electrochemical chips can be prepared for less than USD1.00 per device, with some designs costing as little as USD0.05241,242,243. These screen-printed paper analytical devices avoid expensive noble-metal inks and sophisticated readout hardware, echoing the broader trend toward minimalist, point-of-care architectures highlighted for MIP-based systems. Similarly, integrating MIPs as synthetic recognition elements on reusable carbon or GCEs can be produced at a total cost of less than USD2.10 per test (including all consumables), while the same glassy carbon electrode can be reused for up to 100 measurements, further lowering the per-test cost when the transducer is cycled244. Even sub-cent sensor costs between 0.01 and 0.09 USD have been reported for paper-based MIP platforms that still operate at femtomolar-level limits of detection, underscoring how mass production and simplified imprinting can drive prices down without sacrificing sensitivity245. These ultra-low-cost designs are particularly attractive for heavy metal sensing in resource-limited or field settings, where single-use formats, ease of storage, and minimal sample preparation are essential. Continued development of low-cost conductive inks, recyclable substrates, and scalable MIP integration strategies (e.g., roll-to-roll imprinting or in situ electropolymerization) is therefore pivotal to translating electrochemical HM sensors from laboratory prototypes into commercially and regulatorily viable environmental monitoring tools246. From a regulatory standpoint, the adoption of voltammetric methods is hindered by the gap between advanced sensor technologies and established environmental standards. Current frameworks, such as those mandated by the EU DWD, and US EPA, still exclusively recognize ‘gold standard' laboratory protocols like ICP and AAS247. The recast EU Drinking Water Directive has introduced new lower thresholds for heavy metal contamination in drinking water (e.g., Pb reduced from 10 µg/L to 5 µg/L by 2036, Cr from 50 µg/L to 25 µg/L), creating an urgent impetus for sensitive in‑situ electrochemical sensors that can meet these stricter limits248. The current lack of harmonized protocols governing calibration, validation, and reporting, regulatory acceptance of electrochemical sensing remains a major barrier. Establishing regulatory readiness will require broad cross-validation efforts with benchmark methods in real-sample matrices, coupled with the introduction of enforceable criteria for sensor performance and reliability249. Commercialization demands a staged (technology readiness level) TRL roadmap: bench-top drop-cast electrodes are re-engineered into screen-printed carbon inks on flexible substrates at TRL 4-5, followed by roll-to-roll gravure production at TRL 6-7 to drive unit costs below USD 0.20 and guarantee batch-to-batch electrochemical uniformity. Initial market entry should target early-adopter sectors, such as mining remediation, industrial discharge monitoring, and resource-limited settings where rapid on-site turnaround outweighs laboratory precision. Finally, integrating the printed sensor with a miniaturized potentiostat, low-power microcontroller, and telemetry module will yield autonomous field-deployable nodes, closing the gap between laboratory prototypes and widespread IoT-enabled environmental monitoring networks205,250,251,252. In summary, despite substantial progress, several critical knowledge gaps continue to hinder the translation of advanced voltammetric sensors into robust field technologies. No universal anti-fouling strategy has yet demonstrated stable, weeks-long performance in untreated natural waters with high organic loads, and protocols for calibration, interference testing, and long-term stability assessment remain poorly harmonized across laboratories, limiting comparability and regulatory acceptance. ML models trained on specific sensor batches or local water matrices frequently fail to generalize to new sites or electrode platforms, underscoring the need for large, open-access voltammetric datasets and domain-adaptive algorithms. In parallel, the synthesis of high-performance materials such as MOFs and COFs is often too complex and costly for large-scale manufacturing. Addressing these challenges will require coordinated, interdisciplinary efforts focused on developing scalable, low-toxicity anti-fouling strategies validated in long-term field trials, establishing community-adopted validation protocols, creating rich open datasets coupled with transferable ML models, and simplifying synthetic routes to enable cost-effective production of next-generation sensing materials. The convergence of advanced electrode materials with microfluidic platforms and ML algorithms represents a paradigm shift in environmental monitoring, enabling the transition from discrete laboratory measurements to continuous, autonomous, and in-situ analysis. Microfluidic devices, often termed LOC systems, provide tight control over very small fluid quantities, supporting automated sample handling, reagent mixing, and embedding of sensors in a compact device, which markedly cuts sample and reagent requirements, decreases contamination likelihood, and enhances analytical throughput253. With accelerating industrial development, the detection and removal of heavy metals from wastewater and various water resources have become a pressing necessity, given the substantial risks they pose to human health and aquatic environments. Despite offering excellent sensitivity and precision, traditional laboratory techniques such as ICP-MS and AAS are not ideal for large-scale, on-site monitoring, primarily due to their high expense, operational complexity, and the requirement for sample collection, transportation, and prior pretreatment52,254. Because of these restrictions, there has been growing interest in portable and on-site voltammetric platforms, which combine device miniaturization, low-cost operation, and accelerated analysis with the capacity to conduct real-time or near-real-time surveillance using minimally processed samples210,254. By incorporating techniques like ASV and SWV, these devices deliver the high analytical sensitivity demanded for trace metal quantification, making them ideal candidates for decentralized environmental monitoring applications52,210. In recent years, work in this field has largely targeted the enhancement of sensor capabilities by integrating advanced nanostructured materials and streamlined electrochemical platforms to resolve challenges such as interference from dissolved oxygen and reliance on time-consuming pretreatment procedures. An illustrative case is an “all-in-one” electrochemical sensor based on a dual SPCE system. Here, one electrode is engineered with an electrocatalytic oxygen-filtering layer made of Au nanofoam combined with Pt nanoparticles, which removes dissolved oxygen in the vicinity of the electrode and therefore obviates the sample purging step. The second working electrode, covered with polypyrrole, Au nanofoam, and WS₂ nanosheets, facilitates simultaneous electrochemical detection of Hg(II), Pb(II), and As(III). The system provided LODs that were below the US EPA guidelines for drinking water, indicating its effectiveness for reliable field analysis in challenging matrices, including river water202. In an alternative strategy for streamlining on-site analyses, investigators introduced a local electrochemical acidification technique for the sample solution. This approach employs a platinum grid acidifier electrode that electrolyzes water to generate protons directly inside the measurement cell, thereby eliminating the requirement for external reagents for pH adjustment. Applied to Pb(II) determination using a gold screen-printed electrode, the system provided a detection limit of 6 μg/L calculated based on the 3σ method in simulated ultrapure water, below the WHO guideline value for lead in drinking water, and exhibited a linear response in the range of 10–100 μg/L223. The technological evolution of these analytical platforms has also resulted in fully automated stand-alone systems tailored for extended or remote operation. An illustrative example is a portable flow system based on ASV, incorporating flow cells, a potentiostat operated via remote control, and automated software that supervises the entire measurement sequence, including sensor cleaning and subsequent regeneration. This approach has been applied successfully to the simultaneous analysis of Cd (II), Pb (II), and Cu (II) in river water, with detection limits in the sub-parts per billion (ppb) domains, underscoring its effectiveness for continuous, online monitoring of natural water bodies254. Further expanding the range of detectable metals, a solid bismuth microelectrode was utilized for the on-site determination of Se (IV) ions. By applying differential pulse CSV, this eco-friendly, miniaturized sensor achieved an exceptionally low detection limit 0.055 μg/L calculated based on the 3σ method in simulated ultrapure water with a short accumulation period, while exhibiting high accuracy and strong anti-interference performance in environmental water samples using the standard addition method255. Likewise, a portable sensing platform based on a disposable screen-printed electrode bearing an in-situ deposited bismuth film was constructed for the determination of Cd(II) and Pb(II). The approach demonstrated excellent analytical performance, providing linear calibration ranges of 20–80 μg/L for Cd(II) and 10–80 μg/L for Pb(II), with LOD of 1.20 μg/L and 0.96 μg/L, respectively256. The integration of microfluidic architectures with LOC platforms and screen-printed sensors marks a significant advancement in strategies for detecting and removing heavy metals from wastewater. Microfluidic platforms overcome the constraints of conventional analytical techniques by miniaturizing and integrating the entire analytical workflow onto a single chip, thereby realizing the concept of a micro total analysis system (μTAS). This downscaling facilitates rapid measurements, minimizes reagent consumption, and supports real-time analyses in on-site settings. Importantly, such devices can be interfaced with highly sensitive detection strategies, among which SPEs have gained prominence as the preferred transducer owing to their low cost, disposability, and suitability for large-scale fabrication257,258. Screen-printed sensors represent a particularly attractive option for electrochemical detection within LOC platforms, providing a means for highly sensitive and selective determination of heavy metals. Their analytical performance can be further improved by surface modification with nanomaterials. As an example, a flexible device based on a SPE modified with MoS₂ NPs exhibited outstanding capabilities for Cu(II) sensing, with a wide linear range of 3.18 × 102–6.35 × 10⁴ µg/L and a low LOD of 1.99 × 10 µg/L259. Similarly, a novel paper-based SPE modified with rGO and a Bi film was developed for the simultaneous detection of lead and cadmium. This sensor exhibited impressive linear ranges of 5–100 µg/L for Pb(II) and 5–80 µg/L for Cd(II), with ultra-low detection limits (S/N = 3) of 0.17 µg/L and 0.32 µg/L, respectively, while requiring only a 200 μL sample volume260. In another application, PbF/SPE for the electrochemical sensing of molybdenum (VI), achieving a remarkably low detection limit of 0.020 µg/L calculated based on the 3σ method in simulated ultrapure water over a linear concentration range of 0.096–4.80 µg/L, which highlights its capability for ultra-trace monitoring in natural waters261. For sample preparation, which plays a pivotal role in achieving accurate detection, microfluidic chips can incorporate functions such as preconcentration. In this context, a LOC device based on a GO-Poly dimethyl siloxane composite was engineered to enable reversible adsorption and preconcentration of lead ions. This configuration improved the sensitivity of subsequent electrochemical measurements using a screen-printed carbon electrode by as much as 30-fold, thereby permitting analysis of samples that had previously fallen below the 0.5 μg/L LOD262. This synergy between the functionalized extraction material and the integrated electrochemical sensor clearly demonstrates the strength of such integrated systems257,263. Integrating ML and data-analytic approaches with voltammetric methods marks a major advancement for the detection and removal of heavy metals from wastewater and potable water treatment facilities. ML frameworks, particularly supervised schemes such as SVMs, artificial neural networks, and convolutional neural networks (CNNs), can be trained on broad datasets to detect weak patterns and correlations that remain undetected by standard analytical tools. The constraints of traditional voltammetric techniques arise mainly from the superposition of voltammetric peaks corresponding to metals having similar oxidation potentials and from the disturbing effects of coexisting inorganic ions or organic materials264. By integrating advanced data analytics, these challenges are addressed via the deconvolution of convoluted signals, the retrieval of key features from whole voltammograms instead of focusing only on peak currents, and the development of predictive modeling frameworks that elevate both sensitivity and selectivity for multi-metal analysis47,219. This synergistic approach not only enhances analytical performance but also facilitates the development of automated, real-time water quality monitoring systems that are crucial for effective heavy metal remediation. The efficacy of ML in voltammetry is fundamentally dictated by the nature of the input data and the architecture of the selected model. Existing studies frequently utilize either extracted features (e.g., peak heights, peak potentials) or complete voltammograms, which typically comprise hundreds of data points representing the entire current-potential curve. While feature extraction reduces computational overhead, training models on full voltammograms allows deep learning architectures to capture subtle, non-linear patterns and matrix-induced baseline variations that traditional peak analysis might overlook265. Supervised learning schemes are predominantly employed to address specific analytical challenges. For example, Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) and RBF-ANNs have been successfully applied for the simultaneous quantification of overlapping signals. A recent study reported ASV method incorporating RBF-ANNs for the simultaneous quantification of lead and tin at a bismuth film electrode, achieving limits of detection (S/N = 3) of 1.13 μg/L for lead and 0.74 μg/L for tin with corresponding linear ranges of 4–150 μg/L and 4–110 μg/L, respectively36. CNNs excel in processing raw spectral data directly. A recent IoT-enabled electrochemical sensor utilized a CNN to analyze DPV signals, enabling the classification of Cd(II), Pb(II), Cu(II), and Hg(II) with an accuracy of 99.99%, and the device achieved a detection limit of (3σ method) of 111 µg/L, 128 µg/L, 87.6 µg/L, and 144 µg/L, respectively47. Furthermore, SVMs have proven effective for complex mixtures; when combined with DPV, SVM algorithms markedly enhanced the coefficient of determination R2, yielding limits of detection of 3040 µg/L for europium, 17,201 µg/L for gadolinium, and 22,652 µg/L for dysprosium266. This demonstrated capacity to resolve convoluted signals from elements with closely related electrochemical behaviors evidences the power of data analytics to broaden the functional scope of voltammetric methods. As water treatment facilities encounter ever more complex contaminant signatures arising from industrial effluents, deploying such advanced analytical techniques becomes crucial for directing appropriate removal strategies and for rigorously confirming treatment efficacy. To progress toward truly field-deployable voltammetric systems, the distinct functional roles of ML must be explicitly delineated and rigorously validated. Peak deconvolution represents a central application, where overlapping signals such as Pb(II)/Sn(II) or Cd(II)/Pb(II) are resolved using advanced architectures like cross-attention feature fusion networks (CAFFN) combined with optimization algorithms, enabling the processing of CV data with coefficients of determination of 0.97 for Cd(II) and 1.00 for Pb(II) while markedly reducing the mean absolute percentage error compared with conventional frameworks219. Baseline correction constitutes a second critical domain, as voltammetric responses frequently exhibit baseline drift due to capacitive currents or matrix effects; here, chemometric strategies such as principal component analysis (PCA) have proven highly effective for baseline correction and noise suppression, exemplified by an arsenic sensor coupling stripping voltammetry with PCA-based analysis to adaptively remove matrix-dependent background contributions and achieve a detection limit of 0.03 μg/L with a sensitivity of 176.34 μA μM−1, surpassing the WHO guideline for arsenic in drinking water267, while more recent automated platforms such as ElectroML leverage PCA–Random Forest (PCA–RF) algorithms to automatically identify and subtract non-Faradaic background currents, which is essential for ultra-trace analysis268. A third functional role is multi-ion quantification, where CNNs and multi-output neural architectures learn direct mappings from full DPV waveforms to concentration vectors, thereby resolving mutual interference among coexisting ions in complex matrices such as industrial wastewater47. Drift compensation forms a fourth pillar, since long-term deployment inevitably induces signal drift through electrode fouling or environmental fluctuations; in this context, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) in combination with online impedance spectroscopy have been explored for drift correction during extended operation269. Finally, field-data interpretation remains a distinct and challenging role, because although on-device IoT integration enables real-time safe/unsafe classification, performance is still highly vulnerable to matrix variability, as demonstrated by a smartphone-based CNN that exhibited excellent metrics under controlled conditions but degraded substantially when environmental conditions changed, underscoring the necessity of robust, context-aware validation strategies270. The shift toward fully sustainable and circular material strategies in the water sector marks a fundamental departure from traditional linear “take-make-dispose” frameworks, moving instead to systems that emphasize resource recovery, waste valorization, and material regeneration. A representative example of this paradigm is the design of an electrochemical sensor incorporating a nanocomposite of green-synthesized nanocellulose and MnO₂ nanoparticles, functionalized with the Schiff base ionophore 5-BHAHS. The sensor enables simultaneous quantification of Hg(II), Cd(II), and Pb(II), achieving detection limits as low as 24.1 µg/L for Hg(II), 13.5 µg/L for Cd(II), and 6.22 µg/L for Pb(II). Its validated performance in real wastewater and tap water, along with pronounced selectivity, emphasizes the suitability of bio-derived materials for constructing sensitive, non-toxic, and mechanically stable analytical systems for water analysis271. At the same time, additional sustainable approaches aim to reduce waste generation and energy demand throughout the sensing operation. A notable example is a novel “all-in-one” electrochemical platform designed to overcome the persistent problem of oxygen interference in ASV, which has conventionally required laborious sample deoxygenation using inert gases. By incorporating some oxygen-removing electrocatalyst directly onto a disposable screen-printed carbon electrode, this configuration eliminates the need for that extra purging step and its associated resource use. The resulting system supports ultrasensitive determination of Hg(II), Pb(II), and As(III) at concentrations below the strict drinking-water limits established by the U.S. EPA, thereby demonstrating its suitability for rapid, on-site analyses without reliance on complex laboratory facilities202. Beyond the mere synthesis of novel materials, a genuinely circular paradigm in sensor design emphasizes the valorization of waste streams as active sensing components. In one pioneering investigation, spent coffee grounds functionalized with FeO were used to modify a glassy carbon electrode for the simultaneous determination of Pb(II) and Cu(II). This strategy not only diverts a ubiquitous organic residue from disposal but also generates a highly efficient sensing interface. The electrode delivered strong analytical figures of merit, exhibiting a linear range for Pb(II) from 1.0 to 800 µg/L with a detection limit of 1.0 µg/L. For Cu(II), the linear range spanned 5.0 to 800 µg/L with a detection limit of 2.4 µg/L using the 3σ method in simulated pure water, confirming that waste-based materials can provide sensitivities comparable to those of conventional sensor platforms272. This concept is further supported by broader reviews, which suggest that steel-based industrial waste represents a sustainable alternative source of iron oxide nanoparticles for electrode modification, contributing to the resolution of concerns about both the reproducibility and the cost of commercially available chemicals273. Voltammetry represents a robust, field-deployable alternative to centralized laboratory techniques for tracing heavy metal quantification, offering a unique combination of high sensitivity, cost-effectiveness, and rapid response. While the rational design of advanced nanomaterials, including MOFs, CNTs, and bio-derived composites, has significantly enhanced analytical performance, several critical challenges remain to be addressed before widespread commercial deployment. A primary bottleneck is the long-term operational stability and fouling resistance of modified electrodes when exposed to complex environmental matrices containing high concentrations of organic matter and competing ions. Furthermore, the transition from laboratory-scale proof of concept studies to robust, decentralized sensor networks requires a more rigorous focus on reproducibility and the standardization of fabrication protocols. Future research efforts should prioritize the development of sustainable, circular material strategies, such as the valorization of agricultural and industrial waste into high-performance sensing interfaces. The integration of artificial intelligence and ML algorithms offers a promising pathway for the deconvolution of overlapping voltammetric signals, enabling the simultaneous and accurate detection of multiple metal species in real-world samples. Additionally, the synergy between microfluidic “lab-on-a-chip” platforms and IoT technology will be instrumental in realizing autonomous, continuous monitoring systems for wastewater surveillance. Bridging the gap between fundamental material innovation and practical engineering solutions is imperative for the mitigation of global water contamination risks. By addressing these technical and operational hurdles, voltammetric sensors can play a pivotal role in the transition toward more resilient and sustainable water management frameworks, ultimately safeguarding both ecosystem health and public safety on a global scale. This review underscores the necessity of an interdisciplinary approach that combines chemistry, material science, and data analytics to overcome the current limitations of electrochemical sensing technologies. The paper reviews research linking regular exercise to a wide range of health benefits, including a lower risk of death, better mental health, stronger cognitive function, and greater resistance to age-related decline. The evidence also suggests that combining aerobic activities such as walking, running, or cycling with resistance training may provide especially powerful benefits. Dr. Macdonald argues that physical activity should be viewed not only as a way to avoid disease, but also as a tool for maintaining strength, mobility, and independence throughout life. The review also takes a closer look at protein recommendations. Current UK guidelines are based primarily on preventing protein deficiency in sedentary adults. However, the paper points to newer research suggesting that physically active people, older adults, and pregnant women may benefit from significantly higher protein intake. The review further highlights evidence that higher-protein diets can support fat loss (due to increased satiety and thermic effect), making them beneficial for body composition as well as overall health. Importantly, the paper notes that higher protein consumption is not limited to meat-based diets. With thoughtful meal planning, plant-based diets can also provide sufficient protein, as demonstrated by the growing number of vegan powerlifters and bodybuilders. Rather than replacing existing recommendations, Dr. Macdonald suggests supplementing them with guidance focused on what he calls "optimal health outcomes." When we see a stereotypical image of a hunched-over slow, fragile person with ill health, in their later years, it seems like an inevitable consequence of "Father Time," however, I propose that in most cases, it is evidence of a non-evidence-based lifestyle.
