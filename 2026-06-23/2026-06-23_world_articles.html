150,000 square feet of barnacles and sea gunk: The latest problem in the Strait of Hormuz
Source: {'href': 'https://www.cnn.com', 'title': 'CNN'}    Published: 2026-06-23 09:00:26

“Four months?” said Derek Hamm of Obsessive Compulsive Divers in Marathon, Florida. Barnacles, mussels, clams, algae and other warm-seawater-dwelling fauna and flora have latched onto hundreds of massive oil tankers anchored in the Persian Gulf over the past several months. To get the ships in sailing condition, all those crustaceans, bivalves and more will have to be cleared by large crews of scuba divers with an unappealing job title: “bottom cleaners.” “In the maritime world, it's not so weird,” said Hamm, a professional bottom cleaner. Biofouling, maritime industry lingo for the wildlife that accumulates on ships, presents a more complex problem than you might think. Oil tankers stuck in the Strait of Hormuz can't get moving until they unstick what's been stuck onto them. It's yet another complication in getting the world's oil flowing again after months of war created the world's biggest energy supply shock in history. With 600 anchored ships looking to clear the strait, that's some serious work – and a ton of hours. “The work is straightforward and not complicated, but those ships are just way too big for individual divers,” said Brian McCauley, owner of McCauley Mooring and Diving, which offers bottom cleaning services in Long Island Sound. Bottom cleaners use lances to scrape biofoul off hulls. When they're extra stubborn – as barnacles tend to be – divers can use power sanders or hydraulically powered pressure cleaners, powered by an onboard generator. But bottom cleaners also need to take care not to scrape the ship's paint and special coating, which helps prevent biofoul buildup. Damaged coating can cause significant problems, leading to violations of ecological regulations and insurers' biofoul clauses. Propellers can be particularly burdensome because rotors often need to be removed, cleaned and reinstalled, requiring some real elbow grease. The instant demand surge for their services has allowed bottom cleaning teams to up their fees by a few thousand dollars. They're now charging upwards of five figures per ship, said Aron Sørensen, chief environment officer at BIMCO, an international organization of ship operators. Ships, like airplanes, are designed with fluid dynamics in mind, and biofoul seriously messes that up. Biofouled ship walls make vessels significantly less fuel efficient – and much more expensive to move around, especially lugging oil over thousand-mile distances from the Middle East to Asia or Australia. Fuel makes up about 50% of a ship's expense, said Neil Roberts, head of marine and aviation at Lloyd's Market Association, which provides technical expertise to Lloyd's maritime insurance business. Severe growth on propellers can render them completely useless over time, although that's more typical for boats that have been anchored for several years, said Carolyn Shearlock, owner of boat enthusiast website The Boat Galley. And critters love to set up inside intake valves, breaking boats' cooling systems, Hamm noted. Maritime regulations require boats to clear barnacles and other biofoul before they reach port. Trapped between the barnacles can be several invasive species that wreak havoc on marine environments. And insurers include bottom-treatment clauses to ensure ships they cover are in compliance and running as efficiently as possible. It's not a new concern: Centuries ago, warships were built with copper bottoms to prevent worms from boring into the wood and allowing water inside, which would add significantly to ships' weight. Some ships, eager to get out of harm's the war's way, may have their vessels towed tug their boats out of the strait before they're cleaned. But inside or outside the strait, removing biofoul will need to be done before they go on their way. Minesweepers need to trawl through the narrow channel to remove the threat of ships blowing up while they exit or enter. Financiers and insurance companies will need to give the OK for companies to go – a big question as the ceasefire deal takes new twists and turns every day. That's why the oil market won't instantly turn back on like a light switch even as the Strait of Hormuz has reopened.
