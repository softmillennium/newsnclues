CBDCs Aren't Dead Yet—Major Central Banks Quietly Advance Initiatives Amid Stablecoin Dominance
Source: {'href': 'https://www.ccn.com', 'title': 'CCN.com'}    Published: 2025-11-06 13:17:15

The Central Bank of West African States is preparing a CBDC pilot. In 2025, stablecoins have largely stolen the limelight from Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs). However, around the world, central banks are quietly progressing with their various CBDC initiatives. For instance, the Central Bank of West African States is preparing to launch its CBDC pilot in the new year. Starting around 2019, what had previously been a niche interest among payment nerds captured the public imagination. In 2020, China's e-CNY pilot garnered significant media attention. By 2024, digital pounds, euros, and dollars seemed inevitable, with more than 90 central banks in the exploratory stage and 44 live pilots underway. On the campaign trail, Donald Trump slammed CBDCs as a form of government surveillance and vowed to shut down the Fed's digital dollar explorations. Washington's stablecoin push serves as an important corollary to the anti-CBDC movement. While both technologies address calls for currency digitization, stablecoins present a much more immediate reality. By the time more central banks make their move, stablecoins worth trillions of dollars may already be in circulation. With stablecoins dominating the global conversation, CBDC plans appeared to stall. Although some central banks have moved them onto the back burner, they continue to make quiet progress. Central bankers' enthusiasm isn't always matched by political appetite. The same concerns that motivate Trump's anti-CBDC agenda continue to fuel resistance elsewhere. For instance, while the European Central Bank is pressing ahead with plans to launch a digital euro as early as 2029, lawmakers are less convinced. Ongoing pilots and explorations don't mean CBDCs are guaranteed.

They extracted unencrypted passwords directly from server memory. The ICO's message was clear: if you're handling data at this scale, there's no excuse for basic authentication failures. Advanced had implemented multi-factor authentication across much of its infrastructure.
