Still, the idea got its hooks in me because it seems so far from plausible," admits playwright Levi Holloway as we discuss the stage production of Paranormal Activity. The multimillion-dollar film franchise has spawned seven films so far, and now a theatrical production that has garnered solid audiences and reviews in Chicago is headed to Los Angeles' Ahmanson Theatre, with plans to arrive in London's iconic West End in early 2026. "I spoke with Felix a couple of times, and we really hit it off. We were so simpatico in our notion of the genre and our love for the uncanny, the strange, and 1970s horror, as well as great films like Don't Look Now and Jacob's Ladder. "I came up with a loose pitch that was mostly ridiculous, and Paramount, Simon, and Felix all bit. Paramount didn't lay down a hefty mandate. They just said, 'It should probably be about a couple who are haunted by something,' and that was it. We did have many masters to serve, so it was our pleasure to run amok all over any mythology they had created, not as a reduction, but because we wanted to do our own thing." In the story, James and Lou move from Chicago to London to escape the past, but they soon discover that places aren't haunted, people are. "I live in Chicago, and I had to go to London to meet Felix over the course of a year, and I became completely enamored with the city. I was newly married, so I was like, 'Okay, well, how about a couple from Chicago going to London for work?' What a big stretch of the imagination." "You are right, though, in saying London is drenched in ghost stories and folklore, and it just feels a lot more tactile over there. It was an exciting sandbox to be in. In any good horror story, you have to isolate your main characters and you have to answer the question right away, especially in a play." He partly answered it with the production design. "My goal was to have a single set. When it comes to horror on stage, I think it serves the work well to keep your characters on one set and to make that set a character in itself," he reveals. "I wanted to make them strangers in a strange land that was familiar, but somehow not familiar, with that sheen of bizarro over it. This version of Paranormal Activity isn't about demons, God, the devil, or anything else; it's about ghosts. My idea of a ghost is that people are haunted, not places, so that's another reason you can't run." Paranormal Activity takes place on a single set, but that set is a multi-level house, which complicates things and can lead to mishaps. "When I wrote the script, I set it in a two-story house, half imagining what that execution would be. Fly Davis is our scenic designer and has done an incredible job," Holloway enthuses. "I knew that to honor what the film does, you have to immerse the audience and instantly make them into a community, sitting in the dark, watching something they shouldn't be watching. That reminded me of Rear Window or When a Stranger Calls. "In the films, it's just a couple in a house, and it's unremarkable, but the set is designed within an inch of its life. Everything is alive in that house, but figuratively, and has a narrative responsibility tied to it. You watch a married couple very familiar to you, arguing in the kitchen, but if your eye wanders up to the bedroom upstairs, maybe a little piece of storytelling is up there, too. It felt like a great way to pull the audience in, but, like you say, 100 million things can go wrong. With tension being key to Paranormal Activity's success in keeping things intimate and audiences on the edge of their seats, does that limit the production from playing specific venues? "You have to serve a lot of masters when you're trying to dream up something like that, but a lot of it you don't have control over," Holloway muses. "If your biggest problem is that the house you've placed your show in is too big, that's a pretty good problem to have. If we were in an arena, I would have some concerns, but I think we're pretty confident that the intimacy holds even in a huge house. Right now, in Chicago, I think we seat around 700, and that's not nothing. When you place it in a too small a house, the texture may change, which might cause the audience to withhold their response. There's something about safety and numbers that I think works very well for the show." If you've been lucky enough to see Paranormal Activity already and plan to see it again in another city, don't expect the same show, as it continues to evolve. "I write for the actor, I write for the human, so those scenes would change from what we've done before. "There's always that gap between intent and impact, but it changes all the time, and it streamlines, and it mostly just gets pared down to the point where over the course of two iterations, I've lost about 30 pages off the script. I only have so much confidence in the ink. It's a negative space which is on brand with the films." As we conclude our chat, one thing we've been very clear about is steering clear of spoilers. "You have to guard your monster," the playwright concludes. "There are some things you can't withhold from an audience. I think that when you're dealing with a piece of work like this, it creates something conspiratorial in the audience, a shared experience that becomes precious to them.

The play centers around newly married couple James and Lou who move from Chicago to London to escape the past. But they soon discover that places aren't haunted and deeply hidden secrets about each other will make them question how well can you really know another person? L–R: Cher Álvarez and Patrick Heusinger in PARANORMAL ACTIVITY at Chicago Shakespeare Theater. Thanks for taking the time to speak with me today, Levi. I have read about your history of night terrors and dreams. How did that draw you to write plays filled with both horror and humor? A story about who I am or what the world means to me. But I'm led by those stories and try to put them down in ink in a shape that makes sense to me as soon as I wake. To me, plays, like dreams, have no rules. Cher Álvarez in PARANORMAL ACTIVITY at Chicago Shakespeare Theater. Describe working with collaborator Felix Barrett on the production in Chicago which runs just prior to the Los Angeles production. Writing Paranormal Activity for the stage, collaborating with Felix Barrett in London - working to create an actual nightmare - has been a dream. I like to think Felix and I are the same species of storyteller, though really that's giving myself too much credit. We do share a language though, especially when it comes to the horror space. Working with him has made me a better artist. Paranormal Activity draws themes from the award-winning film series of the same name, a franchise of seven horror films that follow supernatural hauntings, primarily centered around a demonic entity. When writing Paranormal, I really only ever concerned myself with the first film. Patrick Heusinger in PARANORMAL ACTIVITY at Chicago Shakespeare Theater. I was more interested in a ghost story. I think we're all haunted in some way. And I think a marriage can bring many ghosts. I think when we enter into a marriage, we're forced to contend with those ghosts, to appease them somehow. I wanted to write about how sometimes broken people find each other and try to fix one another, and how often that is an entirely tragic and fruitless endeavor. Are the two characters in Paranormal Activity, newly-married couple James and Lou, related to the first film's characters Katie and Micah? And if not, how did you develop them? James and Lou are my invention and like all my characters, much of me is in them. I think all writers do that, place themselves, however abstracted, within the people they create. Why did you decide to set the play in London, and why do the characters move there from Chicago? So, I was writing from much of my own experience. I also wanted to isolate the characters, give them no connections abroad. It's another version of a cabin in the woods. We don't have the luxury of doing that on stage since everything is done live and in the moment. So, we must capture the attention of the audience from moment to moment to make them feel what they are seeing is real, even if they don't believe their eyes. L–R: Cher Álvarez and Patrick Heusinger in PARANORMAL ACTIVITY at Chicago Shakespeare Theater. Astronauts could soon be able to move with greater ease, thanks to a soft robotic exosuit developed by researchers at the University of Bristol.Not only does the technology have extraterrestrial benefits, but it could also help people who need support with their mobility on earth, too. Worn underneath the spacesuit, the exosuit features artificial muscles that work automatically to help astronauts reduce muscular fatigue while maintaining natural movements during future Moon and Mars missions. Here, the exosuit was tested as part of an international ‘proof of concept' simulated space mission run by the Austrian Space Forum.Dubbed the ‘World's Biggest Analog', the mission saw 200 scientists from 25 countries working together on different experiments and operational simulations across four continents and reporting back to the mission control base in Austria. The experiments evaluated comfort, mobility and biomechanical effects when performing planetary surface tasks ,such as walking, climbing and load-carrying on loose terrain. “Fortunately, my grandmother worked as a tailor and she was able to give me some advice,” Dr Pulvirenti said. The artificial muscles in the suit consist of two layers: an outer nylon layer and an inner thermoplastic layer that allows airtight inflation.The anchoring components, such as the waistband and knee straps, are made from Kevlar for high strength and tension resistance. Dr Pulvirenti said: “The hope is that this technology could pave the way for future wearable robotic systems that enhance astronaut performance and reduce fatigue during extra-vehicular surface activities. “I would love to continue developing this technology so that it could eventually be tested at the International Space Station.” “It's exciting that this technology could also potentially benefit people too,” Dr Pulvirenti continued. “This exosuit is assistive, meaning it artificially boosts the lower-limb muscles, but we have also separately developed a resistive exosuit, which applies load to the body to help maintain muscle mass. “Our next goal is to create a hybrid suit that can switch between assistance and resistance modes as needed, which could be of great benefit for people in need of support with mobility going through physical rehabilitation.”
