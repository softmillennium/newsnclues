A continuous feedback loop of media reporting, political opportunism, protest groups and social media algorithms are fuelling the spread of conspiracy theories rather than one specific cause, says new findings from the Researching Europe, Digitalisation and Conspiracy Theories (REDACT) project. A series of reports published by the pan-European research project, led by Professor Clare Birchall, Professor of Contemporary Culture in the Department of English, shows how conspiracy theories differ by country due to social, historical and political factors. The REDACT project is funded by CHANSE: a collaboration between 27 funding organisations from 24 countries. Fourteen researchers from around Europe produced seven reports looking into some of the unique aspects of how mis- and disinformation circulate online. Yet in recent years, conspiracy theories have become increasingly widespread and normalised, rather than merely on the sidelines of society. Downplaying their impact or dismissing them does not make these theories disappear – it actually allows them to take up all the oxygen on a topic and dominate discussion. Although conspiracy theories were once seen as existing on the fringes of society, the research shows that politicians and news coverage often contribute to these theories going viral. This creates a ‘feedback loop' between the internet, protest groups, political parties and mainstream media reporting, blurring distinctions between the online and offline spread of conspiracy theories. The specifics of the conspiracy theory vary by country, but reflect anxiety about changing cultural values and demographics, even in spaces where levels of immigration are, in fact, low.
