Wander to any turf field on any given night and you're bound to find a ball and a group of people playing until daylight dissolves or the artificial lights go out. Futsal players whip out their freestyle moves on the concrete courts at Judkins Park. Brass horns and a drum line march to the stadium from Pioneer Square before Sounders matches. Fans pack Ballard's Rough & Tumble pub on Reign FC game days. Signs of just how much soccer pulses through this city are everywhere. Fans will don ponchos and raincoats to cheer on their teams. In Seattle, rain is merely a backdrop, not an obstacle to the game. “Those rainy Seattle nights just embody what Seattle soccer is all about,” says Jordan Morris, a midfielder for the Sounders who grew up on Mercer Island. Morris mentions another defining characteristic of Seattle soccer, something his head coach, Brian Schmetzer, refers to as “Northwest grit” — an attitude that drives players and fans to simply outwork their opponent. It's this attitude that led Seattle from humble soccer beginnings to hosting six World Cup matches this summer. Frank MacDonald is a historian of all things soccer in Washington state. The Sounders played their inaugural season when MacDonald was 14 years old. “The first home game was on Mother's Day, and I remember hearing the game on the radio and the cheers — and all I knew about it to that point was playing a little bit of soccer in P.E.,” MacDonald says. “I was from a town that was mainly a baseball, football, basketball town, as most were back then.” MacDonald went on to serve as a PR assistant for the team, back when it was in the North American Soccer League, which folded in 1984. Soccer originally piqued his interest because of its worldliness. As far as MacDonald knows, the first documented soccer game in Washington was played just a few miles from his hometown of Centralia, in Chehalis, in 1890, one year after Washington became a state. “The game came from the lumberman, and then I found that it came from miners who came from Wales and Italy, and they were up in Black Diamond,” MacDonald says. At the time, the region was experiencing rapid population growth, with many new residents coming from Canada, Germany, Sweden, Norway and England, attracted by the promise of workable land. “You'd have sailors that would come in to play, and then the railroad came into Tacoma and extended to Seattle, which brought more (people),” MacDonald says. As they moved to the region, immigrants gifted Washington with soccer. They taught them how to love the sport in an impassioned way that previously could only be found overseas. MacDonald explains that in the 1960s, European immigrants to Washington were mostly skilled technicians and engineers who moved for jobs at Boeing or the shipyards. Schmetzer's father, Walter Schmetzer, was part of this wave of newcomers, emigrating in 1962 from Germany, where he played in his state's first division. Not only would all three of Walter's sons go on to play professionally, but he'd also open up the first soccer store in Seattle. “Mostly immigrant coaches patiently taught us the game. The women's game took longer to catch on, but when Title IX passed in 1972 and prohibited sex-based discrimination in public schools, Washington became a hotbed for producing some of the top talent in the country. “My brothers played and I had to watch them because they didn't have girls soccer and it was a time when girls didn't join the boys teams,” says Cindy Gordon, who played on the first U.S. women's national soccer team, formed in 1985. Its roster included seven other players from Washington state. That 1985 team was instrumental in establishing women's soccer in the U.S. on the international stage and paving the way for the pivotal 1999 national team, which would win the FIFA Women's World Cup in front of 90,000-plus fans in Pasadena, Calif. That game would make Mia Hamm a household name and inspire future generations of talent, including Abby Wambach and Alex Morgan. I've been at five FIFA World Cups, but always on the inside. For the first time this June, I'll experience the World Cup as a fan and as a new Seattle resident. In my role at Visit Seattle, I am helping shape Seattle's visitor economy, and I see something both familiar and special. I've seen how global soccer tournaments transform cities like Miami, not just during the matches themselves, but in the months leading up to them and long after the final whistle blows. Now, as both a resident and someone who once helped evaluate Seattle as a host city, it feels like I have come full circle. In 2021, I visited Seattle as part of an inspection team evaluating whether the city could host the World Cup. We looked at everything: stadium infrastructure, hotel capacity, training facilities, transportation, safety — and equally important, cultural fit. It's a chance to invest in public spaces, strengthen connections across neighborhoods and create experiences that will endure long after the tournament ends. It happens in parks and local businesses filled with watch parties; in downtown neighborhoods hosting cultural celebrations; in restaurants and bars where strangers become friends over a shared love of the game. Here in King County, Visit Seattle's projections estimate the tournament could generate at least $845 million in direct economic impact and support tens of thousands of jobs. But those numbers only tell part of the story. Imagine citywide celebrations connecting neighborhoods from Lumen Field to Seattle Center along the new Unity Loop. Picture public viewing spaces along the waterfront park, fan festivals downtown and local businesses hosting themed events that reflect the global cultures arriving on our doorstep. For example, Cincinnati, not traditionally known as a soccer city, became a destination for fans traveling from across the country.
