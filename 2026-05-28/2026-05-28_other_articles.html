Alien abductions and fake arrests? Americans share excuses to stay home
Source: {'href': 'https://uk.news.yahoo.com', 'title': 'Yahoo News UK'}    Published: 2026-05-28 08:39:46

By the age of 32, Americans plan their social calendars around their sleep schedules. That's according to a new survey of 2,000 general population Americans, which revealed the average respondent will start prioritizing their sleep over plans with loved ones in their early thirties. But instead of just saying “no” when a plan would interfere with their shut-eye, some people will turn to creative excuses. The survey asked respondents to share the wildest excuses they've ever used to get out of plans, and one said, “I told someone that an alien spaceship abducted me and put me back after the plans I was invited to.” Conducted by Talker Research on behalf of Avocado Green Mattress, the survey found the average respondent will turn down plans if they begin after 9 p.m. Equally, they don't want to be social too early in the morning — saying “no” to plans that begin before 7:36 a.m.

When the Pentagon released its first tranche of 162 declassified UAP files on 8 May under the PURSUE initiative — the Presidential Unsealing and Reporting System for UAP Encounters — the most discussed footage was not a sleek disc or a hypersonic tic-tac. It was a glowing, eight-pointed, shape-shifting object captured by an infrared sensor over the Middle East in 2013, catalogued as DOW-UAP-PR38, still officially unresolved after thirteen years. Within hours, Florida Representative Anna Paulina Luna posted an image of a “biblically accurate” angel beside the Pentagon still. Let us be precise about what the footage actually shows. Defence analysts and optical physicists have noted that the eight-pointed geometry is consistent with bokeh and diffraction artefacts produced when an infrared sensor images an out-of-focus point source — a military flare, a distant aircraft light — through an aperture with a characteristic blade pattern. The Pentagon's own documentation flags several files in the release as probable optical effects, including a 2024 Syria observation explicitly described as a “light/glare halo effect.” The evidentiary base, in short, is thin. It is why the biblical frame was adopted with such speed and such market depth, and what that adoption reveals about the strategic architecture of disclosure itself. The grainy infrared footage carries almost no informational content in isolation. Yet the implied volatility of these releases is enormous. Each ambiguous frame functions as a deep out-of-the-money option on existential risk: extraterrestrial contact, divine manifestation, adversarial hypersonic technology. This is where the biblical framing becomes critical. The eight-pointed star over the Middle East does not need to be explained; it needs only to resonate.
