Oh, but San Francisco is currently home to one. And it's the scariest play this horror movie fan has ever seen. But you only have a few days left to catch it on its current San Francisco run. Of course, I'm talking about “Paranormal Activity,” which runs through March 22 at A.C.T. But those reviews were all coming from theater people, while I'm a horror movie guy — so definitely representing two different realms of the arts world. I've seen all the “Paranormal” films — some of them multiple times. And I just didn't think this play, which is an original story inspired by and set in the same kind of universe as the movies, could manage to be as impactful as the big-screen offerings. “Paranormal Activity” surpassed all my expectations — by a wide margin — and I would have to say it's even better than the film series itself. And it doesn't work out so well for the married couple, who deal with all kinds of hair-rising situations in their new home. Yet, what makes the show work so well is that Chicago playwright Levi Holloway (known for the haunting Broadway play “Grey House”) and director Felix Barrett (from the British theater company Punchdrunk) keep things small and manageable, working to create and sustain a creepy atmosphere where jump scares could lurk around any corner. And when it's time for one of those jump scares, or a visual that sends a chill down your spine, Chris Fisher is there waiting for you. Fisher, a Tony-Award-winning master of illusion known for his work in such popular productions as “Back to the Future,” “Harry Potter and the Cursed Child” and “Stranger Things: The First Shadow,” delivers big time in “Paranormal Activity.” His illusions and technical effect work fits so seamlessly into the production — like something that you'd see from ILM in a big-budget movie — that I sill have no idea how any of those tricks of the trade were accomplished. And pretty much all of those illusions involved people — as opposed to big “Beetlejuice” style puppet creatures — which made the whole thing more wonderfully perplexing and effective. But they better do it soon, since “Paranormal Activity” closes its San Francisco run on Sunday. These strange pink rocks just revealed a hidden giant beneath Antarctica
Source: {'href': 'https://www.sciencedaily.com', 'title': 'ScienceDaily'}    Published: 2026-03-18 10:41:39

Bright pink granite boulders scattered across the dark volcanic peaks of the Hudson Mountains in West Antarctica have led scientists to a remarkable discovery. Beneath Pine Island Glacier lies an enormous buried granite mass, nearly 100 km wide and 7 km thick, roughly half the size of Wales in the UK. A research team led by British Antarctic Survey (BAS) analyzed the granite by examining the radioactive decay of elements trapped inside tiny mineral crystals. Even with their age determined, their journey to the mountaintops remained unclear until scientists gathered new data from aircraft surveys over the region. Using highly sensitive gravity measurements collected by BAS' Twin Otter aircraft and others, researchers detected an unusual signal beneath the glacier. Connecting the surface boulders to this deep underground formation solved a long-standing mystery. It also revealed that Pine Island Glacier once moved very differently, pulling rocks from its base and carrying them uphill when the ice sheet was much thicker. By understanding past ice thickness and movement patterns, scientists can improve computer models used to predict how Antarctica's ice sheets may respond to future climate change. Dr. Tom Jordan, lead author and geophysicist at BAS, analyzed the airborne data. By combining geological dating with gravity surveys, we've not only solved a mystery about where these rocks came from, but also uncovered new information about how the ice sheet flowed in the past and how it might change in the future." Better understanding these processes will help refine models that estimate future sea level rise. "Rocks provide an amazing record of how our planet has changed over time, especially how ice has eroded and altered the landscape of Antarctica. "By identifying their source, we have been able to piece together how they got to where they are today, giving us clues about how the West Antarctic Ice Sheet may change in future -- information that is vital for determining the impact of sea level rise on coastal populations around the world." This research shows how combining geology and geophysics can uncover hidden features beneath Antarctica and deepen our understanding of the forces shaping the planet. Opinion: CT lawmaker wants taxpayers to fund UFO study. It raises questions on priorities.
Source: {'href': 'https://www.courant.com', 'title': 'Hartford Courant'}    Published: 2026-03-18 09:18:10

Connecticut lawmakers have introduced legislation that would require the state to study unidentified aerial phenomena (UAP) — better known to most people as UFOs. 5422, currently before the legislature's Appropriations Committee, would require the University of Connecticut to conduct a statewide study examining unexplained aerial sightings and to evaluate whether Connecticut should establish a government center dedicated to studying them. It also instructs the university to consult with multiple state agencies — including the Department of Emergency Services and Public Protection, the Department of Energy and Environmental Protection, and the Office of Military Affairs — and review relevant data collected by those agencies. The report would be due to lawmakers by July 1, 2027. Supporters argue the study could provide scientific insight into unexplained aerial activity. In written testimony to the Appropriations Committee, UConn Assistant Dean Kylene Perras stated that a meaningful study would require dedicated funding rather than being absorbed within existing university operations. According to UConn's estimates, a limited project involving one faculty researcher and graduate assistants would cost approximately $150,000. A broader research effort involving multiple faculty members could reach roughly $300,000. The university also noted it is operating under “significant financial constraints,” warning that an unfunded mandate could divert resources from other academic priorities. In other words, if lawmakers want a UFO study, taxpayers will likely be footing the bill. In testimony submitted to the committee, Department of Emergency Services and Public Protection Commissioner Ronnell Higgins raised concerns about the bill's requirement that agencies share data with university researchers. Much of the department's information is sensitive and restricted to law enforcement or authorized entities due to public safety and national security considerations. Higgins indicated that existing confidentiality and security rules could prevent the university from accessing certain data the bill assumes will be available. Connecticut lawmakers are currently debating how to manage rising electricity rates, housing shortages, and tens of billions in long-term pension obligations. Against that backdrop, the Appropriations Committee is now reviewing whether to commit public funds to a study of unidentified aerial phenomena. The bill itself does not explicitly appropriate funding. Whether it advances may ultimately say as much about legislative priorities as it does about unidentified objects in the sky.
