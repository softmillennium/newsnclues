How FIFA's World Cup pricing dwarfs projections in original North American bid
Source: {'href': 'https://www.nytimes.com', 'title': 'The New York Times'}    Published: 2025-12-12 17:13:51

Fabrice Coffrini / AFP via Getty Images Ticket prices released by FIFA this week for the men's World Cup in 2026 were, on average, 174 per cent higher than projected in the official bid book submitted by the joint proposal from the United States, Canada and Mexico to soccer's world governing body. As part of an official FIFA process to be considered to host a World Cup, bidding nations must submit a bid book, which can be studied by both FIFA and federations across the world, who in turn judge its merits and decide who to vote for. The so-called ‘United' bid, which brought the American, Canadian and Mexican bids together under one umbrella, was published by FIFA in March 2018. The bid said that it made projections after “careful research and consultation with various resources and industry experts.” This week, in a phase which opened on Thursday, FIFA sold tickets in its “lottery phase” for specific matches, adjusting its prices based on the anticipated demand for each fixture. The vanishingly small number of Category Four tickets available in previous phases of sales were not present at all for this lottery phase. This represents an increase of 204 per cent for those seeking to watch the USMNT play Paraguay, Mexico play South Africa, or Canada play the winner of a UEFA playoff between Wales, Bosnia & Herzegovina, Italy, or Northern Ireland. An even more dramatic increase is present in the average price across the three categories for the World Cup final, which will take place at MetLife Stadium in New York/New jersey on July 19. While the bid book projected an average price of $1,099 across the three categories, FIFA has increased this by 459 per cent, with their average at $6147. The increases in the group stage, round of 32 and round of 16 is more modest, but still substantial, with increases of 49 per cent, 61 per cent and 94 per cent respectively. FIFA's elimination of category four tickets also counters the projections in the bid book, which had indicated that seven per cent of available seating would be set aside for the lowest price point. FIFA did have some of these available in previous phases of sales, priced at $60 for some group stage matchups, but they never confirmed how many existed and those which did appeared to be very small in number, often limited to the upper corners of upper decks of NFL stadiums. Football Supporters Europe has called on FIFA to “immediately halt” ticket sales for participating member associations (PMA) due to what it calls a “monumental betrayal of the tradition of the World Cup”. More than two dozen American cities are eager to host matches as part of U.S. Soccer's joint bid with Mexico, Jamaica and Costa Rica for the 2031 FIFA Women's World Cup. Those cities, however, declined to sign long-form agreements that FIFA expected ahead of last month's bid deadline. So far, they have only been willing to sign non-binding, one-page memorandums of understanding provided by U.S. Soccer. These fall well short of the commitments required to make the bid compliant with FIFA's rules, which state that host city and stadium agreement templates must be submitted “signed and in unaltered form.” FIFA plans to formally award hosting rights to the four Concacaf member nations at its annual congress on April 30, 2026, in Vancouver. Whether it will do so without more concrete commitments from American host cities remains to be seen. Under the agreements they signed in 2017, those cities have taken on most of the costs of hosting matches and FIFA fan fests — a burden local leaders have pegged at $100 million to $200 million per city — while having limited ability to generate offsetting revenue. Each city is allowed to sell up to 10 supporter packages (apart from New York/New Jersey, which can sell 20), but the 16 host cities across the U.S., Mexico and Canada have thus far combined to announce only 35 supporters, or 2.2 per city, with six months remaining before the tournament. FIFA has softened some of its most onerous demands on 2026 host cities, eliminating a requirement that cities provide free public transportation to matches and offering them more flexibility to determine the scope of their fan fests. “There's had to be this growth and evolution between when the [2026] bid was first sent out to all of us and where we are today,” Zaileen Janmohamed, president and CEO of the Bay Area Host Committee, said at a public event in June. “There has been some progress in that, and I think that progress will continue for the Women's World Cup. Several representatives for prospective host cities spoke with Sports Business Journal on the condition of anonymity to ensure FIFA doesn't hold their participation against their market in the selection process. They all stressed that they want to host the competition, but need certainty the terms will be more favorable than in 2026. U.S. Soccer issued a statement, saying in part, “We look forward to continued collaboration with local partners and FIFA as we prepare to deliver a tournament of unprecedented success and drive the women's game forward.” In April 2025, U.S. Soccer formally confirmed its interest in teaming with other Concacaf member nations to bid for the 2031 Women's World Cup. At an information session for leaders from interested cities in July, U.S. Soccer assured the cities that FIFA's terms would be more practical and take their concerns into account, but the national federation could not yet provide details. It wasn't until early November — weeks before the bid submission deadline — that the cities got their first look at FIFA's proposed terms. With the cities themselves, those included public transit and fan fest requirements. Even with those assurances, though, host city leaders were uncomfortable signing the underlying agreement. For starters, the cities are seeking to negotiate new terms on a much wider range of issues than were carved out in the side letters, including potential revenue sharing. Furthermore, several city leaders felt it was unreasonable to be given mere weeks to commit to terms for an event more than five years away, particularly with some in the throes of preparation for the 2026 men's event. “Some of these things have to go in front of city councils and get approvals, and that just doesn't happen overnight,” one host city leader said. Multiple sources said leaders from nearly all of the prospective U.S. host cities signed a draft letter to FIFA informing the governing body of their decision to decline to sign the agreements and requesting broader negotiations. The cities agreed and are now awaiting guidance from either FIFA or U.S. Soccer on next steps. Despite U.S. Soccer's previous insistence that it only wants to host the event if it benefits host cities, several host city leaders said they are unsure whether U.S. Soccer will advocate for them in negotiations with FIFA or prioritize getting FIFA what it needs to secure the bid. A source with knowledge of U.S. Soccer's thinking said the organization is aiming to get firmer commitments than the MOUs from host cities ahead of the FIFA Congress in April, but there is an understanding that some key issues won't be resolved until after the 2026 World Cup. Those issues could be agreed upon during FIFA's 2031 host city selection process, which would likely take place in 2027. In that case, cities that are most amenable to FIFA's terms would likely have a leg up in the selection process. There's a widely held sentiment that the issues at stake are reconcilable.
