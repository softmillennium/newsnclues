Theatre Review: "Paranormal Activity" Takes Live Theater to Scary New Heights
Source: {'href': 'https://weliveentertainment.com', 'title': 'We Live Entertainment'}    Published: 2025-11-18 18:54:13

This production is built on “how did they do THAT?” energy, and it delivers. The plot centers on Lou (Cher Alvarez) and James (Patrick Heusinger), an American couple who've just relocated to London for James's job. They've been placed in a lovely craftsman-style home, the sort of quaint London dwelling that practically begs to be haunted. They're putting an ocean between themselves and James's mother, Carolanne, played by Shannon Cochran, with an enjoyably meddling edge. (And yes, naming her after “Carolanne” is a delightful wink to Poltergeist.) But Lou insists she's fine, and for a brief moment, the newly arrived couple actually believes they're about to embark on a fresh start. At first, it's the small things: power outages, water faucets turning on by themselves, doors that refuse to stay closed. But as Lou becomes convinced something supernatural is inside the house – and as James tries very, very hard to be the rational one – the audience watches their grip on reality begin to slip. Once James finally accepts that something is happening, they call in the wonderfully quirky ghost-hunting podcaster Etheline Cotgrave (Kate Fry). Let's just say her visit doesn't go well, and the haunting escalates into a full-force assault. To be clear, the story of Paranormal Activity isn't breaking new ground. But seeing this kind of story unfold live gives it a fresh charge. One of the defining traits of the films is the found-footage style that encourages audiences to scan every corner for a flicker of movement. The stage production cleverly replicates that feeling. With Gareth Fry's sound design building the franchise's signature low, ominous rumble (the audio clue that a scare is coming), the entire house set becomes a minefield of suspense. The two-story design ensures the audience's eyes are constantly darting, trying to anticipate which hallway, staircase, or shadowy nook will erupt next. And many of those moments truly land. The audience I saw it with screamed, gasped, clutched strangers, and occasionally laughed just to release the tension. I won't spoil a single effect, but several of them are as impressive as anything I've seen in a Broadway-scale thriller. The actors' volume is often so low that major plot points get swallowed, perhaps intentionally, to make the audience lean in before a scare hits with a jump-worthy jolt. The second act is also dimly lit to the point of murkiness, which contributes to the mood but sometimes makes it difficult to see who's where and what's happening. Still, those issues don't sink the production. If anything, they feel like growing pains for a show experimenting with how far live theater can push 21st-century horror. And as someone who genuinely loves the Paranormal Activity films, I wasn't convinced the franchise would translate to a stage. For fans of the movies, this is a must-see. For theatergoers looking for something different, atmospheric, and seasonally spooky, this is a wildly entertaining night out. Paranormal Activity isn't just a theatrical adaptation; it's a reminder that live theater, with the right team, can still find thrilling new ways to make an entire audience jump out of their seats. Machine learning framework can scan for signs of extraterrestrial life
Source: {'href': 'https://phys.org', 'title': 'Phys.org'}    Published: 2025-11-18 14:54:32

A machine learning framework can distinguish molecules made by biological processes from those formed through non-biological processes and could be used to analyze samples returned by current and future planetary missions. José C. Aponte, Amirali Aghazadeh, and colleagues analyzed eight carbonaceous meteorites and ten terrestrial geologic samples using two-dimensional gas chromatography coupled with high-resolution time-of-flight mass spectrometry. Using this data, the authors developed LifeTracer, a computational framework that processes mass spectrometry data and applies machine learning to identify patterns distinguishing abiotic from biotic origins. A logistic regression model trained on compound-level features achieved over 87% accuracy in classifying samples as meteoritic or terrestrial. The framework identified polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons and alkylated variants as key predictive features, with naphthalene emerging as the most predictive compound for abiotic samples. According to the authors, the approach enables scalable, unbiased biosignature detection and could be a powerful tool for interpreting complex organic mixtures that will be returned by current and future planetary sample return missions. More information: Daniel Saeedi et al, Discriminating abiotic and biotic organics in meteorite and terrestrial samples using machine learning on mass spectrometry data, PNAS Nexus (2025).
