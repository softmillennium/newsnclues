But if you'd like a little cheat sheet for the mythical creatures that permeate Anne Rice's Immortal Universe, we are here for you. Let's start with the beings that built Anne Rice into a household name: vampires. With eternal life, Rice's vampires achieve a remarkable physical beauty, but also an ennui that have made these characters so richly loved by millions of readers. Think of it like borrowing money from your pain-in-the-butt relative who will always want something back in return. The origins of this species remains a little hazy, but it is believed that they are the souls of humans who have escaped from Hell. The journey and long spans of time, however, make their memories a bit untrustworthy. Witches in Rice's world are humans born with special supernatural sensitivities, oftentimes extra sensory perceptions or the power of telekinesis. The ability to practice witchcraft is usually genetic, but not always. (Rice doesn't like sticking to preestablished rules, explaining much of her popularity.) We're basically getting into monster cereals territory here, but mummies play a big part in Rice's fiction. They aren't usually called mummies, though, but rather Immortals. They first come up in The Vampire Chronicles as a legend — creatures impervious to death who aren't harmed by sunlight. Over time, we learn their origins are with the Egyptian Pharaoh Ramses, who drank a stolen, magical elixir. Oddly enough, it was the third book in this series about eternal life that Rice was working on when she passed away. First introduced in 1990's The Witching Hour and given prime focus in Taltos four years later, the taltos are a species that, on first glance, look human, but are usually taller, thinner, and have bones mostly made of cartilage. In ancient times, the taltos, who originated in the very north of today's Scotland, tended to be rather clumsy and childlike. The taltos have superior strength and an enhanced libido. Though The Wolf Gift Chronicles sold well, they were poorly received by readers. (If you want to see some howling reviews, poke around on Reddit.) They are the souls of mortal beings who have died, but can become visible to witches (or other groups that have supernatural sensitivities). They can sometimes inhabit corporeal form, though it's not easy — and just temporary. Ghosts aren't just humans — witches and taltos can die, and so can vampires (though it takes some extra effort) — so in a way, ghosts aren't really their own species. Angels are noncorporeal creatures who exist outside of typical time and space. Their main gig is to perceive the Universe and remark upon its beauty. One such angel, however, decided to rebel against this way of life. His name was Memnoch, though most of us know him as Satan or the Devil. This does not involve Leonardo DiCaprio trudging through the snow and eating buffalo hearts, but in Rice's world, revenants have it pretty rough. They are mortals who are in the process of being turned into vampires ("The Dark Gift," it is called), but are buried, entombed, or prevented in some way from feeding for long enough that their evolution is prevented — and they go a little nutty. The revenants in Anne Rice's Immortal Universe on AMC thus far have functioned essentially as "zombie vampires" — mindless, dangerous, and certainly gross. They were dispatched to Earth to assassinate a rogue named Amel, a spirit who is actually the progenitor of the vampire race. The replimoids rebelled against their creators and have been hanging around ever since. They are mortal but have extremely long lifespans, and can basically pass for human. Each generation of replimoid is a little less like the ones originally made by "The Parents" — you can kinda tell by how much gold is in their hair. Another creation that appears in Rice's fiction is God. For it is He who created the angels, and thus Memnoch, a fallen angel, who God basically sees as the loyal opposition. Alien Probes May Already Be Hiding in Our Solar System, Study Suggests
Source: {'href': 'https://indiandefencereview.com', 'title': 'Indian Defence Review'}    Published: 2025-11-09 12:15:00

Self-replicating alien probes may already be operating in our solar system, quietly carrying out long-term missions. These so-called von Neumann probes, named after the mathematician who conceptualized them, could potentially explain the absence of direct contact with alien civilizations—if we know where, and how, to look. Over time, scientists involved in SETI (Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence) have considered these probes as one of the most efficient ways an advanced species could explore the galaxy. Now, a new paper by Professor Alex Ellery, an engineer at Carleton University's Centre for Self-Replication Research, is reigniting interest in this hypothesis by pointing out that such probes may have already visited—if not remain operational—in the solar system. The study, published as a preprint and highlighted by Universe Today, emphasizes that future SETI efforts should expand beyond the search for radio signals and focus instead on hunting for technosignatures left behind by these potential machines. According to Ellery's findings, self-replicating probes would likely follow a systematic, resource-efficient process. They would begin by harvesting materials from moons and asteroids, chosen for their accessible raw components. These activities, in turn, could lead to more advanced missions, including long-term observation or even engineering projects such as O'Neill cylinders. The predictable pattern of their activities, Ellery argues, would likely generate detectable technosignatures. These could include unusual isotope ratios or subsurface magnetic anomalies, particularly on the Moon, which he identifies as an ideal staging ground due to its composition and proximity. As Ellery explains in comments to Universe Today, the driving forces behind such missions might be far more utilitarian—or even existential. Whether facing the threat of stellar evolution, hostile civilizations, or runaway technologies, advanced species might choose to send autonomous machines as military scouts, colonization tools, or even as cultural emissaries. “Science per se is not much of a driver,” he noted. Ellery's research positions the Moon as a potential hotspot for finding traces of alien technology. According to his study, specific isotopes like Thorium-232, Neodynium-144, and Barium-137 might reveal the presence of nuclear-powered replication systems. These materials could indicate that probes had once operated nearby—or still do. The Moon's rich subsurface metals, particularly nickel, cobalt, and tungsten, would have made it an ideal target for resource extraction. Ellery even raises the possibility that advanced civilizations might leave behind “gifts”, such as buried universal constructor machines—technologies only accessible to species that reach a certain level of advancement. While speculative, this idea points toward the need for a broader definition of SETI success, one that accounts for indirect signs of intelligent life rather than explicit communication. Supernatural Season 16 Can Bring Dean Back From The Dead Permanently, Despite Season 15's Ending
Source: {'href': 'https://screenrant.com', 'title': 'Screen Rant'}    Published: 2025-11-09 03:30:00

Supernatural did the thinkable in season 15's finale, as Dean Winchester met his untimely end. While official news on Supernatural season 16 has been unforthcoming over the past 5 years, Jensen Ackles and Jared Padalecki have each expressed their willingness to return when the time is right, all but guaranteeing the Winchester brothers will be back on our screens sooner rather than later. It goes without saying that, were Supernatural season 16 to ever happen, Sam and Dean Winchester would need to be reunited. Dean's death actually does very little to impede the siblings teaming up in a potential revival. As seen in The Winchesters, Dean enjoys more or less free rein roaming around different realities, so if his own timeline came under threat and Sam needed help, Dean could quite easily pop down to Earth on a temporary visit. As such, it's incredibly likely that Supernatural season 16 would be a "one last case" arrangement where Dean is given leave from the afterlife to assist Sam and protect the world from the latest spooky bad guy. The tragic expectation, however, would be that Dean ascends back to Heaven once again when Supernatural season 16's case is closed. Supernatural's finale all but confirms this, as we see flashes of Sam's life without Dean, right up until his own demise as an old man in a terrible wig. Dean Winchester dying in Supernatural's final episode had been a long time coming. As mentioned previously, however, the manner of Dean's last hunt proved disappointing. The Winchesters then removed any weight behind it by confirming Dean isn't exactly stuck in Heaven. At this point, it would be better for Supernatural to drop the idea of Dean being dead altogether. The show had an opportunity to kill off Dean properly in season 15's finale. Because there is another fitting ending that Dean could have after Supernatural season 16, and it involves a storyline from the main show that was never completely finished: Lisa and Ben. After living with Lisa and her (potentially their) son between Supernatural seasons 5 and 6, Dean ended up realizing that his presence put the pair in danger. He had their memories wiped and never spoke of them again, but there's no doubt Lisa and Ben represented Dean's "happily ever after." The most satisfying ending for Jensen Ackles' character in Supernatural season 16 would be a reunion with the family he left behind, and closure on an unfinished relationship that remains arguably Dean's biggest regret right up until Supernatural's final episode. As an added bonus, further Supernatural revivals could then avoid the obstacle of bringing Dean back from the dead altogether. The only problem would be how Supernatural can align Dean coming permanently back to life and having a family with Sam's flashforward from the series finale, in which Dean is very clearly still deceased. Under normal circumstances, blatantly backtracking on a flashforward could be viewed as a lazy retcon, but given the lack of love afforded to Supernatural's final episode - everything from Sam's older appearance to Dean's lackluster last hunt - it seems unlikely many would complain about Supernatural quietly relegating Sam's later years from official canon.
