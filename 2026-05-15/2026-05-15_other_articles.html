United States officials released more than 160 previously classified files last week. The documents provide a granular look at over 400 reported UFO incidents. Dr Greg Seab, a retired astrophysicist from the University of New Orleans, welcomed the shift toward openness. Seab spent nearly forty years teaching astronomy to future scientists. He argues that releasing these unclassified records is a vital step for public trust. Despite the sheer volume of reports, Seab remains a firm sceptic. He suggests that, so far, the evidence is lacking. He sees no proof of technology that breaks known physical laws and found no smoking gun pointing to extraterrestrial visitors. You've got to have skeptics scrutinize it and not just true believers,' Seab told 4WWLTV. 'I've read analysis of previously available UFO tapes that attributed a lot of their extraordinary maneuvers to the fact that the camera is not as steady as people thought it was,' he explained, also pointing out that distance can affect the perception as well. While Seab doubts these narratives, he is not a nihilist regarding alien life. Current estimates suggest there are roughly two trillion galaxies in the observable universe. In a space that vast, Seab believes it is highly unlikely that Earth holds the only spark of life. He suspects we are not alone in the dark. However, Seab does take issue with how aliens are portrayed in popular culture. Science fiction often depicts extraterrestrials as beings of immense, god-like intellect. 'I strongly believe that there's extraterrestrial life,' Seab said. 'Whether it's intelligent enough is a little harder, but I'm really waiting for their flying saucer to land on the White House lawn.' Recent research conducted by scientists, including Gideon Yoffe of the Weizmann Institute and Fabian Klenner of the University of California, Riverside, suggests a novel technique for identifying more profound fingerprint signatures in the quest for extraterrestrial life. The study found that amino acids produced by organisms exhibited greater diversity and a more uniform distribution throughout a sample compared to those formed abiotically. Truth be told, this do not completely confirm the presence of alien life.

Source: {'href': 'https://splashmags.com', 'title': 'Splash Magazines Worldwide'}    Published: 2026-05-15 05:33:36

Goodman Theatre continues its 100th anniversary season with the Chicago Premiere of York Walker's play Covenant. With a successful performance, the Off-Broadway production received high acclaim and was marked as New York Times' Critic Pick in 2023. Two years after leaving home, Johnny “Honeycomb” James (Debo Balogun) returns to Georgia as a successful musician. Rumors that he sold his soul to the devil divide the town, leaving some dazzled by his success and others deeply suspicious. Drawn into the mystery are Johnny's childhood sweetheart Avery (Jaeda Lavone), her devout mother (Anji White), her younger sister Violet (Felicia Oduh), and Violet's best friend Ruthie (Ashli René Funches). Avery chooses to leave home and build a life with Johnny, despite the heartbreak it causes her family and Ruthie. But when the couple eventually returns, Avery seems unsettlingly changed. As tensions rise, faith is tested and supernatural forces begin to overtake their small home. Malkia Stampley directs Walker's script with striking precision and a strong sense of wonder, skillfully balancing its themes of faith, family, and the supernatural. Both Stampley and Walker demonstrate a keen understanding of pacing, drawing audiences effectively in through tender, intimate moments as well as heart-pounding scenes of intensity. Music director and composer Mike Przygoda, alongside sound designer Dee Etti Williams, craft a rich sonic landscape that skillfully balances music and spirituality, presenting them as deeply connected and occasionally in conflict. High praise and recognition should also be given to Benjamin Barnes and Trent James, whose stage effects are among the production's most impressive achievements. Their work delivers several jaw-dropping moments, particularly during Scene Eleven's possession sequence and the play's shocking epilogue. These scenes are so striking audiences are likely to respond with audible gasps and excited whispers as they process what unfolds onstage. The cast of Covenant delivers an impressive display of musical talent, emotional depth, and physical expression throughout the ninety-minute production. Jaeda Lavone portrays Avery as a young woman determined to escape her conservative upbringing in search of freedom and opportunity. Oduh portrays Violet as a young girl deeply affected by her mother's emotional distance and her older sister's unsettling transformation. Through York Walker's haunting script, Malkia Stampley's assured direction, and a deeply committed cast and creative team, the production delivers a chilling exploration of faith, family, ambition, and the cost of desire. Blending supernatural horror with emotional realism and powerful musical performances, Covenant leaves audiences unsettled long after the final scene fades to black. COVENANT performs until May 31st in Goodman's Owen Theatre.

New UFO Files Suggest Something Strange Is Happening in The Skies
Source: {'href': 'https://www.sciencealert.com', 'title': 'ScienceAlert'}    Published: 2026-05-15 00:11:44

The US Government has released a new trove of documents on various cases of "Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena" (UAPs) – many of which would have been described in the past as Unidentified Flying Objects or UFOs – including photos, videos, and reports of unexplained events sighted in the sky and in space. Taking this new release together with other reports from the past few years, a picture of what might be behind UAPs – and the current state of our understanding – begins to emerge. Some images, like those that appear to show unidentified lights recorded by astronauts from the surface of the Moon, are most likely just visual noise or other image artifacts. Astronauts have also reported seeing bright lights with the naked eye. These may be flashes from high-energy cosmic rays, which are more prevalent outside Earth's protective magnetic field. Something is happening: Other cases are more difficult to immediately dismiss. Some recent examples demonstrate that something is occurring, even if we do not know what. In 2020, the US Navy declassified three videos recorded by F/A-18 Super Hornets, showing mysterious "Tic Tac" objects flying in ways that defy current understanding of technology, without any obvious propulsion. Advanced sensors on fighter aircraft detected and tracked these phenomena, which suggests they were some kind of real objects rather than due to equipment errors or erroneous noise. It appeared to be recorded by an MQ-9 Reaper drone, which was tracking a UAP. The object seemed to be deflected from its direction of travel momentarily, suggesting it was a real physical thing, but it appeared to suffer no damage and continued on its course. In several cases, US Navy Destroyers apparently met mysterious "drone swarms". While these may have been conventional drones, there were no apparent launch vessels nearby, which could have allowed these relatively small drones (with presumably short range) to be where they were. We also know that in 2023, the US used an F-22 fighter to shoot down a Chinese high-altitude spy balloon over the continental US. Also in 2023, the US engaged several other objects over North America, but both the US and Canadian governments have refused to provide any information on what they were. Some leap to the idea that non-human intelligences are at work. It may be fun to think of aliens or interdimensional beings, but this is not really a satisfying answer to the UAP question. Perhaps the "best" explanation we have is drones, or other known technologies. This still seems partial, as some phenomena seem to exceed current technological capabilities, and it leaves open the question of who is behind the phenomena. Perhaps operated by "friendly" forces – within the US military or allied defense manufacturers – testing their products against unknowing military operators to see how they respond. Similarly, the incidents may be tests carried out by adversaries such as China. But still, some incidents are hard to explain given what we know about physics. The object in the "Tic Tac" videos doesn't seem to behave how any kind of aircraft should, showing no signs of propulsion. However, this too seems partial – there are multiple cases of warplanes and warships detecting these objects with multiple sensors operating on different frequencies, both actively and passively. This lack of answers is perhaps the most intriguing part.
