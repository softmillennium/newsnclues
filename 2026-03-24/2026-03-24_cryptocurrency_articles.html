Earth.org states that “blockchain is a decentralized, distributed ledger technology that securely records transactions across a number of computers.” By spreading data out across computers, referred to as “nodes” in the context of blockchain, information stored on these chains is generally immutable. As such, they cannot be changed without altering all subsequent blocks and acquiring network consensus. These properties typically help make blockchain inherently transparent and secure, in turn setting up blockchain to be particularly useful for operations that require transparency without sacrificing speed or sustainability. Tracking sustainability indicators like fuel consumption can be challenging, especially as supply chains become more complex and interwoven over time. Through blockchain technology, businesses are able to make supply chains more transparent since blockchain excels in recording information that cannot be tampered with after the fact. This information may then inform whether companies are following through on their sustainability claims, ultimately improving overall accountability within a given system. When properly managed, self-contained crypto-based reward systems can potentially serve as an effective means of incentivizing certain actions, as is the case with recycling and public transport use. Blockchain technology can help municipalities track waste from its source to disposal. By encouraging these actions with crypto-based tokens, towns and cities can potentially build local circular economies. In turn, these economies potentially offer citizens a new way to earn goods and services in exchange for supporting sustainable habits. Since blockchain networks operate using multiple, distributed nodes, they have no central entity and can therefore theoretically exist anywhere with an internet connection. As such, blockchain technology basically allows for a number of decentralized applications, including resource management. It's also thought that blockchain may support the development of decentralized energy grids to help store surplus energy sources from renewable means, namely solar panels and wind turbines. This excess energy could potentially be sold, traded or bought by consumers, incentivizing the adoption of renewables and possibly cutting down on transmission losses. Blockchain may also be able to assist in the real-time monitoring of gas, water and electricity usage via smart meters. Some cryptocurrency networks, such as Solana, can potentially play an important role in supporting green blockchain projects. Solana is known for its ability to host decentralized apps (dApps) and other blockchain functions. It has the infrastructure needed to easily develop eco-focused applications. The energy costs for these developments on other networks could be a concern, which is why Solana may be particularly well-suited for these kinds of projects. Hybrid blockchain models like Solana's may be a powerful solution in this regard. Blockchain is still fairly new and therefore remains in a gray space, legally speaking. Despite its novelty, blockchain holds promise as a potential solution to many energy and transparency-related concerns. Its inherent immutability and decentralized nature make blockchain simultaneously flexible and impenetrable as needed, making it a strong candidate for implementation in future sustainable technologies.
