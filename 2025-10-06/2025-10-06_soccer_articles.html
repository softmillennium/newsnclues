2026 World Cup price jumps kick in early, with FIFA raising costs amid high demand
Source: {'href': 'https://www.nytimes.com', 'title': 'The New York Times'}    Published: 2025-10-06 23:31:18

Demand for World Cup tickets is surging despite sky-high prices. FIFA raised ticket prices for at least nine 2026 World Cup games after just one full day of sales, in response to demand that seems undeterred by record-setting price tags. The assumption was that most price adjustments would occur after the Dec. 5 World Cup draw, when matchups will be set and demand will increase for games involving popular teams. But on Friday, in the second window of the very first lottery phase, fans logged into FIFA's ticketing portal and already saw some prices that were slightly higher than the ones first revealed Wednesday. On Monday, in the third window, there were at least a few more upticks. According to screenshots sent by fans to The Athletic, Category 1 prices also increased Friday by a similar amount — roughly 5% — for three of the four quarterfinals; at least three Round of 16 matches; the first Round of 32 match at SoFi Stadium in Southern California; and the USMNT's third group game, also at SoFi. For the Round of 16 game at MetLife Stadium in New Jersey, the Category 1 price jumped from $895 on Wednesday to $935 on Friday, then to $980 on Monday — at which point the game's entire first-phase allotment sold out. The adjustments, the availability of tickets after 48 hours of purchasing, and exorbitant price listings on resale sites, suggest that some fans are more than willing to pay the prices that others have deemed “astonishing” and “unacceptable.” Multiple fans who won the opportunity to purchase tickets on Monday told The Athletic that they ultimately didn't buy any — in some cases because prices were too high, in other cases because, after less than three full days of sales, nearly all coveted tickets were gone. By Monday evening, none were available for the Round of 16, quarterfinals, semifinals or final. FIFA made around 1 million tickets — roughly 15% of the total inventory — available in this first “Visa presale” phase. (More will be available for all games in later phases.) From a pool of around 4.5 million entrants, it randomly selected a lucky few for the first of several time slots: Wednesday at 11 a.m. But within a few hours of the curtain raising last Wednesday, before most fans got through a digital queue, tickets to the final — priced from $2,030 to $6,730 — were no longer available. By Wednesday night, the thousands of tickets made available for Mexico's three group games were also gone. By the end of that first 24-hour window, nearly all Category 4 allotments and many Category 3 allotments — the cheaper categories — had also sold out. The sales likely confirmed suspicions that, despite the backlash to FIFA's initial prices, they were actually an underestimate of market value and demand. “We will adapt prices as per the demand we see, as per the remaining inventory,” a FIFA official said last month. FIFA oversees global soccer and funnels a majority of its revenue back into the sport, and has been capitalizing on that excess World Cup demand in a variety of ways. On Thursday, FIFA also launched its World Cup ticket resale platform, where some of Wednesday's buyers quickly listed tickets for several times the price they'd paid a day earlier. As millions of fans were shut out of this first lottery phase, anxiously awaiting acceptance emails that haven't yet come, FIFA has sold many “RTB” tokens for hundreds or thousands of dollars apiece. They promise buyers the ability to purchase one or two World Cup tickets to a specific match, in an unknown section, for a to-be-determined full price. “Owning an RTB does not include the cost of the ticket(s) itself,” FIFA Collect clarifies on an FAQ page. “It only gives you access to purchase the ticket(s).” On Saturday, FIFA Collect went to market with its latest batch of RTBs. There were 12 available for Mexico's third group match, priced at $3,999 each, and they quickly sold out. And according to spinoff websites, last week and the mid-September week of ticket lottery applications were FIFA Collect's two most lucrative weeks on record, with over $2 million in primary sales — mostly of RTBs — in each seven-day span. RTB holders do not yet know when, or at what price, they'll be able to actually purchase tickets. A third lottery phase will then open after the World Cup draw in December. In this Visa presale phase, more entrants could be selected for purchasing windows between now and Oct. 21, but supply is waning. And only three matches — two in Kansas City, one in Santa Clara, California (near San Francisco) — had any standard Category 3 availability. Henry Bushnell is a senior writer for The Athletic covering soccer.

Is the 2026 World Cup Only for the Rich? Fans Slam Soaring Ticket Prices
Source: {'href': 'https://www.newsweek.com', 'title': 'Newsweek'}    Published: 2025-10-06 19:02:00

If you're planning to attend the 2026 FIFA World Cup, prepare for sticker shock. And that is before prices begin rising through FIFA's controversial new dynamic pricing system. Final tickets in East Rutherford, New Jersey, are now starting around $6,300. On the resale market, some are already listed for more than $25,000. Supporters and politicians argue FIFA, despite being a nonprofit, is prioritizing revenue over access. The system, familiar to fans of U.S. sports and air travel but largely foreign to European and South American soccer, adjusts ticket prices based on real-time demand. In Qatar, the highest price for a final ticket was $1,607. “This is the first time in history FIFA has used dynamic pricing for a World Cup,” said Heimo Schirgi, FIFA's Chief Operating Officer for 2026, in an interview with ESPN. Under dynamic pricing, ticket prices rise or fall depending on demand, but few fans know what they're buying when sales begin. With the tournament schedule still incomplete, tickets are sold without team assignments. That uncertainty makes early buying a gamble, but one many are still willing to take. For some, this World Cup could be the last chance to witness greatness. Lionel Messi and his Argentina squad have already qualified. FIFA President Gianni Infantino has defended the pricing model, pointing to examples where they have said dynamic pricing has helped fans. At the 2023 Club World Cup, also held in the U.S., tickets for a semifinal between Chelsea and Fluminense dropped from $474 to just $13 within days. But fan groups say that's little comfort for those who bought early. In East Rutherford, many were frustrated when they paid top dollar for what turned out to be an underwhelming matchup. Others who waited bought tickets for less than the cost of a stadium beer. “This is not ‘make football truly global,' this is the privatization of what was once a tournament open to all,” said Ronan Evain, executive director of Football Supporters Europe, in an interview with The Athletic. “FIFA's leadership seems unable to understand that it needs fans in the stands. None of this exists when you set such prices.” Some tickets to the final have already been listed for more than $25,000. At previous World Cups, combined fees were closer to 10 percent. FIFA defends the model as aligned with standard ticketing practices in North America. In public statements, the organization has said that revenues from both primary and secondary sales will be reinvested into global soccer development. It has set a $13 billion revenue target for the 2023 to 2026 cycle, up from $7.6 billion during the four years leading to Qatar. Some economists have argued that the approach, while unpopular, is economically sound. “FIFA deserves a lot of criticism,” they wrote, “but its pricing proposal at least acknowledges the real-world trade-offs that this complex event must navigate.” Prices for similar seats can vary widely depending on when a ticket is purchased. For supporters with limited means, that unpredictability could make access less equal. Democratic New York State Assemblyman and mayoral hopeful Zohran Mamdani has become one of the most vocal political critics of FIFA's strategy. He launched a campaign titled “Game Over Greed,” calling on FIFA to end dynamic pricing, reinstate resale caps and reserve 15 percent of tickets for local residents at discounted prices. “The biggest sporting event in the world is happening in our backyard,” Mamdani said, “but most New Yorkers will be priced out of watching it live.” As pressure builds, FIFA has shown no signs of altering course.
