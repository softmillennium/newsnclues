Desperate to find a glimmer of originality in a story previously told, screenwriters seek out new ways to revive dead killers for another round of slash and hack. Yet every time they come back, their knives get duller. To compensate for these diminishing returns, horror sequels will often empower their villains with supernatural abilities that justify resurrection at the expense of scares. The moment Halloween II reveals Michael as Laurie Strode's brother, the series begins a slow and inevitable decline into schlock. Like much of its throwback slasher antics, Black Phone 2 follows a similar trend. This time they're at the frigid Alpine Lake summer camp, which just so happens to be the camp their mother, Hope (Anna Lore), used to work at. Afflicted with expository dreams that point her to local murders, Hope uses her shining to glean that The Grabber was killing Alpine campers. Black Phone 2 emphasizes Gwen's power more than before, using her dreams to fill in Black Phone backstory and stage Wes Craven-inspired action sequences in which the Grabber, now a ghost, attacks Gwen in her dreams. While these sequences make Black Phone 2 play like a remake of A Nightmare On Elm Street, it's really the Halloween sequels that the movie is grabbing from. They were latchkey kids of the late '70s whose family life was torn apart by their mother's suicide. Meanwhile, the otherworldly powers at play stood on a firmer foundation, doubling as a metaphor for the kids finding inner strength and determination against their attackers. Black Phone 2 makes him an all-powerful being with ice skates for feet who can only be killed by following a specific set of rules: Find the dead campers' bodies, and the Grabber will return to Hell. It's easy to understand why some horror filmmakers go this route for their accidental franchises. The Saw movies made a complicated mess of their timeline by justifying how Jigsaw kept making a comeback every year. Black Phone 2 joins other supernaturally bent sequels, like Halloween and Friday The 13th, to stretch out the longevity of a series that never thought it'd be a series. These killers are at their scariest when they exist in familiar worlds, ones the audience can identify—like an everyday summer camp terrorized by a disgruntled employee, or a neighborhood menaced by a senseless killer. Chicago Shakespeare Theatre's “Paranormal Activity,” with Cher Álvarez/Photo: Kyle Flubacker This fascination continues to this day, particularly in film, with “found footage” techniques in “The Blair Witch Project” (1999) and “Paranormal Activity” (2007) heightening the horror through a new sense of realism. In time for Halloween, Chicago Shakespeare Theater makes horror more real still in “Paranormal Activity,” running through November 2, a stage adaptation of the hit film by Oren Peli. Directed by Felix Barrett, the play uses practical special effects, intense sound design and horror-trope-inspired lighting techniques to provide a sense of realism that is unmatchable in film. A new story by Levi Holloway, of a young couple who move to another country to escape the demons of their past, is a fresh take on the franchise, folding elements from the film into the story in ways that are surprising and unexpected. On the heel of their one-year wedding anniversary, wife Lou (Cher Álvarez) and husband James (Patrick Heusinger) have recently moved from the United States to England for a fresh start. The couple's ostensible happiness is fake, and it is revealed that Lou suffers from depression because of terrifying phenomena that only she can perceive. But when James starts hearing invisible hands knocking at the door and seeing ghostly lights creeping around hallways, it becomes clear that it's not all in Lou's head. Chicago Shakespeare Theater's “Paranormal Activity,” with Patrick Heusinger/Photo: Kyle Flubacker From that point, anything can happen, and your eyes will greedily inspect every nook of the setting—a bifurcated, two-story, five-room house—for the twitch of a shadow or the reappearance of a dreadful ghoul. Heusinger plays James as a masculine “bro,” whose bulging muscles are of no use against an ephemeral attacker. Álvarez dials the creep factor to eleven as she gradually morphs Lou from an anxious young woman into a demon-possessed succubus. In all, the play comes together as a satisfying blend of fantasy and reality. Waves of excitement rushed through the crowd during centerpiece reveals. The experience was like watching a horror flick in one's living room with (hundreds) of friends. The not-so-obvious trickery of American spiritualism is revived with gusto in “Paranormal Activity,” a sensational start to the spooky season. “Paranormal Activity” runs through November 2 at Chicago Shakespeare Theater, 800 East Grand.
