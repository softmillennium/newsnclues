This has given rise to a series of new workplace trends from the viral "Gen Z stare", to the "office siren" aesthetic and even TikTok slang like "ick" becoming normalized in professional lingo. The National Museum of the US Air Force in Dayton, Ohio, features around 350 planes and missiles in its collection, but there's one aircraft in the World War II Gallery that attracts a particularly large crowd. Bockscar, a Boeing B-29 Superfortress bomber, dropped the Fat Man atomic bomb on Nagasaki in 1945, leading to Japan's surrender and the end of World War II. When accompanied by a museum volunteer, visitors can walk under the plane to see where the 10,000-pound bomb was loaded. The bombing of Nagasaki initially killed an estimated 40,000 people and injured around 60,000 more, according to the US Department of Energy's Office of History and Heritage Resources. Japan didn't surrender after the US dropped the first atomic bomb on Hiroshima on August 6, 1945. After Bockscar dropped a second atomic bomb on Nagasaki on August 9, the emperor of Japan announced their surrender on August 15, bringing World War II to a close. Additional factors, such as Japan's weakened military resources and the Soviet Union's invasion of Manchuria on August 9, are also understood to have contributed to Japan's surrender. Bockscar was based at Wendover Army Air Field in Utah. The plane flew from Utah to Tinian in the Northern Mariana Islands and then from Tinian to Nagasaki. Bock normally flew Bockscar, but switched planes with Maj. Charles Sweeney, commander of the 393rd Bomb Squadron, for the bombing of Nagasaki. At Nagasaki, Bock flew The Great Artiste, and Sweeney piloted Bockscar. The Fat Man atomic bomb weighed 10,000 pounds and exploded with 20,000 tons of TNT. The museum refurbished a demilitarized Mk III bomb to look like the Fat Man, which was painted yellow so that it could easily be seen after it was dropped. Lt. Charles Levy took this colorized photo of the Fat Man's explosion from aboard The Great Artiste. The exact number of people who died in the bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki will never be known. The "high" estimate, which anti-nuclear weapons scientists and Japan released in the 1970s, counted 140,000 deaths at Hiroshima and 70,000 at Nagasaki for a total of 210,000 lives lost.

They call it the "postmodern cycle," which they say is the fourth long secular bull run since World War II. The strategists see a structural shift in stocks, with new factors starting to drive returns — and by extension, new opportunities for investors. "The reason we called it the 'Postmodern' cycle is that we thought it would have elements that mirrored experiences of historical cycles but would also contain distinct and unique drivers that would shape investment opportunities differently," the Goldman strategists wrote. There are three things that differentiate the postmodern period from previous structural eras, according to Goldman. New structural bull markets tend to start off with stocks at low valuations. That differs from the past two super cycles, where interest rates generally trended lower. The government will likely continue to run up its balance sheet, Goldman said. That differs from past periods, such as during former President Bill Clinton's term in the late 1990s, when the US budget was in a surplus for the first time in decades. Higher debt levels are another factor driving up borrowing costs in the economy. "Each of these may weigh on aggregate corporate profits, although we are likely to experience wider differences between winners and losers," strategists said. While those changes are largely a net negative on equity prices, that's not to say there won't be bright areas of the market for investors. The bank also highlighted three "mega trends" that it believed could offer "significant" opportunities for investors: US tech stocks have largely been responsible for powering the overall market to fresh records in recent years, and large firms are still shelling out big to invest in AI. Top 5 Things People Do Wrong When Asking for Promotions or Raises

Over the course of my career at companies like Nordstrom, Verizon, and Yahoo, I've hired, developed, and evaluated hundreds of employees. Many of them were smart, capable, and ambitious. If you think the group that got their requests granted was innately more talented or harder working than the group that didn't, think again. Promotions and pay raises are business decisions, not gifts. This means that even the strongest performers will hear "not now" if they ignore the situations that surround them. Here are five of the most common timing missteps I've seen, and what I wish more rising leaders would do differently. Someone gets promoted in January and, by April, they're asking for another raise. I once had a high performer who was promoted to senior manager after a solid year. Less than 90 days later, she sent me a proposal for another title change — accompanied by comp benchmarks and a list of expanded responsibilities. She was doing more, but she hadn't been in the role long enough to demonstrate success at that level, and she lacked the self-awareness that no one should ask for more every quarter. Understand your career path and the next opportunity for growth. Then, give the business time to see your impact before you ask to level up again. I've seen people use early 1:1s to talk about their career goals. before the manager even understands your role (and hers! ), it can create the perception that you're only thinking about yourself. Make yourself even more valuable by showing that you can lead patiently through uncertainty. Be the person who brings stability when others are scrambling. Every once in a while, someone has come to me unexpectedly and said, "I'm ready for the next level." If we hadn't been talking about growth, readiness, or performance over time, it could be challenging to make it happen quickly. Big asks shouldn't come out of nowhere. Depending on other career path timelines you've observed, your own performance, and the standard cycle of mid-year and year-end performance conversations, you might start talking about your career goals once you've been in your role for 6-12 months. Make career growth part of the regular conversation. Use 1:1s with your manager or HR to ask what overdelivering looks like. Get clarity on what's expected at the next level. That way, when you're ready to make a formal ask, your manager's already on the same page. It's usually after you've shown clear results, earned credibility, and positioned yourself inside the broader business picture.
