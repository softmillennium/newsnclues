I tried to buy World Cup tickets. Six hours later, here's what I found.
Source: {'href': 'https://www.usatoday.com', 'title': 'USA Today'}    Published: 2026-04-03 14:19:00

FIFA may have expanded its 2026 World Cup ticket access this week, but the selection is often far from comprehensive — and almost never cheap. On Thursday April 2, I took my turn waiting in FIFA's queue, only to find a very small and very pricey selection of tickets available to purchase. Later, I went to the back of the line to enter FIFA's new resale platform and found many more tickets, but not many bargains. My experience came just a day after FIFA opened World Cup tickets for purchase on a first-come, first-served basis. The so-called "last-minute" sales phase is the first time that tickets are available for purchase immediately ‒ rather than through a lottery. FIFA cautioned fans to check its ticket website regularly, as not every open seat would be available right away. But I wouldn't have guessed just how few tickets actually would be there when I logged in. After periodically checking a very slowly spinning wheel that progressively got smaller as I got closer to the front of the line, I finally had access to buy tickets by 1:39 p.m. The cheapest option was a block of Category 1 tickets for the South Africa vs. South Korea game for $450. Only Category 1 seats were available for the team's opener, behind either goal. After 15 minutes without buying anything, FIFA's system automatically ejected me. One had access to the Iraq vs. Norway game in Boston, with only $500 seats open. Another found Cape Verde-Saudi Arabia tickets for the relatively low price of $140. Another had USA-Paraguay access with tickets ranging from $1,120 to $2,735. After checking out the offerings from FIFA's standard ticketing portal, I went to the back of the line and waited another three hours for a chance to use its in-house resale/exchange marketplace. According to FIFA, its marketplace "enables fans who can no longer attend matches to sell their tickets to other fans in a secure and authorized environment, safeguarding against invalid or unauthorized resale." In other words, this is FIFA's answer to third-party resellers like the StubHubs and SeatGeeks of the world. And very much like the standard platform, most were quite pricey. These less expensive seats have been difficult to find throughout previous phases, as FIFA emphasizes the more expensive Category 1 and 2 tickets. Category 4 tickets for the USA-Paraguay game still ranged from $644 to $4,024. How could Category 4 seats to the USA-Paraguay match ($4,024) be so much more than Category 1 seats in FIFA's standard platform ($2,735)? It could be a seller looking to artificially inflate the market, or someone who actually wants to go to the game but couldn't turn down that offer if it arrived. Either way, just because someone lists tickets for a certain price doesn't mean someone will buy them at that price. As an example, there were some games that would theoretically be on the cheaper end of the scale that were not. The cheapest tickets for an Ecuador-Curaçao match were Category 3 seats that ranged from $207 to $2,875. There were also Category 4 tickets for the USA-Turkey game that started at $161. In the current landscape, that is sadly something of a bargain. Prices could still fluctuate wildly between now and the tournament. But overall, both the standard ticket portal and resale marketplace paint a bleak picture. For a tournament that promised a large range of affordable tickets during the bid process, it has turned into a venture designed to milk every last cent from the paying public. As The Athletic has noted, early returns from FIFA's dynamic pricing model — supposedly a venture that could benefit fans if games have low demand — have mostly resulted in higher prices. Though FIFA's marketplace is designed to take the place of third-party resellers, that doesn't mean those sites aren't in the business of World Cup ticket sales. Adidas has come a long way from the stereotypical 32-panel black-and-white checkered ball.
