UCLA's latest Hollywood Diversity Report found that as the number of streaming films decreased, so did minority employment across directors, writers, leads and overall cast. As budgets tighten, opportunities for filmmakers of underrepresented backgrounds are always the first to be squeezed out.” They believe diversity is treated as expendable or optional with budgets are cut. No score yet, be the first to add. Much is made in current dance culture about electronic music as an outlet for queer joy. Earlier this year, for example, when Charli XCX declared that “the dancefloor is dead” in her latest news-cycle-consuming PR stunt, it provoked worldwide outcry from DJs and producers who argued that denying dance music's modern ubiquity robs Black and queer communities of the ecstatic temples that they built, across decades, as an antidote to adversity. And while that retort isn't inaccurate, it can be a little unintentionally reductivist. These emotions are just as central to the Black queer experience as joy, and some of the most interesting electronic music—Lotic's eerie experimental R&B noise, SHERELLE's breakneck acid house, Jasmine Infiniti's dark industrial techno—comes from producers who are unafraid to peer into the obscured corners of their own identity and reflect back what they find. Dion McKenzie, who produces and DJs under the name TYGAPAW, is one such artist. Born in Mandeville, Jamaica, and based in Brooklyn, their sprawling techno compositions take cues from dancehall, experimental electro, industrial, hardcore, and footwork to plumb the extremes of Black club music. Their third album, the acronymically titled Together You Gather All Power Applied Worldwide, takes the form of an at times exuberant, at times unsettling amusement park dark ride through the history of the African diaspora, dragging you through room after room of piercing synths and bone-rattling beats. No score yet, be the first to add. On the album's first track, “Can I live”, Nigerian-American artist and poet Precious Okoyomon recites a litany that begins as a call for self-betterment: “May I never be afraid of anyone, especially myself,” she says, over a low-boil, droning synth loop. Synthwork is one of TYGAPAW's fortes, and they frequently use sharp frequencies, jittery arpeggios, and ghostly, atonal lines to maintain an atmosphere of cold paranoia. All of the vocals on Together You Gather All Power Applied Worldwide are spoken-word and delivered by Black female artists, like a Greek chorus, providing an array of stark and personal perspectives on race and gender. The album's two standout tracks are arresting for opposite reasons. On “Exorcise the Language of Dominion,” TYGAPAW finally lets us experience the aforementioned queer joy, as artist, DJ, and writer Juliana Huxtable quasi-raps surreal lines like “If I throw my pussy in the air, it'll turn into sunshine? Ok…” It maintains the album's intensity, but its sense of play is a welcome respite. TYGAPAW started their gender transition in 2023, right as they were beginning work on Together You Gather All Power Applied Worldwide. It's loud and bright, but it's not an easy listen, which makes the closer, “Black Trans Masculine Experience (Instrumental)” indispensable to the album's story. While soothing, the song isn't merely a comedown. Traveling through the album's darkness is necessary to arrive at this sense of peace and with every synth stab and pounding beat, TYGAPAW gets closer to ushering in the world they wish to inhabit.
