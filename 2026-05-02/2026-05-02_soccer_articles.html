Dallas gets the World Cup, but FIFA controls the stage and match-day operations
Source: {'href': 'https://www.dallasnews.com', 'title': 'Dallas News'}    Published: 2026-05-02 12:04:49

When FIFA takes over the AT&T Stadium in Arlington for matches beginning in June, it controls access, branding and the match-day environment. That split turns the run-up into a long pregame, with Dallas largely responsible for making it work and FIFA stepping in to control the main stage. The work is laid out in more than 240 pages of a World Cup hosting agreement and related documents reviewed by The Dallas Morning News, offering one of the clearest looks yet at how responsibilities are divided. The state ordered release, allowing limited redactions for financial and proprietary details. The documents show FIFA pays certain fees, including stadium rental, but Dallas, as the “host city authority,” agreed to cover its obligations, mostly at its own expense. City Council member Chad West said the World Cup presents a balance, with real costs but potential gains for small businesses and sales tax revenue. West said his main concern is transportation, particularly how people will get around. Many fans are from cities “where you can walk or take public transit,” he said. The agreement splits transportation duties, with most public-facing responsibilities falling to local authorities. The host must provide game-day transit capable for large crowds, while also managing transit hubs, signage and traffic controls across the region. FIFA, meanwhile, covers dedicated transportation for its own officials, staff and teams. The agreement requires Dallas to deliver security “in accordance with applicable standards,” working with local and federal agencies to establish controlled perimeters, screening points and restricted zones around the stadium and event sites. It also requires a “clean stadium,” stripping out outside advertising and controlling what can be seen and sold not just inside the venue, but throughout a broader surrounding area including neighboring buildings, parking lots and streets. FIFA controls major game-day revenues, including ticketing and merchandise.
