The proposed EHRChain framework addresses these challenges by incorporating advanced extensions to the baseline ABE model, specifically to support: Revocation of access rights – removing user privileges promptly when their authorization status changes or security risks are detected. Under conventional ABE, decryption is rigidly policy-bound: if a user attribute set does not match the embedded access policy, access is unequivocally denied. While this ensures strict adherence to predefined policies, it can hinder timely interventions in acute medical emergencies, where treating physicians or authorized responders may require immediate access to life-critical patient data. This attribute is issued by a designated authority, such as a hospital administrator, security officer, or a smart contract-governed trust entity, following the verification of emergency conditions. Time-bound validity: Emergency access credentials are embedded with cryptographically enforced expiration timestamps, automatically revoking privileges after the predefined duration (e.g., 30 minutes to 2 hours), thereby preventing prolonged or unmonitored access. Scope-limited access: Emergency attributes are strictly bound to relevant datasets or patient records associated with the ongoing critical event, minimizing unnecessary data exposure. Immutable on-chain auditability: Each emergency access request, approval, and usage instance is permanently recorded in the blockchain ledger, enabling post-incident forensic analysis, regulatory compliance verification, and deterrence against misuse. Multi-factor verification: Prior to attribute issuance, an additional verification layer–such as biometric confirmation or secondary authorization from another administrator–can be enforced to strengthen trust in the emergency request. This design ensures that emergency access is operationally efficient while preserving the overarching privacy and compliance guarantees of the system. Access revocation presents a well-documented challenge in traditional ABE systems, as the attribute–ciphertext relationship is static and re-encryption typically requires system-wide re-keying. In healthcare settings, where staff assignments, operational roles, and trust statuses can change frequently, this limitation can result in security risks if not addressed effectively. To overcome this, EHRChain incorporates a dual-layered revocation strategy: Expiration-based key validity: All attribute keys are issued with cryptographically embedded expiration timestamps. Users must periodically request renewed keys, which are only issued if their current role and authorization remain valid, thereby automatically removing access for inactive or reassigned personnel. Proxy-assisted re-encryption: Upon the occurrence of a revocation event, such as role reassignment, employment termination, or detected key compromise, a trusted proxy entity (e.g., a hospital blockchain node) re-encrypts affected ciphertexts using updated access policies. This ensures that previously valid credentials cannot be used to decrypt data post-revocation, without necessitating full re-keying of the entire user base. The integration of proxy-assisted re-encryption provides a scalable and resource-efficient mechanism for dynamic policy enforcement, essential for large-scale healthcare deployments. The practical realization of the proposed EHRChain framework is grounded in healthcare-specific regulatory, operational, and compliance imperatives, ensuring its suitability for deployment in real-world clinical environments. The framework adopts a permissioned blockchain architecture in conjunction with a consortium-governed IPFS network, enabling authenticated participation within a secure and controlled trust domain. This design not only mitigates the risk of unauthorized node inclusion but also inherently satisfies the stringent privacy, security, and accountability requirements associated with the management of sensitive electronic health information. From a regulatory perspective, EHRChain is architected to align with internationally recognized compliance regimes, including the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) in the United States, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in the European Union, and established interoperability specifications such as HL7 and Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR). Conformance with these standards is achieved through the integration of Attribute-Based Encryption (ABE) for fine-grained, policy-driven access control; immutable blockchain-anchored transaction logs for verifiable auditability; and the off-chain storage of encrypted EHR data within IPFS to prevent unauthorized data disclosure. Collectively, these mechanisms safeguard the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of medical records while supporting comprehensive compliance auditing. Operational viability is reinforced through the modular and standards-compliant architecture of the framework, which facilitates seamless integration with existing Hospital Information Systems (HIS) and Regional Health Information Exchanges (HIE) without necessitating disruptive infrastructure replacement. This integration model reduces deployment complexity and cost while preserving continuity of care and existing clinical workflows. Furthermore, as demonstrated in “Scalability and latency analysis of the EHRChain framework”, empirical performance evaluations confirm that EHRChain sustains high throughput and low latency transaction processing under varying concurrent user loads, validating its scalability and robustness for large-scale healthcare ecosystems. In addition, governance policies embedded within the permissioned network enforce role-based participation, transaction validation rules, and jurisdiction-specific compliance constraints. These measures ensure that deployment across multiple healthcare organizations can be tailored to local regulatory requirements while maintaining interoperability across the broader ecosystem. As such, EHRChain not only offers a technically robust solution but also demonstrates regulatory and operational readiness for secure, scalable, and privacy-preserving electronic health record management in diverse healthcare settings. This research presents a comprehensive evaluation of a novel framework aimed at enhancing the security and efficiency of blockchain-based medical record management systems. Utilizing Ethereum smart contracts and detailed transaction payload analysis, the study explores various aspects of performance, including execution time, latency, and throughput. Our findings indicate that the proposed framework offers substantial improvements over existing methods. Through rigorous testing using tools like Remix, Metamask, and the Rinkeby test network, we have shown that the framework can handle complex transactions with improved speed and reduced delays. The detailed analysis of transaction payloads and their encoding demonstrates the robustness and scalability of our approach, ensuring that the system can support a high volume of transactions while maintaining security and integrity. Furthermore, the study provides insights into the costs associated with Ethereum transactions, offering a clear understanding of the economic implications of deploying such a framework in real-world scenarios. The research introduces a robust framework to enhance the security and efficiency of blockchain-based medical record management systems, showcasing significant performance improvements in execution time, latency, and throughput. Future studies could also delve into optimizing the gas consumption and transaction fees associated with Ethereum, making the framework more cost-effective for large-scale deployments. Another potential direction is the development of more sophisticated algorithms for dynamic access control, ensuring that only authorized users can access specific medical records based on evolving policies and user roles. Finally, real-world implementation and testing in healthcare environments will be crucial to validate the framework's practical utility and identify any additional challenges that may arise in a production setting. These future efforts will help in refining the framework, making it a viable solution for secure and efficient medical record management on the blockchain.
