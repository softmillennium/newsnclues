These forms can be created in minutes, with clean and clear formatting, official-looking images and video, and—most importantly of all—a genuine Google Docs URL that your web browser will see no problem with. Scammers can then use these authentic-looking forms to ask for payment details or login information. Students and staff at Stanford University were among those targeted with a Google Forms link that asked for login details for the academic portal there, and the attack beat standard email malware protection. These scams can take a variety of guises, but they'll typically start with a phishing email that will try to trick you into believing it's an official and genuine communication. Our inboxes are regularly filled with requests to reset passwords, verify details, or otherwise take action. Even worse, the instigating email might actually come from a legitimate email address, if someone in your social circle, family, or office has had their account hijacked. This email (or perhaps a direct message on social media) will be used to deliver a Google Forms link, which is the second half of the scam. This form will most often be set up to look genuine, and may be trying to spoof a recognized site like your place of work or your bank. The form might prompt you for sensitive information, offer up a link to malware, or feature a phone number or email address to lead you into further trouble. It's also easy for scammers to quickly close and reopen these forms on different random URLs, making it harder for them to be caught by security software. Since they're free to create, scammers aren't losing anything by making vast numbers of them. The same set of common sense measures are usually enough to keep yourself safe against most scams, including this one. Be wary of any unexpected communications, like unusual requests for friends or password reset processes you haven't yourself triggered. In general, you shouldn't be entering any login information or payment details into a Google Forms document (it will start with docs.google.com in your browser's address bar). These forms may look reasonably well presented, but they'll lack any advanced formatting or layouts, and will feature Submit and Clear form buttons at the bottom. Google Forms should also have either a “never submit passwords” or “content is neither created nor endorsed by Google” message at the bottom, depending on how the form has been set up. These are all signs you can look for, even if the link to the form appears to have come from a trusted source. And if you're being asked for important information, then get in touch with that source directly. Even just knowing that this kind of scam exists is a step toward being better protected. As always, keeping your mobile and desktop software up-to-date helps too. This won't necessarily flag a suspect form, for the reasons we've already mentioned, but it should mean that any malicious links you're directed to are recognized. When you're not wearing it, store your sleep mask in a secure location such as inside your nightstand or a dresser drawer to keep dust and dander buildup at bay. Also, tempting as it is, try not to perch it on your head in the morning—hair oils, sweat, and hair products can transfer onto the mask that way. Avoid leaving a sleep mask in direct sunlight or hot spots, such as a windowsill or car dashboard, since heat and UV exposure can wear down sleep mask materials like silk, elastic, foam, and gel. If you're taking it on the go, keep it in a small pouch or case to protect it from dirt and grime in your bag and to prevent straps from getting tangled or stretched.
