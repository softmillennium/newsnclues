The exchange of insults last week between Pakistan Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar and Afghan Foreign Minister Amir Khan Muttaqi was perhaps ordinary diplomatic sparring on the surface but more than quietly being an in-secret rebalancing of regional diplomacy. This dialogue is proof of a strategic re-think between Kabul and Islamabad on the connectivity of the region and shared economic interests. It is centered on the Uzbekistan-Afghanistan-Pakistan (UAP) rail corridor project—a game-changing corridor that will redefine the economic and geopolitics of Central and South Asia. The bilateral Pakistan-Afghanistan relationship has been war-, suspicion-, and security-narrative-oriented so far. Both countries are eager to experiment with a more pragmatic relationship founded not on ideology or security but mutual strategic and economic interests. The UAP railroad is one actual platform for that change. It's a simple but persuasive idea: construct a fast, efficient, and inexpensive transportation route that reduces trade friction and opens up new economic opportunities for the three nations. To Central Asian landlocked nations, UAP line presents a worthy substitute for the conventional northward directions through Russia. This change of focus also aligns with a larger tendency in international politics, insofar as states increasingly seek cooperation based on converging economic interests, not ideological compatibility. The UAP project aligns with such thinking in promoting connectivity instead of confrontation. It does so in attempting to replicate a form of “functional institutionalism” for regional cooperation on infrastructure, trade, and logistics. Afghanistan, long perceived as instability and uncertainty, is instead being repackaged as not a hinderance but a transit necessity—central to the corridor's success, and regional stability more broadly. When Foreign Minister Dar traveled to Kabul in April 2025, talks focused on outlining the parameters of UAP. The latest follow-up phone call is evidence of continuity of purpose, and this would suggest that both sides recognize long-term benefit in entrenching cooperation. These developments also coincide with growing regional investment interest from China in developing infrastructure, notably as part of its Belt and Road Initiative (BRI). Although the UAP railway is not officially a BRI project, its strategic needs are extremely complementary to China's master plan of regional integration and therefore makes it even more geopolitically relevant. Albeit in words, assurances notwithstanding, Pakistan cannot be sure the Taliban-infested regime would be in a position to contain militancy, especially from groups such as the Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP). While Islamabad, Kabul, and Tashkent converge towards each other, India risks being left behind in a growing connectivity matrix. As Turkiye and China have been developing deepening regional interests, Indian passive strategy can turn into isolation unless India reaffirms engagement with its neighbors in a positive way. It offers a forum on which economic realism can replace decades of political rigidity. The corridor does create a form of interdependency, however, that, if well managed, can cut across the political pendulum swings and ideology swings of its member states. This is making way for more integrated diplomacy between both of them, an arena where infrastructure is crafted as a dialogue and stability tool and not necessarily development. As the UAP railway project moves ever more rapidly ahead and new efforts at diplomatic normalisation are underway, a new day can be viewed to be on the horizon for Central and South Asia. A time when geography no longer means fate, but opportunity—when diplomacy flows not merely through embassies and communiqués, but through corridors linking countries, markets, and peoples together. If they are preserved, the UAP railway can also easily become a symbol of what regional cooperation can achieve in an increasingly dominated world by infrastructure and interdependence. Tyrannosauroids were giant apex predators that walked on two legs, had huge heads with sharp teeth and tiny arms. They are part of the larger tyrannosauroid family and were thought to have evolved from smaller species — but until now there has been little fossil evidence to support this idea. The dragon prince lived 86 million years ago and looked much like a tyrannosaur, but it was only about 13 feet (4 meters) long, weighing in at 1,650 pounds (750 kilograms). Many later tyrannosaurs were much bigger, with T. rex reaching 41 feet (12.5 m) long and weighing up to about 23,000 pounds (10,400 kg). The dragon prince also had a smaller head and longer arms compared to later tyrannosaurs. Voris thinks the specimens are small adult individuals, rather than young dinosaurs. He identified a slew of features that are indicators of maturity, including fused-up vertebrae, prominently developed small horns and the nasal bone having a wrinkled texture. Filtering terrestrial contamination in the search for alien signals
Source: {'href': 'https://phys.org', 'title': 'Phys.org'}    Published: 2025-06-11 13:11:04

Image of a radio telescope located at the Green Bank Observatory. This is what a recent study published in The Astronomical Journal hopes to address as a team of researchers investigated how machine learning could be used to search for extraterrestrial technosignatures while simultaneously identifying radio contamination from human radio signals. This study has the potential to help radio astronomers develop more efficient methods in searching for and identifying radio signals from extraterrestrial civilizations. The study was led by Snir Pardo from the School of Physics and Astronomy at Tel-Aviv University in Israel, whose team collaborated with the Breakthrough Listen project to obtain spectrograms from more than 300 stars less than 50 parsecs (163 light-years) from Earth. What makes this study unique is the researchers used a combination of computer models and machine learning algorithms to analyze the data with the goal of identifying candidate signals that could originate from an extraterrestrial technosignature and not from a human source radio signal. We leveraged a minimal set of assumptions and simple operations, in order to search efficiently for technosignatures in radio observations of nearby stars. Future work should explore incorporating adaptive multiscale techniques to further automate the vetting process and expand search efficiency." The Breakthrough Listen project is an astronomy project whose goal is to identify technosignatures of radio and laser communications using the aforementioned Parkes Observatory and Green Bank Observatory while being based at the Berkeley SETI Research Center. While no confirmed technosignatures have yet to be received by scientists on Earth, the closest humanity has come to receiving a signal from another world happened August 15, 1977. Despite several attempts to obtain a similar signal from its supposed origin of the constellation Sagittarius, astronomers couldn't find another signal like it and have yet to receive anything close to the intensity of this original signal. This study demonstrates how the SETI Institute continues to broaden its methods for using radio astronomy in searching for and identifying radio signals from extraterrestrial civilizations, noted as technosignatures here. Aside from using radio astronomy to search for technosignatures, the goal of SETI is to broaden our understanding of searching for life beyond Earth through collaborations on active and retired space missions, including Cassini, New Horizons, Opportunity, Curiosity, Kepler, and TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite). As machine learning continues to advance, so will its ability to potentially identify technosignatures from outside Earth and demonstrate that we might not be alone in the universe. More information: Snir Pardo et al, Using Anomaly Detection to Search for Technosignatures in Breakthrough Listen Observations, The Astronomical Journal (2025).
