Dunamu and Hana Financial Launch Blockchain-Based Remittance Platform With POSCO International
Source: {'href': 'https://financefeeds.com', 'title': 'FinanceFeeds'}    Published: 2026-04-29 13:59:32

Dunamu, the operator of South Korea's largest cryptocurrency exchange, Upbit, has signed a memorandum of understanding with Hana Financial Group and POSCO International to jointly develop a blockchain-based cross-border remittance platform to reduce transfer costs and settlement times for corporate clients. The agreement, signed on Wednesday at Hana Financial Group's headquarters in Seoul, brings together the financial institution's foreign exchange network, POSCO International's global supply chain operations, and Dunamu's proprietary GIWA Chain blockchain infrastructure. Hana Financial Group and Dunamu had already completed a proof-of-concept project in February, successfully replacing traditional SWIFT messages with GIWA Chain across Hana Bank's domestic and overseas branches. POSCO International will lead business applications by leveraging its extensive global trade network as a real-world testing environment. Dunamu will provide the underlying technology infrastructure through GIWA Chain, a layer-2 blockchain designed to handle high transaction volumes while enhancing data privacy. During the proof-of-concept phase, Dunamu also applied its proprietary privacy protocol, BOJAGI. Built on zero-knowledge proof technology, the protocol allows transaction validity to be verified without exposing sensitive financial data, a feature the companies described as critical for regulatory compliance in cross-border financial applications. Dunamu CEO Oh Kyung-seok said he expects GIWA Chain's technology to serve as “a foundation for a more efficient and transparent on-chain financial environment.” Hana Financial Vice Chair Lee Eun-hyung called the agreement “an important turning point in combining digital assets” with the traditional industry and finance. The partnership reflects a growing trend among South Korean financial institutions exploring blockchain infrastructure to modernise cross-border payments. The global remittance market was valued at $860 billion in 2024, according to World Bank data, and industry analysts project that blockchain-based deposit token systems could reduce cross-border settlement costs by 40 to 80 per cent while improving transaction speed by 90 per cent.

Tennessee just became the second state to ban 'crypto ATMs'. Here's why
Source: {'href': 'https://www.tennessean.com', 'title': 'The Tennessean'}    Published: 2026-04-29 12:00:00

Tennessee became the second state in the United States to ban convertible virtual currency (CVC) kiosks in April. CVC kiosks have come under scrutiny for facilitating scams. According to America's Credit Union, "CVC kiosks - also known as 'crypto ATMs' or 'Bitcoin ATMs' - allow customers to exchange government-issued currency (fiat) for cryptocurrency (like Bitcoin)." Online scams are nothing new in the modern world, but these machines make them a bit easier to happen than they used to be. Scammers often impersonate banks or government agencies, claiming unauthorized charges or demanding payment for unpaid taxes and fees. They may also execute tech support scams, falsely alleging that malware has infected the victim's computer and directing them to make payments via a CVC kiosk to fix the issue. The main reason for banning these machines is their high rates of fraud. These kiosks, resembling traditional ATMs, are often located in supermarkets and convenience stores. The bill passed both Tennessee chambers without a single dissenting vote and takes effect on July 1, 2026. After that date, it will be a criminal offense to operate or own one of the machines, including those already installed. According to the new law, a virtual currency kiosk is an electronic terminal that facilitates the exchange of virtual currency for money, bank credit, or other virtual currencies, and can be connected to exchanges or hold digital assets directly. These crypto ATMs are scattered around the state, and while there is no official list, tracking websites let you find locations in your city. So far, Tennessee and Indiana are the only states to pass legislation that fully bans the CVC kiosks. Jordan Green covers trending news for The Commercial Appeal and Tennessee.
