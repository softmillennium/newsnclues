At the age of nineteen, he married his cousin Frida Goldman. In lieu of a traditional dowry, Firer requested funds to publish his first book: Hemdat Tzion (Kraków 1936) – collected analyses of Talmudic passages. After the war, he spent time in a Displaced Persons camp in Ulm, Germany, where he contributed to the reconstitution of Jewish life by opening a Yeshiva. He initially lived in an immigrant transit camp in Pardes Hanna and subsequently moved to Ramat Gan and then to Bnei Brak. In addition to his local rabbinic duties, Firer taught at various Torah institutions, including Yeshivat Kerem B'Yavneh – the first hesder yeshiva that combined advanced Talmudic studies and army service. He began his responsum by pointing out that the Sages had considered the possibility of humans landing on other planets. – it did not impact the question as to whether there was extraterrestrial Halakha. Firer was quick to point this out, and he went looking elsewhere to find a source for halakhic observance in Space. As could be expected, there were no legal sources that dealt with the question directly, so Firer needed to expand the parameters of his search. In order to guide readers, Firer stated his conclusion before presenting his creative argument: “It may indeed be said that there is no obligation to observe Torah and commandments on the Moon. After examining the sources, this is the impression one receives.” **** Angelic Arguments In his quest for sources, Rabbi Firer identified a non-halakhic narrative that could underpin his ruling (Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 88b-89a). God instructed Moses to respond, and after he overcame his fear of the angels, he pointed out how the Ten Commandments were not relevant to heavenly angels. After recounting this colourful Talmudic passage, Firer wondered why this was a zero-sum game: Perhaps the Torah could remain in heaven with the angels and be given to terrestrial-bound humans? The same Torah cannot be binding both in heaven and on earth. Because all those commandments that are dependent on location, such as the Temple with its sacrifices, can only exist in one place and not in two places.” Similarly – explained Rabbi Firer – laws tied to a specific time can only be observed in places that have the same concept of time; namely, places on planet Earth, but not elsewhere in the universe. Once Moses bested the angels, it was decided that Torah would be given on Earth and according to Earth's reality. Rabbi Firer was aware that he was on shaky ground: non-halakhic, narrative passages like the story of Moses in heaven are hardly cited for legal determination. Firer therefore explained that he cited the tale to explain the legal reality, not as a source for Jewish law: “For this assertion that there are many commandments which, in the form as we have them, have no place on the Moon – requires no proof. It speaks for itself.” **** The Indivisible Whole Rabbi Firer had dismissed commandments linked to location or time, but there are many Jewish laws with no such limitations. According to Halakha, a person must always love their neighbour, return lost articles, save someone who is in danger, assist an animal in distress, never eat pork, and more. Once again, Rabbi Firer offered an original reading of a classic rabbinic text to support his conclusion that Torah, in its entirety, is inseparable from life on Earth. **** Lunar Emigration and Down-to-Earth Law Rabbi Firer took his conclusion one step further: “A Jewish person who relocates from planet Earth to the Moon with no intention of returning shall be governed by the laws of his new place, and he is exempt from the commandments. This follows the established rule regarding one who moves from place to place without intending to return – that the laws and customs of the destination apply to him, both in stringency and in leniency. Perhaps Firer felt that he had taken his conclusions too far, so he signed off his responsum by acknowledging the unripe nature of the discussion: “It goes without saying that the matters discussed in this essay represent only a first reflection, a modest beginning of a discussion on a question that, before long, will reverberate through the expanse of two worlds.” **** Rabbi Firer's striking attitude to Jewish law in Space was an outlier.
