Why Revolution return can put Matt Turner back on course for USMNT, World Cup
Source: {'href': 'https://www.nytimes.com', 'title': 'The New York Times'}    Published: 2025-08-06 14:19:32

Turner came from nowhere to become a top performer in MLS – he didn't even play organized soccer until he was 14 – and rose all the way up in the ranks to become the USMNT's first choice at the 2022 World Cup and earn a move to Arsenal. The last few years have proven to be challenging, however, and now, 10 months out from the next World Cup, he needs a reset. New England is an ideal spot for him to do it, but for reasons that go far beyond the simple fact that this was where he played more than 100 games between 2016 and 2022 and built up his name. Sure, it is nice that he's coming back to a familiar spot. His wife is from the area, he has a young family and there will be a level of comfort. It is possible to lose your sense of gravity. Playing time at Arsenal, then Nottingham Forest and Crystal Palace, has not been easy to come by. That kind of environment naturally affects a player's confidence. At Palace most recently, it was very clear that Dean Henderson was the first choice and the main focus. Then, when Turner was transferred to Lyon by Forest earlier in the summer, the whole situation became complicated by the French club's highly-publicized financial issues, which briefly included a forced relegation scare. So after all of that, how can Turner reclaim his top form, and why is New England the place to do it? I spoke on Tuesday with Kevin Hitchcock, the Revs' long-serving goalkeeping coach and whose presence is one of the main reasons why I think this move makes so much sense. Hitchcock is like the goalkeeper whisperer, one of the very best coaches for that position that you could find. After working with him, Turner transferred to Arsenal for $6m in 2022. In 2023, Djordje Petrović commanded around $18m plus add-ons from Chelsea. Aljaž Ivačič has improved drastically and recorded a career-high eight clean sheets this season, along with a solid 2.5 percent increase in his save percentage from last year. Hitchcock believes that over the past couple of seasons, Turner has found himself stuck in a difficult space, although not from lack of effort, but from the psychological weight of knowing that, no matter how well he trained, a real opportunity might never come. I've been there, and it can be paralyzing. But now, with a fresh dynamic and mutual respect between them, Hitchcock feels well-positioned to challenge Turner in ways he hasn't been pushed in a while to reignite the fire and bring out his very best. Hitchcock thinks it will take two weeks to bring back the sharpness, and the process of getting him back to his best will continue in the months ahead. Turner is a very vocal goalkeeper, which can help the New England backline. He will be asked to do more and to be accountable for organizing what has been a back five for most of the season, mostly featuring wingbacks Peyton Miller and Ilay Feingold, with Mamadou Fofana, Tanner Beason and Brayan Ceballos in the middle. The first challenge will also be an important one. Both his mindset and Hitchcock's are to get him back in position as U.S. No. 1, with the annual January training camp a firm target. For me, Hitchcock's input is going to be crucial. If Turner has had a lack of direction in terms of coaching, having been second-choice at his English clubs, he now has a coach who every day wants to push him to be better on certain drills and will emphasize the repetition of those drills. Of course, all will also depend on how committed Turner is to getting back to his best and the level of effort he puts in. Rewind nine years: Turner and I spent a lot of time together at the Revolution. It was a difficult time for me, as my sons had been born prematurely and then-coach Jay Heaps had given me permission to not train as frequently so I could spend time at the hospital. So I would try to make up for it at other times, and from the start Turner would always come up to me and say he was there for whatever I needed. He was young for a keeper, around 22, he was third on the depth chart and just making his way in the league. I would grab a couple of bags of balls and practice my finishing, and he would be there facing the shots for as long as I wanted. That's how he was then and I don't think it has changed. Having been a late bloomer, he knew this was his opportunity, and he was going to work as hard as needed, which meant we were able to help each other. That describes what kind of guy he is. His personality is great for the locker room. He would also hang out with all the guys, he wasn't one of those members of the goalkeepers club that would only spend time with other keepers. No one had a bad word to say about him. The foot movement and timing just weren't there, but he worked relentlessly at it. For that top USMNT spot, he is competing against guys who are also in MLS. He is not up against Premier League players like Tim Howard and Brad Guzan from previous years. It is Matt Freese, whom Mauricio Pochettino started over Turner during the Gold Cup, Zack Steffen (who has had some injury woes), Patrick Schulte and Chris Brady. When Turner is playing regularly and is sharp and in tune with himself, he can be the best of the whole bunch. His distribution is good enough at the international level. What we have seen from him recently is not the real Matt Turner, because that's what happens when you are not playing consistently. Even warming up before facing Panama at the Concacaf Nations League earlier this year, he looked a little slow to react, and the goalkeeping coach was scoring on him in the corner. His typical pushoff and explosiveness were not there. At the end of the game when Panama scored, Cecilio Waterman didn't hit his strike with great technique, but Turner didn't have the pushoff to get to what was a very savable shot. The psychological part of being a goalkeeper should not be underestimated. Decisiveness, confidence, reading each step of the game, it all drops off when you don't play. What he really wants is the feeling of being a vital player on a team again, being relied upon consistently in tough moments and the magical feeling of making big saves again. You never know if a move is going to have the perfect ending. Modern soccer comes with so much unpredictability, Turner has had highs and lows, especially recently, winning the FA Cup with Crystal Palace, then the drama around his move to Lyon, and now coming back home, just weeks apart. This change has the right feel about it though, and the prize if it goes well is clear and obvious. Al-Hilal banned for missing Saudi Super Cup in Hong Kong, blaming Club World Cup
Source: {'href': 'https://www.scmp.com', 'title': 'South China Morning Post'}    Published: 2025-08-06 03:46:48

Federation takes action after the holders pulled out of this month's Super Cup citing player fatigue Al-Hilal will be banned from next year's Saudi Super Cup if they qualify, after their recent withdrawal from this month's four-team competition in Hong Kong. The Saudi Arabia Football Federation's (SAFF) disciplinary and ethics committee announced the decision on Tuesday. It came after holders Al-Hilal pulled out of the Super Cup, scheduled to be played in Hong Kong from August 19 to 23, citing player fatigue following their Club World Cup campaign. They lost 2-1 to Fluminense in the quarter-finals on July 4. “Al-Hilal refused to participate in the Saudi Super Cup for the 2025-2026 season after the official schedule for the competition was issued,” the disciplinary committee said in a statement. “As a result, the committee ruled that Al-Hilal had violated Article (59-3) of the Disciplinary and Ethics Regulations and fined the club 500,000 Saudi Riyals (US$133,000) to be paid to the SAFF,” the statement added.
