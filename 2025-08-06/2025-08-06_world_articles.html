The New American Hustle: Trump Is Changing Corruption—and Making It More Dangerous
Source: {'href': 'https://www.foreignaffairs.com', 'title': 'Foreign Affairs'}    Published: 2025-08-06 04:00:00

Since its founding in 1922, Foreign Affairs has been the leading forum for serious discussion of American foreign policy and global affairs. The magazine has featured contributions from many leading international affairs experts. MICHAEL JOHNSTON is Charles A. Dana Professor Emeritus of Political Science at Colgate University. Whether he is launching cryptocurrency schemes or accepting a Qatari jumbo jet or issuing pardons for the relatives of major donors, Donald Trump has drawn frequent accusations of corruption in the early months of his second term as U.S. president. Each instance of corruption raises critical concerns about misused authority, undemocratic governance, or foreign influence. But larger trends in corruption are notoriously difficult to assess. Until recently, the United States, like most other market democracies, was generally regarded as not suffering unduly from corruption. Measures such as the 1977 Foreign Corrupt Practices Act, which prohibits U.S. citizens and businesses from bribing foreign officials (and whose enforcement the Trump administration has announced plans to scale back), made Washington a global leader in anticorruption. Yet amid questions about potential ethical standards violations on the U.S. Supreme Court, the increased influence of special interests in U.S. politics, and financial scandals involving several members of Congress, that good reputation was slipping even before Trump took office for the second time. At a moment when the United States' institutional guardrails are eroding, the Trump administration has embraced a style of governance in which power flows directly from a single leader, creating opportunities for personal deals to drive official decisions. That sort of corruption threatens free political and economic processes and can, eventually, become integral to the way the entire system functions—making it highly resistant to reform. In its most basic definition, corruption is the abuse of public roles and resources for private benefit. But any definition leaves considerable room to dispute a given practice as corrupt, and manifestations of corruption vary widely. It makes little sense to suggest that corruption in Denmark, a perennially high-ranking country on the Transparency International scale, is the same as corruption in, say, Cameroon, which ranks much lower, and that Cameroon simply has more of it. Yet it is possible to categorize the sources of corruption into four types: influence markets, elite cartels, oligarchs and clans, and official moguls. But they correspond to meaningful differences in how countries operate. Some forms of corruption enable violence, whereas others may be an alternative to it. Some severely disrupt economic and political development, whereas others strengthen alliances among elites that can facilitate rapid growth. Historically, corruption in the United States has primarily happened within an influence market—a system in which political influence and access are tradeable commodities. This form of corruption is typically found in societies with mature economies and open politics. Strong institutions prevent state capture but also make influence worth paying for; shaping a government decision is only valuable if it is likely to be implemented. Countries such as Bulgaria, Portugal, and Slovakia do not place legal restrictions on lobbying, either. Many others maintain lobbying registers to improve transparency; some (such as Denmark, Germany, and Sweden) require groups to register and some, such as Israel, do not. Because all these systems accommodate well-funded organized interests, outright bribery is often unnecessary. Public resentment of such activities can fuel populist grievances and help antisystem political parties gain ground. In countries with only moderately strong institutions, which are often in the process of liberalizing or reforming, a different type of corruption may emerge: the elite cartel. But that stability persists only until it doesn't; complex, ad hoc elite coalitions can perform poorly during crises or even trigger them. In South Korea, chaebols had a long-standing practice of making major payments to the country's presidents to secure favorable lending terms and thus lock up significant credit and investment for themselves; when the 1997 Asian financial crisis hit, large numbers of nonperforming loans hobbled the banking sector, worsened currency depreciation, and accelerated stock market declines. A less stable and more disruptive form of elite corruption emerges in places that have very weak institutions and that are undergoing rapid political change. Overtly criminal factions such as Mafias or drug cartels can take over policing, law and contract enforcement, and other functions that would otherwise be the job of the government. Russia fit this category in the 1990s, when oligarchs and Mafia groups seized the opportunity of post-Soviet privatization to take control of several business and government sectors. Today, oligarchs in the Philippines lead powerful political families with their own regional bases of operation; they engage in various licit and illicit enterprises, buy votes, and capture important segments of the bureaucracy. In Mexico, some regions are dominated by drug cartels whose plata o plomo (“silver or lead,” that is, take a bribe or take a bullet) practices combine bribery, extortion, and violence. In other countries with very weak institutions, a different mode of corruption develops: the official mogul. Politics and decision-making are dominated by a single individual, family, or junta. These leaders seize corrupt benefits not only for themselves but also to reward loyalists. The extensive corruption built into such a system inflicts serious damage on economic and political development, just as in a system dominated by oligarchs. But it does not lead to as much violence or insecurity, because there is little opportunity for weakened institutions and opposition groups to contest the power of the dominant leader. Trump has turned his personal brand into business ventures, entering opaque licensing agreements with firms that sell Trump-labeled merchandise. Personal deal-making has become a feature of U.S. foreign relations under Trump, too. What is notable is how Trump uses the authority of his office and how his supplicants—family members, cryptocurrency investors, business partners, U.S. government officials, and foreign governments—behave toward him. Instead of using traditional institutional channels to seek explicit influence over policy, they are currying personal favor and showing fealty to the president himself. In turn, Trump is using the power of his office not only to enrich himself but to reinforce his position as sole decision-maker, pursuing a degree of personal dominance that has not been seen before in American politics. The United States, of course, has seen its presidents tainted by corruption before. But neither episode directly involved or enriched the presidents themselves. And when more recent presidents have found themselves embroiled in scandal over abuses of power, they have faced significant pushback: Richard Nixon was forced to resign in 1974 after Watergate, and in the late 1990s Bill Clinton became the first president to face an impeachment trial in more than 130 years on charges related to his affair with Monica Lewinsky. Trump has yet to encounter such consequences in his second administration. Trump's style of corruption will not transform the United States overnight. In state and local government, the system is still operating as it did before. Patronage and pay-to-play schemes that have nothing to do with Trump will still unfold at county courthouses, and some state legislators will still gratefully receive political contributions from corporations, such as those the fracking industry has doled out in Pennsylvania. The United States' economy and political system are too large and diverse to surrender quickly to the personal domination of a president. Moreover, the main players in U.S. influence markets may resist changes to a system that serves them well. By carefully maintaining their long-standing relationships with legislators and regulatory agencies, the big-money donors, lobbyists, labor unions, and major corporations that many Americans see as corrupt could keep an even more pernicious form of corruption in check. But the United Sates could conceivably become an illiberal democracy or something akin to what the political scientists Steven Levitsky and Lucan Ahmad Way have termed a “competitive authoritarian” state. The country has already begun drifting in this direction: the administration's efforts to intimidate universities, law firms, and media organizations are first steps toward weakening civil society and undermining the pluralism and open debate that are essential for liberal democracy. In time, the United States could come to resemble, for example, Hungary under Prime Minister Viktor Orban. Corruption at the top thus further undermines democracy by weakening both institutional and political restraints on centralized power. The form of corruption Trump has displayed could have a similarly damaging effect on democratic norms and practices at home. It also exposes the United States to harmful influence from abroad. Protections against such influence are built into the country's institutions: foreign nationals are barred from contributing to federal election campaigns, lobbying by foreign governments and political parties must be disclosed under the 1938 Foreign Agents Registration Act, and most other foreign interests must register their political activities under the more lenient 1995 Lobbying Disclosure Act. Personal wheeling and dealing is outside the bounds of anticorruption regulation. And strongmen who wield power in similar ways—leaders such as Orban, Putin, or North Korea's Kim Jong Un—recognize Trump's governing style. They will not be shy about taking advantage, offering direct deals with Trump (such as by making investments in his cryptocurrency) to secure favorable terms in diplomacy and trade, and to prevent Washington from meddling in their countries or what they consider to be their spheres of influence. When corruption becomes an integral part of the way a political leader makes decisions—or grants favors and stifles opponents to secure his grip on power—most familiar reform tactics, which are often narrowly focused on combating bribery, are insufficient to contain it. The Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, a leader in international anticorruption efforts, even canceled its working group on bribery's long-planned mission to Hungary in 2024—the first time it has taken such a step—because the Orban government had consistently failed to act on earlier OECD recommendations. Supreme Court decisions have lifted restrictions on corporate donations to political campaigns; sharply limited the legal boundaries of what constitutes an “official act” by an elected official in federal bribery statutes (ruling in the case of former Virginia Governor Robert McDonnell that calling other politicians and hosting events at the governor's mansion were not, in fact, “official acts” taken in support of a businessman who had previously given him gifts); and deemed that bribes paid to elected officials are mere “gratuities” and legal, if delivered after an official decision has been made. A leader can only wield personal authority as Trump does in the absence of strong political constraints. Such checks are built into the U.S. Constitution; the Foreign Emoluments Clause, for example, prohibits federal officials from receiving gifts or compensation from any other government or ruler without congressional consent, and the Domestic Emoluments Clause prohibits presidents from receiving any compensation beyond their fixed salary from the federal government or any U.S. state. So far, however, Trump's allies in Congress have almost wholly abdicated this role, and although lower courts have issued injunctions against some of the administration's actions and policies, the Supreme Court has repeatedly found ways to expand executive power.This problem is fundamentally a political one. Because top-level corruption and the erosion of democratic constraints are mutually reinforcing, pushing back on the misuse of presidential authority will become only more difficult as time goes on. The targets of Trump's lawsuits must vigorously defend themselves, universities must defy demands that they cease programs Trump dislikes, and law firms must refuse to provide complimentary legal services to Trump's cronies and favored causes. What is happening today in the United States has clear elements of tragedy, but it is not yet a one-man show.
