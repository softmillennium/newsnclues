Pochettino, USMNT believe they can beat anyone at the World Cup. They may be right.
Source: {'href': 'https://www.nytimes.com', 'title': 'The New York Times'}    Published: 2025-11-21 19:41:04

The U.S. men's national team and head coach Mauricio Pochettino have made a powerful statement. The recent upturn in results, highlighted by this week's 5-1 demolition of Uruguay, has led to a completely different feel about the squad and its chances at next summer's World Cup. We saw it in the opening half hour against Uruguay when players were flying around the field and ripping apart a South American opponent. We saw it previously, too, in the late-game brawl against Paraguay days earlier. He has pushed these guys to another level, and the team now truly looks like it believes it can beat anybody. We'll find out in the summer – and perhaps a few months prior in friendlies against top European competition – but you now feel the U.S. has a fighting chance of making a serious impact in the World Cup. The dismantling of Uruguay was so far removed from those painfully soulless defeats to Turkey and Switzerland back in June that at times it felt unreal. As I watched the goals flying in during that first-half blitz against Marcelo Bielsa's side, I found myself checking the team sheet to make sure that this really was a recognized Uruguay squad. I suspect the coach was being diplomatic with that final part of his statement, I think he was saying that the reality he found with the USMNT was much, much worse than even critical fans and pundits realized. When Pochettino began experimenting with his roster, calling up a host of players – some of whom had fans and pundits scratching our heads – we presumed he simply hadn't been happy with the technical level that he had found when he took over the team. He wasn't trying to finesse a slightly better range of options for the World Cup. No, he was blowing up what existed before, he was smashing the sense of entitlement that some may have had, he was creating a new environment and setting new standards. He was deliberately destroying a team culture that he knew wasn't strong enough to achieve his goals. After a while, we started to realize that his roster selections were sending a message to the so-called established players. Reminding them that they couldn't take their places for granted. But even this didn't fully grasp what the Argentine was undertaking. It wasn't just a subtle message to some players who might have been feeling too comfortable, a signal that would result in them being eventually recalled with a little more humility. It's hard to imagine that Pochettino is now going to discard those players he has called up in recent windows and who have responded so well. After watching Pochettino snap over rather harmless questions regarding the squad's “regular” and more experienced players, I am even more convinced of that. I have always said this team needed a coach from Europe who could raise the demands and level of expectations. An American coach attempting Pochettino's strategy – playing MLS guys ahead of Europe-based players, leaving out more-established stars – would not have treaded through the backlash so defiantly. Pochettino, however, can step in and say, “I don't care who you are.” He can command respect because of his CV: he reached a Champions League final and has coached the best players in the world, including Lionel Messi, Kylian Mbappé, Neymar, Harry Kane and Son Heung-min. When talking to players like Christian Pulisic, Yunus Musah and Weston McKennie, Pochettino can draw a line in the sand. If the players thought they were getting the smiling, friendly, ‘players' coach' that Pochettino was presented as when he took over, they now know that there is another side to him. “Big balls … he showed great character,” was Pochettino's assessment then. Pochettino's strategy over the last year took fortitude, too. There was no guarantee that the players he turned to would have delivered in the way that they have. There was no certainty that the players would have responded to his disruption of the culture by creating a new one. The way he has handled Gio Reyna is another element of his strategic approach. Reyna was left off rosters, giving him time to sort out his club future. After the 2022 World Cup drama, Reyna faced significant backlash, but his attitude appears to have changed significantly. While his quality might still be lacking due to injuries and a lack of confidence, Pochettino decided that now was his chance to come in and perform. And with his displays in this window – an early goal vs. Paraguay, an assist off the bench vs. Uruguay – Reyna went from being on the outside to putting himself right back in contention for the 26-man World Cup roster. Giving the team fight and spirit isn't just some exercise in machismo, it's actually fundamental to creating the edge that winning teams have. When you have a number of talented and athletically gifted players, having more intensity and being up for it more than your opponent is what truly separates teams. If a team is consistently winning second balls, challenges and aerial duels, it will generate more chances and be more dangerous. The results of this radical overhaul are clear in the emergence of players who have improved with every camp. We've seen significant gains from players like Arfsten, Freeman, Berhalter and Tanner Tessmann. It would be nuts now to say that Berhalter is not going to be in the World Cup roster. The final roster decisions will be complex, particularly in midfield, where you now have major improvement from players like Brenden Aaronson, Cristian Roldan and Tessmann. But whatever that final 26-man roster looks like, the mentality and culture of this team are now set. The USMNT is on a path that instills confidence, rather than concern. As the World Cup approaches, unease looms over the identity of the USMNT
Source: {'href': 'https://www.starsandstripesfc.com', 'title': 'Stars and Stripes FC'}    Published: 2025-11-21 19:00:00

Soccer, particularly men's soccer, in the United States occupies an idiosyncratic space in the nation's sports landscape. Meanwhile, the US Men's National Team doesn't really have a comparable counterpart when it comes to sports in the US and, in fact, is not the most popular men's national team in the country. Perhaps Olympic basketball, or the Olympics more broadly, compares but that's more of an American all-star team that shows up every four years expected to win a gold medal while the USMNT plays regularly in games of varying importance until the World Cup which it is not expected to win. Recent comments from Mauricio Pochettino highlight this when the manager called for fans to support the team saying, “In Argentina, Argentina is more important than any name. So we need our fans to follow and support the national team. We need to build that relationship.” This comes after he practically begged fans to support the team after a loss to South Korea in September. Landon Donovan called supporter chants in the US boring and told the Unfiltered Soccer Podcast, “Chanting something like, ‘Timmy Howard.' It's like, guys, do something fun. If you scroll through social media or Instagram and come across fun or famous chants in the U.K., they're so creative and brilliant whether you like it or not.” Indeed, two World Wars and one World Cup is the height of creativity. However, these views aren't new and have been parroted by fans, coaches, players, and pundits for over a decade. They come from a place of unrealistic expectations and the assumption that because the United States is doing something it must be the best at it and reflect an unease Americans have when that is not the case. There will be hype, media coverage, and players will have a chance to turn into legends. And yet, with just months to go before the tournament kicks off, the manager is pleading with fans to pay attention to the team. Growing the sport and an identity around it is an ongoing process and the US Soccer Federation announced its “Pathway Strategy” to grow the game by making it more accessible to youth players and try to foster a common philosophy amongst clubs so that young players can be successful in the sport. All of this speaks to a country that is forging its own identity within the sport and navigating a unique sports entertainment market compared to nations where soccer is the national sport. The chants won't sound like they do in England, the support won't be the same as it is in Argentina, the development won't be the same as it is in Iceland or France or Germany, and there isn't promotion and relegation to solve all of these problems. Expecting that the United States men will be an exceptional force that galvanizes supporter passion in a very short modern history compared to countries that don't have generational attachments to other national pastimes reflects vain presumptions that discount how expansively the sport has developed. Ultimately, the support for the team and its identity will emerge on its own and cannot be outsourced to another country.
