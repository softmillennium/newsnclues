Diesel Engine Runaway Is The Scariest Thing That Can Happen To A Diesel Engine
Source: yahoo    Published: 2025-03-22 20:25:00

Internal combustion engines are incredibly complicated machines that many of us interact with on a daily basis, but don't completely understand. Unlike gasoline-burning engines, diesel engines don't require a spark to burn fuel and operate. Most automotive diesel engines don't have any spark plugs; instead, diesel engines rely on compression to ignite the fuel. The combustion chamber inside each cylinder in a diesel engine facilitates combustion by housing air at extremely high pressures, which when compressed by the piston and combined with diesel fuel, heats up and ignites the fuel. Most diesel engines have an oil separator that prevents volatile derelict oil from entering the combustion chamber, mitigating the likelihood of dangerous blow-by. Regular maintenance and inspection of key components such as turbochargers, fuel injectors, and air intake systems can preemptively catch a failing component that could lead to runaway. Older, high-mileage diesel engines are at higher risk for engine runaway, but can be fortified against it by using aftermarket inline devices that shut off the engine's air supply at excessive engine speeds. Some older diesel engines have a tube to ventilate extreme crankcase pressure which helps prevent flammable oils from leaving the crankcase and entering the combustion chamber. The only way to stop diesel engines experiencing runaway is by either completely cutting off the fuel source, or the air source. Turning off the ignition and/or removing the key will have no effect on the runaway reaction.
