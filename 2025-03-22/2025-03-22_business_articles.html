It's time to buy when markets reach 'peak uncertainty.' Are we there yet?
Source: cnbc    Published: 2025-03-22 00:00:00

The moment of maximum uncertainty is a time to buy, but discerning when we've reached peak uncertainty can be challenging. Every investor knows, or should know, that the moment of maximum uncertainty is a time to buy. The only trick, then, is discerning when we've reached peak uncertainty. The stifling lack of clarity about the breadth or severity of the Trump administration's promised "reciprocal" tariffs due on April 2 is the obvious eye of this uncertainty storm. But swirling around it are immigration restrictions, headlong and haphazard reductions in Federal spending and a separate but related confidence shock weighing on consumer behavior. Friday's midday rally in the S & P 500 from an early 1% decline to a minimal gain by the close came after President Trump said there would be some "flexibility" in the application of those April 2 trade measures. A vague and entirely reversible utterance, but the upward twitch in the index sends the message that after a month of volatile trading, the market is looking for a resolution. Renaissance Macro Research points out that since 1928, when S & P 500 futures have risen by more than 1% during the week, they have continued to rise about two-thirds of the time in subsequent weeks. According to Ned Davis Research U.S. equity strategist Ed Clissold, after an initial rebound rally, there is typically a period of churn and retesting. "The duration of the retesting phase can be roughly proportional to the original decline," he says. The Feb. 19 peak in the S & P 500 to the March 13 low was 16 trading days. Another 16 days would bring us to April 4, near President Trump's April 2 deadline for instituting reciprocal tariffs. Since tariff concerns were the main catalyst for the selloff, the April 2 deadline could bring a resolution, one way or the other. The S & P 500 Volatility Index put in a nice spike, peaking near 30 and then declining below 20, often a sign the fever has broken.

Caesars Entertainment is a diversified gaming and hospitality company operating through several segments: Las Vegas, Regional, Caesars Digital, Managed and Branded, in addition to Corporate and Other. Earlier this month, Carl Icahn and Caesars reached an agreement in which the company consented to expanding the size of the board to 12 directors and appointing Jesse Lynn (general counsel of Icahn Enterprises) and Ted Papapostolou (chief financial officer of Icahn Enterprises) as directors to the company's board. Icahn agreed to abide by certain customary standstill and voting provisions. This is not Carl Icahn's first foray at Caesars. He filed a 13D in February 2019 stating then that he believed the board should conduct a strategic review with a view toward a sale of the company being the optimal path to shareholder value creation. On March 1, 2019, Icahn and Caesars entered into a director appointment and nomination agreement, pursuant to which John Boushy, Matthew Ferko, and Christopher Williams resigned from the board and James Nelson, Courtney Mather, and Keith Cozza were appointed to fill the resulting vacancies. Caesar's stock price topped $119 per share on Oct. 1, 2021, but has since dropped below its pre-merger value in 2020, despite revenue increasing from $9.6 billion in 2021 to $11.2 billion today and operating income increasing from $1.7 billion to $2.3 billion over those respective periods. Caesars recently entered into an agreement to expand the size of the board to 12 and appoint Jesse Lynn and Ted Papapostolou to the company's board. Icahn stated that they look forward to working with management and the Board to maximize value for all shareholders. A spinoff or separation of Caesars' digital business could unlock a ton of value, as Digital's standalone valuation would represent 15% to 25% of the company's total current enterprise value – significantly higher than what its implied valuation is right now within Caesars. This separation would also allow investors the option to invest in a consistent legacy casino business or a riskier high-growth digital business. The company could retain a piece of the digital business or enter into an agreement with the new entity to run the business. Carl Icahn has a tremendous history of creating value at casinos, having acquired and sold several properties, including the Stratosphere and Tropicana.
