The performance partnership will see the team utilise Hytro wearables and technology as part of its world-leading approach to athletic performance and recovery. Hytro's patented blood flow restriction (BFR) clothing is designed to help athletes train more effectively and recover quicker, with over 200 professional sports teams across various leagues using their products. Blood flow restriction – a compression around the top of the thigh or arm to temporarily reduce circulation – increases muscle stress and makes training more impactful and efficient. The results are stronger muscles, less recovery time, and reduced soreness, according to scientific studies cited by Hytro. Hytro's technology may also have a vital application for astronauts, as muscle mass and bone density can be affected by spending time in microgravity. The Fram2 mission, operated by Elon Musk’s SpaceX, will test the use of Hytro's BFR products during exercise in space. During the three-to-five-day mission, the crew will wear blood flow restriction products made by Hytro and Delfi Medical Innovations. The tests are part of a project in collaboration with several universities and space technology leaders. This study is a massive step for Hytro and the wider BFR community, according to Dr Joe Handsaker, Hytro's chief innovation officer. If successful, it could unlock new research into exercise efficiency in micro-gravity and even accelerate Musk's quest to populate other planets. Maintaining muscle mass and function is crucial not only for spaceflight but also for everyday lives, said co-study lead Dr Tom Maden-Wilkinson.

Personnel consultant: If this question falls in the interview, it goes!
Source: businessinsider    Published: 2025-03-29 10:41:26

According to the personnel consultant Kristine Capek, the key to a successful job interview is competence-based questions, which enable a precise assessment. The application process is no longer just about a one-sided assessment - rather about getting to know each other at eye level. Companies should specifically ask about applicants' motivation - for example: "Why did you apply for this position?" An ideal interview starts with small talk, creating a pleasant atmosphere and relaxing the situation. This is followed by a brief introduction to the company and hiring management, but short and concise, as the focus is on the applicant, not the management or team. Many companies present themselves in detail, while applicants lose themselves in irrelevant details. Companies should target the interview and ask more specific questions, rather than saying "Tell something about yourself." The key to a successful interview is competence-based questions. For a successful interview, companies define in advance exactly which skills are required for the open position. Applicants should focus on the skills and strengths they have developed in previous roles and what added value they could offer in their potentially new role. One question that is often asked from above and puts applicants under pressure is "Why should we hire you?" This question is a warning signal, says Kristine Capek, as it creates an unnecessary sales situation. Companies should formulate the question differently to put applicants less under pressure - for example: "Why did you apply for this position?" The latter question aims at what applicants are interested in the position and the company. Applicants can react confidently to problematic questions by answering with a professional explanation of their qualifications.
