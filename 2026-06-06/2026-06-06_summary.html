However, data contradicts this, showing that India has consistently led the world in crypto adoption rates for several years. Below is the full translation of the report: The Golden Age of Indian Crypto Looking back at the crypto bull market from 2020 to 2021, India was far from an ordinary emerging market. The keywords of that cycle—retail influx, altcoin rallies, DeFi explosions, NFT breakthroughs, and global developer expansions—were all concentrated in the Indian market. Data from Chainalysis shows that from July 2020 to June 2021, the scale of the Indian crypto market surged by 641%; among transactions initiated by Indian addresses, DeFi-related transactions accounted for 59%, higher than in Vietnam and Pakistan. Institutional-level large trades exceeding $10 million made up 42% of the total trading volume, indicating that India had already moved beyond pure retail speculation, with a more diversified and mature trading structure. Additionally, there are many noteworthy examples. The local exchange WazirX surpassed 10 million users in 2021, with new registrations from small towns and suburban areas increasing by 700% year-on-year; CoinSwitch Kuber secured $260 million in funding led by a16z and Coinbase Ventures, entering the unicorn club; CoinDCX also became a unicorn in 2021. Projects like Polygon, created by Indian entrepreneurs, have risen to prominence, transforming India from a major crypto consumer market into a hub for crypto technology development. Polygon, originally founded as Matic Network in 2017, aimed to address Ethereum's scalability issues and has since grown into a leading scalability infrastructure within the Ethereum ecosystem. Following Polygon, a wave of protocols and companies founded by Indian teams or led by Indian founders has emerged, including EigenLayer, Avail, Sentient, Stader Labs, Biconomy, OpenFX, FalconX, Instadapp, and more. Is the Indian Crypto Market in a State of Stagnation? Today, the Indian crypto market is far from its previous hustle and bustle, with multiple factors contributing to this change. 2022 marked a watershed year for the industry. As the global crypto market entered a bear phase, India simultaneously implemented new crypto regulations—imposing a 30% income tax on profits from virtual digital asset (VDA) transactions, and in July of the same year, introducing a 1% tax deducted at source, which severely impacted the trading activity of local exchanges. A survey by the Indian policy think tank Esya Centre found that after the introduction of the 1% withholding tax, many Indian users shifted to offshore platforms that are harder to regulate; from July 2022 to July 2023, over 90% of crypto asset transactions by Indian investors moved to overseas exchanges. Regulatory pressure continued to escalate thereafter. By 2025, the Indian tax authorities intensified crypto tax audits, with the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) issuing audit notices to 44,057 taxpayers involved in crypto trading who had not reported their assets in the VDA section of their income tax returns. In contrast, tracking offshore traders became significantly more challenging. This ultimately created a distorted situation: stringent rules primarily constrained compliant local platforms and law-abiding users, further accelerating the industry's outflow. However, determining that the Indian crypto industry is cooling off solely based on a quiet market is misleading: the market has merely faded from the spotlight, and demand and trading volume have not disappeared. Data from Chainalysis confirms that India has topped the Global Crypto Adoption Index for three consecutive years in 2023, 2024, and 2025—while the heat has faded, real demand remains. A deeper structural change lies in the core of the market. In earlier years, India relied on a massive influx of retail investors for speculation; now, the industry's focus has shifted towards developers, startups, underlying infrastructure, and institutional application scenarios. Hashed Emergent statistics show that the number of Web3 startups in India has surpassed 1,250, with total financing exceeding $3.5 billion since 2020; in 2025 alone, Indian entrepreneurs raised a total of $626 million, with a resurgence in Series B and later-stage financing after three years of stagnation, raising $396 million throughout the year. India remains the world's second-largest hub for Web3 developers, accounting for 15.2% of the global total; in 2025, on-chain capital inflow in India reached $338 billion, nearly doubling from the previous year. The conclusion of this article arises from this: on the surface, affected by taxes and regulations, local exchanges in India have seen a decline in trading volume, and retail enthusiasm has waned, making the Indian crypto industry appear to be cooling; however, digging deeper into the data, India remains in the top tier of global crypto penetration rates, with the industry's focus steadily shifting towards developers, startup projects, public infrastructure, and enterprise applications. In short, the Indian crypto industry has not perished; it has merely shed the bubble of the bull market and entered a phase of mature development. Data Source: Chainalysis Chainalysis' 2025 Global Crypto Adoption Index evaluates 151 countries, with statistical dimensions divided into total trading volume on centralized platforms, retail centralized trading, DeFi on-chain transfers, and large institutional centralized trades. India ranks first in all four subcategories and overall, making it one of the few markets where retail, DeFi, and institutional funds are simultaneously active. One reason for India's strong performance in the rankings is the country's structural demand for stablecoins. India has one of the largest remittance markets globally, with millions of families and workers regularly making cross-border remittances. In this context, stablecoins can play a practical role: they provide faster settlement speeds, easier access to dollar-pegged value, and can reduce friction in cross-border payments, especially for users facing high fees, slow bank transfers, or difficulties in obtaining traditional dollar accounts. Macroeconomic factors also support the demand for stablecoins. As the rupee weakens against the dollar, some users may no longer view dollar-pegged digital assets as speculative crypto products but rather as tools for preserving and increasing value. For freelancers, exporters, overseas workers, and families receiving remittances, stablecoins can conveniently meet local currency needs and seamlessly connect with global dollar payment methods. Of course, some funds circulate through gray payment channels, posing compliance risks, but the core of India's demand for stablecoins stems from genuine financial needs: cross-border remittances, low-cost currency exchange, accelerated settlement, and protection against currency depreciation. This also explains why, despite heavy tax burdens and unclear regulations, stablecoins remain an important component of crypto trading in India. At the same time, it is necessary to objectively and dialectically view the Chainalysis rankings. India's high score stems from two major statistical loopholes: first, the all-dimensional indicators are tied to purchasing power parity GDP per capita, which is about $11,160 for India in 2025, a low base that naturally inflates the ranking; second, the country's large population means that if recalculated on a per capita basis, India's ranking would fall out of the global top twenty. Even so, from the overall national scale, India's crypto remittance and trading volume still ranks among the top globally. Data Source: Hashed Emergent, CoinSwitch Following the introduction of the withholding tax from 2022 to 2023, a large amount of capital flowed overseas, but recently, retail funds are slowly returning to compliant local exchanges. According to CoinSwitch data, in 2025, the spot trading volume of mainstream cryptocurrencies in India surged by 114% year-on-year, with new traders increasing by 27%; investors aged 18 to 25 contributed half of the trading volume, with 37.6% of active traders aged 18 to 25 and 37.3% aged 26 to 35, indicating that the young demographic continues to form the foundational demand for crypto in India. Data Source: Hashed Emergent, Pi42 The derivatives market is growing even more rapidly. Pi42 data shows that from 2024 to 2025, the proportion of users aged 18 to 25 among new users in the Indian crypto derivatives market skyrocketed from 24% to 61%; regionally, trading volume in eastern India increased sixfold, while northeastern and central regions each saw a fourfold increase, indicating that crypto derivatives are no longer limited to first-tier cities like Mumbai, Bangalore, and Delhi, but are continuously penetrating lower-tier markets. The key to this surge lies in the proliferation of local language crypto media in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, and Bengali on YouTube. The growth of the derivatives market is not only reflected in the increase in registered users. The average transaction amount per trade rose from $1,051 in 2024 to nearly $1,960 in 2025; the proportion of high-frequency daily traders increased from 45% to 60%. Derivatives are a high-risk category, but data shows that Indian users are shifting from merely holding coins to actively trading. This habit is also supported by the mature derivatives landscape in the country: India's on-exchange options nominal trading volume ranks among the top globally, and a large number of retail investors are familiar with high-volatility, high-frequency derivatives, naturally extending their investment preferences to the crypto derivatives space. Looking at recent trends in the global cryptocurrency market, some believe that the market is becoming calm due to a slowdown in the emergence of new concepts and poor price performance of altcoins. However, others argue that the market has matured, pointing to the proliferation of stablecoins and the strong interest of global financial institutions in on-chain finance. The same logic applies to India, where the market is moving towards maturity. Compared to the financing frenzy of 2021 to 2022, the overall fundraising total for Indian crypto startups has declined, but the number of financing rounds for mature projects in Series B and beyond has significantly increased. The Developer Ecosystem is Becoming More Complete Assessing industry maturity cannot rely solely on transaction volume and financing; developers are the core leading indicator. Developers in the crypto space are not ordinary workers; they are the supply engine for protocols, applications, underlying infrastructure, development tools, and new products. A growing developer base indicates that the industry is accumulating capacity for the next bull market. Data Source: Hashed Emergent, Devfolio From this perspective, India firmly occupies a key position in the global Web3 landscape: local Web3 developers account for 15.2% of the global total (up from 12% in 2024), ranking second only to the United States, and it is the fastest-growing hub for Web3 talent globally. The Electric Capital 2024 Developer Geography Report corroborates this: U.S. developers account for 19%, India 12%, and the UK 4%; among the world's leading economies, only India's developer share has significantly increased, while the U.S. share has declined year by year. In terms of regional distribution, Bangalore leads the nation with 23.6% of developers, followed by the Delhi capital region at 11.8%, Mumbai at 6.4%, Pune at 3.4%, and Hyderabad at 3.2%. Bangalore's advantage is based on the foundation of the country's traditional IT industry, supplemented by regular hackathons and talent incubation projects from communities like Solana's Superteam and Ethfolio, continuously supplying new talent; the spread of talent across Delhi, Mumbai, Pune, and Hyderabad indicates that India's Web3 talent is not concentrated in a single city, but is rooted in the mature IT industry across the country. Data Source: Hashed Emergent, Devfolio A notable feature of India's developer ecosystem is its youthfulness. According to a developer survey conducted by Hashed Emergent and Devfolio, 82.2% of respondents are aged between 18 and 25, with about 70% being students. This suggests that India's current Web3 ecosystem is less of a mature labor market and more of a vast talent reservoir, which will gradually flow into the market in the coming years. Another point worth noting is that the developer community is no longer composed solely of newcomers. The survey shows that 42.6% of Indian Web3 developers have more than two years of experience, 33.2% have one to two years of experience, and 24.2% are novice developers with less than a year of experience. With new developers still joining and the proportion of developers with over two years of experience significantly increasing, it can be inferred that the ecosystem is gradually maturing. The level of global collaboration is also continuously rising. Among Indian developers who have been in the industry for less than a year, 18.9% participate in multinational remote teams; among those with over two years of experience, the proportion of cross-border collaboration skyrockets to 55.4%. As technical capabilities improve, Indian developers are becoming deeply embedded in global Web3 projects. This means that India is not only supplying talent for its domestic market but is also growing into a hub for outsourcing developers on a global scale. The reality of salaries further reinforces this trend: the starting salaries for local internet jobs in India are relatively low, while the cost of living is rising. Global crypto projects offer remote work opportunities, better pay, and flexible growth paths, making Web3 an important channel for Indian tech talent to connect with the global labor market and realize their technical value. India's crypto landscape is no longer just a massive retail trading market; it simultaneously boasts a large user base and a multitude of builders. If the industry were truly cooling off, developer participation, hackathon conversions, global team collaborations, and protocol ecosystem investments would all shrink, but in 2025, various data points are trending upward—beneath the surface of declining trading activity, the foundational developer base supporting the next cycle continues to strengthen. Stablecoins in the Indian Market Another sign of the maturation of the Indian cryptocurrency market is the rise of stablecoins. In the past, keywords used to describe the Indian cryptocurrency market included exchanges, retail fund inflows, and altcoin investments. However, recently, attention has shifted to more fundamental financial infrastructures such as payments, settlements, remittances, and asset digitization. This indicates that the market is moving beyond simple speculative demand and entering a phase of considering how to apply blockchain to the actual financial system. In India, this shift first appeared at the startup level. Mudrex's B2B cross-border payment project Saber Money focuses on completing corporate cross-border settlements using stablecoins. In early 2026, Saber joined the Circle payment network as a licensed partner, leveraging stablecoins to facilitate cross-border payments in rupees; Circle opened a payout channel in India through Saber: traditional NEFT transfers take two hours, while IMPS/RTGS settle nearly in real-time. Although this move does not signify that stablecoins have officially obtained legal payment status in India, it marks the interconnection of India's domestic settlement system with the global stablecoin network. Indian entrepreneurs are collectively betting on stablecoins through Web3 payment infrastructure companies. Transak secured $16 million in strategic financing in 2025, led by Tether and IDG, to enhance stablecoin payment infrastructure; a local tech team developed the Lightning Network payment project Speed, which combines the rapid settlement of the Lightning Network with the price stability of stablecoins like USDT, targeting applications in e-commerce settlements, creator payments, platform reconciliations, and cross-border remittances. However, when examining India's stablecoin market, we must also consider the regulatory paradox. Stablecoins have become essential tools for global cross-border payments due to their low costs and fast settlements, but privately issued stablecoins pegged to the dollar conflict with the Reserve Bank of India's currency control and monetary sovereignty policies, and the legal definition of stablecoins' foreign exchange attributes remains ambiguous. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) maintains a high-pressure stance on private stablecoins due to concerns over macro-financial stability, monetary sovereignty, payment system security, and cross-border capital controls. The root of the contradiction lies in the RBI's long-standing intervention in the foreign exchange market to stabilize the rupee's exchange rate, which naturally rejects private stablecoins that can bypass the official banking foreign exchange system and freely hold and circulate dollar assets. Top-level policies are more inclined to promote central bank digital currencies and the UPI official payment network rather than support private dollar stablecoins. For stablecoin startups and investors, in addition to regulatory uncertainty, they also face headwinds from top-level policies that inherently oppose private dollar currencies. This contradiction precisely reflects the maturity of the Indian market. In immature markets, stablecoins are often used for exchange deposits and short-term speculation; even with tightened regulations on private stablecoins in India, startups continue to focus on cross-border applications, while the central bank simultaneously explores controllable digital currencies through CBDC pilot programs. India has not fully opened up stablecoins but is leveraging official systems to address the real pain points that stablecoins aim to solve, such as high remittance fees, delayed settlements, and global payment interoperability. However, this does not necessarily mean that stablecoins will see widespread adoption in India's domestic payment market. More realistic opportunities may lie in cross-border application scenarios, especially considering that India is one of the largest remittance-receiving countries globally, with annual remittance inflows exceeding $100 billion. Stablecoins can provide an attractive alternative for international transfers by lowering costs, increasing settlement speeds, and more conveniently accessing dollar-pegged value. However, the prospects for stablecoins in the domestic market appear much dimmer. India already has UPI (Unified Payments Interface), which is an instant, free, and deeply integrated payment network widely used by consumers and merchants. Therefore, even though stablecoins still hold appeal for remittances, offshore value transfers, and global interconnected financial activities, their competitiveness as everyday domestic payment tools may not match that of UPI. The structural paradox of the Indian crypto market is quite prominent. The country boasts a vast user base, a rapidly growing developer community, and accelerating enterprise-level applications, yet the regulatory environment remains in a gray area of neither fully opening up nor explicitly banning. Currently, India lacks comprehensive cryptocurrency legislation and a dedicated regulatory body, with the market primarily relying on tax rules and anti-money laundering requirements for oversight. Furthermore, how to handle stablecoins, token issuance, and tokenized assets within virtual digital assets (VDAs) remains unclear. Reflecting on India's regulatory history, this ambiguity becomes even more evident. In 2018, the RBI issued a ban prohibiting banks from providing services to crypto companies, which was declared unreasonable and overturned by the Supreme Court of India in 2020; in the following years, Congress made multiple attempts to legislate but failed to pass a crypto ban bill. From a legislative perspective, India cannot legally comprehensively ban cryptocurrencies before new laws are enacted; from a fiscal standpoint, taxes related to cryptocurrencies are a stable source of revenue, with just the 1% withholding tax bringing in $6 to $7.5 million in revenue for the fiscal year 2024-2025, giving the government no financial incentive to shut down the industry entirely. Ultimately, India has formed a compromise regulatory model that does not rely on specific industry regulations but instead uses tax laws, anti-money laundering rules, and regular administrative inspections to manage an industry that is difficult to eliminate. Regulatory audits will continue to escalate in 2024-2025, with regulators relying on big data to trace tax evasion related to crypto and penalize non-compliant platforms, raising the participation costs for compliant users and local platforms, thereby forcing funds out of the formal market. This approach may facilitate short-term regulatory oversight and tax collection but severely restricts long-term industrial development. India has a top-tier scale of crypto users and developers globally, but project teams remain unclear about licensing qualifications, product compliance boundaries, and regulatory details for DeFi and non-custodial wallets. The key to breaking the deadlock in India lies not in tax collection but in establishing clear regulations that guide the industry towards sustainable development domestically. Taxation: Originally Intended for Traceability, Now Forcing the Industry Overseas The most practical bottleneck is taxation. Profits from VDA asset transactions in India are uniformly subject to a 30% income tax, with an additional 1% tax deducted at source (TDS) on qualifying transactions. The withholding tax has the most significant impact on short-term high-frequency traders: 1% of funds is deducted from each transaction immediately, and traders must wait until tax season to apply for refunds, resulting in a large amount of circulating capital being frozen for extended periods. High-frequency traders see their principal repeatedly deducted each month, significantly reducing capital utilization, making high-turnover trading commercially unfeasible. The original intention of the tax system was to control speculation and track the flow of funds, but the reality is that local trading continues to flow out. Research by Esya Centre indicates that after the introduction of the 1% TDS, 3 to 5 million Indian users shifted to overseas platforms; from July 2022 to July 2023, the trading volume of Indian users on offshore platforms exceeded $42 billion. The outflow of funds undermines the government's original intentions. Once trading moves to offshore platforms and over-the-counter P2P channels, the difficulty of regulatory tracking and tax collection increases. Estimates suggest that only 9.02% of the crypto assets held by Indian investors remain on compliant local platforms; if the TDS rate were reduced to 0.01%, it could guide trading back to local markets and potentially increase overall tax revenue. The tax issues in India's crypto landscape are no longer just about high rates; the distorted tax system is continuously pushing compliant trading towards gray and offshore markets, deviating from the original intention of regulatory transparency. To effectively manage the market in the long term, India needs to reassess its high withholding tax policies. Regulation Centered on Anti-Money Laundering: India's Financial Intelligence Agency as a Substantial Entry Barrier Currently, the core of India's crypto regulation revolves around anti-money laundering. Starting in March 2023, local virtual asset service providers must register with the Financial Intelligence Unit of India (FIU-IND) and implement compliance obligations such as customer due diligence, reporting suspicious transactions, appointing dedicated anti-money laundering personnel, establishing internal controls for anti-money laundering, and adhering to travel rules. The regulatory authority of India's financial intelligence agency continues to strengthen. In October 2025, the FIU under the Ministry of Finance issued violation notices under the Anti-Money Laundering Act to 25 overseas exchanges, including Huione, CEX.IO, and BingX; in 2024, Binance was fined 188.2 million rupees (approximately $2.25 million) for failing to complete FIU registration, while the FIU simultaneously pushed the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology to ban non-compliant overseas platforms. Meanwhile, compliance registration has also become a pathway for overseas giants to return to India: in March 2025, Coinbase completed FIU registration and obtained qualifications for compliant operations in India, with Reuters confirming that Indian crypto service providers must register with the FIU and fulfill anti-money laundering obligations. In short, India's current regulatory framework is based on anti-money laundering registration rather than a detailed licensing entry system. This set of rules establishes the minimum industry baseline, but the entry boundaries for various products remain vague; for DeFi protocols, non-custodial wallets, and decentralized applications, there is no intermediary entity, leaving the question of who bears anti-money laundering obligations and how they are implemented unanswered in current regulations. Stablecoins: Strong Demand, Regulatory Focus on Central Bank Digital Currency Stablecoins represent the biggest opportunity in the Indian market, while also being the area with the most concentrated regulatory red lines. With a large volume of remittances, abundant demand from freelancers and B2B settlements, high mobile payment penetration, and active on-chain transactions, Hashed Emergent believes that despite limited deposit and withdrawal channels, the potential for private stablecoins in India is among the highest globally. However, the top-level regulatory attitude is extremely conservative. The foreign exchange classification of stablecoins remains unresolved, with the RBI prioritizing the promotion of CBDCs. RBI Deputy Governor T. Rabi Sankar publicly stated in 2025 that stablecoins give rise to illegal cross-border payments, circumvent capital controls, impact domestic monetary policy, weaken the banking intermediary system, and threaten the stability of the financial system. India is highly sensitive to dollarization, with regulatory concerns that dollar-pegged stablecoins could squeeze the circulation of the local currency and weaken the effectiveness of capital controls and monetary policy. Official documents have repeatedly warned that dollar stablecoins could impact UPI and other domestic benchmark payment infrastructures. The current situation is that private B2B cross-border and offshore payment projects are continually testing USDC stablecoin settlements, but the central bank and policymakers prioritize supporting CBDCs and the UPI ecosystem, refusing to allow private stablecoins to become central to the domestic payment system. The gap between policy and market demand is difficult to bridge, making it challenging for India to leverage the global settlement advantages of stablecoins while integrating them into the local financial system under existing policies. Asset Tokenization: Broad Prospects, but Lack of Legal Framework Global traditional financial institutions are accelerating the on-chain pilot projects for tangible assets such as bonds, funds, real estate, bank deposits, and carbon credits. India is following up with explorations through the International Financial Services Centres Authority (IFSCA) sandbox, the Finternet project, and capital market tokenization pilots, but lacks specific token legislation, making the on-chain implementation of tangible assets face obstacles due to the forced application of traditional financial regulations. The IFSCA's 2025 consultation document on asset tokenization clearly states: this document does not constrain central bank digital currencies, general cryptocurrencies, or NFTs, but only seeks rules for asset tokenization, covering categories, issuance structures, asset custody, trading and settlement, investor rights, and risk control dimensions. The document indirectly confirms regulatory recognition of the long-term value of tokenization, but supporting regulations are still in the research and exploration stage. The challenges of tokenization are not only technical but also involve many interwoven issues, such as how tokens represent ownership of real assets, whether token transfers are considered legal ownership transfers, how to protect token holders' rights in the event of a custodian's bankruptcy, how foreign exchange controls apply when foreign investors participate, and how to handle profits from token trading under tax law. Tokenization is both a sign of the Indian industry moving towards maturity and a reflection of the complexity of the domestic regulatory system. India has leveraged UPI and Aadhaar to establish top-tier public digital infrastructure globally, but tokenization touches upon multiple legal frameworks, including property rights, foreign exchange, and securities laws; to achieve large-scale implementation, it requires not only technical pilots but also accompanying property legislation and investor protection rules. The Internal Paradox of the Developer Ecosystem India boasts the world's second-largest Web3 developer community, but the industrial value created by local talent is difficult to retain domestically. Many developers work for overseas protocols or projects registered offshore, or Indian founders register projects in Singapore, Dubai, the British Virgin Islands, or Delaware in the United States. This is evident when reviewing the list of companies mentioned earlier that are in the B-round and subsequent financing stages. Many Web3 giants associated with Indian founders or talent, such as EigenLabs, Avail, Biconomy, Instadapp, and FalconX, do not have their organizational structures based in India. Instead, they tend to establish their company structures in offshore jurisdictions like Singapore, Dubai, the British Virgin Islands, or Delaware. Notable exceptions are Indian exchanges like CoinSwitch and CoinDCX, which are rooted in the domestic market, but offshore registration is often the more common choice among protocol and infrastructure companies. Offshore registration is not merely a subjective preference of the founders. The high registration costs for Indian crypto companies, difficulties in opening bank accounts, unclear regulatory rules, high personal income tax on equity incentives, and the convenience of offshore dollar financing compel entrepreneurs to choose between registering overseas or remaining in India to work remotely on overseas projects. While talent remains in India, the benefits of companies and industries flow abroad. The consequences are evident: India continuously exports developer talent, but project equity, intellectual property, and long-term tax sources all flow out. The "world's second-largest Web3 developer market" only represents talent reserves, not the competitiveness of the domestic industry. For India to capitalize on its developer dividend, it must not only expand the number of programmers but also establish friendly regulations, optimize the tax system, ease bank account openings, and improve investment and financing regulations to retain local startup teams. Only by keeping project teams can India upgrade from a talent supplier to a global Web3 industry hub. In summary, the Indian crypto industry has not gone quiet; it is steadily maturing in a diversified direction. Early industry data was dominated by the number of exchange users and altcoin bull markets; now, the indicators are more multidimensional: ranking first in the Global Crypto Adoption Index, with annual on-chain inflows of $338 billion, nearly doubling average transaction amounts in derivatives, a resurgence in mature B-round financing, a leading mobile wallet penetration rate globally, and a batch of giants landing Web3 pilot projects. The existing shortcomings of Indian crypto are not due to a lack of demand; user, entrepreneur, developer, and on-chain capital data are all abundant. The pain points lie in the supporting systems not keeping pace with market size: high taxes are forcing capital into offshore and gray markets; anti-money laundering regulations maintain compliance baselines, but product-specific rules are lacking; the cross-border demand for stablecoins is clear, but the central bank prioritizes supporting official digital currencies; the potential for asset tokenization is enormous, but property rights and supporting legislation are absent. The next phase of India's crypto development will not depend on how many new retail investors are added, but on building adaptive systems to accommodate existing users and builders. If India can adjust its tax structure, establish clear rules for stablecoins, tokenization, and DeFi, and strike a balance between consumer protection and innovation, then the large-scale adoption of its cryptocurrencies could translate into genuine financial infrastructure innovation.

For several years policymakers, investors, and business leaders have debated two technologies that are reshaping markets, business models, and even public policy itself: AI and cryptoassets. Although both technologies have generated headlines, attracted billions in investment, and prompted debates around consumer protection and national security, the federal government's approach to oversight of these innovations has diverged in a substantial way. Recent developments surrounding artificial intelligence highlight just how different these approaches have become. On June 2, President Trump signed an executive order focused on artificial intelligence and cybersecurity. Rather than imposing mandatory pre-approval requirements on AI developers, the order establishes a voluntary framework that encourages AI firms to provide models to federal agencies for cybersecurity testing before public release. Agencies would have up to 30 days to evaluate these systems, with a specific focus on cybersecurity and critical national security risks. The Trump administration has emphasized that the objective is to enhance national security while avoiding regulations that could undermine American competitiveness in the global AI race. The order is notable not only for what it contains, but also for what it omits. Earlier proposals reportedly contained longer review periods and more oversight. The final version instead reflects a preference for voluntary cooperation between government and private industry, consistent with broader efforts to maintain U.S. leadership in AI development while lowering regulatory burdens. This is a decidedly different approach from the U.S. federal government has treated cryptoassets since they achieved mainstream adoption. For much of the last decade, federal oversight of crypto has centered on enforcement, compliance, and jurisdictional disputes. Rather than creating a unified framework from the outset, different agencies have asserted authority over different aspects of the marketplace. The SEC has focused on securities-related questions, the CFTC has emphasized commodities oversight, while Treasury Department and FinCEN have concentrated on anti-money laundering compliance. The result has been a regulatory environment that many market investors and policy advocates viewed as fragmented and reactive rather than coordinated and pro-innovation. Even as policymakers have become increasingly supportive of digital asset innovation, crypto businesses continue to operate within a framework that prioritizes reporting, disclosure, registration, custody standards, and financial surveillance. Rules such as 1099-DA reporting requirements, expanded broker definitions, anti-money laundering obligations, and tax compliance expectations demonstrate that the government's primary concern has historically been transaction monitoring and financial integrity. Federal AI policy throughout 2025 and 2026 has consistently emphasized innovation, infrastructure development, and global competitiveness. Administration policy statements have repeatedly focused on maintaining American leadership in AI, reducing barriers to development, and creating a national framework that avoids a patchwork of state-level regulations.Bitcoin and cryptoassets are fundamentally financial technologies. As a result, regulators naturally view them through lenses that have traditionally governed money, securities, commodities, and financial markets. Artificial intelligence raises a different set of questions. Policymakers are increasingly concerned about cybersecurity, military applications, workforce impacts, economic competitiveness, and geopolitical leadership. That distinction helps explain why federal agencies appear willing to collaborate with AI developers through voluntary testing programs while simultaneously maintaining detailed compliance frameworks for crypto firms. Neither approach is necessarily right or wrong, as each reflects the perceived risks associated with the underlying technology. Organizations implementing AI should expect increasing scrutiny around cybersecurity, governance, model risk management, and operational controls. The focus will likely remain on responsible deployment rather than direct regulation of every use case. Crypto businesses face a different reality. Even as legislative initiatives such as stablecoin legislation and market structure reform continue to advance, firms should expect ongoing emphasis on tax reporting, transaction monitoring, custody controls, and financial disclosures. In other words, Washington increasingly views AI as an innovation challenge and crypto as a financial oversight challenge. The long-term question is whether these paths eventually converge. As AI becomes embedded within financial services, capital markets, payments, and investment decision-making, regulators may find it increasingly difficult to separate technology oversight from financial oversight. For now, however, the message from Washington is clear: artificial intelligence is being governed primarily through a lens of national competitiveness and cybersecurity, while bitcoin and crypto continue to be governed through the established frameworks of financial regulation and compliance. That difference may prove to be one of the most important policy trends shaping both industries over the next several years.

Researchers identify hidden self-harm histories using machine learning
Source: News-Medical    Published: 2026-06-06 03:53:00

Important mental health history is often present in medical records but hard to find, especially when it is missing from the diagnosis codes that clinicians, researchers and health systems use to search and count conditions. A new study led by researchers at The University of New Mexico School of Medicine analyzed electronic health records for more than 1.3 million patients served by the Veterans Health Administration (VHA). Highlighting a common gap in how health systems track self-harm, the researchers found that diagnosis codes captured only about one-fourth of clinically documented self-harm history. "For research and planning, if we only count what is easy to see in diagnosis codes, we may substantially underestimate the need for mental health services," said Christophe Lambert, PhD, professor and interim chief of the Division of Translational Informatics in the UNM School of Medicine's Department of Internal Medicine, and the study's corresponding author. "Better measurement can help health systems plan better, help researchers study care more accurately and eventually help clinicians know when a patient may need a closer look." The study, published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research, used a novel machine learning method previously developed by members of the research team. Following expert chart review and statistical calibration, the researchers estimated that documented self-harm was present in about 7.9% of those patients seen by VHA clinicians – more than four times the 1.85% visible through diagnosis codes alone. The gap matters because missed history can affect clinical awareness, research findings and planning for mental health services. Problem lists – the notations providers compile of their patients' health conditions – showed another visibility gap. They are meant to flag important conditions for clinical teams, but in real-world care they are not always complete or consistently maintained. Among veterans with a diagnosis code for self-harm, 22.6% had self-harm or a history of self-harm listed on their VHA problem list. That means even when self-harm appeared in diagnosis codes, it was often missing from one of the record's most visible summary fields. Past self-harm is clinically important because it is one of the most important predictors of future self-harm and suicide risk. It can also shape how care is delivered, including how clinicians think about depression, PTSD, bipolar disorder, substance use, traumatic brain injury and other conditions that might occur alongside self-harm. The authors note that VHA already uses specialized suicide and overdose reporting tools and does not rely only on diagnosis codes or problem lists to monitor suicide risk. This study looked at a different but related question: How much past self-harm history is visible in the parts of the record that researchers, care teams and health systems can most easily quantify and review at scale? "This is a systems-level visibility problem," Lambert said. "The record can be enormous. In our chart review, some patient records had more than 500,000 lines of notes. No clinician can be expected to read all of that during a normal visit." The study did not try to predict future self-harm or determine with certainty whether any one patient had self-harmed. Instead, the team tested whether a computer model could use patterns in structured electronic health record data to estimate the probability that self-harm history was present but missing from diagnosis codes, then compare those probabilities with expert review of clinical notes. To do that, the team used a method called PULSNAR - Positive Unlabeled Learning Selected Not At Random, which was built for messy real-world health data. Most machine learning methods need clear examples of both "yes" and "no" cases. But in medical records, a missing diagnosis code does not prove that a patient never had the condition. PULSNAR works with that uncertainty. It learns from patients who do have a code, then estimates how many similar patients might be present among those without a code. Its key advantage is that it does not assume coded cases are random and allows for the fact that some cases are more likely to be coded than others. "Medical records can make self-harm hard to see in more than one way," said Praveen Kumar, PhD, the study's first author. "Sometimes the history is in a clinician's note but not in the diagnosis codes. Other times, the record may contain risk factors, injuries, poisonings, or behaviors that are consistent with self-harm, even though the record alone does not prove what happened or why. "Our method can help flag both patterns for review. This study could verify the first pattern, because the evidence was already in the notes. The second pattern may be just as important, but confirming it would require talking with patients or using information beyond the medical record." The research team included experts from the UNM Health Sciences Center, the Raymond G. Murphy Veterans Affairs (VA) Medical Center, Vanderbilt University Medical Center, the VA Tennessee Valley Healthcare System, the VA Office of Mental Health, Greer Black Company, and the UNM Department of Economics. The team brought together expertise in medical informatics, computer science, psychiatry, biomedical informatics, economics, statistics and health services research. The self-harm study is part of a broader research program using positive-and-unlabeled learning to find conditions that may be under-recorded in standard medical data, the investigators said. The team has already published a related study using this approach to detect under-coded opioid use disorder, and ongoing work is extending it to other conditions where the medical record may not show the full picture, including unrecognized PTSD, depression, bipolar disorder and sleep disorders. The method could complement broader VHA mental health and suicide-prevention efforts by adding a scalable way to measure conditions that may be under-recorded or hard to see in standard medical data. The investigators emphasized that the method is still a research tool and is not ready to be used by itself in clinical care, although with further development, it could help health systems better estimate under-recorded mental health conditions, find documented history that is not clearly visible, and identify records that may warrant closer review. "Self-harm history matters too much to stay buried in records that are not practical to review line by line during routine care," Lambert said. "Our work is about helping researchers and health systems find documented history and clinically relevant patterns in the data, so care teams can have a more complete picture of the people they serve." Source: News-Medical    Published: 2026-06-06 02:33:00

The study, published in Volume 153, Issue 9 of the journal Development on May 14, 2026, by The Company of Biologists, is the first to precisely map endogenous UTY occupancy across the human genome and demonstrate that UTY remains functionally involved in transcriptional regulation during early human development. The human Y chromosome has lost many of its ancestral genes over millions of years of evolution. Yet a small number of genes, including UTY, have been evolutionarily retained despite their weak expression and reduced enzymatic activity. Why these genes persist has remained a longstanding question in chromosome biology. The work provides a new perspective on Y chromosome biology-not as a static genetic structure, but as a chromosome potentially undergoing continuous functional transition even in modern humans. Varicella-zoster virus (VZV) is a clinically important human neurotropic herpesvirus that causes varicella (chickenpox) as primary infection, typically in children. After primary infection, VZV establishes lifelong latency in sensory ganglia and can later reactivate and cause herpes zoster (shingles). Globally, varicella and shingles affect a substantial number of individuals each year. Although people with shingles can recover without specific antiviral treatment, the persistent, often severe pain associated with the disease can lead to depression and significantly decrease quality of life. Currently, acyclovir and its derivatives or similar compounds are the primary FDA-approved treatments for VZV infection. However, resistance to acyclovir, often because of mutations in the viral thymidine kinase, is increasingly observed in clinical settings. Therefore, alternative antiviral strategies against VZV are needed. Propolis (also known as "bee glue"), a resin-like substance produced by bees, has well-documented antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties. The authors of this article investigated the antiviral potential of propolis against VZV in in vitro systems including a cell culture model, human skin, and human dorsal root ganglia (DRG) tissue. ARPE-19 cells, human fetal skin, DRG tissues, and the VZV pOka-Luc-GFP strain (expressing luciferase and GFP for viral monitoring) were used. Propolis (Brazilian green propolis in DMSO) cytotoxicity was assessed with MTT assays. Viral replication was quantified with time-dependent luciferase assays. RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) was performed to analyze differential gene expression in propolis-treated (0.0625% and 0.125%) and control cells (with/without VZV), and was followed by Gene Ontology and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathway analyses. Additionally, an acyclovir-resistant VZV mutant (VZV-delTK, lacking the thymidine kinase gene ORF36) was generated with BAC technology for a mechanistic comparison of propolis and acyclovir. MTT assays indicated that propolis at ≤0.125% had low cytotoxicity to ARPE-19 cells. Luciferase assays demonstrated that propolis inhibited VZV replication in a concentration-dependent manner. This efficacy was consistent in skin and DRG tissues, in which 0.1% propolis approached acyclovir's antiviral activity. RNA-seq revealed that propolis altered host gene expression. Among the 207-1035 differentially expressed genes identified across concentrations, the enriched pathways included glycolysis/gluconeogenesis, calcium signaling, and ferroptosis. Propolis also inhibited VZV RNA transcription and splicing. Notably, propolis effectively inhibited the acyclovir-resistant strain VZV-delTK, whereas acyclovir did not, thus confirming the treatments' distinct mechanisms. Propolis exhibited significant antiviral activity against VZV in both cellular and tissue models, through a mechanism distinct from that of acyclovir. Andoni Iraola's Liverpool in-tray: Get Alexander Isak firing, convince Alisson to stay and figure out what to do with Florian Wirtz

Liverpool have a new manager. Just over a year after Arne Slot led the Reds to a record-equalling 20th English championship, the Dutchman has been replaced in the dugout by Andoni Iraola. The change of coach has already lifted spirits around Anfield after a disastrous Premier League title defence that featured 12 defeats, while Liverpool were also hammered at home by Crystal Palace in the fourth round of the Carabao Cup, humiliated by Manchester City in the FA Cup quarter-finals and swept aside by Paris Saint-Germain in the last eight of the Champions League. Slot certainly wasn't solely responsible for the Reds' remarkable regression, and he probably would have been forgiven a trophy-less campaign had his side played with any great intensity. However, Liverpool looked mentally and physically frail for the majority of the season, so it was not in the least bit surprising that the supporters grew weary of watching their side struggle to score goals and keep clean sheets on a weekly basis. Sporting director Richard Hughes has, thus, tasked Iraola with once again fixing a broken team, having previously hired him at Bournemouth back in 2023. The Basque did a remarkable job with very little money to spend at the Vitality Stadium and, after losing nearly his entire defence last summer, somehow led the Cherries to a historic sixth-placed finish in last season's Premier League. The fact that he pulled off that remarkable feat by playing 'front-foot football' is the main reason why Hughes & Co. believe Iraola is the ideal man to turn things around at Anfield. However, it's not going to be easy for Iraola. As the Spaniard sits down to start work in his new role, he has an awful lot of problems to address before the new season begins in August. Here, GOAL looks at the most pressing items in Iraola's in-tray: Usually, the last thing a manager wants is to see an important player heading off to a major international tournament. However, Slot argued shortly before he was sacked that Alexander Isak's participation in the World Cup might actually be a good thing for Liverpool. After all, it's not as if the Sweden international was at the risk of burnout. He only started 13 times during his first, injury-plagued campaign on Merseyside. So, one could easily understand why Slot thought it would be "very helpful" for Isak to play "a lot of games" at the World Cup. Of course, the obvious risk is that he breaks down again in North America, but Isak unquestionably needs minutes more than any other player in the Liverpool squad. Indeed, it would be a massive boost for Iraola if the British-record buy were to come out of the tournament unscathed and with his confidence restored after stringing together some starts and maybe even scoring a couple of goals. The early signs are encouraging, too, with Isak having come off the bench to score a stunner in Sweden's friendly against Norway on Monday. Given Iraola joined Bournemouth three years ago, he's obviously familiar with the Newcastle version of Isak - and that's the version Liverpool need next season. Another four-goal campaign is utterly unthinkable for a £125 million player, so while the new coach will want Isak back for as much of pre-season as possible to impress upon him the importance of pressing, the World Cup could prove key to helping him get the very best out of his new No.9. Iraola worked wonders with Eli Junior Kroupi at Bournemouth. Who knows what he could achieve with a fully-fit Isak at Anfield? It will be a whole lot easier for Iraola to get Isak firing if he can figure out how to unlock Florian Wirtz's enormous potential. For a split-second midway through the 2025-26 campaign, it actually felt like everything was going to click into place for Liverpool. Isak confidently converting a lovely little through-ball from Wirtz in the 2-1 win at Tottenham on December 20 provided the long-suffering supporters with a glimpse of a brighter future. It proved nothing more than a glimpse, though, because Isak was left with a broken leg after being wiped out by Micky van de Ven immediately after scoring and wouldn't feature again in the Premier League for a further four months. Of course, Wirtz continued to show flashes of his undoubted genius - particularly while working in tandem with Hugo Ekitike before the Frenchman's devastating, year-ending injury. However, while fate conspired against the Germany international on more than a couple of occasions, there's no denying that Wirtz went missing far too many times, and particularly in big games. The hope is, though, that the shift in style we're now set to see under Iraola will benefit the £100m signing from Bayer Leverkusen, who should take up the No.10 role in the coach's preferred 4-2-3-1 formation. Wirtz, though, is also under pressure to prove that he can cope with the intensity and physicality of the Premier League - and thus dismiss claims that Liverpool would be better served by deploying the hard-running Dominik Szoboszlai in front of Ryan Gravenberch and Alexis Mac Allister. Getting the make-up of the midfield right really is of the utmost importance to Iraola, because the Reds were repeatedly overrun last season, with Gravenberch providing more attacking threat but less defensive cover, and Mac Allister appearing utterly exhausted. Do not be surprised, then, if Iraola asks his new employers for the specialist No.6 Liverpool have lacked for so long to lighten the load on Gravenberch, restore some badly needed balance to the side and allow Wirtz to go and do what he does best: create. Wirtz obviously also needs more quality options ahead of him, particularly as there's now a glaring hole on Liverpool's right wing. Despite giddy talk of a rapid return for Mohamed Salah following Slot's sacking, the Egyptian King won't be coming back to Anfield anytime soon, and the search for a worthy heir to the throne has already begun. It's obviously a thankless task, given we're talking about an inside-forward who both scored and assisted more goals than any other Liverpool player in Premier League history. Of course, the club could - and indeed should - have pre-empted this problem by bringing in Antoine Semenyo last summer. Instead, he was allowed to join rivals Manchester City for a bargain £64m during the winter transfer window - and Liverpool are now going to have to pay an awful lot more than that if they're to land their top target, Yan Diomande. Still, the work done by Iraola at Bournemouth not only with Semenyo but also Rayan suggests that no matter which winger the Reds sign to replace Salah, he's capable of taking their game to a whole other level. Veteran captain Virgil van Dijk may have at least one more big season in him, but while Joe Gomez may stick around for another year despite more talk of a move away from Merseyside, the versatile England international simply can't be counted on to stay fit. Given nobody can be sure how long it will take teenager Giovanni Leoni to get back up to full speed after his ACL injury, the onus is very much on new arrival Jeremy Jacquet to hit the ground running. The problem, of course, is that the 20-year-old spent a significant spell on the sidelines himself last season. However, the word is that Jacquet has made a full recovery and should be fully fit for the start of pre-season, which is just as well, really, as Iraola needs the Frenchman to quickly show why the Reds were willing to invest so much money in a defender with just 37 top-flight games under his belt. Unfortunately for forward-thinking Liverpool, both of those deals now look dreadfully ill-advised.
