A Perspective on the Indian Cryptocurrency Market: Descending into Silence or Moving Towards Maturity?

Original Title: Has India's Crypto Market Gone Quiet or Grown Up? However, data contradicts this, showing that India has consistently led the world in crypto adoption rates for several years. This report from Four Pillars comprehensively deconstructs the current Indian crypto market from multiple dimensions, interpreting signs of industry maturity from the perspectives of developers, cross-border payments, and venture capital changes, while also detailing the risks posed by the tax system, central bank policies, and talent outflow, engaging in a dialectical discussion on whether the Indian crypto market is maturing or stagnating in constraints. Data from Chainalysis shows that from July 2020 to June 2021, the scale of the Indian crypto market surged by 641%; among transactions initiated by Indian addresses, DeFi-related transactions accounted for 59%, higher than in Vietnam and Pakistan. Institutional-level large trades exceeding $10 million made up 42% of the total trading volume, indicating that India had already moved beyond pure retail speculation, with a more diversified and mature trading structure. The local exchange WazirX surpassed 10 million users in 2021, with new registrations from small towns and suburban areas increasing by 700% year-on-year; CoinSwitch Kuber secured $260 million in funding led by a16z and Coinbase Ventures, entering the unicorn club; CoinDCX also became a unicorn in 2021. Projects like Polygon, created by Indian entrepreneurs, have risen to prominence, transforming India from a major crypto consumer market into a hub for crypto technology development. Polygon, originally founded as Matic Network in 2017, aimed to address Ethereum's scalability issues and has since grown into a leading scalability infrastructure within the Ethereum ecosystem. Following Polygon, a wave of protocols and companies founded by Indian teams or led by Indian founders has emerged, including EigenLayer, Avail, Sentient, Stader Labs, Biconomy, OpenFX, FalconX, Instadapp, and more. Is the Indian Crypto Market in a State of Stagnation? Today, the Indian crypto market is far from its previous hustle and bustle, with multiple factors contributing to this change. As the global crypto market entered a bear phase, India simultaneously implemented new crypto regulations—imposing a 30% income tax on profits from virtual digital asset (VDA) transactions, and in July of the same year, introducing a 1% tax deducted at source, which severely impacted the trading activity of local exchanges. By 2025, the Indian tax authorities intensified crypto tax audits, with the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) issuing audit notices to 44,057 taxpayers involved in crypto trading who had not reported their assets in the VDA section of their income tax returns. In contrast, tracking offshore traders became significantly more challenging. This ultimately created a distorted situation: stringent rules primarily constrained compliant local platforms and law-abiding users, further accelerating the industry's outflow. Data from Chainalysis confirms that India has topped the Global Crypto Adoption Index for three consecutive years in 2023, 2024, and 2025—while the heat has faded, real demand remains. In earlier years, India relied on a massive influx of retail investors for speculation; now, the industry's focus has shifted towards developers, startups, underlying infrastructure, and institutional application scenarios. Hashed Emergent statistics show that the number of Web3 startups in India has surpassed 1,250, with total financing exceeding $3.5 billion since 2020; in 2025 alone, Indian entrepreneurs raised a total of $626 million, with a resurgence in Series B and later-stage financing after three years of stagnation, raising $396 million throughout the year. India ranks first in all four subcategories and overall, making it one of the few markets where retail, DeFi, and institutional funds are simultaneously active. In this context, stablecoins can play a practical role: they provide faster settlement speeds, easier access to dollar-pegged value, and can reduce friction in cross-border payments, especially for users facing high fees, slow bank transfers, or difficulties in obtaining traditional dollar accounts. For freelancers, exporters, overseas workers, and families receiving remittances, stablecoins can conveniently meet local currency needs and seamlessly connect with global dollar payment methods. Of course, some funds circulate through gray payment channels, posing compliance risks, but the core of India's demand for stablecoins stems from genuine financial needs: cross-border remittances, low-cost currency exchange, accelerated settlement, and protection against currency depreciation. This also explains why, despite heavy tax burdens and unclear regulations, stablecoins remain an important component of crypto trading in India. India's high score stems from two major statistical loopholes: first, the all-dimensional indicators are tied to purchasing power parity GDP per capita, which is about $11,160 for India in 2025, a low base that naturally inflates the ranking; second, the country's large population means that if recalculated on a per capita basis, India's ranking would fall out of the global top twenty. Following the introduction of the withholding tax from 2022 to 2023, a large amount of capital flowed overseas, but recently, retail funds are slowly returning to compliant local exchanges. Pi42 data shows that from 2024 to 2025, the proportion of users aged 18 to 25 among new users in the Indian crypto derivatives market skyrocketed from 24% to 61%; regionally, trading volume in eastern India increased sixfold, while northeastern and central regions each saw a fourfold increase, indicating that crypto derivatives are no longer limited to first-tier cities like Mumbai, Bangalore, and Delhi, but are continuously penetrating lower-tier markets. The key to this surge lies in the proliferation of local language crypto media in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, and Bengali on YouTube. The average transaction amount per trade rose from $1,051 in 2024 to nearly $1,960 in 2025; the proportion of high-frequency daily traders increased from 45% to 60%. A Large Number of Companies Entering the B-Round Financing Maturity Stage Benchmark companies that are either locally established or led by Indian founders include: Assessing industry maturity cannot rely solely on transaction volume and financing; developers are the core leading indicator. Developers in the crypto space are not ordinary workers; they are the supply engine for protocols, applications, underlying infrastructure, development tools, and new products. Bangalore's advantage is based on the foundation of the country's traditional IT industry, supplemented by regular hackathons and talent incubation projects from communities like Solana's Superteam and Ethfolio, continuously supplying new talent; the spread of talent across Delhi, Mumbai, Pune, and Hyderabad indicates that India's Web3 talent is not concentrated in a single city, but is rooted in the mature IT industry across the country. A notable feature of India's developer ecosystem is its youthfulness. This suggests that India's current Web3 ecosystem is less of a mature labor market and more of a vast talent reservoir, which will gradually flow into the market in the coming years. With new developers still joining and the proportion of developers with over two years of experience significantly increasing, it can be inferred that the ecosystem is gradually maturing. The level of global collaboration is also continuously rising. Among Indian developers who have been in the industry for less than a year, 18.9% participate in multinational remote teams; among those with over two years of experience, the proportion of cross-border collaboration skyrockets to 55.4%. As technical capabilities improve, Indian developers are becoming deeply embedded in global Web3 projects. This means that India is not only supplying talent for its domestic market but is also growing into a hub for outsourcing developers on a global scale. Global crypto projects offer remote work opportunities, better pay, and flexible growth paths, making Web3 an important channel for Indian tech talent to connect with the global labor market and realize their technical value. However, recently, attention has shifted to more fundamental financial infrastructures such as payments, settlements, remittances, and asset digitization. This indicates that the market is moving beyond simple speculative demand and entering a phase of considering how to apply blockchain to the actual financial system. In India, this shift first appeared at the startup level. Although this move does not signify that stablecoins have officially obtained legal payment status in India, it marks the interconnection of India's domestic settlement system with the global stablecoin network. Indian entrepreneurs are collectively betting on stablecoins through Web3 payment infrastructure companies. Transak secured $16 million in strategic financing in 2025, led by Tether and IDG, to enhance stablecoin payment infrastructure; a local tech team developed the Lightning Network payment project Speed, which combines the rapid settlement of the Lightning Network with the price stability of stablecoins like USDT, targeting applications in e-commerce settlements, creator payments, platform reconciliations, and cross-border remittances. However, when examining India's stablecoin market, we must also consider the regulatory paradox. Stablecoins have become essential tools for global cross-border payments due to their low costs and fast settlements, but privately issued stablecoins pegged to the dollar conflict with the Reserve Bank of India's currency control and monetary sovereignty policies, and the legal definition of stablecoins' foreign exchange attributes remains ambiguous. The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) maintains a high-pressure stance on private stablecoins due to concerns over macro-financial stability, monetary sovereignty, payment system security, and cross-border capital controls. Top-level policies are more inclined to promote central bank digital currencies and the UPI official payment network rather than support private dollar stablecoins. For stablecoin startups and investors, in addition to regulatory uncertainty, they also face headwinds from top-level policies that inherently oppose private dollar currencies. In immature markets, stablecoins are often used for exchange deposits and short-term speculation; even with tightened regulations on private stablecoins in India, startups continue to focus on cross-border applications, while the central bank simultaneously explores controllable digital currencies through CBDC pilot programs. India has not fully opened up stablecoins but is leveraging official systems to address the real pain points that stablecoins aim to solve, such as high remittance fees, delayed settlements, and global payment interoperability. However, this does not necessarily mean that stablecoins will see widespread adoption in India's domestic payment market. More realistic opportunities may lie in cross-border application scenarios, especially considering that India is one of the largest remittance-receiving countries globally, with annual remittance inflows exceeding $100 billion. Stablecoins can provide an attractive alternative for international transfers by lowering costs, increasing settlement speeds, and more conveniently accessing dollar-pegged value. India already has UPI (Unified Payments Interface), which is an instant, free, and deeply integrated payment network widely used by consumers and merchants. Therefore, even though stablecoins still hold appeal for remittances, offshore value transfers, and global interconnected financial activities, their competitiveness as everyday domestic payment tools may not match that of UPI. Currently, India lacks comprehensive cryptocurrency legislation and a dedicated regulatory body, with the market primarily relying on tax rules and anti-money laundering requirements for oversight. Reflecting on India's regulatory history, this ambiguity becomes even more evident. In 2018, the RBI issued a ban prohibiting banks from providing services to crypto companies, which was declared unreasonable and overturned by the Supreme Court of India in 2020; in the following years, Congress made multiple attempts to legislate but failed to pass a crypto ban bill. From a legislative perspective, India cannot legally comprehensively ban cryptocurrencies before new laws are enacted; from a fiscal standpoint, taxes related to cryptocurrencies are a stable source of revenue, with just the 1% withholding tax bringing in $6 to $7.5 million in revenue for the fiscal year 2024-2025, giving the government no financial incentive to shut down the industry entirely. Ultimately, India has formed a compromise regulatory model that does not rely on specific industry regulations but instead uses tax laws, anti-money laundering rules, and regular administrative inspections to manage an industry that is difficult to eliminate. Regulatory audits will continue to escalate in 2024-2025, with regulators relying on big data to trace tax evasion related to crypto and penalize non-compliant platforms, raising the participation costs for compliant users and local platforms, thereby forcing funds out of the formal market. This approach may facilitate short-term regulatory oversight and tax collection but severely restricts long-term industrial development. Taxation: Originally Intended for Traceability, Now Forcing the Industry Overseas Profits from VDA asset transactions in India are uniformly subject to a 30% income tax, with an additional 1% tax deducted at source (TDS) on qualifying transactions. The withholding tax has the most significant impact on short-term high-frequency traders: 1% of funds is deducted from each transaction immediately, and traders must wait until tax season to apply for refunds, resulting in a large amount of circulating capital being frozen for extended periods. High-frequency traders see their principal repeatedly deducted each month, significantly reducing capital utilization, making high-turnover trading commercially unfeasible. Research by Esya Centre indicates that after the introduction of the 1% TDS, 3 to 5 million Indian users shifted to overseas platforms; from July 2022 to July 2023, the trading volume of Indian users on offshore platforms exceeded $42 billion. Estimates suggest that only 9.02% of the crypto assets held by Indian investors remain on compliant local platforms; if the TDS rate were reduced to 0.01%, it could guide trading back to local markets and potentially increase overall tax revenue. The tax issues in India's crypto landscape are no longer just about high rates; the distorted tax system is continuously pushing compliant trading towards gray and offshore markets, deviating from the original intention of regulatory transparency. Regulation Centered on Anti-Money Laundering: India's Financial Intelligence Agency as a Substantial Entry Barrier Currently, the core of India's crypto regulation revolves around anti-money laundering. Starting in March 2023, local virtual asset service providers must register with the Financial Intelligence Unit of India (FIU-IND) and implement compliance obligations such as customer due diligence, reporting suspicious transactions, appointing dedicated anti-money laundering personnel, establishing internal controls for anti-money laundering, and adhering to travel rules. The regulatory authority of India's financial intelligence agency continues to strengthen. Meanwhile, compliance registration has also become a pathway for overseas giants to return to India: in March 2025, Coinbase completed FIU registration and obtained qualifications for compliant operations in India, with Reuters confirming that Indian crypto service providers must register with the FIU and fulfill anti-money laundering obligations. In short, India's current regulatory framework is based on anti-money laundering registration rather than a detailed licensing entry system. This set of rules establishes the minimum industry baseline, but the entry boundaries for various products remain vague; for DeFi protocols, non-custodial wallets, and decentralized applications, there is no intermediary entity, leaving the question of who bears anti-money laundering obligations and how they are implemented unanswered in current regulations. The IFSCA's 2025 consultation document on asset tokenization clearly states: this document does not constrain central bank digital currencies, general cryptocurrencies, or NFTs, but only seeks rules for asset tokenization, covering categories, issuance structures, asset custody, trading and settlement, investor rights, and risk control dimensions. The challenges of tokenization are not only technical but also involve many interwoven issues, such as how tokens represent ownership of real assets, whether token transfers are considered legal ownership transfers, how to protect token holders' rights in the event of a custodian's bankruptcy, how foreign exchange controls apply when foreign investors participate, and how to handle profits from token trading under tax law. While talent remains in India, the benefits of companies and industries flow abroad. The consequences are evident: India continuously exports developer talent, but project equity, intellectual property, and long-term tax sources all flow out. For India to capitalize on its developer dividend, it must not only expand the number of programmers but also establish friendly regulations, optimize the tax system, ease bank account openings, and improve investment and financing regulations to retain local startup teams.
