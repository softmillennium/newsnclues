While there were probably plenty of confused baseball fans during the Dodgers' first annual Hololive Night on July 6, 2024, there were likely nearly as many Hololive fans scratching their heads while wondering if it's normal for a player to score three home runs in a single game (it isn't, by the way). Beyond being an unexpected crossover between the MLB and the VTubing agency Hololive Productions, it was a night that drove home a point. Something that began in Japan as a niche within a niche had even penetrated America's pastime: VTubing had gone global. The term VTuber stands for “Virtual YouTuber,” and it's when a performer represents themselves with a digital avatar (usually with an anime aesthetic) on platforms like YouTube, Twitch, and more. These avatars are often 3D models rigged to match the performer's movements, so if they speak or blink, their model will too. Using these puppeteered characters, VTubers do everything from streaming in familiar formats, like live playthroughs of video games, to belting out songs inspired by the Japanese music idol industry. Although it varies, many VTubers have intricate fictionalized backstories. Some basically never break character, while others don't have much “lore” behind their avatar to begin with. Around the same time, companies like Hololive Production began to pop up, recruiting talent to stream, create music, and eventually perform live. After gaining popularity in Japan, Hololive's English-speaking branch debuted with five members in September 2020, including the previously mentioned Gawr Gura. Timing was definitely a big part of this bump in popularity. The COVID-19 pandemic created a widespread sense of isolation that streamers helped fill, all while anime and anime-adjacent aesthetics (like those used by most VTuber avatars) were also on the rise. As for why streamers have chosen to become VTubers, one reason is privacy and safety. Many Vtubers are women who use virtual avatars to help avoid harassment from an infamously misogynistic streaming landscape. The space has also become a place for queer exploration, especially among independent VTubers, with some using their models to navigate gender presentation and non-conformity. To dig more into the VTubing phenomenon, we spoke with Takanashi Kiara, a member of the most popular VTubing agency, Hololive. She is part of the company's first generation of English-speaking talent, Hololive Myth, and has released several singles, such as”EGO,” and two albums, including her latest, Vogelfrei. As is common with Hololive, her character has an involved backstory. She's an immortal phoenix who has existed since time immemorial. She also owns a fast-food restaurant, KFP, which is staffed by her fans who have been zoomorphized into chickens (don't ask where this place sources its meat). Thanks to her fluency in both Japanese and English, Kiara has often served as a bridge between the English and Japanese-speaking sides of Hololive, coordinating a talk show to introduce overseas viewers to the Japanese branch's members.
