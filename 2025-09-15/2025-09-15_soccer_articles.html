FIFA World Cup: Impact of expanded format and what to expect with 48 teams
Source: {'href': 'https://www.flashscore.com', 'title': 'Flashscore.com'}    Published: 2025-09-15 11:11:25

This historic change is set to redefine the way in which the world's biggest sporting event plays out. Still, at the same time, it also raises questions about quality, logistics and tradition. Fans all over the world are already looking forward to the feel of a tournament on an unprecedented scale, with announcements of the World Cup 2026 tickets and early details of match schedules. The jump to 48 teams alters this balance completely. For many countries that have struggled to qualify in their regions because of the massive competition, the door is now open. This inclusivity will ensure that fans from more corners of the globe will see their national flags represented on the grandest stage of football. The prospect of greater representation fits in with the World Cup's identity as a genuinely global festival. However, inevitably, there are worries about whether the quality of play will still be at the same elite level. For smaller footballing nations, the expansion is a once-in-a-generation opportunity. The experience of playing against the best in the world can catalyze development in football back home, inspiring new generations of players and driving investment in infrastructure. With the increasing number of teams involved, there will likely be more cases of mismatched fixtures in which footballing heavyweights will be pitted against inexperienced teams that have little hope of winning. Some of the best World Cup memories have come from when underdogs defied odds and expectations, like Cameroon in 1990 or Costa Rica in 2014. With over 100 games to be played in multiple cities in three vast countries, travel and scheduling will become a larger factor than ever before. Teams may be required to travel great distances from fixture to fixture with little or no rest time between. For players who already have gruelling schedules with their clubs, the demands of a longer World Cup could take its toll on physical limits. Squad depth will prove more important, as managers will have to rotate effectively to ensure that their players are kept fresh. Teams with deep rosters of talent may be better positioned to deal with the grind, while lesser teams could falter under the pressure. For fans, the expansion is all opportunities and challenges. On the one hand, an increase in games means more opportunities to attend games and support their countries. The spread of games throughout the United States, Mexico and Canada also means that fans from different regions have an easier time accessing matches without having to travel across continents. On the other hand, such a large tournament may make it more difficult for travelling fans to follow their teams consistently, as fixtures may be scheduled thousands of miles apart. Ticket demand is already high with anticipation growing as details of matchdays continue to emerge. Supporters are making sure they get their places in stadiums because this World Cup will be like no other in terms of scale and energy. Balancing accessibility and affordability will be crucial if the tournament is to maintain its reputation as a global festival for fans, rather than simply a commercial venture. The 2026 World Cup is not just another version of football's most significant event - it is a turning point in the game's history. The move from 32 to 48 teams is a more expansive vision of inclusivity, expansion and global participation. It provides hope to emerging nations, it provides excitement to fans wanting new stories and it allows players to make their mark on the biggest stage. Yet with these opportunities come challenges that cannot be ignored.
