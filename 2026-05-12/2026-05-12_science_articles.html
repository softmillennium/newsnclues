RAF kinases are key effectors in the RAS–RAF–MEK–ERK signaling pathway, making them important targets for the development of cancer therapeutics. Here we investigate the variable potency of DFG-out-stabilizing RAF inhibitors in mutant KRAS-expressing cell lines. We demonstrate that inhibitor potency correlates with basal RAF activity, with more active RAF being more sensitive to inhibition. We further show that DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors disrupt high-affinity RAF–MEK interactions, promoting the formation of inhibited RAF dimers. Furthermore, we identify cobimetinib as an MEK inhibitor that uniquely sensitizes RAF kinases to DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors by disrupting autoinhibited RAF–MEK complexes. Building on this insight, we developed cobimetinib analogs with enhanced sensitization properties. Together, our findings provide a mechanistic framework for understanding the cellular determinants of DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitor sensitivity and offer strategies for optimizing synergistic RAF–MEK inhibitor combinations. The RAF family protein kinases, consisting of A-RAF, B-RAF and C-RAF (RAF1) in humans, are major downstream effectors of activated RAS1. In the absence of active RAS, wild-type (WT) RAF kinases are maintained in an autoinhibited monomeric state within a complex with inactive MEK and two 14-3-3 proteins2,3,4. Activation of RAS recruits RAF homodimers and heterodimers to the plasma membrane, where they phosphorylate the activation loop of MEK (MEK1/2)5. The resulting activation-loop-phosphorylated MEK1/2 then activates its sole substrate, ERK1/2, through activation loop phosphorylation. ERK1/2, in turn, phosphorylates multiple substrates to modulate signaling and transcriptional programs that drive diverse cellular processes including differentiation, proliferation and survival6,7. Several first-generation, ATP-competitive RAF inhibitors have been approved as single agents or as combination therapies with MEK inhibitors by the US Food and Drug Administration10. These inhibitors stabilize a conformation of RAF's ATP-binding site where a helix in the N-terminal lobe of the kinase domain—called the αC helix—is outwardly displaced from its active conformation. While effective against B-RAF V600 mutants, these αC helix-out-stabilizing inhibitors paradoxically activate downstream MEK and ERK (Fig. 1a) across a wide concentration range in cells expressing non-V600 B-RAF mutants or mutant RAS11,12,13,14. Inhibitors that stabilize the DFG-out conformation, where the DFG motif in RAF's activation loop is flipped, have been shown to effectively blunt downstream signaling in multiple cellular contexts15,16,17. Unlike αC helix-out-stabilizing inhibitors, DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors potently block kinase activity despite promoting dimer formation, as they lack negative cooperativity between inhibitor-bound protomers (Fig. Consequently, this class of inhibitors holds promise for treating cancers with dysregulated RAS and RAF activity. Furthermore, it is unclear whether the same MEK inhibitors that have been successfully paired with αC helix-out-stabilizing inhibitors in treating mutant B-RAF V600 tumors will show similar effectiveness in combination with DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors. Here, we demonstrate that the potency of DFG-out-stabilizing RAF inhibitors is highly signaling state dependent in cells that express mutant KRAS and WT RAF kinases. We find that, counterintuitively, the more active RAF is within a cell, the easier it is to inhibit, with differences in basal RAF activity accounting for observed variations in potency. By quantifying intracellular target engagement across different RAF signaling complexes, we show that inhibitor potency is determined by the ease with which DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors recruit autoinhibited, monomeric RAF into dimers. A screen for synergistic MEK inhibitor cotreatment strategies revealed that cobimetinib uniquely sensitizes RAF to DFG-out inhibition by disrupting autoinhibited RAF complexes and promoting active dimers. Leveraging this insight, we show that analogs of cobimetinib can be generated that provide enhanced sensitization to DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors. Together, our findings provide a mechanistic framework for understanding the intracellular determinants of DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitor sensitivity and inform the development of synergistic cotreatment strategies. We observed that C-RAF phosphorylated at its major autoinhibitory site (S259) is minimally recruited into inhibitor-promoted heterodimers (Extended Data Fig. 6e), which is also likely true for A-RAF and B-RAF. Thus, these subpopulations of RAF, which are stably associated with inactive MEK and two 14-3-3 proteins and can only be recruited into active dimers after dephosphorylation by the SHOC2:PP1C phosphatase complex49, likely do not contribute to DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitor sensitivity. Therefore, we speculate that RAF activity in mutant KRAS-expressing cells is governed by a combination of RAS–GTP levels and the balance of RAF homodimers and heterodimers recruited by active RAS. We performed a systematic analysis of DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitor sensitivity in mutant KRAS-expressing cell lines but it is unknown whether the trends we observed generalize to cancer cells that express other mutant RAS isoforms and/or B-RAF mutants. AZ628 has been shown to have a stronger effect on cell viability as a single agent in mutant NRAS-expressing cells than cells that express mutant KRAS42 and we speculate that this is because of comparably higher basal RAF kinase activity and an increased ability of DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors to block MEK and ERK activation. However, this conjecture needs to be validated. While αC helix-out-stabilizing inhibitors effectively suppress B-RAF-V600E-driven signaling, they are less effective against non-V600 mutants. Our finding that B-RAF-G615R is sensitized to DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors suggests that other B-RAF mutations disrupting autoinhibitory RAF–MEK interactions may share this property. The low pERK IC50 values across all four DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors in MDA-MB-231 cells (Fig. 2d–f), corresponding to the only mutant KRAS-expressing line we profiled that coexpresses an RAF mutant (B-RAF-G464V), suggest that mutations not directly disrupting RAF–MEK interactions may also confer sensitivity, likely by promoting higher basal RAF dimerization50 and bypassing the potency-limiting step of recruiting monomeric RAF into dimers. Whether DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors will act as potent single agents or require synergistic MEK inhibitor cotreatment will depend on the specifics of pathway dysregulation. We profiled how diverse MEK inhibitors affect RAF's intracellular interactions and sensitivity to DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors using short cotreatment regimens and concentrations of each that provided a similar level of partial (85%) downstream inhibition. We found that cobimetinib was unique in providing sensitization to DFG-out-stabilizing RAF inhibitors, which is consistent with its promotion of B-RAF:C-RAF heterodimers and disruption of high-affinity RAF–MEK interactions2. Our results are concordant with a study showing that cobimetinib was the most synergistic MEK inhibitor when combined with AZ628 in cell viability assays across multiple mutant KRAS-expressing cell lines; the study also showed that, under longer cotreatment regimens, cobimetinib allowed AZ628 to more potently block ERK activation in A549 cells42. Several MEK inhibitors that were not in our panel have also demonstrated synergy, albeit less than cobimetinib, with AZ628 in cell viability assays under prolonged cotreatment regimens42,51 and further studies are warranted to determine whether they provide sensitization by a similar mechanism as cobimetinib. Cobimetinib's superior synergistic effects with AZ628 in cell viability assays suggest that our analogs with increased sensitization properties may also provide greater synergy when combined with DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors in blocking the proliferation of mutant KRAS-expressing cells. We found that occupation of B-RAF's and C-RAF's ATP-binding sites with DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors recruits them into dimers from autoinhibited, monomeric complexes. Our results suggest that MEK forms higher-affinity interactions with inactive, monomeric B-RAF and C-RAF, which are likely mediated by autoinhibitory activation loop interactions, than within dimeric RAF complexes. On the basis of this mechanistic observation, we generated cobimetinib analogs that we predicted would differentially modulate the conformation of an α-helix in the N-terminal region of inactive MEK's activation loop. We observed that the degree to which these structurally similar inhibitors disrupted autoinhibitory RAF–MEK interactions directly correlated with their sensitization of RAF to DFG-out-stabilizing inhibitors. Although we identified an MEK inhibitor that more effectively disrupts high-affinity RAF–MEK interactions and provides greater sensitization than cobimetinib, the molecular details for how it achieves these properties are not known. Future structural studies of MEK inhibitor-bound RAF–MEK complexes will provide insight into how these analogs modulate MEK's activation loop and guide further optimization. Together, our findings provide mechanistic insights into the sensitivity of RAF kinases to DFG-out inhibition and demonstrate how cellular signaling state impacts inhibitor potency. How these effects on direct pathway inhibition translate to antiproliferative activity under prolonged treatment remains to be determined; however, our work supports further development of MEK inhibitors optimally tuned to synergize with DFG-out-stabilizing RAF inhibitors. "We're showing that life does not only produce molecules," said Fabian Klenner, UC Riverside assistant professor of planetary sciences and co-author of the study. "Life also produces an organizational principle that we can see by applying statistics." The researchers discovered that amino acids found in living systems tend to be both more varied and more evenly distributed than amino acids formed through nonbiological processes. Fatty acids showed the opposite trend, with nonliving chemical processes producing more even distributions than biological ones. According to the team, this is the first study to show that this underlying signature of life can be detected through statistics alone, without relying on any single specialized instrument. That means the approach could potentially work using data already being collected by current and future space missions. The findings arrive at a time when planetary exploration is advancing rapidly. Missions studying Mars, Europa, Enceladus, and other worlds are producing increasingly detailed measurements of organic chemistry. However, interpreting those chemical signals remains a major challenge. Because of that, simply detecting these compounds is not considered strong enough evidence to confirm life. "Astrobiology is fundamentally a forensic science," said Gideon Yoffe, postdoctoral researcher at the Weizmann Institute of Science in Israel and first author of the study. "We're trying to infer processes from incomplete clues, often with very limited data collected by missions that are extraordinarily expensive and infrequent." Ecologists measure biodiversity using two main concepts: richness, which describes how many different species are present, and evenness, which measures how uniformly they are distributed. Yoffe first encountered this framework during doctoral studies in statistics and data science, where diversity metrics were used to uncover patterns in complicated datasets, including research involving ancient human cultures. Using roughly 100 existing datasets, the scientists examined amino acids and fatty acids from microbes, soils, fossils, meteorites, asteroids, and synthetic laboratory samples. They also observed that biological materials formed a continuum ranging from well preserved to heavily degraded. Even samples that had undergone significant degradation still preserved traces of this organizational structure. Even so, the team believes this framework could become a valuable addition to future planetary missions searching for evidence of life beyond Earth. "Our approach is one more way to assess whether life may have been there," Klenner said. "And if different techniques all point in the same direction, then that becomes very powerful." Further, offspring born to infected dams exhibit maternally derived neutralizing antibodies and transient protection upon postnatal challenge. Together, these findings, considered alongside emerging epidemiological evidence, identify pregnancy as a critical context for OROV infection and underscore the need to evaluate risks to pregnant individuals in endemic regions. Traditional cementitious materials, primarily based on Portland cement, destabilize in hot, corrosive geo-fluids, impeding the advancement of geothermal systems as a scalable energy solution. Yet, identifying geo-stable alternatives that meet multifaceted performance demands throughout the inherently non-equilibrium structural evolution over geothermal well lifetimes remains a fundamental challenge. Here, we present a cure-to-service cement design principle that orchestrates the co-evolution of mechanical properties, phase development and permeability from initial cure through prolonged service, confining metastability by decoupling early-stage Al/Si release to enforce a controlled reaction toward stable aluminosilicate forms. In-situ synchrotron XRD and geochemical modeling show preferential Al dissolution and rapid boehmite formation, while delayed Si availability suppresses premature intermediate formation. Under exposure to supercritical H2O, analcime serves as a Na+ reservoir whose gradual dissolution feeds Na-rich phyllosilicates (e.g., paragonite). The nascent composite gains strength and toughness while maintaining low permeability as metastable species convert to stable phases through staged crystallization.
