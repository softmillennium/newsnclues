After investigating a reliquary that appeared to move on its own this week, the Diocese of Lincoln has determined that the phenomenon is not a supernatural occurrence. Bishop James Conley of Lincoln told The Pillar Thursday that diocesan officials think a slight vibration in the wall, from the HVAC and electronic systems, was enough to cause the delicate reliquary to shake. On October 8, the diocese received reports of a reliquary holding a relic of St. Gemma Galgani that appeared to be moving without an apparent cause. The relic was displayed in a shadowbox with a number of other relics at St. Thomas Aquinas Church, located at the Newman Center on the University of Nebraska – Lincoln campus. Videos posted online showed the reliquary of St. Gemma appearing to vibrate and move, while the other reliquaries around it remained still. “Tests were conducted including moving the reliquary of St. Gemma from the hook it was on to a different hook. This and the entirety of our full investigation has led us to conclude this incident was not of supernatural origin.” Conley told The Pillar that this is the first time as bishop that he has investigated a potentially supernatural occurrence. “There's a canonical process on any kind of unusual [phenomenon], whether it be somebody claiming to have an apparition, a statue claiming to be crying,” Conley said. “These kinds of things are not that uncommon, and sometimes they're real and sometimes they're not.” When the relic stopped shaking after it was moved to a new pin - and another relic started shaking when placed on the first pin - officials were able to conclude that something was causing the pin at that particular location to vibrate, separate from the relic itself. This year, he said, non-students were visiting the church to pray and noticed the vibrating relic. “Not only is that a great thing to encounter the real presence of Christ in the Eucharist,” the bishop said, “but also to get to know more about St. Gemma.”

Supernatural star Rob Benedict, who played Chuck/God, spoke exclusively with ScreenRant to reveal his idea for the show's ending, and it's so much better than the Supernatural finale we got. Debates continue even to this day regarding which Supernatural ending was better, and fans remain divided. There are, therefore, many opinions when it comes to how Supernatural should have ended, but none sounds quite as good as Rob Benedict's pitch for God's role in the finale. Supernatural began with two brothers battling monsters and searching for their father, but it ultimately culminated in an epic showdown against God himself in season 15. Sadly, this didn't mean that Sam and Dean were safe. On the contrary, it's revealed that God had actually been keeping Sam and Dean alive, essentially giving them plot armor. With that now gone, Dean loses his life not in some immense battle between good and evil but rather by being slammed into rebar by a vampire. This proved incredibly controversial, even after Sam and Dean reunited in Heaven at the end of the episode, because it felt so unceremonious. However, Chuck/God actor Rob Benedict shared a theory that would have given the show a very different ending—and it's so much better for that very reason. And, you know, in season 11, he's like, I just thought of my ending. He said he's going to be the bad guy, they're going to take him down, take his powers away. This is so much more than just any old theory, though; this alternate ending actually makes more sense than the real conclusion. While there were concerns that making God the ultimate villain could be jumping the shark, this was actually a thrilling direction for the show to take, and given that Supernatural had increasingly focused on angels, demons, and even the Devil, it wasn't all that difficult to believe when it happened. Benedict actually spoke about that in his interview with ScreenRant as well, sharing: "Ultimately, I think it's kind of brilliant, and I got used to fans saying, 'We hated you in the final season.' So, I thought that was kind of cool." The only issue with this plot was, arguably, that they really were able to take God down, despite his being all-knowing, all-powerful, and omnipresent. Yes, Dean and Sam had the help of Jack, who was a Nephilim (half-human and half-angel) born of Lucifer himself, but this was still a feat a bit too grand to just accept. Rob Benedict's idea for the Supernatural finale completely resolves this issue, and it simply makes a ton of sense. If this were all part of God's plan, then that reveals in a much more believable way how/why Sam, Dean, and Jack were able to stop God. It also makes sense that, even after being 'defeated,' God would still be in control. This also would have been an ending that offered more closure, which was particularly needed because the ending the show had planned, which brought all the fan-favorite characters back together in Heaven, wasn't possible due to COVID. In fact, this alternate conclusion would have resolved some complaints about Dean's death, too. Dean died many times in Supernatural, be it from Hell Hounds claiming him when his demon contract was up or the more than 100 times he died in the episode "Mystery Spot," but that didn't stop his final death from causing outrage. That is, Dean was killed by a random vampire with no extraordinary powers compared to the average monster, and that felt, to some, like a disservice to the character and all that he had survived. Rob Benedict's Supernatural ending would resolve this issue by revealing that, even then, Dean only died in this way because it was what God wanted. This would actually make even more sense in light of Sam and Dean so blatantly turning on God and taking his powers. Whereas God had long kept Dean and Sam safe because they were his 'favorites,' after they fought against him, God undoubtedly no longer had any interest in protecting the brothers. Presumably, just the opposite was true, which would also make this conclusion logical; Supernatural didn't invent the notion that God is vengeful, and this could be seen as God's punishment. Instead, this Supernatural ending would have wrapped everything up in a way that explained how God could have been defeated, ameliorated some of the pain surrounding Dean's death, and brought the show to a clear conclusion befitting its 15 seasons.
