For another, more so than any other Trek show, Strange New Worlds has always been building toward a set endpoint. To that end, “Hegemony, Part II” opens with the sort of big impressive space battle that has become a hallmark of Alex Kurtzman-era Trek. On the medical-drama side, Pike's girlfriend Captain Marie Batel (Melanie Scrofano) has been infected with Gorn eggs that will hatch in less than a day. While Strange New Worlds' second season was filled with high points (I'm still smiling thinking about the Lower Decks crossover episode), it also sometimes felt like a selection of special, high-concept character episodes strung together rather than a true ensemble show. “Hegemony, Part II,” however, gives everyone a chance to shine by pairing them up in clever ways—from Scotty's funny but also quietly heartbreaking dynamic with his ageless former engineering professor Pelia (Carol Kane) to Spock (Ethan Peck) and Nurse Christine Chapel (Jess Bush) going full Grey's Anatomy melodrama in the sick bay. My favorite is actually the La'An/M'Benga/Ortegas/Sam quartet just because it's so unexpected. I feel like there are characters in that group we've barely seen interact before, but thrusting them together into a high-stakes escape mission highlights both what they bring to the table as individuals and how Starfleet's collaborative nature allows any set of the Enterprise crew to work in harmony. Even in their greatest moments of trauma, the Enterprise crew are still thinking about others—like Ortegas fighting through her life-threatening injuries to fly her friends to safety. That's what happens when Pike realizes that Una and Uhura's research into Gorn hibernation/hunting patterns could be the key to stopping a full-on armada invasion of Federation space. More than anything, “Hegemony, Part II” is an episode about relying on faith to get you through seemingly impossible odds. And while you could imagine a different version of this episode that lets at least one thing go wrong (particularly Spock and Chapel's improvisational surgery on Batel), it feels right that a big splashy premiere lets our heroes win on every front. Spock and La'An know what it's like to feel a connection that doesn't work out because of bad timing. It seems like Strange New Worlds might be tying two of its iconic all-powerful imps together. But, honestly, with all the Godlike Beings who popped up in the original series, why couldn't one of them have been a Q? In their own ways, “Hegemony, Part II” and “Wedding Bell Blues” are both optimistic episodes that serve as a welcome reminder of what Strange New Worlds does best.
