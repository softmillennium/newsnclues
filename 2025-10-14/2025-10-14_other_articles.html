This series, coordinated and moderated by Ignasi Ribas, a researcher at the Institute of Space Studies of Catalonia and the Institute of Space Sciences of the CSIC, will explore innovative research and cutting-edge projects in astrobiology with figures such as Charles Cockell, David Kipping, Frances Westall and Laura Kreidberg. On Tuesdays from 21 October to 11 November, the CosmoCaixa Science Museum will host the series of lectures ‘The Frontiers of Astrobiology', which offers a journey to the limits of astrobiology to explore how and where life could exist outside Earth. In four lectures, different specialists will address the study of extremophile microorganisms as a model for assessing the habitability of other worlds, the origin of life on Earth to recognise biological signals on distant planets, the comparison between our solar system and other planetary systems to understand whether Earth is unique or shares features with other habitable worlds, and the latest advances in answering the big question: Are we alone in the universe? ', which explores our fascination with the idea of extraterrestrial life and how we have imagined worlds beyond our own, and projects some possible futures through science and technology. The series kicks off on 21 October at CosmoCaixa with the lecture ‘Extreme Life on Earth and in Space' by Dr Charles Cockell, an international authority on the study of life in extreme conditions. In this session, he will discuss how microorganisms manage to survive in inhospitable environments and what this reveals about the possible habitability of other worlds. Based on pioneering research, some of it carried out in underground laboratories or during space missions, the lecture will offer the keys to understanding this knowledge, which drives exploration and future human settlement in space, and will bring us closer to the possibility of determining whether microbial life could exist beyond Earth. The second session will take place on 28 October and is entitled ‘Searching for extraterrestrials... through time'. In this meeting, David Kipping, PhD in Physics and Astronomy, will address the question of our place in the cosmos based on the few reliable data available. He will analyse the silence of the universe, without signals, and why these apparent absences impose limits on what could exist. With a critical eye, he will also explain how humanity's short technological history conditions the search and will present plausible scenarios for future contact and its impact on space exploration. Dr Westall will also share with attendees her research on the oldest biological traces on Earth and how they guide the exploration of exoplanets. This scientist, whose career ranges from the discovery of fossilised bacteria in marine sediments to working at NASA, will offer a unique insight into early geobiological processes and their usefulness in recognising life on Mars and beyond. To close this cycle, on Tuesday 11 November, Dr Laura Kreidberg, PhD in Astrophysics, will give a lecture entitled ‘Is Earth really special?'. The expert will discuss whether Earth is an exceptional planet or just one of thousands discovered outside the solar system. Thanks to the James Webb Space Telescope, it is now possible to study the atmospheres of similar worlds and assess their potential habitability. Kreidberg will explore the latest advances in this search and analyse Earth-like worlds, from lava planets with constantly changing atmospheres to systems that reveal unique secrets about planetary evolution. Supernatural Spent 15 Seasons Setting Dean Winchester Up for 1 of TV's Most Controversial Endings
Source: {'href': 'https://www.cbr.com', 'title': 'Comic Book Resources'}    Published: 2025-10-14 05:30:00

However, like most long-running shows, the popular horror series was criticized for fumbling its ending. After going up against countless foes and even God himself, fans felt it was justified that Supernatural's favorite character was given the happy ending he deserved all along. However, the million-dollar question is, did Dean want what everyone thought he wanted? Was dying on a hunt the goodbye that Supernatural had always intended for its most exciting lead? Dying is subjective in the world of Supernatural. Dean's resurrection in Season 4 and return from Hell set a precedent that the higher purpose associated with the Winchester bloodline wasn't going to be endangered by something as mere as death. Therefore, whether it was intentional or accidental, Sam and Dean died and came back to life at least a dozen times in the course of 15 seasons. That's why, after a certain point, it became routine for the brothers to leverage death in favor of completing the mission, giving the impression that it was a luxury that they could manipulate. Sam arrived just in time for a final goodbye and to hear his brother say the words he had been saying since the very beginning; for Sam to live a long, normal life. However, why couldn't Dean find it in his heart to have the same wish? What's more, why couldn't the show give Dean or the audience some closure, especially when his character had gone through valuable changes? It felt too ordinary for a legendary character like Dean to go out on a random vampire hunt after surviving angels, demons, and God's wrath. From the moment Dean called Sam for help in the first episode to his final farewell, he had been on a continuous journey with an ever-evolving character. Throughout the series, Dean slowly learned that he was more than a soldier and his brother's keeper. Therefore, it seemed more frustrating than befitting to disregard all that character development in favor of such a mundane end. On the other hand, it's highly likely the show might have been going for a grounded death for Dean after having so many near-death experiences in epic battles. It might have been symbolic of how, after everything, Dean was only human. Even if they hadn't fought angels and monsters from Purgatory, Dean might have died anyway on some random hunt, maybe even without his brother. Unfortunately, even if fans were to accept this explanation after almost five years, it still completely undermines every facet of Dean's character development. Even if it had been for a little while, Dean deserved some downtime of his own. It couldn't have been explicitly for family, but he could have at least said goodbye to the hunting world after being consistently at war for more than a decade. He was practically thrown into this tumultuous life by his father and was forced to play a parental role when he himself needed one. Therefore, that insatiable need for "hunting things and family business" was only an unintended consequence of being on the road with John. Luckily, Sam had the opportunity to get out, but Dean had been hardwired to believe, because of his circumstances, that he was the one to carry the burden, whether it was of supporting his father or protecting Sam. Regardless, even if it felt that Dean was meant to be a hunter all along, that was not the emotional input his character deserved, especially in the end. Supernatural implied that Dean died doing what he loved, but he would have loved more to have a little piece of the average life. It's safe to say that, in contrast to the lukewarm effect of the finale, episodes like "Regarding Dean" had more depth and heart. This particular episode is more meaningful than what the series finale intended to deliver. It was an underrated storyline that highlighted Dean's sentimental value and poignancy in the narrative, in addition to being the show's constant comic relief. Seeing the helplessness in both Sam and Dean's eyes as the latter slowly loses his sense of identity is what fans truly deserved out of Dean's final goodbye. To him, forgetting himself was far more significant than hunting or saving the world, proving Dean was more than everyone's sacrificial lamb or hero. Dean had finally learned that his life was worth something and that he didn't need to lay it down every chance he got. His sense of self-preservation had been dormant for so long that it took him quite a while to value his life, but in the end, it amounted to pretty much nothing with that run-of-the-mill vampire hunt. After ascending to Heaven, Dean continued to explore multiple timelines, fighting the good fight and looking after his parents in a universe where they had a happy ending. Ultimately, it all boils down to the fact that Dean Winchester's legacy is too big to end, and it even transcends time. Dean Winchester may have died, but The Winchesters proved that his spirit, impact, and story are far too powerful to stay buried.
