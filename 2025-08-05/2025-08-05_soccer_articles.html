Source: {'href': 'https://en.albat.com', 'title': 'Al Bat'}    Published: 2025-08-05 12:55:00

A standout performance can launch a career into superstardom, but for some, that single tournament is as good as it gets. These are the players who became household names for a few glorious weeks, capturing the imagination of fans with their skill, passion, and goals, only to recede from the global spotlight afterward. Here are of the most memorable one-tournament wonders in World Cup history. Russian striker Oleg Salenko etched his name in the record books by scoring an incredible five goals in a single match against Cameroon at the 1994 World Cup. This feat, combined with a penalty in another game, made him the tournament's joint Golden Boot winner. However, his international career was brief, and he never reached that level of performance again. Known as "Totò," Schillaci was a late inclusion for Italy's squad at the 1990 World Cup. He came on as a substitute in the first match and scored, sparking a run of six goals that saw him win both the Golden Boot and Golden Ball awards. However, he struggled with injuries and lost his form shortly after, scoring just one more goal for the national team. Fabio Grosso's performance in 2006 was the stuff of legend. He was a hero in that tournament but remained a more understated figure in the years that followed. At 38 years old, Roger Milla came out of retirement to represent Cameroon in the 1990 World Cup. He scored four iconic goals and celebrated with his famous corner flag dance, leading the Indomitable Lions to the quarter-finals. He scored three goals and was the team's talisman. However, he is perhaps most remembered for his dramatic penalty miss in the final minute against Uruguay, which would have sent Ghana to the semifinals. World Cup vs. European Championship: A Comparison of the Soccer World Cup and Euros
Source: {'href': 'https://www.stadiumrant.com', 'title': 'Stadium Rant'}    Published: 2025-08-05 08:00:00

The European Championship is a pan-European continental tournament, while the World Cup has teams from all continents. Those are two soccer tournaments that have similarities and differences. To begin with, the structures of both competitions are similar. Both are held every four years and contain group stages of four teams, followed by the finals. Likewise, in both, the top two finishers progress into a knockout stage. Some third-place teams can also make it through in the European Championships, and it will be the same in the FIFA World Cup from 2026. Minnows like Trinidad and Tobago, Panama, Qatar (automatically qualified), Zaire, El Salvador, North Korea, and Bolivia have made it to the main event, and many such lowly-ranked teams get thrashed. African nations have improved, but have sometimes provided easy ammunition in the finals for the Europeans. For example, Yugoslavia hammered Zaire 9-0 in the 1974 World Cup. While it might seem obvious to suggest that the World Cup is harder to win, the Euro championship does not include such easy teams in the group stages. There is no doubt that 9-0 scorelines never happen in the Euro tournament, unlike when Germany beat Saudi-Arabia by the same scoreline in the 2002 World Cup. The flavor of European football can be more defensive, and the World Cup benefits from the Brazil and Argentina teams with great forward players. Greece won the Euros with numerous 1-0 wins. They did not attack so much, but were very organized and difficult to beat. It was getting men behind the ball tactics that worked a treat. For they do not, with the likes of Holland and Portugal providing fantastic attacking players. Yet, there is a bigger defensive trend in European soccer. For Brazil, and South American soccer more generally, there has always been a bigger emphasis on flair, creativity, and an attacking mindset. Seldom have we ever seen European teams match that, but the 1954 Hungarian side was one of the closest matches. Both tournaments provide top-notch football (from the best sides at least).
