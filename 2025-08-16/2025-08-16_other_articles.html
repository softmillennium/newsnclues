Justin Ling: Critics of forest fire restrictions are painted as conspiracy theorists. This is why they might not be

Nova Scotia is, if you listen to a small-but-loud chorus, sliding into “authoritarianism” and “totalitarianism.” It is the testing ground for draconian “climate lockdowns” and a sign of a malignant government “paranoia.” For most Nova Scotians, their government's decision to ban anyone from entering the woods for any reason, save on their own property — issued with the blunt warning of “STAY OUT OF THE WOODS” — is a necessary precaution to prevent forest fires amid a bone dry summer. But many others, mostly elsewhere in Canada, see this hiking-and-camping ban as something more sinister: that “it's deja vu.” And it is a blinking indicator that, while many of us may be keen to move on from those wretched years, COVID-19 — and our governments' response to it — still looms large in the minds of many. It has made them cynical and distrustful. Earlier this week, army veteran Jeff Evely incurred a $28,872.50 fine for venturing into the woods in Cape Breton. It wasn't accidental: He walked up to a conversation officer and announced “I'm going to go in the woods over there.” During the pandemic, Evely became convinced that our freedom and liberties were slipping away. He believed the lockdowns and vaccine mandates were part of a larger plan to impose total government control, and he thinks his province's forest ban is a continuation of that plot. Evely may be an extreme example, but he's a citizen like the rest of us. And any citizen in a liberal democracy generally asks themselves two questions about state action in tough times: Do I trust my government to effectively manage this crisis? We can all agree that shelter-in-place orders during a mass shooting are reasonable, but that a citywide lockdown to find the mayor's missing cat would not be. This isn't just a perception problem. Think back to 2020: Canadians overwhelmingly heeded stay-at-home orders, understanding them as necessary. In the ensuing two years, however, compliance for heavy-handed measures declined precipitously. We saw our leaders — some who are still in power — as incompetent and prone to overreaction. At the same time, they expected the public's faith to return, as though trust is some default position: It is not. Public relations firm Edelman conducts an annual “Trust Barometer.” Between 2021 and this year, they observed a 21-point jump in the number of Canadians who worry that our leaders “purposely mislead people by saying things they know are false or gross exaggerations.” When governments deliver instructions to their citizens in exceptional times — don't go outside, wear a mask, don't start fires, get vaccinated — success is measured by compliance. This is the second time Nova Scotia has had to resort to such a ban on its sprawling forests. I asked when we may finally see a public inquiry into how our governments responded to the pandemic. Such a public inquiry is likely to tell us that, yes, governments did overreach when they imposed arbitrary lockdowns and curfews, and no the science did not support such measures. Doing so, of course, would provide ammunition to the conspiracy theorists — who are wrong about an awful lot, but right about a select few things. Hence why that former public health official believed it would never happen. We're entering into challenging times, and there are already so many malign pressures weakening trust in our institutions — sloppy faux-journalism on social media, outright misinformation, catchy conspiracy theories, amongst others.
