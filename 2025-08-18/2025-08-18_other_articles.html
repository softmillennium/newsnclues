Warmer-sounding LLMs are more likely to repeat false information and conspiracy theories
Source: {'href': 'https://the-decoder.com', 'title': 'the-decoder.com'}    Published: 2025-08-18 15:40:26

A research team at the University of Oxford set out to make language models sound warmer and more empathetic, but ran into some unexpected side effects. They used a dataset of 1,617 conversations and 3,667 human-LLM message pairs, all biased toward warmer, more empathetic responses. The warmer models were more likely to reinforce conspiracy theories, repeat false information, and give questionable medical advice. The original models had error rates between 4 and 35 percent, but the "warmer" versions saw those rates rise by an average of 7.43 percent. This pattern held true for every architecture and model size the team tested. The researchers called it "a systematic rather than model-specific phenomenon." There was another side effect: the warmer models became much more likely to agree with users, even when the users were wrong. This tendency was especially strong when users expressed emotions. The effect was most dramatic when users showed sadness; in those cases, the reliability gap between warmer and original models nearly doubled to 11.9 percent. When users expressed admiration instead, the gap shrank to 5.23 percent. These versions proved at least as reliable, and sometimes even improved by up to 13 percent. Even simple system prompts nudging models toward warmth produced similar, though smaller and less consistent, effects compared to full fine-tuning. The team says these findings matter for building and governing human-like AI systems. They reveal a fundamental trade-off: optimizing for one positive trait can undermine another. Current evaluation practices, the researchers argue, might miss risks like these, since standard benchmarks don't pick up on them. They call for new development and oversight frameworks as AI systems become more involved in everyday life. The debate over warm vs. cold LLM behavior isn't just theoretical. In April, OpenAI rolled back a GPT-4o update because it flattered users too much and encouraged problematic behavior. When OpenAI later released GPT-5, it was criticized for being too "cold" compared to the "warmer" GPT-4o. But as this study suggests, those changes may come at the cost of reliability.

NASA tracks 'alien' object speeding towards Earth at 135,000 mph: Arrival time revealed
Source: {'href': 'https://www.the-express.com', 'title': 'Daily Express US'}    Published: 2025-08-18 12:56:51

Scientists are on high alert as an unidentified object has been detected speeding towards Earth. The nature of this object remains a mystery, but one expert suggests it could be extraterrestrial in origin. Harvard professor, Professor Loeb, has been outspoken about the potential existence of spaceships in the cosmos, and now speculates that this object could be a "mothership". What we know so far about this unidentified flying object First spotted on July 1, if the object turns out to be an alien vessel, Loeb estimates that such a craft would arrive on Earth between November 21 and December 5, 2025. This so-called 'interstellar' object is only the third of its kind ever observed in our solar system. 3I/ATLAS is currently zooming towards the inner solar system at a staggering speed of 135,000 miles per hour. It's also expected that the object will vanish behind the sun in September, and won't reappear in telescope view until November or December. Loeb cautions that this might indicate extraterrestrial presence – since the object could simply be evading Earth's detection systems and observational equipment. We better be prepared for both possibilities and verify that all interstellar objects are just rocks." He even proposed this bizarre trajectory – which contradicts the natural flow of the solar system – might represent a "defensive manoeuvre," as though an alien vessel were attempting to dodge a particular path. The mysterious object will also cruise past Venus, Mars and Jupiter. He was puzzled by the object's absence of a "tail". Comets viewed through telescopes display a distinct tail composed of dust. Most alarmingly, Loeb hinted the spacecraft might even be "hostile". He recommended we transmit a message to the potential ET visitors through radio waves. However, he has now reversed course, suggesting this could be seen as a threat to unknown visitors. Chris Lintott, an astronomer at the University of Oxford, has rejected his assertions, labeling them "absolute nonsense". He told Live Science that the theory about an alien probe is an "insult to the exciting work that is being done to understand this object". They added that we shouldn't worry, as this is still 130 million miles away.
