For now, Badyal and other insiders are the only ones trying out Amazon Leo's satellite service on a beta-testing basis, but it won't be long before the first customers will be able to sign up. Badyal, who leads the effort as vice president of Amazon Leo, can hardly wait. “That, to me, is the ultimate milestone,” he said. “That's why all of us have been working on this — to get it out there, get it in the hands of the customers.” RELATED: Amazon Leo gets ready to roll out satellite broadband service — and double its pace Amazon Leo won't be entering virgin territory. For years, SpaceX's Starlink network has enjoyed the dominant position in the market for satellite broadband services via low Earth orbit. Pieces of the puzzle are coming together on the consumer side as well: Although Amazon Leo hasn't yet announced plans for pricing and availability, it has released information about three levels of service, offering downlink speeds that range from 100 megabits per second to 1 gigabit per second. This week, the FCC released information about Amazon Leo's Wi-Fi routers. During today's luncheon presentation, Badyal and Chris Weber, Amazon Leo's vice president for business and product, shared a few inside stories about the network's development. Before joining Amazon, Badyal worked at Starlink's satellite development operation in Redmond, Wash., and was famously fired by SpaceX CEO Elon Musk in June 2018. Not long afterward, Badyal met with Amazon founder Jeff Bezos, who he said was “extremely passionate and bullish” about creating a satellite broadband network. “The next thing you knew, I said, ‘OK, I will come and help you build this constellation and make this vision come true,'” Badyal recalled. Badyal and five other engineers worked out the design for the satellite constellation in an office that was blocked off with curtains. The engineers wrote up a vision document that ran longer than Amazon's traditional six pages. And putting “Amazon” at the front of the name “really means a lot, around trust and credibility,” he said. Badyal said the hardest challenge to solve didn't have anything to do with the satellites themselves, but with building low-cost customer terminals. “I had to call the team that night and just tell them what an incredible job they'd done. It's because it's this mission of delivering connectivity to underserved and unserved communities across consumers, government and business,” Weber said.
