King Edward VII and Queen Alexandra were a glamorous couple who established their own vibrant court at Marlborough House, filled with contemporary art, opulent balls, and society events. They were famous for their style, with Alexandra named by Vogue as 'the legitimate head of fashion throughout the British dominions'. The exhibition will feature over 300 items from the Royal Collection, including fashion, jewellery, paintings, photographs, books, sculpture, and ceramics. Visitors will see works by celebrated names such as Carl Fabergé, Frederic Leighton, Edward Burne-Jones, Laurits Tuxen, John Singer Sargent, Olive Edis, Philip de László, William Morris, Oscar Wilde, and Edward Elgar. The royal couples lived lavish, sociable, fast-paced lives, embracing new trends and technologies. They collected works by great contemporary artists of the period, including a previously unseen study of Sleeping Beauty by Sir Edward Burne-Jones. Their private residences at Marlborough House and Sandringham House were fashionably cluttered interiors with decorative objects and family photographs covering every surface. A Cartier crystal pencil case set with diamonds and rubies will be on show for the first time, along with over 20 items by Fabergé. The royal family surrounded themselves with fashionable society figures - known as the 'Marlborough House Set' - and their lives were full of garden parties, concerts, sporting events, and costume balls. Visitors will see souvenirs and mementos from these events, including a never-before-seen photograph of Edward in fancy-dress at a ball celebrating Queen Victoria's 1897 Diamond Jubilee. The royal couples passionately explored new artistic movements such as Aestheticism, Art Nouveau, and Arts and Crafts, reflected in their collections. A copy of Oscar Wilde's Poems will be on display, personally inscribed by the author, along with an early edition of the first book printed by William Morris's Kelmscott Press. The royal family also embraced the new medium of photography, using portable Kodak cameras to capture official events and family moments. Visitors will see items from their travels on five continents, including an Egyptian scarab brooch given to Alexandra by Edward following his tour of the Middle East in 1863. Four years into George V's reign, war broke out, and the glitz and glamour of the Edwardian age came to an abrupt end. The exhibition will feature haunting wartime landscapes by Olive Edis, Britain's first official female war photographer, and Frank O. Salisbury's painting showing the unveiling of the Cenotaph on 11 November 1920. The Edwardians: Age of Elegance is at The King's Gallery, Buckingham Palace, from 11 April to 23 November 2025.
