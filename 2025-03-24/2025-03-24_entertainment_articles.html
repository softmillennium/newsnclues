Every Hollywood Movie Faces an Intermission in Indian Theaters, and Even Filmmakers Can’t Stop Them

Source: indiewire   Published: 2025-03-24 18:00:00

Martin Scorsese wanted "Killers of the Flower Moon" to be experienced in one unbroken stretch, but Indian theaters did not follow his vision. Though the word "Intermission" is not explicitly flashed on the screen during showings of Hollywood movies in India, Indians are accustomed to this time-honored tradition of forced intervals. Every Hollywood movie released in Indian theaters has a mid-screening intermission, including Christopher Nolan's "Oppenheimer" and James Cameron's "Avatar: The Way of Water." For Western audiences, this abrupt pause may seem like a momentum-killer or even sacrilegious. For Indian audiences, it's part of their cinematic experience. Akkshay Rathie, film exhibitor and director of Ashirwad Theatres, said that the food and beverage sales account for almost 30-40 percent of cinema's revenue in India. In India, a lack of intermissions could mean a loss to business. Theater owners consider these few minutes as prime-time advertising slots: appealing, lucrative, and short. Rathie added that movie-watching is centered around the intermission, which aligns with the country's unique theater-going experience. Devang Sampat, managing director of Cinepolis India, said that exhibitors strive to find a natural pause that aligns with the film's rhythm. However, the audience may not always be aligned. For movies such as "Dunkirk" and "Joker: Folie à Deux," theaters received complaints and requests to shift the interval timing. Christopher Nolan has long advocated for his films to be experienced without any interruptions, but even Nolan's movies were not spared in India. Rathie said that exceptions could be made if a major, Oscar-winning Hollywood director or studio had a clear directive. However, exhibitors are unlikely to comply as it is seen as impractical to hold audiences captive without a break. Sampat echoed a similar sentiment; "There have been cases where studios or directors have expressed a preference for an uninterrupted screening." Recent films such as "Dune: Part Two," "Gladiator II," "Furiosa: A Mad Max Saga," and "Wicked" stretching beyond the two-and-half-hour mark have led industry insiders to feel that Indian audiences are accustomed to intermissions. Rathie added that many Hollywood studios have accepted this as a part of the localization process and the country's unique theater-going experience.
