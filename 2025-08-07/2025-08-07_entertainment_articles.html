Filmmaker Kayci Lacob knew she wanted to capture everything about growing up in the heart of Silicon Valley during the early-2000s tech boom for her directorial debut. She also knew that her first film deserved to be in theaters, and so Lacob did what most established auteurs are still hesitant to do (other than Francis Ford Coppola with “Megalopolis”): She is self-distributing her debut “Everything to Me” for a theatrical run and “betting” on audiences to buy tickets. “Everything to Me” isn't without star power. “The Haunting of Hill House,” “Ponyboi,” and “You” actress Victoria Pedretti leads the film as Claudia, a woman reflecting on her career trajectory and life choices years after aspiring to be the next Steve Jobs as a Stanford freshman (Abigail Donaghy plays a younger version of the character). Writer/director Lacob counts fellow Northern California native and “Lady Bird” helmer Greta Gerwig among her filmmaking “heroes.” And while Gerwig's “Barbie” proved that women are a massive theater-going audience, female directors have still scrambled in the indie space to find funding, let alone theatrical distribution. Lacob is redefining — and to use Silicon Valley tech speak, disrupting — the path for first-time filmmakers by self-distributing the theatrical release of “Everything to Me.” “We decided to self-distribute this movie through Concourse Media for a theatrical run by betting on our audience,” Lacob told IndieWire. “But getting noticed by buyers is a whole other ballgame. As young Gen-Z women are funding the reopening of almost century-old theaters and bypassing large studios to ensure theatrical debuts, it seems as though the next generation of female filmmakers are dedicated to preserving the future of theatergoing more than anyone. Below, director Lacob unpacks her critically acclaimed “Everything to Me” and shares with IndieWire why she was determined to release the film in theaters without waiting for a studio. IndieWire: As a fellow Bay Area native, what inspired the story of “Everything to Me”? There were two huge influences bearing down on me, outside of my family, that led to this film. One was the Bay Area itself, which I view almost as its own character in my upbringing. This place has soul and is a part of who I am; it really shaped my view on the world. Part of this is because I grew up in a really special time, right at the change of the millennium. There have been many eccentric and prolific innovators in Silicon Valley who have changed the world, but Steve was an entity all his own. It seemed as though he was creating technology, movies, music, and stores just for me. Being surrounded by all of this created both significant challenges and beautiful opportunities for those of us who grew up there. Before the millennial minimalism aesthetic took over, our world was colorful and changing constantly. We were living in a cautionary tale, but we were also there for the dawn of the digital age. I wanted a movie that captured all of these emotions, partially because I wasn't even aware of it all while it was happening. Much like our adult Claudia in the film, I had to turn back and look at my life, my experiences, and where I'm from to try to answer the questions, “Who am I and what do I want to say?” It was through this exploration that Claudia's story started to take shape.We can all look back at a slate of movies by a single filmmaker and think that their journey was deliberate, that they knew exactly who they were and what they wanted to say about the world from the get-go. As long as I knew what I wanted, that's what mattered.One more piece of advice that I can't emphasize enough for a young filmmaker, especially a young woman, is to surround yourself with the best talent possible.
