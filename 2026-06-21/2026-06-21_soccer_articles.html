The study, co-authored by a Teesside University PhD graduate, suggests that long-distance travel, time zone changes, and disrupted sleep are major factors affecting player performance and recovery. "The challenge becomes even more complicated when these environmental stressors are layered on top of long-haul travel and compressed match schedules. "In North American soccer, research has demonstrated that it is often not one isolated journey that creates problems, but the cumulative effect of repeated exposure over time exponentially increases these challenges." Medical and performance staff from professional clubs took part in the study by completing surveys. More than 90 per cent of practitioners said the repeated travel demands throughout a season -or during periods of congested fixtures -had a negative impact on player performance and recovery. The report highlights the scale of travel in North American professional football, where teams can face flights of up to six hours, cross three time zones, and travel more than 2,800 miles for one fixture. Such conditions contrast sharply with European leagues, where journeys are generally much shorter, and raise concerns about similar challenges for international teams during the 2026 World Cup. Suggestions include prioritising sleep, optimising arrival times, managing nutrition, and maintaining consistent routines. Arriving earlier in the day and staying overnight instead of travelling immediately after matches are believed to help players recover more effectively. Professor Paul Chesterton, from Teesside University's School of Health & Life Sciences, said: "This research highlights that travel in elite North American soccer should not be viewed as isolated journeys, but as a cumulative load that can influence performance across a season. "With the World Cup taking place across three countries, understanding how factors such as travel duration, time zones and disrupted sleep affect players is increasingly important.
