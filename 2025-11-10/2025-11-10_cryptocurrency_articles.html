Why SWIFT and Ripple Are Really Competing for Institutional Legitimacy, Not Technology | Credit: Veronica Cestari /CCN The main issue between Ripple and SWIFT lies in a simple question: how global money moves. Ripple enables real-time settlements through blockchain-based technology that transfers value between digital wallets across its RippleNet network. SWIFT, by contrast, coordinates cross-border payments through its global messaging infrastructure used by more than 11,000 financial institutions and in more than 200 countries and territories. It does not move money directly but sends secure payment instructions through applications that connect participating banks' internal systems. Their competition is about which one financial institution can rely on to support global finance. Because SWIFT and Ripple provide fundamentally different services, the question almost answers itself. Both systems are scaling rapidly, but their growth underscores different strengths: SWIFT's reach and compliance depth contrast with Ripple's agility and expanding use of blockchain for liquidity management. This divergence sets the stage for technical convergence under shared standards like ISO 20022. SWIFT evolves within the established banking framework, while Ripple continues to innovate outside it. It creates a structured language for financial data, allowing legacy institutions and new technologies to communicate seamlessly. This shift is making interoperability between SWIFT and Ripple increasingly practical. SWIFT's migration to ISO 20022 and Ripple's early compliance with the standard establish a foundation where both can operate within the same ecosystem while maintaining their distinct functions. In short, ISO 20022 acts as the bridge between traditional finance and blockchain-based networks. It enables data interoperability, reduces friction in cross-border transactions, and lays the groundwork for a future where SWIFT and Ripple can coexist within a unified payment infrastructure. Ripple's distributed-ledger technology enables quick processing, but institutions weigh other factors before adopting a system at scale. While Ripple challenges traditional inefficiencies, SWIFT's institutional network still defines trust in global finance. The competition between SWIFT and Ripple is unlikely to produce a single winner. Instead, the future of global payments is heading toward a segmented model where both systems serve different markets and transaction types. In this hybrid future, SWIFT maintains its role as the backbone of institutional finance, while Ripple grows as a complementary network that addresses liquidity constraints and near-instant payments. The dividing line will not be technological capability but rather institutional purpose with both systems coexisting in a global architecture that prioritizes interoperability. The rivalry between SWIFT and Ripple has become a driver of innovation rather than conflict. SWIFT maintains its position as the backbone of institutional finance, trusted for security, governance, and global reliability. Bank of England proposes ownership limits in new stablecoin rules
Source: {'href': 'https://uk.finance.yahoo.com', 'title': 'Yahoo Finance UK'}    Published: 2025-11-10 12:18:25

The Bank of England has partly softened proposals for ownership restrictions on UK stablecoins as it pushes forwards with new rules for the cryptocurrency. On Monday, the central bank said it is planning to temporarily impose caps on holdings of stablecoins, which are cryptocurrencies pegged directly to traditional assets such as real currency. The plans indicate that individuals will be limited to owning up to £20,000 in UK stablecoins that are considered systemically important. It said most businesses would have a limit of up to £10 million. Last month, Governor Andrew Bailey said the UK should “reap the benefits” of stablecoins as part of the country's financial system and said it would be “wrong” to be against them as a matter of principle. In a new consultation document, the Bank said it is “preparing for a future where new forms of digital money may be widely used for payments alongside existing ones, offering valuable choice for the public”. It said its proposed limits would include allowing stablecoin issuers to have as much as 60% of the backing assets in short-term government debt. Sarah Breeden, deputy governor for Financial Stability at the Bank, said: “Our objective remains to support innovation and build trust in this emerging form of money. “We've listened carefully to feedback and amended our proposals for achieving this, including on how stablecoin issuers interact with the Bank of England. “These proposals are fit for a future where stablecoins play a meaningful role in payments, giving the industry the clarity it needs to plan with confidence.”
