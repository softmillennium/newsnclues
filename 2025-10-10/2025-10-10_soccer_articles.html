After paying hundreds or thousands of dollars for the “right to buy” 2026 World Cup tickets, some fans now say they feel “ripped off,” “scammed” or “taken advantage of” by FIFA and its digital collectibles platform, FIFA Collect. Over the past year, FIFA and a crypto partner, Modex, have sold tens of thousands of “Right To Buy” tokens on the platform. Each token cost hundreds of dollars and promised buyers the ability to purchase one or two World Cup tickets at a to-be-determined later date, for a to-be-determined full price. Many saw the so-called “RTBs” as a clever way to bypass ticket lotteries, and perhaps more cost-effective than buying on the secondary market, where tickets are currently listed for far more than their original price. “This is so bad, there's no value in buying a RTB,” another said. Multiple RTB holders told The Athletic that they and many others had assumed they'd be able to use the tokens to buy more affordable tickets. Early buyers were told by FIFA Collect in a December 2024 email reviewed by The Athletic: “Tickets will be available across different categories, providing options to suit various preferences and needs.” Jon Heath, one of those buyers, told The Athletic: “The whole point for me was (seemingly) access to face value tickets in cheaper categories.” Instead, having already spent thousands of dollars apiece on RTBs for the U.S. men's national team's opening match, he'll now have to pay an additional $1,940 per Category 2 ticket or $2,735 per Category 1 ticket. FIFA Collect administrators said Tuesday that 70 per cent of the tickets that will soon be made available to RTB holders will be in Category 1, the most expensive category; 28 per cent would be in Category 2; and a mere 2 per cent would be in Category 3. Many also rushed to sell their RTBs on FIFA Collect's secondary marketplace. Before Tuesday's Discord call with users, dozens of RTBs per day were being resold for profit. Some RTB holders pointed out that, in some cases, the price of their RTB plus the price of Category 1 tickets they'll be able to buy is roughly equivalent to or less than the current price of those same tickets on FIFA's resale platform. “I feel ripped off,” said Weston Binford, who, over the past month, spent thousands on RTBs for several matches in Texas, but now feels “turned off” because the tickets themselves are too expensive. Blok, an avid user who spoke on the condition that he be identified only by his online alias, told The Athletic: “We were taken advantage of and we seem to have no voice.” For months, some of these same users had been asking FIFA Collect for answers: When would they be able to convert their RTBs into tickets? On Sept. 19, in an attempt to quell frustration, an administrator wrote to Discord users: “Guys, rest assured, you will be content with the outcome. But even as FIFA's first 2026 World Cup ticket sales phase opened, answers never came. In fact, FIFA never announced any ticket prices beyond a minimum and maximum. FIFA Collect did, though, continue to aggressively promote and sell RTBs through last weekend. On Saturday, it went to market with new batches of “City Glory” packs. Those, it seems, were some of the last sales before a November RTB conversion window, during which, a FIFA Collect administrator said, holders will be able to exchange their tokens for “what we call a digital collectible ticket” — an NFT that will then convert into an actual ticket in May or June, weeks or days before the World Cup begins.
