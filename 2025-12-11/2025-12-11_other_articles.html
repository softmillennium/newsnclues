We investigate the microlensing detectability of extraterrestrial technosignatures originating from Dyson sphere ‚Äì like structures, such as Dyson Swarms surrounding primordial black holes (PBHs). These hypothetical swarms consist of stochastically varying, partially opaque structures that could modulate standard microlensing light curves through time-dependent transmission effects. We introduce a probabilistic framework that includes a stochastic transmission model governed by variable optical depth and random gap distributions. We perform a parameter scan and generate heatmaps of the optical transit duration. We study the infrared excess radiation and peak emission wavelength as complementary observational signatures. Our findings provide a new avenue for searching for extraterrestrial advanced civilizations by extending microlensing studies to include artificial, dynamic modulation signatures. This Classic Supernatural Episode Is the Scariest 44 Minutes of Winchester Lore
Source: {'href': 'https://www.cbr.com', 'title': 'Comic Book Resources'}    Published: 2025-12-11 09:00:00

"Saving people, hunting things, the family business" is a quote that has become synonymous with Supernatural, but no other season encapsulates this feeling like Season 1 of Supernatural does. Before the horror show fell into overarching storylines that lasted seasons, it was known for its snappy monster-of-the-week format, where Sam and Dean Winchester hunted down a different demon in every episode and actually saved countless lives while doing so. While the Winchesters were tracking down paranormal absurdities across the country, they were also rebuilding a bond that they had lost for many years. In Season 1, Episode 5, "Bloody Mary," Supernatural was at its finest, terrifying viewers with a familiar monster, while also giving them much-needed emotional payoff. The legend of Bloody Mary is one that has been worn thin over the past few decades, especially through email forwards that simplify it to the point where it ceases to be scary. According to the lore, if one faces a mirror and says "Bloody Mary" thrice, a woman will kill them instantly through the reflective surface. The episode begins as the scariest stories do: at a slumber party where three teen girls are playing sleepover games. Charlie and Jill dare Lilly to go to the bathroom mirror and say "Bloody Mary," which she does, but nothing happens. As Sam and Dean descend on Toledo, Ohio, for another monster-of-the-week case, Jill decides to repeat the phrase "Bloody Mary" jokingly into a mirror, and soon sees the same dark figure and suffers the same agonizing death as Steven. Trying to find a pattern, Sam and Dean inspect the mirrors that the two victims died in front of, and found the names of Steven's wife and a little boy who had died in a hit-and-run case, whose car details matched Jill's. Sam and Dean find a vengeful spirit named Mary Worthington, who had been killed in front of a large mirror when she was a teenager, and when her murder was not solved, she started attacking those whom she thought had secretly killed other people, the way she had been. The mirror found its way to Toledo, and the brothers then worked towards saving Charlie, who was being followed by Mary because she once had a boyfriend who died by suicide when she left him. Supernatural could have taken the easy route and had Mary kill the people who summoned her, but writers Eric Kripke, Ron Milbauer, and Terri Hughes Burton chose to add layers to a story that had been done to death. They refreshed it by giving Mary an origin story; she used to be a young girl whose life was ended in an untimely manner. So, she made it her afterlife's purpose to avenge those who had killed people and gotten away with it. Mary Worthington's crusade for justice matched Sam Winchester's own guilt about his girlfriend, Jessica's death. Mary Worthington's spirit tried to attack Sam, and he, too, bled from his eyes, but the key lay in Sam finally trusting himself and his brother that he did not kill anyone.
