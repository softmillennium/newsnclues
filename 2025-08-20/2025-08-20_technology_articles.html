Pixel 10 Family Hands-On: Gemini Hops Into the Driver's Seat as Hardware Takes a Backseat

To be fair, there are hardware upgrades to the Pixel 10, but most of them are under-the-hood improvements for stuff like performance and battery life—it's a spec bump year for Google. Since these are the 10th-generation Pixels, Google said it wanted to give a nod to the original “Really Blue” Pixel. I like the color a lot, though I didn't have the new “Blue Shadow” Samsung Galaxy Z Fold 7 and Z Flip 7 on hand to compare the shade of blue. Either way, blue is definitely in this year—unless you're Apple, orange will apparently be the iPhone 17 Pro's new special color. The Pixel 10 also comes in “Frost” (pastel purple), “Lemongrass” (yellow), and “Obsidian” (black). But, wait, what's that inside the pill-shaped camera bar? Yes, that's a third camera lens—a 10.8-megapixel telephoto with 5x optical zoom. It's the first time that a regular Pixel has three cameras. The image sensors are also smaller, which may mean worse low-light performance. However, it's possible that improved software and AI processing can make up for the hardware downgrades. The 6.3-inch LTPO OLED display is the same size as before, but it is brighter (up to 2,000 nits for HDR and up to 3,000 nits for peak brightness), so visibility outdoors should be better. Google is also introducing a new MagSafe-like magnetic charging puck called Pixelsnap. And for the first time on any Pixel phone, there's built-in Satellite SOS, which works more or less the same as it does on newer iPhones. There's a gray “Moonstone” color that has shades of purple under specific lighting, and the “Jade” is a lime-ish colored green-yellow. It's a bummer there's no “Indigo” blue for the Pixel 10 Pros. Both phones have the same 6.3-inch and 6.8-inch LTPO OLED screens, but they're even brighter than the Pixel 10 (up to 2,200 nits for HDR and up to 3,300 nits for peak brightness). These are “Pro” phones, so you do get more RAM (16GB) and additional storage capacities to choose from (128GB, 256GB, 512GB, or 1TB). The potential game-changers are in the AI and Gemini features, which I'll get to in a minute. There's not much to say about the foldable except that it isn't a threat to the thinner and lighter Galaxy Z Fold 7. Google has polished up the rough edges (i.e., there's a new gearless hinge that Google says is twice as durable as the one on the Pixel 9 Pro Fold), and the cover screen is a teensy bit larger at 6.4 inches, which also means the 8-inch foldable screen is also a smidge larger. Google also says the folding screen is more durable. Well, first, it's finally IP68 rated for dust and water resistance, which makes it as durable as non-folding flagship phones. And apparently, it can handle 10 years of folding with the new gearless hinge. Also, with two layers of “anti-impact film,” the Pixel 10 Pro Fold should be more protected if dropped. Of course, I wasn't able to test out this improved durability, but I appreciate that Google has bolstered the design cause foldables are fragile as hell. There's a larger 5,015mAh battery (up from 4,650mAh) with Qi2 wireless charging and Pixelsnap compatibility, a faster Tensor G5 chip, and Satellite SOS. The Pixel 10 Pro Fold comes with 16GB of RAM and 256GB, 512GB, or 1TB of storage, and you can get it in two colors (Moonstone or Jade). If you've been paying attention to the world of consumer tech, you'll already know that phone hardware has matured to the point where it's now all about what kind of AI features tech companies can shove in there to make new phones worth buying. Everybody's trying to figure this out, not just Google. All Pixel 10 Pro phones (10 Pro, 10 Pro XL, and 10 Pro Fold) come with a free year of Google AI Pro—a $250/month subscription that gets you the best of what Google's AI has to offer, including access to Veo 3, the video generator that's taken the world by storm since its debut at I/O. As you can tell from the mild hardware refresh, the Pixel 10 phones are just vehicles for Gemini, and some of the new AI features that Google has come up with seem genuinely useful. “Magic Cue” is on-device “personalized intelligence” that helps you surface relevant information within apps and then suggests quick actions to take. For example, say you're calling up a restaurant to make a reservation; Magic Cue could show relevant info like directions to the place or opening and closing hours. Magic Cue feels like the start of agentic and ambient computing, where we spend less time interacting nonstop inside of apps, and AI gives us a helping hand. I also demoed a new Gemini-powered version for Google Translate, which uses on-device language processing to essentially deepfake your voice into another language. It worked fast, and hearing myself speaking in German with what sounded like my intonations was both impressive and eerie. This felt like the breakthrough that Google Translate has been working toward to make real-time translation even more frictionless with a phone between two people. Other AI and Gemini features that I saw were located within the Pixel 10's camera app. “Camera Coach” uses Gemini models to show a row of thumbnails with suggestions on how to improve a shot you've taken. It offers up basic suggestions like using the rule of thirds by reframing certain subjects to the left or right and foreground or background, telling you to switch cameras or modes—basically, it's photography for dummies. I won't get much out of it, but I could see it being helpful to regular people who want to take more interesting photos. There's also a new “Auto Best Take” that automatically merges similar photos into one “best” photo and an enhanced “Add Me” feature that ‘photoshops' the photographer into a group shot using pseudo augmented reality. The one Gemini-powered camera feature that I'm most interested in trying in depth is “Pro Res Zoom” for the Pixel 10 Pros. The Pixel 10 (and older Pixel phones) has regular Super Res Zoom, which uses machine learning to sharpen and enhance detail for zoomed-in photos. Pro Res Zoom uses AI and Gemini models to basically generate new detail. In one demo, the difference in detail between Pro Res Zoom on a Pixel 10 Pro and Super Res Zoom on a Pixel 10 was night and day, especially at even higher magnifications like 100x zoom for the Pixel 10 Pros. In my limited demo, we zoomed in on a water tower, and Pro Res Zoom generated wood textures for its exterior. But on text, like signs, it's clear AI still has a way to go. Text did look clearer and more legible, but still a little garbled, like an alien language at times. I'm a realist, and as much as I want foldable phones to be cheaper, I know it'll be a long road to that day, or it may never even come. I also know that regular bar-style smartphones only have so much room for improvement, and so transplanting AI into them and eking out small improvements to the displays, battery life, and the cameras is just how things are gonna be for the foreseeable future. I'll need to spend real time with the Pixel 10 phones once I get review units to try, but for now, the hardware is fine. Preorders for the 10 Pro Fold start Aug. 20, and availability on Oct. 9. With the Pixel Watch 4, Google's Smartwatch Is Finally Repairable
Source: {'href': 'https://www.wired.com', 'title': 'WIRED'}    Published: 2025-08-20 16:00:00

All products featured on WIRED are independently selected by our editors. Google's first Pixel Watch was fashionably late to the wrist computing party, arriving in 2022 when the market was already quite mature. And while the smartwatch has since cemented its place among top-tier wearables, it has one red flag: It isn't repairable. For the past three generations, Google has been replacing broken Pixel Watches instead of repairing them. “Watches and wearables are still fairly nascent," a spokesperson explained at a 2024 Climate Week panel when asked why the watch wasn't repairable. The Pixel Watch 4 retains its domed glass design but can be disassembled with a screwdriver, and it uses largely the same-size screws throughout its construction. A customer won't void the one-year warranty if the watch is disassembled for screen or battery repairs, and Google will sell replacements for those components at iFixit. There are several other small improvements to the smartwatch, like longer battery life and a new gesture that lets you talk to Gemini by raising your wrist. (The versions with LTE connectivity cost $100 more, respectively.) You can preorder it now, but unlike the Pixel 10 phones that are available this month, the watch won't be available until October 9. Google invited me to its Mountain View, California, offices to take an exclusive look at a teardown of the Pixel Watch 4. (Google paid for a portion of my travel expenses to see its research facility.) Junyong Park, an industrial designer who worked on the device, began disassembling it, manicured nails and all. Park says it was a “tremendous collaboration” between the design and engineering teams. “We're pretty proud of how this turned out.” Google intentionally made it so that this connector doesn't need to be removed for battery replacements. Kyle Wiens, CEO of iFixit, which has partnered with Google to sell the spare parts for the watch, says these kinds of connectors can be fragile, so it's always better if the device's design lets you replace components without having to unplug them. This is intentional, as you can use the same Torx screwdriver for battery replacements, reducing the number of tools needed for repairs. (A Torx screwdriver is commonly used in electronics repairs and is included in the repair kits sold by iFixit.) Overall, it looked fairly simple, and it took Park less than 13 minutes to remove these components while talking about the process and answering my questions.
