And it fell in line with a trend that now spans the globe. The USMNT, over the past three years, has now been a quasi-away team on home soil against opponents from all five major soccer continents, with respect to Oceania. They drowned out one solitary section of USMNT supporters, who, with the help of a stadium emcee and a drum, tried to build up to the game with a rhythmic clap; several seconds in, the claps could not be heard. They came in national team jerseys with the names of their heroes, Son Heung-min or Park Ji-sung, printed on the back. They came in Tottenham Hotspur and LAFC jerseys for Son. There were Bayern Munich and Napoli kits for Kim Min-jae. Occasionally, amid the seas of red, a Christian Pulisic jersey would appear. They roared when Korean players emerged for warmups and bowed to all sides of the arena. But when the game began, they only had one team to cheer for. U.S. head coach Mauricio Pochettino was asked about them after the match, and a couple months ago, during the Concacaf Gold Cup, he might have launched into a spiel about how U.S. fans needed to bring more passion. “I want to be positive for now,” he said. Part of the trend is irreversible, inescapable in a country as diverse as the U.S., a country with so many immigrant communities that feel drawn to their native country's team because it represents a touchpoint with home. But part of it, too, is about the dullness of this current USMNT and its monotonous, meaningless runway toward the World Cup. And the U.S. team played soccer that was equally uninspiring. Korea, meanwhile, launched a regular cadence of attacks, and fans leapt from their seats each time. They then erupted when Son scored the opening goal. They erupted again when Lee Dong-gyeong doubled the lead. And then, for minutes after the final whistle, they stayed put. They whipped out cameraphones as the Korean players took a lap of honor. They waved, and screamed, and craned their necks for a clear sight of those players, who clapped back at them, then bowed to acknowledge the support. One team (and their fans) seem ready for the World Cup… And their U.S. counterparts, outnumbered and beaten, trudged back to a locker room, far removed from any World Cup excitement.
