Tigrinya, spoken by around 9 million people in Ethiopia and Eritrea, has 335. For Akan, the most widely spoken native language in Ghana, there are none. Marivate led the South African arm of the African Next Voices project, which has made recordings of 18 languages in South Africa, Kenya and Nigeria. Over two years, the three teams collected 9,000 hours of recordings from people of different ages and locations, creating a data set which will be available for AI developers across the continent to use to train models. The researchers would sometimes give native speakers scripts to read, but mostly gave them a prompt and recorded their responses, which were then transcribed. For Isindebele, spoken in South Africa and Zimbabwe, they had such a hard time finding written resources that they resorted to a government manual for goat herders to help write their prompts. African Next Voices has not collected enough data to train a large language model (LLM) like ChatGPT or Gemini, which can cover thousands of topics in detail. However, Marivate says they focused their recordings on specific topics, such as health and agriculture, that were deemed the most important. For her, it's a question of “prioritizing error.” “If somebody just wants to find out what's happening in downtown Nairobi, I can tolerate errors there,” Moorosi said, but mistakes in models that deal with topics like banking or health care could have serious consequences. Moorosi says that without this cultural understanding, it is impossible to make “AI systems perform and make judgments that are aligned to our beliefs and values.” However, a lack of data is not the only challenge facing African AI developers. Most African languages are not codified through dictionaries or grammatical studies. Without rules for spellings, even the most basic text processing becomes difficult.
