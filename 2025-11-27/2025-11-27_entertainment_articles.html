The best Taylor Sheridan shows have accumulated their loyal, and large, followings for fairly obvious reasons: They're just damn good. The 55-year-old actor turned writer-producer knows how to write compelling characters and play them off each other in distinctive settings. Notorious for flying solo and exerting tremendous control over his projects, Sheridan's said he's usually on set during the week and then writes an entire episode of one of his shows on Saturdays — all fueled by regular B-12 shots, of course. Sheridan might do well to value quality over quantity a bit more, and perhaps, with the shrinkage of the streaming ecosystem and his departure from Paramount for NBCUniversal, he'll have an opportunity to reset. Ranking Taylor Sheridan's shows is not massively difficult because there are some that clearly stand out and others that sink to the bottom. Head TV Critic Ben Travers is more of a skeptic — he certainly doesn't agree that Sheridan's success stems from great writing (great casting seems more likely, plus a convenient dearth of prestige-flavored westerns) — and he's only found one or two of Sheridan's shows worthy of weekly viewing. IndieWire's list of Taylor Sheridan's shows, ranked from best to worst, is below. Ben Travers: Not to overextend whatever credit I have as our resident “hater,” but “Tulsa King” is my preferred Taylor Sheridan show because it's the least like all of Taylor Sheridan's other shows. “Tulsa King” is about a rag-tag group of outcasts who try to build something together, rather than a haunted family who fights all the time as their long-sullied legacy falls apart. Which one of those options sounds better to you? Thankfully, Winter made his way back to the fray (off and on), and “Tulsa King” was able to preserve parts of its original tone, while still getting Sylvester Stallone's hands dirty enough to fit in with Sheridan's other tough guys. It's far from the best of either writer (Winter or Stallone), and there's certainly a more serious crime drama stirring somewhere within this silly one. But when so many of Sheridan's shows feel like medieval torture chambers (misery, violence, more misery), I'm going to savor whatever time I can get with a kingly court jester. Christian Blauvelt: The turn away from miserabilism that “Tulsa King” represents for Sheridan is a welcome one, though I can't help think that this “crime comedy” series gets a little too silly for its own good on many occasions. And I wonder how much it would hold together at all if not for the star power of Stallone at its center. I also think that the show gets better once Sheridan left: I found the early part of Season 1 to be kind of a tract-in-show-form where Sheridan could spew his anti-woke sentiments without necessarily giving them much dramatic structure. I found those early eps to be as performatively about the “culture wars” as anything Sheridan has ever done, and given an added degree of inauthenticity because it feels at times that he's really a lefty just dressed in MAGA clothing. Similarly, I found that some of the culture warrior things he said on “The Joe Rogan Experience” ended up verbatim in “Lioness” Season 2. More on that in the “Lioness” blurb, but yes, this is where I can acknowledge that I was so high on the Sheridan-verse I literally listened to his entire three-hour-plus Joe Rogan podcast, which was another reminder about what a terrible interviewer Rogan is and a massive missed opportunity given how few interviews Sheridan grants. Anyway, once “Tulsa King” actually became a show about its characters rather than making a point to ruffle PC sensibilities it became the fun romp you describe. But though we are not here to bury Sheridan, we can certainly be clear-eyed about the limitations of his work as well. Christian: I mean, if any series brings tears to my eyes for the entire final episode that has to be a massive success, and for me “1883” is in every sense Sheridan's most perfectly formed show. This is the rare 10-episode series that actually does feel like a 10-hour movie, one devoid of flab and filler but that also never puts plot before character even as it's exquisitely plotted. A wagon-train Western for the ages, “1883” gives us our chronological first glimpse of the Dutton family as an impoverished first-generation Irish clan in Tennessee heading to parts West for a better life. Yes, it focuses on the trip's grind and misery, but not for the sake of miserabilism; more to establish real stakes, meaning, and purpose. It takes five episodes just for the wagon train to get out of Texas, and though that may be partly due to Sheridan being able to film that much more on his Texas ranch, it emphasizes what those seeking a better life in the West were willing to endure – largely on foot. And like any immigrant story, there's something incredibly hopeful at its core — a hope that justifies the grind. This is also one of the only Westerns other than “Heaven's Gate” to delve deep into the extraordinary diversity of those heading West — that they were very often immigrants from Germany, Sweden, Poland, the Balkans, and all over Eastern Europe, including persecuted Roma. Like “Heaven's Gate,” there isn't much English dialogue you hear from these characters and “1883” focuses on the misunderstandings that arise simply from the language barrier. Sheridan's writing of women can also be very bad across his work… and yet, he delivered the single most complex characterization of his entire writing career in Elsa (Isabel May), whose poetic narration anchors the series. As a classic Western gunslinger who suffers the loss of his entire family from smallpox before deciding to embark on one last journey to the Pacific as a wagon train boss, he ends up tugging at your heartstrings like no one else. Elsa's narration is a crutch, and her observations are too often vague platitudes that betray the insight she should have as a storyteller who knows the whole narrative. To that end, “1883” may acknowledge the American melting pot as it spills out across the plains, but its perspective remains rooted in a white family, which makes it familiar and limited. Women characters rank second only to Native Americans when it comes to Sheridan's vexing portrayals, and “1883” doesn't exactly work to acknowledge the ways in which the country's original settlers built a functioning society. That may not be fair to the story it does tell — with passion, earnestness, and ambition — but it's a historical fact that seems to butt against the writer's preferred American origin story. (And it makes his sadistic streak harder to stomach when the pioneers start dying off in disparate droves.) Elsewhere, James and Margaret Dutton (Tim McGraw and Faith Hill, respectively) don't do much for me as protagonists (or performers), which elevates Elliott's work almost by default. (I say almost because he is quite affecting, even if that SAG Award Actors Award should've gone to Steve Carell for “The Patient.”) Perhaps if “1883” weren't tied to “Yellowstone” — if it had been a true standalone project — some of these slights may have softened. Christian: What's so invigorating about “1923” Season 1 is how much Sheridan works in pure adventure mode. It's not miserabilism for miserabilism's sake, even in the repeated, brutal beatings suffered by Aminah Nieves' Teonna, an Indigenous girl forced to live in an abusive Catholic boarding school. And of course, it's all to set up a thrilling, cross-country revenge story for Teonna, which becomes the best part of Season 2 — maybe the only genuinely satisfying part of Season 2, when all is said and done. Yes, this show fizzles a bit by the end of its 16-episode run, and it's hard not to think that it would have been better served with less. But there's no storyline more purely fun and exciting in Sheridan's canon than that of big game hunter Spencer Dutton (Brandon Sklenar, in a breakout performance for the ages) finding romance with runaway British royal Alexandra (Julia Schlaepfer) on the East African savannah. Here, Sheridan thinks in terms of images for his storytelling in a way he doesn't always: When Spencer and Alexandra are adrift on the high seas following the aforementioned tugboat captain's death, they run afoul of an abandoned ocean liner “ghost ship” that causes them to wreck as well. Unfortunately, Season 2, with the exception of Teonna's revenge arc, does see Sheridan slipping back into his miserabilist tendencies. There's a godawful storyline where Dalton's land baron endlessly engages in sadomasochistic sex with two sex workers he's hired and is trying to turn against each other: Yes, it's trying to make a point about the entanglement of economic power and sexual abuse, but the point is established early and its continued elaboration calls into question Sheridan's own motives. Ben: You've done most of my hater work for me, Christian, so I'll just say Harrison Ford does a lot to make this grim western tolerable. In a rare cowboy role for the movie star, Ford's Jacob Dutton sports a perpetual grimace that's softened by our decades of affection for the actor himself. I don't know if I'd go as far as to say Sheridan relies on that endearing quality — he's going to tell his story, his way, no matter who's in the saddle — but it's certainly helpful to his audience. Ben: “Yellowstone” aside (kind of), Taylor Sheridan doesn't exactly have an established track record when it comes to creating sustainable TV series. But between “1883” (limited series), “Bass Reeves” (canceled after one season), and the relative infancy of his other “hit” shows, it's worth wondering whether any of his TV ideas are built to last. (Even “Tulsa King,” which is about to shoot its fourth season, has faced production issues every year! So the nicest thing I can say about “Mayor of Kingstown” is that it's proven it can go the distance. Relying on its star “Avenger” (if not a star “Avenger”) and a premiere episode twist straight out of “Game of Thrones,” the dark-and-gritty Michigan crime drama has produced four seasons in five years, all of which star Jeremy Renner's ex-con turned unofficial warden of a prison (Kings)town, Mike McLusky. Along the way, it's employed legends like Dianne Wiest and Edie Falco to bring emotional nuance to a series mired in merciless pain. Christian: It is an antihero show in an age of that's moved on from antiheroes — though I would argue that the Duttons in “Yellowstone” have basically become a crime family by that point, unlike their noble presentation in “1883” and “1923” — but the reason for that may also be that “Mayor of Kingstown” was the first series Sheridan wrote even though it wasn't the first to air. So it strangely has a whiff of the mid-2010s in many respects. Not particularly memorable, but, as you've said, oddly sustainable? Ben: Ah, “Yellowstone.” The unexpected hit that became the backbone of an entire network (and then its streamer, and then, come 2029, another streamer). The Kevin Costner-starring Western certainly benefited from his star power, helping to lure an audience eager for the sweeping American grandeur captured in “Dances with Wolves” and the next iteration of iconic cowboys glimpsed in “Silverado,” “Wyatt Earp,” and “Open Range.” While Sheridan's breakthrough series certainly delivered on those fronts — John Dutton's existential battle against progress, time, and death itself proved to be the series' most arresting conflict, which was only emphasized by Sheridan's many, many, many montages of cowboys riding across the ranch's purple mountain majesty — the behind-the-scenes battles between “Yellowstone's” principal patriarchs ruined a delayed, extended, and ultimately nonsensical final season. And when I say “ruined,” I mean the best of “Yellowstone” fell to Earth alongside its omnipresent worst traits: uneven pacing, clichéd, redundant character development, and a brutal streak that made scenes too silly to take seriously and too nasty to savor. Even for those who thought parts of “Yellowstone” were worthy of the awards love that never came, the ending emphasized exactly why Sheridan's first TV series deserved exactly what it got: big ratings, little relevance. Christian: I do agree that the way “Yellowstone” ended was so disastrous it mars the entire show — to me it's far worse than what happened with the “Lost” or “Game of Thrones” finales, and there's part of me that thinks “Yellowstone” should be ranked last here as a result. Especially since “1883” and “1923” do so many more interesting things with this kind of material. That said, there are times throughout its run that you can see very clearly why it became such a phenomenon, and I think part of it is due to its pacing: This could be as languid a series as any on TV, one largely about character rather than plot (which is why the plot-heavy contortions of its final season are such a betrayal). This is a show where you suddenly could have Gil Birmingham giving a long monologue about the relationship between men and wolves, and how each exist to keep the population of the other in check — several minutes in, I let out an audible “This is great.” Having the endless mean-spiritedness of Jamie (Wes Bentley) and Beth (Kelly Reilly) curdle into conspiracy nonsense and an ultimate knife fight very much did not. This is one of the most perceptive depictions in American media of that precise moment that Reconstruction transitioned into Jim Crow. The eight-part series tells an effective story about Reeves going from enslavement to the life of wearing a badge and building a family. It's overarching plot then concerns strange rumors of a “Mr. Sundown” who is spiriting away Black men accused of (often spurious) crimes, and Reeves' investigation has compelling implications for whether slavery ever fully went away or just became the culture of incarceration that's defined American judicial life ever since. The premiere is a regurgitation of Bass Reeves' Wikipedia page, the legendary lawman's characterization is as broad and accessible as any stock cop on CBS, and the whole show only came to be because Sheridan thought he could fit Reeves into the “Yellowstone” universe, couldn't quite make it work, and then stuck his name on a seasonal anthology that only lasted one season. Like much of Sheridan's work, it may have good ideas and great production values, but it struggles with the core of great television: characters. I love Oyelowo, and he does his darnedest to liven up a rudimentary historic retelling, but his good-hearted lawman is too disengaged from the world around him. The series isn't interested in the nuanced internal conflicts that must have roiled inside a former slave who chose to protect and serve slave owners, a Black man forced to fight for the Confederate army, or even a pacifist who picked up a gun every single day. Christian: One of Sheridan's greatest skills is spotting rising talent to champion: Isabel May in “1883,” Brandon Sklenar in “1923,” and I'd argue Laysla de Oliveira in “Lioness.” The spectrum of material she's asked to play in Season 1 is extraordinary: Stuck in an abusive relationship and working as a fast-food fryer, de Oliveira's Cruz finds a way out when, during a fight with her hateful significant other, she literally stumbles into a Marine recruiting office. It puts her on a journey to becoming a covert operative in the CIA's Lioness division, where certain women agents go undercover in terrorist organizations, drug cartels, and more. Of Mexican and Syrian heritage, Cruz, who can speak Arabic, is perfect to go undercover inside an Al-Qaeda-style terrorist organization. She's to befriend the daughter of its leader, who, along with her father, leads a luxe life of designer apparel and luxury goods. I'm not sure there's an actor in any of these shows who's been asked to do more than de Oliveira. It all boils down to a remarkably critical take on U.S. adventurism abroad, down to the inspired choice of casting “Egyptian Jon Stewart” Bassem Youssef as the amiable terrorist leader. Nicole Kidman as a CIA operative with a disturbingly husky “American” accent does not lend sympathy to the U.S. cause, nor does Zoe Saldana as an operative almost pathologically capable of severing the brutality of her job from her cozy private life. It's all full of Sheridan indulgences, including him playing a devil-may-care Special Forces operative who can't keep his shirt on and spouts lines he spoke verbatim about “not being able to have a conversation” about trans issues on “The Joe Rogan Experience.” But working in an “action mode” suits Sheridan, especially as he doesn't indulge in jingoism. Ben: I'm not sure if I'm fully sold on “Lioness” as a critical take on America's international policy — for all the moral hesitancy over what their missions ultimately accomplish, there's still too much “rah rah” support for the military industrial complex to be seen as a full-on condemnation — but I have to say, as a pure narrative, I find it tremendously dull. That, and Nicole Kidman gets stuck with one of the worst line readings of her career, while Zoe Saldana barely survives her heated confrontation with the titular lioness. There's something to be said for men sitting and shouting at each other in rooms, stirring up trouble until it nearly starts a war, while the women do the real work outside in the field… but “Lioness” doesn't fit that feminist interpretation, as much as it wants you to believe it can. Ben: Before you even get into the ugliness within “Landman's” story, you have to first consider how fundamentally irresponsible it is, even as a piece of fiction. Here's a show so in love with the sound of its own ultra-conservative voice, not only is it happy to prop up the oil industry by spouting lies about clean energy, it's also happy to cash the checks Big Oil throws its way to keep spreading misinformation about why oilfields are the future and wind turbines are for weaklings. No one measures up to Tommy's standards, and he may like it that way, but it makes for an exhausting viewing experience — who wants to let some old, stuck-up asshole bullshit for hours on end, when he's so sure he knows better than everyone but remains completely oblivious that he's blabbing out of his ass? It's a testament to Thornton's tough-talking charms that “Landman” is still watchable, just as it's a black mark against Taylor Sheridan that he can't see the tragedy inherent to Tommy's story — a man trapped in the American dream as it rots beneath his feet. Then there's “Landman.” Stephen Rodrick summed it up best in Rolling Stone when he asked of “Landman,” “Why does Jerry Jones have better lines than Demi Moore?” And that's even before considering the unfortunate writing for Ali Larter and Michelle Randolph's characters. Frankly, much of what I dislike about “Landman” is what similarly rubbed me the wrong way about those early eps of “Tulsa King”: You're right, Ben, that this series is “built on provocation.” It's almost like if Billy Bob Thornton's evil assassin character in the first season of “Fargo” was suddenly given a heroic reframing — Sheridan is all-in on valorizing the petro economy. It speaks to how muddled, perhaps deliberately, his politics are that he can have the entire Dutton franchise be about preserving and respecting the land (and demonizing miners at multiple turns) and then make a show with basically the theme “fuck the environment.” It's also just incredibly wasteful when it comes to its cast: That's what you do with Jon Hamm?
